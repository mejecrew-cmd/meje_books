# Chapter 3. Why Worldview?

If verisimilitude is the quality standard for a worldview, what remains is a question of value. Why would an organization pay for work that carries waste and luxury as required elements? What does a worldview earn back? Chapter 3 answers this question in the language of industry. The skeleton of the answer is expansion: in a structure where one world stretches across multiple media, multiple works, and multiple businesses, a worldview stops being a cost and becomes infrastructure. This chapter fixes, in order, the concepts that make up that structure.

## 1. OSMU: One Source Multi Use

The most familiar name for expansion is OSMU. One Source Multi Use — the strategy of leveraging a single piece of source content across multiple media and products — is a term the Korean content industry has especially favored. The typical path runs from a successful webtoon to a drama, from the drama to a game, from the character to dolls and stationery. The economic logic of OSMU is clear. A proven source lowers the probability of failure, and a fandom already formed substitutes for the initial marketing cost. The key advantage is that a move to a new medium never starts from zero. Because the source's value survives even a failure, there is also a risk-diversification effect: one failed adaptation does not erase the fandom of the original. But the success or failure of this strategy depends on what exactly gets carried over. Carrying over only the plot makes it an adaptation; carrying over only the character makes it a licensed product. There is a separate asset that transfers undamaged even when the medium changes, and that asset is the world. The plot inevitably changes to fit the grammar of each medium, but the laws, geography, and history of the world in which those events occur remain the common ground. The more OSMU repeats, the more the substance of the "one source" shifts from a specific work toward that work's worldview. Repeated use redefines what the source actually is. The signal of this shift is the moment fans start calling a franchise by the name of its world rather than the name of its work. Inverting this observation produces the next concept.

## 2. MUOS: An Inversion of Direction

MUOS is OSMU inverted — the idea of positing multi-use first and designing the one source backward from it. If OSMU is a path that starts from a successful work and expands after the fact, MUOS is a path that starts from an expansion plan and works the source out backward. It assumes from the outset that the property will unfold into dramas, games, performances, and merchandise, and asks what kind of source could withstand all of that use. The answer to that question cannot be a single work. A completed work is already optimized to the grammar of one medium, so every move to another medium costs a retrofit. The only source that can withstand every use is something prior to any medium — the worldview. Under the MUOS regime, the worldview is not a supporting document but the first deliverable. Works are derived from the worldview, in that order. The document exists first, and content is born by referencing it. Idol-group world concepts are the representative case: the world and its settings are designed before debut, and music videos, albums, and content are released as fragments of that world. There is no single work anywhere that corresponds to a "one source," and yet the universe exists. The audience meets the world only through its fragments, and the whole of the world exists only inside the document. MUOS, however, carries risk in the opposite direction as well. OSMU is safe because it expands after verification; MUOS is an investment made before verification, so the quality of the worldview's design directly determines the business risk. This is the industrial reason design must become a profession.

## 3. Transmedia: Narrative That Crosses Media

If OSMU and MUOS are the language of business, the name for the same phenomenon in the language of narrative is transmedia. Transmedia storytelling, as media scholar Henry Jenkins codified it, refers to a mode in which a single story world unfolds split across multiple media, with each medium making its own distinct contribution. The difference from simple porting matters here. Repeating the same plot with only the medium changed is adaptation. Transmedia distributes different pieces across media — a film handling the main story, a game handling the prequel, a webtoon handling a supporting character's backstory — so that the audience assembles the whole world for themselves out of the pieces. Making the labor of assembly itself the fun of participation is where this method wins or loses. For this structure to hold, the pieces need a common ground on which to interlock. If the chronology doesn't line up, assembly fails; if place names differ, the sense that this is the same world breaks. That common ground is the worldview, and the precision of that ground is the quality of the assembly. As the number of media grows, the web of references grows exponentially, so the cost of managing that ground scales with the size of the expansion. In transmedia, the worldview document becomes more than creative reference material — it becomes the interface specification between media. Each production team maintains compatibility by referencing the same document even when they cannot see each other's work in full. Several franchises have already proven, at the cost of expensive tuition, how a transmedia project run without a specification document comes apart.

## 4. IP Expansion: From Copyrighted Work to Intellectual Property

The industry calls this asset that crosses media IP. Short for intellectual property, it means, legally, the rights to intangible assets such as copyright and trademark, but in the content industry's idiom it refers to the expandable content asset itself. A work and an IP hold different status. A work is a completed copyrighted work, and an IP is the bundle of elements extractable from that copyrighted work for repeated use — character, world, names, and symbols belong here. To say a work "became an IP" means an asset that keeps earning after the work itself has finished being consumed has been extracted from it. Looking at the list of what gets extracted reveals where the worldview stands. Characters have a lifespan: actors age, voice actors change, generational taste shifts. An individual story ends once consumed. What lasts longest is the world that held them. A world can keep producing new characters and keep absorbing new stories. What long-lived IPs share is that they start on the strength of a character and are sustained on the strength of a world. As long as the stage holds while flagship characters go through generational turnover, the fandom continues. The practical translation of "IP expansion," then, is the work of cutting new access roads into the worldview. Whoever knows where a road can be cut — that is, whoever holds the map of the world — holds the initiative in expansion. An organization that expands without a map ends up depending, every time, on the memory of an outside creator.

## 5. Attracting Investment: Why Worldview Becomes an Investment Factor

The fact of holding a map carries weight in investment screening. There is a logic of screening behind why the importance of worldview has grown large enough to become an important factor in investment. What an investor buys from content is not one finished work but a future stream of revenue. Whether a single work succeeds is almost impossible to predict, but how far it could expand if it does succeed can be screened to some degree. Screening a conditional structure rather than an uncertain single event fits the grammar of finance. The evidentiary record for that screening is the worldview document. A document with a chronology, a list of regions and characters not yet shown, and marked entry points for derivative works becomes due-diligence material for the claim that this content can extend into series, spin-offs, and other media. A project's expansion plan without a worldview document, conversely, is no different from a verbal promise. A single page of expansion roadmap in a business plan and the stack of setting documents that supports that roadmap carry entirely different weight in screening. Here the form of the document becomes an issue again. A world that exists only in the creator's head, settings scattered across chats and notes, cannot pass due diligence. What the screener examines is not the creator's passion but the organization's asset, and an asset must exist as a document. The earlier requirement — the form of a deliverable that can be handed off — returns here as a matter of money. Even the same world carries a different collateral value depending on its level of documentation. Sorting out a worldview is therefore both support for creation and preparation for asset due diligence.

## 6. The Emergence of Derivative Versions: The Diversity of Storytelling

When expansion is carried out, a phenomenon inevitably arises: the derivative version. A derivative version is a version that has forked off from the same worldview into a distinct form. The world of the original webtoon and the world of its drama adaptation are the same world and yet different. There are broadly three causes. The first is the constraint of medium: the grammar of storytelling differs by medium, so the moment of transfer forces adaptation. A novel's internal monologue must become an event on screen, and the setting gets adjusted in that process. The second is the separation of production bodies: a different company, a different director, a different writer will produce a different interpretation even when handed the same document. The difference in interpretation can be controlled but not eliminated — it can only be reduced through contracts and editorial review, and attempting total control costs you the partner. It is more realistic to set the bounds of an adapter's discretion in advance. The third is time: a world that runs on for more than a decade gets rebooted to match shifts in the sensibility of the era, and a reboot is a sanctioned derivative version. A world that holds out without renewal sinks along with its dated sensibility, so a reboot is also a condition of longevity. What matters is that all three are not accidents but the normal cost of expansion. A world with not a single derivative version is a world that never expanded. The number of versions is roughly proportional to the number of expansions that world has undergone. So the goal is not preventing forks but managing forks. The problem needs to be moved onto the ground of assuming the fork and controlling how it forks. The principles of that control are the next subject.

## 7. Managing Derivative Versions: Principles for Handling Forked Versions

The first principle for managing forks is designating the master canon. The master canon is the official version that serves as the reference when versions conflict — it specifies which version holds Canon status. The original is often the master canon, but this is not a given: there are cases where a screen adaptation takes over master-canon status, and multi-canon regimes that maintain a separate master canon per medium. Either way, the decision must be spelled out in a document; an undesignated master canon gets decided by politics once a dispute breaks out. What isn't fixed in a document ends up being decided, in the end, by whichever side speaks loudest. The second principle is recording the differences. Manage what differs between versions with a comparison table instead of hiding or neglecting it. If there is a list of settings changed in the drama version, the next derivative work can choose which side to follow. Without that list, a third version makes yet a third variation without knowing the difference between the first two, and the world diverges without control. The third principle is formalizing quarantine. Accommodate attempts that cannot coexist with the master canon by creating sanctioned quarantine zones — side-stories, parallel worlds, if-stories. Quarantine is not exclusion; it is the provision of a laboratory. A world that has room to test departures while protecting the credit of the main line lasts longer. If a path is also prepared for promoting an experiment to master-canon status once its results are verified, the quarantine zone doubles as a new department. The three principles share one denominator: turning the fork itself into an object of record.

## 8. Versatility: The Marketability of a Worldview

A world under management becomes a world that can be sold, and here the property of versatility becomes the issue. Versatility is the degree to which a worldview is less bound to a specific work, a specific medium, a specific creator. The higher the versatility, the more works a world can produce in more hands. Campaign settings sit at the extreme of versatility, because they are designed to be usable by whichever game master picks them up. In practice, famous settings keep receiving new work decades after their original author has left. At the opposite extreme are worlds inseparably fused with one writer's style. The factors that raise versatility can be named concretely: a conflict structure that doesn't depend on a specific protagonist, gaps in the chronology not fixed to a single era, and base rules that don't stray far from genre grammar. There are factors that cut versatility the other way too: a structure where a single character is the world's whole reason for existing, core settings realizable only in one specific medium, and internal circumstances entangled with a creator's personal affairs. The point to watch is that versatility is not an unconditional virtue. A world usable for anything is indistinguishable from a world with no features at all. The versatility of a blank page is not the versatility of a world. A world has to withstand many hands while still holding its own distinct laws. Versatility is a condition of marketability, not a condition of appeal, and this tension comes up again when we turn to the profession.

## 9. Fan Imagination: The Conditions Under Which Another Story Emerges

Of all the uses of a worldview, the hardest to price is fan imagination. In a well-built world, the audience begins imagining beyond the story shown to them, on their own. They guess at a supporting character's past, picture a region never shown, and tell what-ifs. This spontaneous production materializes as fan works, as the fan community's verification games, as Fanon. Seen through industry's eyes, this is unpaid content production and a churn-prevention device at once: fandom rides out the gap before the next official work with content it made for itself. The conditions under which fan imagination ignites are observable. First, a gap has to be visible. Something mentioned but not explained is the point where imagination catches fire. A world where everything has been explained, paradoxically, leaves nothing to imagine — a completed encyclopedia can be read, but not played with. Second, the edges of the gap have to be solid. The surrounding settings have to be internally consistent for inference about that gap to become play; the gap in a world without rules produces indifference, not imagination. For inference to hold together there must be clues, and clues come only from consistent surrounding settings. Third, a signal is needed that the imagination is respected. If official moves that trample Fanon repeat, the fandom stops imagining. In short, fan imagination is the result of cultivation, not free range. The arrangement of what is explained and what is left empty is exactly that cultivation design. This arrangement is another name for the same problem as managing the relationship between Canon and Fanon.

## 10. Deliverables-First: Why the Deliverable That Can Be Handed Off Comes First

One condition runs through the whole list of uses. Passing investment screening, managing derivative versions, and preserving compatibility across media all presuppose a document. This is why the first practical principle of this series is deliverables-first: the principle that at every stage of worldview work, the form of a deliverable that can be handed off takes top priority. The condition of being handoff-ready breaks down into three requirements. The recipient must be able to find the item they want, cite what they found along with its source, and identify what has been updated. These are the three requirements of being searchable, citable, and trackable. If even one of the three is missing, what exists is a document, not a deliverable. Information that cannot be found is information that does not exist; information without a source is information that cannot be trusted; information without a history is information whose staleness cannot be known. A world that lives only in someone's head, an agreement reached in conversation, fragments of documents scattered across folders — none of these satisfies any of the three. This principle may look like it collides with the romance of creation. In fact, it is the opposite. Because the document protects the world, the creator can leave with peace of mind, can return, can be replaced. A deliverable does not replace the creator; it keeps the creator from becoming a consumable. In an organization where people are held hostage by their own memory, even a vacation or a job change becomes a crisis for the world. What is the job that can produce that deliverable, and how does that job differ from a writer's? The story of use passes, this way, into the story of the profession.
