# Chapter 7. World Types III: Tradition and Genre Fiction

Having mastered the dials of time and space, we now move to the warehouse of worlds humanity has already built. From myth to medieval fantasy, traditional narrative and genre fiction are ready-made worlds with hundreds to thousands of years of accumulated precedent behind them. The guiding question of this chapter is: how does genre become the law of a world? The answer, stated first: genre is not a catalog of stories but a bundle of laws that has passed the test of repeated verification, and for the designer it is a warehouse of parts, open at no charge.

## 1. Myth: The Grammar of the World's Origin

On the warehouse's deepest shelf sits myth. Myth is the narrative that explains the origin of the world and of humankind, treating creation, the genealogy of gods, and the establishment of order. From the standpoint of worldview work, myth's standing is special, because myth is itself the first worldview work humanity ever carried out. Where did the world come from, why does it look like this, where do we go when we die — myth is what a community made when it agreed on answers to these questions, and that is exactly what the designer does for an invented world. Worldview design is the secular descendant of myth-making. In practice, myth is used in two ways. One is borrowing as raw material. Greek, Norse, and East Asian mythic systems are shared assets with no copyright, so gods, monsters, and symbols can be freely drawn from them, and just as exorcism borrows the authority of religion, a designer can borrow myth's name recognition as collateral for verisimilitude. Bring in Ragnarök from Norse myth, and the weight of the apocalypse arrives fully loaded without any explanation. The other is imitation as structure: planting a myth inside the invented world itself — an origin narrative internal to that world. A world whose inhabitants believe a creation story feels different in depth. The design implication is to design the internal myth as distinct from the facts. The gap itself, between the true history of creation and the myth the inhabitants believe, is an excellent setting resource, and story comes out of that gap. The discovery that the myth is wrong, the pursuit of a truth the myth has concealed — these are, in themselves, verified plots.

## 2. Legend and Folklore: Narrative Attached to Place and Daily Life

If myth handles the origin of the world entire, legend and folklore handle the tier beneath it. Legend is narrative tied to a specific place, person, or object: this boulder was thrown by a mighty warrior; a serpent-dragon lives in that pond. Folklore covers more broadly the beliefs and customs embedded in daily life — the taboo against stepping on the threshold; moving house on the day without the roving spirit, an auspicious day on the Korean folk calendar; and a jangseung, a village guardian totem post, at the village entrance. It is a layer more often transmitted by word of mouth than recorded, more often observed than believed. As worldview material, this layer's value lies in its locality. If myth is a world's shared, common setting, legend is setting local to a region. Planting a different legend in every village is the most proven way to give space density, and it is exactly the storied back alley of a village-scale world discussed in the previous chapter that legend occupies. Open-world games planting rumors and unofficial histories region by region use the same technique. Folklore's value lies in its closeness to daily life. Taboos and customs are laws etched into residents' everyday behavior, so they carry naturally in dialogue and description of action — a channel for showing the world without explaining the setting. The design implication is a methodology of collection. When building the legends and folklore of an invented world, sampling the structure of real folkloric material stabilizes quality. Follow the skeleton that actual legends share — the combination of physical evidence presented, origin explained, and a moral drawn — and an invented legend takes on the texture of a collected one. The reader senses a difference in texture before logic, which is why the value of learning from samples runs high.

## 3. Running in Parallel: A Mythic Layer Beside Reality

Traditional narrative does not live only in museums. In worlds set in the present day, myth and legend come back to life as a layer running parallel to reality. There is an office for the gods in the middle of the city; somewhere on a subway line, a train bound for the afterworld stops. This is the standard grammar of the strand called urban fantasy — the concealment structure of the unrealistic present discussed earlier, combined with material drawn from traditional narrative. In Korea, material from afterlife folklore and musok (Korean shamanism) has combined with this grammar to form its own distinct strand. The parallel structure is powerful because it lets a work draw on the verisimilitude of two worlds at once. The layer of reality supplies the economy of present-day fiction; the mythic layer supplies symbols and rules verified over thousands of years. The jeoseung-saja — the reaper who escorts the dead to the afterworld — and the dokkaebi, a mischievous spirit of Korean folklore, are ready-made characters needing no introduction, and folklore has already agreed on their rules. To a reader who knows about buckwheat jelly and a wrestling match, a dokkaebi's contract is a rule that needs no relearning. The design's focus is translation — the work of translating traditional beings into modern institutions and occupations: turning the afterworld into a government office, an oracle into a contract, and exorcism into a paid service. The correspondence table that carries out this translation is this type's core document. The more precise the translation, the more the friction between the two layers converts into humor and pathos. The design implication is managing distance from the source. How much of the folkloric original to preserve and how much to bend must be stated as a standard in the document. Damaging the original loses readers who know the tradition; slavishly following the original loses novelty. Setting the gauge of that distance is a design judgment, and once the gauge is documented, later creators can keep varying the work from the same distance.

## 4. Satire: A Device That Mirrors the World

The warehouse of traditional narrative also holds worlds built for a special purpose — worlds built to criticize reality, the worlds of satire. The lands of Lilliput and Brobdingnag that Gulliver visits, and the farm of *Animal Farm*, belong to this family. The structural feature of a satirical world is correspondence: its elements are designed to map one-to-one onto specific targets in reality. The pigs correspond to those in power; the farm's revisions of its rules correspond to real-world propaganda. The instant this correspondence is read, the world operates as a mirror, and the reader's pleasure in decoding that correspondence is this type's distinctive product. Seen through the lens of worldview work, satire is a design with its purpose inverted. An ordinary world is built to exist for its own sake; a satirical world is built to point outward. So the design criteria are inverted too: accuracy of correspondence takes priority over self-sufficient coherence, and the world's details are worked backward from the details of the satirized target. Just as the farm's Seven Commandments were designed with precision as a parody of a real doctrine, the precision of that backward derivation is satire's firepower. The point to watch is expiration. If the real-world target of the correspondence is forgotten, the satirical world becomes a lock with no key. Satires that survive a long time are ones that have hung their correspondence not on a specific current event but on the permanent structure of power and crowds. The design implication is dual-purpose design: a double structure that works as satire for a reader who knows the correspondence and as a self-sufficient story for a reader who doesn't extends a satirical world's lifespan. Keep the correspondence table in the document, but build the work so it stands even without that table. The moment the table is exposed inside the work itself, satire turns into a pamphlet.

## 5. The Western: The Law of the Frontier

We now move on to the shelf of genre fiction. First is the Western. This genre, set on the American frontier of the nineteenth century, has a clear world condition: the frontier, the boundary zone the nation's law has not yet reached. The sheriff is far away and the courthouse farther, so order is made not by institutions but by individuals. The gun stands in for law enforcement, the duel stands in for the trial, honor and reputation stand in for the contract. It is a world where individual quality fills the vacuum left by institutions. Every conventional scene of the Western — the tension in the saloon, the showdown at noon, the gunslinger riding off — derives logically from this one condition. Seen from a worldview standpoint, what the Western demonstrates is the power of designing by condition. Establish only two conditions — a vacuum of law and the individualization of force — and the entire grammar of the Western follows, which is why a Western can hold even when its stage is not a desert. The space Western, set on a frontier among the stars, is the proof: a planet on the edge of settlement reproduces the frontier's condition at a purity even higher than the desert does. The design implication is to register genre not by stage but by condition. Instead of writing Western-flavored in the worldview document, write this region is in a vacuum of central power, and disputes are settled by private force, and that condition will reproduce a Western story on any planet, in any era. Setting written as a condition is a highly portable asset that survives even when the stage is swapped out. This view is put to the test right away in the next two genres.

## 6. Samurai Fiction: A World of Norms

Japan's samurai fiction is often compared with the Western, and the two genres do in fact have a history of adapting each other. But the makeup of their conditions differs. In the world of samurai fiction, the individual holds force, but that force sits not in a vacuum of law but in an excess of norms. Loyalty to one's lord, the honor of the house, and the precepts of bushido form a tight net, and the sword may only be drawn inside that net. Where the Western gunslinger is solitary because he can do anything, the samurai is tragic because what he must do is already fixed. Not a shortage of freedom but an excess of obligation is this genre's air. The genre's standard conflict comes from the same place: the clash between duty and human feeling, the clash between command and conscience, and the death that the norm demands. The extreme rite of seppuku is a declaration that this is a world where the norm stands above life itself, and only a reader who has accepted that declaration receives this world's tragedy in full. As worldview material, what samurai fiction offers is a dial called density of norms. Given the same individualization of force, raise the density of norms and the world tilts toward samurai fiction; lower it and the world tilts toward the Western. The design implication is documenting the norms. When building a world like this, the honor code becomes a setting document more important than physical law. What counts as an insult; what is compelled once an insult occurs; how a violator of the norm is dealt with — this list is itself an event generator. The more elaborate the norms, the more automatically characters fall into dilemmas. Making the norm table produce the dilemma, rather than hand-crafting the dilemma yourself, is this type's design trick.

## 7. Wuxia: Jianghu as a Social System

The third test case is wuxia. This genre, born of the Chinese cultural sphere, has as its world condition the existence of an independent society called jianghu (江湖). Jianghu is a parallel society formed, outside the state's administrative reach, by those who possess martial skill — a complete system equipped with organizations called martial sects, a hierarchy of master-and-disciple relationships, intellectual property in the form of secret manuals, and a debt system of favor and grudge. The tacit premise that the imperial court exists but cannot interfere in jianghu is this world's Article One. That is why the moment someone crosses the boundary between the authorities and jianghu becomes this genre's biggest event. Looking at the makeup of its condition, the Western's power vacuum and samurai fiction's density of norms are combined here in a distinctive way. The state's law is absent, but the code of the martial world exists; the individual's force is maximized, yet bound to the order of the sect. To this, wuxia adds an ingredient of its own: the premise that martial skill is knowledge, accumulated through training and transmitted through secret manuals. Because power takes the form of knowledge, libraries, inheritance, and theft become central to the narrative, and even an institution of luck — the fated encounter — becomes possible. The convention of a secret manual found at the bottom of a cliff is a necessity produced by this system of inherited power. The design implication is the model value that jianghu offers for designing a parallel society. The skeleton of a society that overlaps with the state yet runs on its own rules is being endlessly reused — as a society of wizards, a hunters' association, a bureau for managing the superpowered. Half of modern genre fiction descends from jianghu. The association-and-guild system of hunter fiction reads as close to a modern-language translation of the martial sect.

## 8. Medieval Fantasy: The Standard Dialect of the Genre

The widest space on the genre shelf belongs to medieval fantasy — sword and magic, kingdoms and knights, elves and dwarves and dragons. This world type, whose archetype Tolkien's Middle-earth supplied and whose parts catalog Dungeons & Dragons codified, has by now become less a specific genre than the standard dialect of genre worlds. Calling it a standard dialect is no exaggeration. The composition of races, the existence of magic, a class-stratified society, even the occupational concept of the adventurer — this type's premises circulate among readers worldwide with no separate explanation needed. It is standardized to the point that even the shape of an elf's ears is agreed upon. The reason games and web novels adopt medieval fantasy as their default stage is not taste but this currency — the minimization of explanatory cost. Speak in the standard dialect, and you can move straight to the point. Seen from the standpoint of worldview work, the existence of a standard dialect is both a blessing and a trap. It is a blessing because the parts are standardized: bringing an elf onstage takes one line, and the reader fills in the rest unprompted. It is a trap for the same reason: a world assembled from nothing but standardized parts becomes a world we've seen somewhere before, with no distinguishing power. The design implication is the arrangement of standard and variation. The earlier-discussed layering of versatility and novelty is demanded most bluntly in this type. Lay the world's foundation in the standard dialect to lower the cost of entry, then twist exactly one or two points away from the standard and raise that twist as the world's signboard. A world that twists just the vital point of the standard usually travels farther than one built entirely from scratch. A single twist — a medieval world where magic is fading away, a kingdom where dragons have become bureaucrats — becomes the world's calling card.

## 9. The Inheritance of Genre Convention: Why the Western and Wuxia Resemble Each Other

Sweep the shelf, and one fact comes into view. The Western, samurai fiction, and wuxia were born on different continents, yet their skeletons match — a vacuum in central power, the individualization of force, the monetization of honor and reputation, even the drifting expert as the protagonist type. The three did not copy one another; they arrived at the same condition. Wherever central law does not reach and an individual holds power, a similar order and a similar story grow up, whatever the culture. It is a phenomenon worth calling convergent evolution. Genre convention, then, is a narrative solution that recurs under a particular condition until it hardens into form. The actual lineage also shows cross-pollination: Kurosawa's samurai films were adapted into Westerns, and those Westerns in turn influenced Hong Kong wuxia, with the genres importing each other's solutions and refining their conventions along the way. *Seven Samurai* becoming *The Magnificent Seven* is the representative case of this adaptation history. Convention, in this sense, means a part that has passed the test of this repeated use. The design implication is to take condition, not convention, as the unit of inheritance. Transplant the convention of a duel scene, and you get imitation; transplant the condition that produces a duel, and that world grows its own native duel. Reading a convention's lineage all the way back to its condition is the designer's cultivation, and that cultivation is what licenses the next topic — fusion. A fusion done without knowing the lineage is an assembly that doesn't even know where its parts came from.

## 10. Genre Fusion: How to Merge Laws

The warehouse's parts can be mixed. Genre fusion has become a basic skill of contemporary content, but the principle behind its success and failure is often misunderstood, because fusion is not a mixing of props but a merger of laws. If a historical drama with zombies fails, it isn't because zombies and hanbok clash visually — it's because the point where the two genres' laws collide was never arbitrated. When the zombie's overwhelming numbers meet the historical drama's rank-based order, the world needs to have already decided which gives way first. The craft of merging is stating priority: fixing, in advance, which side wins in a situation where the two genres' laws would give different answers. Mix wuxia with mystery, and the world needs an answer ready for the question can martial skill break open a locked room? If martial skill wins, the rules of mystery must be adjusted; if mystery wins, martial skill must be restricted. Without an answer, the reader doesn't know which genre's grammar to read by, and immersion breaks. Genre is also a rule of reading, so a text with no settled rule delivers confusion of reading method before it delivers any fun. The working procedure resolves into three steps: list each of the two genres' core laws, find the points of collision by cross-checking them against each other, and register a priority ruling at every point. The sum of these rulings becomes the fused world's own distinct law. A well-merged world calls in the readers of both genres together; a world that defers its rulings loses both sets of readers together. Whether fusion succeeds or fails turns not on the freshness of the idea but on how complete the merger rulings are. The typology's one remaining chapter is another section of this parts warehouse — the shelf of the future and of monsters.
