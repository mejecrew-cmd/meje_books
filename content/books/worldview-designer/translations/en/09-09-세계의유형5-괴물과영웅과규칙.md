# Chapter 9. World Types V: Monsters, Heroes, and Rules

The final chapter of the typology deals with cases where the being itself drags a world along behind it. Monsters like the zombie and the vampire, heroes like the superhero, arrive already carrying the conditions of the world they each require, before they are even characters. And beside them sit types like the police procedural and the mystery, where the institution and the rules themselves are the world. The guiding question of this chapter is: what kind of world do monsters and heroes require? Once this question is answered, the typology as a whole is bound together under the lens of combination, and the book moves on to methodology.

## 1. Zombie: The Monster That Became a Disaster

We begin with post-apocalyptic fiction's stock cause. The zombie is the only monster among monsters designed not as an individual but as a disaster. One werewolf is an incident; one zombie is a statistic. This monster's essence lies not in the threat of the individual but in infection as a model of spread, so a zombie story's worldview is, in effect, an epidemic-control simulation. Infection rules function as the world's physical law: what transmits it, how long the incubation period runs, what the fatality rate and conversion speed are, whether treatment and immunity exist. The combination of these figures determines the world's fate. A slow conversion makes drama; a fast one makes disaster fiction; the presence of the immune opens a pilgrimage narrative. This is the type where the power of design variables shows most nakedly — adjusting a few numbers swaps out the whole genre. The zombie's own specifications, whether it runs or walks, whether it has intelligence, belong on the same sheet. What deserves attention is this monster's narrative function. Zombies have no personality, so they cannot play the villain; the opposing party in the conflict is always the surviving humans. The zombie is a pressure device that resets the stage, and the drama comes from the human society under that pressure. The design implication follows clearly. A zombie story's setting budget should go not to designing the monster but to the infection-rules sheet and the rules of survivor society. The zombie is a supporting role; the order that collapses and the order that stands back up are the leads. The fact that what great zombie fiction shares in common is not the monster's personality but the density of its human cast proves the point.

## 2. Vampire: The Most Continuously Updated Monster

If the zombie is an anonymous disaster, the vampire is its opposite number: a monster with a name, a personality, and tastes of its own. It is also the most continuously, most repeatedly reinterpreted being among Western monsters. From the folk corpse that drank blood, to the aristocratic predator, to the cursed lover, to the modern neighbor who walks by day, the vampire has survived by putting on new clothes in every era. What made the updating possible is, paradoxically, a list of rules: bloodsucking, immortality, weakness to sunlight, silver, and holy objects, the threshold that must be crossed by invitation, a body that casts no reflection. This list outlived the characters to become the genre's shared asset, and the writers of each era made their own version by choosing what to keep from the list and what to twist. The items on the list are also terminals of metaphor. Bloodsucking translates into desire and exploitation, immortality into solitude, the threshold rule into the granting of a relationship, and which terminal a given work runs its current through decides that work's theme. The design implication is a methodology of reinterpretation. When writing a traditional monster, first organize the standard rules list as a document, then rule, item by item, on keep, change, or delete. The ruling table itself becomes that work's definition sheet for the vampire. There are cases where a single ruling to delete the sunlight weakness opened a new lineage: the daylight vampire. Keep everything and the monster grows stale; delete everything and there's no reason left for it to be a vampire — so the art of updating is, in the end, the art of editing the list. That a traditional monster is, in the end, a list of rules inherited across generations: this is the lesson this monster offers.

## 3. Werewolf: The Grammar of Transformation

The vampire's longtime neighbor is the werewolf. This monster, a human who turns into a beast at the full moon, has its core not in the beast's specifications but in the structure of the transformation. If the vampire is a monster of existence, the werewolf is a monster of state. It is ordinarily human and periodically becomes a monster, and, decisively, cannot control that conversion by itself. This condition, uncontrollable periodicity, builds the type's entire grammar. The calendar becomes a narrative device, the full moon becomes a deadline, and fear of what one will do becomes the axis of conflict. Unlike monsters whose enemy is outside, the werewolf's enemy is inside itself. In that it is an object of management rather than an object of extermination, it also touches the narrative of illness. So this monster is summoned again and again as a metaphor for the inner other: for a suppressed nature, for a double life. It's interesting that this shares its skeleton with the magical girl's double-life structure, only running in the opposite direction. In one, transformation is power; in the other, transformation is a curse; flip the sign on the same structure and the genre crosses over. The design implication is settling a transformation-rules sheet: the conditions of conversion, the cycle, the warning signs, the level of consciousness during the transformation, and whether memory survives after returning to human form. The setting of consciousness and memory in particular decides this type's temperature. If memory remains, a drama of guilt opens; if it is erased, the grammar of mystery opens. A single scene of discovering blood on one's hands in the morning is the fork between the two branches. This is a type where the morning's memory matters more than the monster's claws.

## 4. Superhero: The World of the Dual Identity

Cross over to a being that transforms but gains a calling instead of a curse, and there is the superhero. This type, a hero who protects society with superhuman power, shares its power system with superpower-battle fiction, but its world structure differs. Where the empowered of superpower-battle fiction generally compete within a society of their own, the superhero operates inside the city of ordinary people. That the superhuman and the everyday sit in the same frame is this type's identity. So the substantive setting work of a superhero world concentrates at the interface between the superhuman and society. The dual identity is the standard solution at that interface. The double life of mask and real name is the same technique as the magical girl's concealment structure, but it carries more weight in that what it protects is not only one's own daily life but the safety of those nearby. Other items at the interface are also objects of design: how far the law tolerates vigilante activity, what the relationship between government and hero is, who rebuilds a destroyed city, how citizens consume their heroes. The more seriously these questions are handled, the more a world becomes a modern superhero story. The genre's history of maturation has been, precisely, a history of the interface questions expanding. Where an early world drew only the hero's exploits, a contemporary world draws the hero registration system and the liability for damages as well. The design implication is to draft the interface document first. Design the hero-related legal system and the terrain of public opinion before the power list. A superhero story's generational turnover has always come not from power inflation but from a reinterpretation of the interface. It is the renewal of the question of what a hero is to society that has renewed the genre.

## 5. Marvel and DC: The Industrialization of the Shared Universe

The greatest legacy the superhero genre has left the worldview industry is not a character but an operating model. Marvel and DC are the specimens that have run a shared universe, decades, hundreds of writers, thousands of works sharing one world, at commercial scale. Individual works happen in different districts of the same world, and crossovers, in which characters move in and out of each other's works, are held as regular events. This model forced the invention of nearly every technique of worldview management. Continuity management, maintaining setting consistency across thousands of works, became a standing job function; reboots and crisis events that liquidate accumulated contradictions became institutionalized; and a multiverse system numbering the world lines was introduced as the management apparatus for canon and derivatives. This is the site where the three principles of derivative-version management discussed earlier were field-tested at the largest scale there is: designating the canon, recording the differences, and formalizing quarantine all run here as standing institutions. The successes and growing pains of the movie universes have shown, further, what transplants and what breaks when this model changes medium. The design implication is the value of the case study. Even for a designer who will never build a shared universe, the record of failure the two companies have accumulated is required reading for every designer. In particular, the cycle by which a crossover's box-office success inflates the debt of continuity, and that debt then blocks new readers from entering, is a pathology every world that dreams of expansion needs to know in advance. By the time the question where do I even start begins to come up, the world has already entered that cycle.

## 6. Fable: A World Where Animals Speak

Move to a different shelf to look at one of the oldest types of all. The fable is a world where animals speak and act like humans, with a history running thousands of years back to Aesop. This type's world rules look simple but are precise. Animals wear their species' conventional character: the fox is cunning, the lion is dignified, the sheep is meek. This typing is the fable's engine. Because species equals character, no character description is needed; the role reads the instant the animal appears. Satire's structure of correspondence is, in effect, already completed at the level of species, and in practice the fable has long served as satire's vehicle. Borrowing an animal's mouth let a writer call a ruler a lion and criticize him while routing around punishment. This is why the fable has flourished in every age of censorship. Contemporary fabular worlds, animated films and comics with animal characters, draw on the same asset. A species stereotype is a character specification that reads instantly, and twisting that specification, a cowardly lion, an honest fox, becomes exactly where a contemporary theme can sit. The design implication is managing a species chart. In a fable-type world, the list of species is itself the character grammar book. It must register the conventional character assigned to each species, the rules governing relationships between species, and the policy for handling ecological conditions such as carnivore and herbivore. The last item especially, how the world handles the ecological reality of predation, is the pressure point that decides a fable world's tone. Fudge it, and you get a world of childlike innocence; face it directly, and you get a social drama wearing a fable's mask. Contemporary animated films that have taken on the coexistence problem between carnivores and herbivores head-on are successful cases of the latter.

## 7. Märchen and Mother Goose: The Laws of the Fairy Tale

On the shelf next to the fable sits the fairy tale. Märchen is the German word for folktale and fairy tale, and the oral stories collected by the Brothers Grimm are its standard sample. Mother Goose is the umbrella name for the English-speaking world's traditional nursery rhymes, short songs carried down on rhyme. What makes this old stratum important as worldview material is its own distinct system of law. The fairy tale's world runs not on the causality of reality but on the causality of story. Events repeat three times; a promise is always tested; a taboo is always broken; good and evil are settled at the end. These rules of number, repetition, and retribution are a form that the medium of oral transmission itself refined, because only structures easy to remember survived. Oral transmission was an editing process centuries long, and everything that remains is a survivor. Contemporary content uses this stratum in two ways. One is reinterpretation: twisting shared assets like Snow White and Little Red Riding Hood into new stories, the same technique as editing a vampire's rules list. The other is borrowing the grammar. Transplant the fairy tale's causality into a modern setting, and a distinctive tone emerges in which the laws of story invade the everyday world. The same principle is why Mother Goose rhymes are so often used as material for horror: the gap between a gentle meter and unsettling lyrics is already a finished device. The design implication is the explicit registration of the fairy tale's laws. When building a fairy-tale-styled world, its story causality, the number of repetitions, the rules of retribution, must be documented with the same standing as physical law. Once the law wavers, the fairy-tale world just becomes a strange world.

## 8. Police Procedural: A World Institutions Make

Past the shelves of monster and fairy tale comes the shelf of institution. The police procedural is a type that takes an institution, the investigative agency, as its world. This type's claim to a place in the typology is clear: a police organization is a complete world that adds its own distinctive components to the corporate grammar covered earlier. Rank and chain of command, the territorial rule of jurisdiction, the license of power that is investigative authority, the behavioral rule of warrants and procedure, adjacent powers such as the prosecution and the press, the parts that make up the world are already installed inside the institution. The designer's job is not to invent the parts but to acquire them accurately. So a police procedural's worldview work is not creation but, as with present-day fiction earlier, mainly reporting and verification: collecting the real institution's specifications precisely, and explicitly managing whatever deviations dramatic need requires. This type's distinctive tension comes from procedure. Procedure is both the instrument of justice and its shackle. You cannot open a door without a warrant; you have to let a suspect go if the evidence falls short. The point where procedure and truth diverge is this genre's standard conflict, and the gauge between an investigation that keeps to procedure and one that crosses it decides the work's color. The design implication is documenting that gauge. Settle, as policy, how far this world's police can depart from procedure and what the cost of departure is. A police procedural with no gauge wavers on different ethics episode to episode, and the audience senses that wavering precisely. Whether this is a world where breaking the rules is permitted or punished is, in this genre, the first article of the worldview.

## 9. Mystery: A World Where the Rules Are the Genre Itself

At the far end of the institution shelf sits a type where the rules are the whole of the world: the mystery. In this genre, a puzzling crime is presented and a detective uncovers the truth through logic, and from a worldview standpoint it is a peculiar case, because the genre's rules are strung not through the world within the work but between the writer and the reader. Mystery's core contract is fair play: the principle that every clue must be disclosed to the reader, and that a solution must not rely on information known only to the detective. This contract even has a history of being written down. Rule lists such as Knox's Decalogue and Van Dine's Twenty Rules were actually drafted, establishing clauses such as the ban on the supernatural and the ban on concealing clues as the genre's law. Across the whole of the typology, this is the one genre where the contract of permission exists as a written document. There is even a named offense for breach of contract: the term after-the-fact trick is this genre's heaviest accusation. From a world-design standpoint, mystery demands precise reverse-engineering. The truth is fixed first, and the placement of clues and the design of misdirection are calculated backward from the ending. Every last detail of the world must be either a clue or camouflage, so unlike worldview work in general, which treats waste as a virtue, this is a type where the function of every setting is audited. The design implication concerns the question of rule priority when fusing genres. When mixing mystery with another type, the first thing to rule on is whether that world's supernatural elements can intervene in the trick. A locked room in a world with magic becomes a puzzle only when the limits of magic are registered. The subgenre known as special-premise mystery is the success story that turned this ruling into the genre's new source of fun.

## 10. Summing Up the Typology: Types Are Combined

The shelf-organizing across five chapters is finished. To close, one fact that runs through the whole typology is confirmed: a type is not a sorting bin but a part, and in practice types are always combined. Looking back at the types covered so far, they were already cases of combination. Magical girl fiction was a combination of the concealment structure, the double life, and the contract; isekai was a combination of the gate rule and medieval fantasy's standard language; space Western was a combination of the physics-handling of space and the frontier condition. The very way a new type is born is a recombination of existing types. The new continents of genre history have mostly been discovered on the sea lanes between existing continents. What makes combination possible is each type's decomposability. Once the core condition and the derivative conventions are distinguished for each type, the condition can be lifted out on its own and merged with another type's condition: lifting only the frontier condition out of the Western and planting it in space, lifting only the uncontrollable periodicity out of the werewolf and planting it in a superpower. The merging procedure is exactly the one fixed under genre fusion: listing the conditions, cross-checking the points of conflict, ruling on priority. So the five chapters of the typology were, in fact, a parts specification all along. From the dial of tense, to the scale of space, to the conditions of tradition, to the grammar of the speculative, to the requirements of being, the designer now knows the parts on the shelf. The design implications attached to each part, which sheet to build, what to settle first, become, as they are, the material of the process. What remains is the process itself: in what order, with what tools, the parts are assembled. Part Three's methodology is that process chart.
