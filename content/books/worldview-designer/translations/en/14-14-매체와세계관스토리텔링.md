# Chapter 14. Media and Worldview Storytelling

A world that has been given a system is now ready to be told. Part 4 deals with the phase in which a world is output as content. When the same world becomes a novel, a webtoon, and a song, each medium demands a different face of the world. The guiding question of this chapter is: why is the same world told differently in each medium? The answer is the material nature of the medium. The forms of space, time, and participation govern the narrative, and the designer must prepare a different cross-section of the world for each medium.

## 1. Formal Characteristics in General: The Medium Governs the Narrative

We settle the principle first. A medium is not a neutral vessel that holds a story; it is a frame that governs the story's shape. The variables that govern it sort into three. First, the total amount of space. A single poem, a sprawling multi-volume novel, and a once-a-week serial differ in the sheer amount of world they can hold. This total dictates the resolution at which the world is exposed. Second, control over time. A novel's reader can read at their own pace and go back, but the audience of a video is carried along by time as it flows. A medium in which control rests with the audience can sustain complex settings; a medium in which control rests with the medium itself allows only settings that are grasped instantly. Third, the channel of the senses. Depending on whether delivery runs through text, image, sound, or an actor's body, which components of the world live and which die is decided. Interiority is the territory of text, and the air is the territory of sound. A component the channel cannot carry gets translated into another component or discarded. The combination of these three variables is each medium's formal characteristic, and this is precisely the basis for the principle, in transmedia, of assigning a different fragment to each medium. It is also why adaptation between media is difficult. Adaptation is not translation; it is redesign into a different combination of variables. Much of the debate over a source work being damaged in adaptation comes from differing understandings of just how unavoidable this redesign is. The design implication is the writing of a medium profile. For every medium a work will enter, laying out the values of the three variables and the requirements that follow from them on a single page gives the skeleton of a per-medium briefing document. The nine topics that follow are the detailed treatment of those profiles.

## 2. Tarot Cards: The Archetype of Combinatorial Storytelling

For the first medium, we place something unexpected: tarot cards. Seventy-eight cards of symbolic imagery are, before they are a divination tool, from the standpoint of worldview storytelling the oldest combinatorial narrative machine there is. The structure makes this clear. Each card is a keyword: the Tower, the Lovers, the Hermit, Death. Every card carries a definition, has symbolism loaded onto it, and even has upright and reversed variation built in. And the cards generate a story by being arranged. The same cards produce different narratives when the combination drawn and the positions laid differ, and the reader completes the story by interpreting the relationships between cards. This is the proposition confirmed in the keyword method, that the combination of finite parts gives birth to infinite stories, realized as a physical device. The principle of the contact point between definition and narrative is also at work here. The instant the Tower and the Lovers are laid side by side, a question is born, and the question calls a narrative. Tarot offers the designer two lessons. One is as a model. A single card's composition — the bundle of name, image, keyword, and symbolic gloss — is an old archetype of the datasheet, and organizing a world's core keywords into card format is excellent practice. The other is as a tool. The technique of actually building a custom deck from a world's keywords and drawing story seeds through random arrangement is used as an idea-generating tool in the writers' room. It is also a test of whether the world is in a combinable state. A world for which no deck can be built means its keywords lack independence and loaded symbolism.

## 3. Poetry, Song, and Audio: The Narrative of Compression and Hearing

We move to the medium group with the smallest amount of space: poetry, song, and audio content. The formal characteristic of these media is extreme compression combined with a single auditory channel. The lyrics of one song run a few manuscript pages at most, and there is no room in that space to explain a world. So this medium group does not explain the world; it evokes it. Rather than the whole of the world, it carries a cross-section of feeling; rather than the trunk of a narrative, the image of a single scene, and the listener's imagination fills in the rest. In worldview practice, this medium group holds two positions. One is as the world's entrance. Theme songs and image songs often become the first point of contact with a world, and a few minutes of auditory experience decides the world's first impression. K-pop's use of worldview is the peak of this route: the structure of carrying fragments of the world in music videos and lyrics and letting the fandom interpret them realizes transmedia's pleasure of assembly at the scale of a single song. The other position is as an artifact internal to the world. This is the work of designing songs that exist inside the world itself: a kingdom's marching songs, sailors' work songs, children's nursery rhymes. As Mother Goose shows, nursery rhymes within a world are a device that compresses and preserves the world's history and feeling, and they have long been proven as a vehicle for foreshadowing as well. The design implication is the preparation of emotional keywords. What this medium group needs supplied is not a summary of settings but the symbol register and the attitude sheet. What fuels this medium is not the world's facts but the world's mood.

## 4. The Novel: The Medium Ruled by Narration

The main stronghold of the textual medium: the novel. The novel's formal characteristics are the existence of a narrator and the reader's control over time. This combination makes the novel the overwhelming medium in worldview capacity. The narrator can go anywhere and say anything. Even a character's interiority, a hundred-year-old backstory, or the structure of an institution can be delivered in a single paragraph of narration, and the reader can linger until it is understood and go back to reread. It is nearly the only medium in which the direct explanation of settings is permitted. The web novel is this form combined with the rhythm of serialization. In exchange for requiring episode-length cuts and a hook in every installment, there is effectively no ceiling on total length, so it can dig the world deepest of all. It is no accident that rule-centered types such as regression fiction and status-window fiction bloomed in the web novel. The fine-grained operation of rules is the territory of text and long breath. The novel's trap sits in the same place. Because it is a medium where explanation is permitted, it is also the medium where the abuse of explanation — setting recitation — occurs most often. That the allowance is large and that readers enjoy it are two different things. The design implication is adjusting the form of supply. Novelists should be supplied with detailed documents that reach down to the world's deepest layers, but the principle that exposure is subordinate to the needs of the narrative must be shared at the contract stage. The novel is the medium where the distinction between the warehouse and the contract is most urgently needed. The greater the freedom granted, the more the discipline must be self-imposed.

## 5. Webtoons, Serialized Comics, and Comics: The Grammar of Panels and Serialization

We move to the visual medium. The formal characteristics of webtoons, serialized comics, and comics are the panel as a unit and the rhythm of serialization. The story is delivered broken into panels, and the reader fills in the leap between one panel and the next. This grammar governs the way worldview is exposed. The narrator's textual explanation dies in a drawn image. In its place, the background comes alive. The street scenery, signage, clothing, and props drawn into a panel's background carry the world without explanation, and the reader reads the world without even noticing they are reading it. This is a medium in which the density of the worldview is translated into the density of background art. The webtoon's vertical scroll adds a variable unique to it. Control over time survives in the reader's hand, since the speed of scrolling belongs to the reader, but the screen's vertical flow governs the staging, and the episode cuts of weekly serialization govern the narrative's rhythm. The page grammar of comics and the pacing of the collected volume are yet another combination. The practical focus is supplying visual specifications in advance. In a visual medium, the world's specifications must be settled not in writing but in drawing. From the appearance of each species, the system of costume, the style of architecture, down to heraldic crests and marks, the visual settings bible is the body of this medium's briefing, and where it falls short, a world with a different-looking background gets drawn every episode. The more the production structure relies on multiple assistants, the greater the value of a visual master canon. The design implication is turning background art into a settings asset. Visual details born during the drawing process are targets for reverse extraction. Only when there is a routine that recovers, into the document, the settings born in the act of drawing do the world drawn in the background and the world of the master canon avoid diverging from each other.

## 6. Animation and Music Video: Controlled Footage

We move to moving images. The formal characteristics of animation and music video are a fully controlled screen and time owned by the medium. Unlike live action, every pixel on screen is a decision made by the production team, so there is, in theory, no limit to the visual realization of a world. A planet with different gravity, a dream space with different logic — both can be drawn. In exchange, since the audience has no control over time, settings must be understood instantly within the flowing screen, and since every frame costs money, the world's exposure is edited by the second. This combination determines the form worldview supply takes. In animation production, the settings bible exists as an institutionalized step in the process. The practice by which art design, character design, and prop design are finalized before the drawing begins, becoming a reference document for the entire staff, is, together with historical fiction's fact-checking dossier, a living specimen of worldview deliverable standards. The reason the animation industry has driven the culture of worldview documentation lies in the process structure itself. The music video is this same controlling power condensed into a length of a few minutes. Since there is no time to complete a narrative, it competes on the density of symbols and scenes, and the grammar of scattering fragments of the world to induce interpretation has become standard. The reason the music video is the lead medium of idol worldviews is the fit between this grammar and the fandom's play of interpretation. The design implication is a sense of the cost per frame. For a video-medium briefing, priority is everything. A ranked list of what absolutely must appear on screen in this world must be supplied before the full list of settings.

## 7. TV Drama, Film, and OTT: The Standards of Video Narrative

We move to live-action video. The formal characteristics of TV dramas, films, and OTT series are the standard running time and the physics of production budget. A film demands a self-contained arc of roughly two hours; a drama demands about an hour per episode and a breath measured in seasons, and this time standard is the first cut that sizes the world it can hold. The condition of being live-action is the second cut. Since every visual element of the world converts into a budget for sets, locations, and visual effects, the scale of the worldview becomes, directly, the scale of the production budget. If the designer takes part at the video-planning stage, they end up jointly working out the economic design of the world — the structure that implies a large world through a small stage. This is where the technique of the low-resolution beyond gets translated into the language of budget. The rise of OTT series has changed this medium group's worldview capacity. A long-form breath spanning repeated seasons permits an accumulation close to the novel's, and simultaneous global release has moved the problem of a worldview's cultural translation up to the planning stage. The source world's names and symbols are put to the test of subtitles and dubbing. This is the point where the notation and translation-principle items of the naming conventions get put into actual practice. The design implication is preparing for adaptation negotiations. Since live-action adaptation forces the compression and transformation of a world, it is the designer's practical task, in dealing with this medium, to see that the principles of derivative-version management — namely the master canon's standing, the record of changes, and the scope of discretion — are written into the contract.

## 8. Theater and Musical: The Grammar of the Live Stage

We move to the stage. The formal characteristics of theater and musical are liveness and physical constraint. The audience and the actors occupy the same time and space, there is only one stage, and scene changes happen right before the eyes. This constraint forces, in the realization of a worldview, a grammar exactly opposite to video's. Because you cannot show, you symbolize. A single chair becomes a throne, the color of the lighting becomes the world's time of day, an actor's body becomes a monster. The audience enters knowing this bargain, and this is also the medium where the suspension of disbelief operates most generously. The audience willingly reads the stage's obvious artifice as the world itself. From the standpoint of worldview supply, this grammar changes the requirements. What the stage wants is not the panorama of the world but its condensate. If this world had to be expressed with one chair and three lights, what would you keep? The priority order of the symbol register, the world's emotional core, and the minimal grammar of space become the content of a stage briefing. The musical combines this with the grammar of song. The form in which the peak of emotion leaps into song demands that the world's emotional axis be laid out song by song, and the list of numbers effectively becomes a map of the world's emotions. Which emotion earns a song is, precisely, this world's emotional priority. The design implication is the value of the condensation test. Even without a plan to stage the work, the exercise of compressing this world into three props and one scene becomes a tool for the designer to interrogate themselves about what the world's core actually is. A world that survives condensation is a strong world.

## 9. Games: The Narrative the Player Intervenes In

We move to the medium of participation. The formal characteristic of games is player intervention, and this one thing creates a fundamental difference from every other medium. The audience of other media receives a completed narrative, but the player of a game acts within the world. The game's world must therefore be designed not as a narrative but as a set of possibilities. Not what happens, but what can happen; not the story, but the rule system for how the world responds to the player's actions, is the substance of a game's worldview. This fully explains why the TRPG's campaign setting was the archetype of the worldview deliverable. The setting book was, from the start, a document standard for a world that presupposed intervention. The digital game brings this premise down to the level of implementation. Since the world's rules must be executed as code, the settings must be translatable into numeric values and conditional statements. Just as status-window fiction re-imported the grammar of games, game worldview documents are, by birth, closely akin to the datasheet. The settings for regions, items, and factions become, directly, the spec sheet for content, and a gap in the settings translates directly into a gap in implementation. Live service adds an axis of time. A world that is operated grows with every update — a world where the registration gate and version control turn over week by week. The design implication is the design of response. The core question of a game briefing is: if the player touches this, how does the world respond? A world rich in rules of response is a living game world, and the source of those rules is the designer's document.

## 10. Merchandise: Storytelling by Objects

The last medium is a medium with no narrative. Merchandise, that is, goods derived from the world. Figures, stationery, apparel, replicas, even themed spaces — merchandise carries no story. There is only the object. And yet the fan receives the world from that object. How is this possible? Because merchandise is the materialization of a keyword. Merchandise is the technique of reification realized in industrial form. A sword-shaped umbrella carries the keyword of the world's iconic weapon onto an everyday object, and a replica of an academy badge makes the intangible fact of belonging touchable. What the fan buys is not a thing but contact with the world, and well-made merchandise appoints its owner a resident of the world. A single school-uniform badge is the mechanism by which a sense of belonging is sold. This medium's requirement is therefore clear. To become merchandise, the world must have keywords capable of materialization. A list of identifiable symbols, emblems that mark belonging, and objects that carry meaning within the world is the raw material of merchandise planning, and a world for which this list is empty has nothing to make but a cup with a logo stamped on it. Merchandise is also the final test of worldview design. If a world can be identified and desired through objects alone, with no support from narrative, that is evidence the world's keywords and symbol system are close to complete. The design implication is the advance design of a materialization list. Mark, at the initial design stage, which of the world's keywords are capable of becoming objects. At the end of the medium chain, the world sheds narrative and remains as objects, and a world that stands even as objects is a world that has passed through every medium.
