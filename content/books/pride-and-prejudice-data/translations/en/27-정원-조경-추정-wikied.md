# *Pride and Prejudice* — Gardens and Landscaping (Inferred/Created)

> ⚠️ **This document is inferential and creative.** It reconstructs the gardens by combining landscape fragments explicitly mentioned in the novel—wilderness, hermitage, shrubbery, gravel walks, paling, and so on—with eighteenth- and nineteenth-century English landscape-garden styles and the scale of each estate.

---

## Overview of Eighteenth- and Nineteenth-Century English Garden Styles

### Capability Brown Style (Naturalistic)
- Mainstream from the late eighteenth to the early nineteenth century
- **Natural beauty with visible artifice removed**
- Curving lakes, broad lawns, and clustered tree planting
- **Pemberley is the finest expression of this style**

### Repton Style (Brown's Successor)
- More elaborate detail
- Decorative elements such as wildernesses and hermitages

### Picturesque
- Nature composed like a landscape painting
- Rugged rocks, waterfalls, and similar features

## 9. Narrative Functions of the Gardens

### Pemberley Garden
- **A visualization of Darcy's character** — natural beauty, restraint, and genuine elegance
- The setting for Elizabeth's change in perception
- "this place" — the place of which she might have been mistress

### Rosings Garden
- **Lady Catherine's display** — formality and artifice
- A space of authority

### Longbourn Garden
- **The wilderness** = stage for Lady Catherine's threats (Ch. 56)
- **The walk** = stage for Darcy's second proposal (Ch. 58)
- **The garden itself becomes the scene of action**

### Hunsford Garden
- Collins's refuge and Charlotte's peace
- The view of Rosings = dependence on a patroness

## 10. Seasonal Changes in the Gardens

### Autumn (Opening of the Novel, September–November)
- Turning oak and beech leaves
- Apple and pear harvest
- The last roses

### Winter (December–February)
- Evergreens stand out: yew, holly, laurel
- Flower beds lie dormant
- "remains of a white frost" (Ch. 30)

### Spring (March–May) — The Hunsford Period
- Buds and fresh foliage
- Spring flowers: daffodils, tulips, lilac
- "weather was so fine" (Ch. 30)
- "every day was adding to the verdure of the early trees" (Ch. 35)

### Summer (June–August) — The Pemberley Period
- Abundant greenery
- Roses in full bloom
- Dense riverside growth
- Hothouse fruit harvest

## 11. Social Meaning of Garden Walks

| Type of Walk | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Walking alone** | Reflection and emotional composure (Elizabeth's walks at Hunsford) |
| **Walking with a sister** | Private conversation (Jane and Elizabeth in the shrubbery) |
| **A man and woman walking together** | A sign of courtship (Darcy and Elizabeth at Pemberley and Longbourn) |
| **Alone together in the garden** | A decisive stage of intimacy (proposal in Ch. 58) |
| **Leading someone into the wilderness** | Forcing a private conversation (Lady Catherine in Ch. 56) |
