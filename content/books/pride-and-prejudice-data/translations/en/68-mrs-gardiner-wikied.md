# Mrs. Gardiner

## Basic Information
- **Original name**: Mrs. Gardiner
- **Korean rendering**: Mrs. Gardiner
- **Relationship**: Mr. Gardiner’s wife; maternal aunt by marriage to Elizabeth and Jane
- **Former residence**: Lambton, Derbyshire, where she lived for “ten or twelve years” before marriage
- **Current residence**: Gracechurch Street, London
- **First appearance**: chunk_07 (Chapter 25)

## Character Profile
A sensible and elegant woman who forms a complementary pair with Mr. Gardiner. The three adjectives “amiable, intelligent, elegant” summarize her nature. If Mrs. Bennet and Mrs. Philips represent the extreme of undiscerning aunts, Mrs. Gardiner is their counterpoint and Elizabeth’s model of adult womanhood. Despite their relatively small age difference, she succeeds in transforming the aunt–niece relationship into one of friendship and mentorship.

Her narrative function unfolds in three stages. First, she plants a seed of reason in Elizabeth’s judgment through an early warning about Wickham. Second, she guides Elizabeth to Pemberley on the Derbyshire journey, creating the setting for Darcy’s rehabilitation. Third, her letter reveals Darcy’s secret intervention, making her the decisive bearer of information that confirms Elizabeth’s love. Few female mentors in Austen’s novels function so consistently and effectively.

Her former residence in Lambton is the device that makes this plot possible. Her prior knowledge of Pemberley and the elder Mr. Darcy makes the travel plan natural and supplies an impartial third-party perspective on Darcy.

## Development by Event

### Act I: Wise Counsel (Chapters 25–27)
- **EVT-022-001** During the Christmas visit, she notices Elizabeth’s interest in Wickham and cautions her gently: marriage to a man without fortune would be imprudent. Rather than issuing a direct prohibition, she respects Elizabeth’s judgment and appeals to reason.
- **EVT-023** She cares for Jane during her stay in London, preserving Jane’s dignity as they endure Miss Bingley’s delayed return visit.
- **EVT-024** She proposes a summer tour of the Lakes to Elizabeth, a proposal that later becomes the starting point of the Derbyshire journey.

### Act II: Derbyshire and Pemberley (Chapters 42–45)
- **EVT-034** When Mr. Gardiner’s schedule is shortened, the destination changes from the Lakes to Derbyshire. She decides to stay in Lambton, where she once lived for ten years.
- **EVT-035** She suggests visiting Pemberley and hears Mrs. Reynolds praise Darcy. Her existing knowledge of the elder Darcy lends further credibility to the testimony.
- **EVT-036** She meets Darcy in person and is astonished by his behavior, wondering how this man could have been so proud toward Elizabeth.
- **EVT-037** She is introduced to Georgiana Darcy with Elizabeth. She keenly detects the feeling between Elizabeth and Darcy but refrains from premature interference.

### Act III: Revelation and the Confirmation of Love (Chapters 46–52)
- **EVT-038** On hearing of Lydia’s elopement, she returns to Longbourn with the others.
- **EVT-041-002** She receives Elizabeth’s letter asking why Darcy attended the wedding.
- **EVT-042** **The decisive letter.** She recounts Darcy’s secret intervention in detail: locating Wickham, paying Mrs. Younge for information, settling the debts, purchasing the ensign’s commission, and providing the settlement. She ends by asking her niece to tell her whether there is some reason her aunt cannot help taking Darcy’s side, gently testing Elizabeth’s feelings. **The letter becomes the catalyst that confirms Elizabeth’s gratitude and love.**

### Conclusion (Chapter 61)
- **EVT-048** At the wedding, the Gardiners appear as the most beloved relatives.
- **EVT-049** They visit Pemberley frequently, and Darcy welcomes Mrs. Gardiner sincerely—a symbol that he has completely overcome his prejudice against the commercial class.

## Core Relationships
- **Elizabeth**: Friend, mentor, and confidante; a rare adult who offers rational counsel without maternal interference
- **Jane**: Cares for her during the London stay and supports her emotionally in the Bingley affair
- **Darcy**: Initially the object of prejudice → reassessed at Pemberley → his secret intervention witnessed → most warmly welcomed relative after the marriage
- **Wickham**: Remembers something of his former reputation in Derbyshire, giving her grounds to warn Elizabeth
- **Mrs. Bennet**: Her sister-in-law; she avoids direct conflict but keeps her distance because of their intellectual disparity

## Translation Notes
- Mrs. Gardiner’s speech and letters exemplify Austen’s ideal of female intelligence: concise yet dignified, and careful not to wound the listener’s pride even when offering advice. Preserve this tone of “restrained intelligence.”
- The letter in EVT-042 is among the novel’s densest passages of exposition. Clearly distinguish chronology, the account of Darcy’s motives, and Mrs. Gardiner’s own emotional comments.
- Preserve the irony between the class contempt in “uncle and aunt in Cheapside” and Mrs. Gardiner’s demonstrably superior cultivation; do not blur the gap between disparaging language and character portrayal.
- Her forms of address for Elizabeth, including “Lizzy” and “my dear niece,” signal intimacy and differ in tone from other adults’ “Miss Bennet.”
