# *Pride and Prejudice* — Religious Life (Inferred/Created)

> ⚠️ **This document is inferential and creative.** Religion is explicitly present in the novel chiefly through Collins's profession and a few references to Sundays. Regency-era English gentry, however, were members of the Church of England, and Sunday worship was nearly obligatory. This document reconstructs the religious life of each family from the period context.

---

## Historical Background: The Church of England in Regency Britain

### Essentials
- **Established church: the Church of England, or Anglican Church**
- All characters are Anglican by default
- **Roman Catholics, Dissenters, and Jews** face discrimination
- Sunday worship is a social duty

### Church Hierarchy
```
Archbishop of Canterbury
  ↓
Bishop — diocesan level
  ↓
Rector — parish priest receiving the whole tithe
or Vicar — parish priest receiving part of the tithe
  ↓
Curate — poorly paid assistant clergyman
```

## 2. A Sunday Service (Inferred)

### A Typical Sunday

#### 8 a.m.
- Rise; change from morning dress into church clothes
- Women: a neat walking dress and bonnet
- Men: black or dark suits

#### 10 a.m.
- **Morning Service**
- Approximately one and a half to two hours
- Uses the *Book of Common Prayer*

#### Order of Worship (Anglican Standard)
1. Morning Prayer
2. Litany
3. Holy Communion, weekly or monthly
4. **Sermon**, usually thirty to sixty minutes
5. Hymns

#### Afternoon or Evening (Possible)
- **Evening Service**, shorter
- Attended by only some parishioners

#### Restrictions on Sunday Activities
- No balls or social events
- No cards or gambling; discouraged even at home
- Walking is acceptable after church
- Light reading is acceptable: the Bible or religious books

## 3. Religious Tendencies by Family (Inferred)

### The Bennets — Conventional Anglicans

#### Attendance
- The family attends every Sunday, by carriage or on foot
- The novel never says Mr. Bennet accompanies them → he may sometimes stay home ★, given his cynical temperament
- Mrs. Bennet never misses church as a social occasion

#### Faith
- **Mr. Bennet:** presumed skeptic who observes only the forms
- **Mrs. Bennet:** shallow belief, socially observant
- **Jane:** possibly sincere and morally serious
- **Elizabeth:** rational faith, between skepticism and observance
- **Mary:** **the most religious**; fond of Fordyce's sermons and moral instruction
- **Kitty/Lydia:** almost indifferent, thinking of officers even on Sunday

#### Sunday Dinner
- Dinner proceeds as usual
- Cards and music are restrained
- Mary sometimes performs religious music, with characteristic self-satisfaction

---

### Collins — A Zealous Anglican Clergyman

#### Duties
- **Rector of Hunsford Parish**
- One principal sermon each week, on Sunday morning
- An afternoon service when possible
- Conducts some weddings, baptisms, and funerals each week

#### Routine
- Prays at dawn every day
- Writes sermons during the week and boasts of it to Mr. Bennet
- Visits parishioners—the sick and poor—as a duty, but reluctantly

#### Faith
- **Preoccupied with appearances, deficient in substance**
- Lady Catherine's influence outweighs theology
- Hypocritically adjusts tithe arrangements to please his patroness

---

### Lady Catherine — Religion as Power

#### Attendance
- Attends Hunsford church on Sundays
- Has a private, formal **family pew**
- May review Collins's sermons in advance

#### Faith
- **Uses religion as an instrument of power**
- Patroness of Collins
- A justification for controlling the poor
- The sincerity of her faith is doubtful

---

### Darcy — A Serious Steward

#### Role
- **Patron of several livings**—Ch. 16: “considerable patronage”
- Has the right to appoint clergymen
- Maintains churches on his estates

#### Attendance
- Attends the Pemberley parish church on Sundays
- Uses the large family pew
- Georgiana accompanies him

#### Faith
- **Serious but undemonstrative**
- Possesses a strong sense of moral responsibility
- His anger at Wickham is moral anger, especially over Georgiana
- Deepens after his own reformation

---

### The Bingleys — Sociable Anglicans

#### Attendance
- Attend Sunday service as a social duty
- Miss Bingley displays her formal church attire

#### Faith
- **Bingley:** kind and easygoing, but not deeply devout
- **Miss Bingley/Mrs. Hurst:** conventional
- **Mr. Hurst:** nearly indifferent

---

### The Gardiners — Civic Faith

#### Attendance
- Attend a parish near Gracechurch Street
- Go as a family, with the children

#### Faith
- **Sound middle-class religion**
- Earnest but not fanatical
- Reflected in Mrs. Gardiner's character

---

### The Lucases — Imitative Anglicanism

#### Attendance
- Attend on Sundays
- Sir William Lucas imitates the social habits of his betters

#### Faith
- Conventional

## 5. Anglican Wedding Procedure

### A Standard Marriage

#### Preliminary Procedure
1. **Publication of banns** in church on three consecutive Sundays
   or a **Common Licence**, issued by the bishop, within a week
   or a **Special Licence**, issued by the archbishop, allowing immediate marriage anywhere

#### The Wedding Day
- **Morning**, usually between nine and noon
- At the **bride's parish church**
- Bride, groom, and two witnesses, usually friends or family
- Approximately thirty to forty-five minutes
- Sign the marriage register

#### Application in the Novel
- **Bingley–Jane:** standard banns, in Meryton or Longbourn parish
- **Darcy–Elizabeth:** possibly a Special Licence, as Mrs. Bennet demands, or banns
- **Wickham–Lydia:** a parish near the Gardiners' house on Gracechurch Street
- **Collins–Charlotte:** Meryton parish

## 6. Baptism and Funerals (Inferred)

### Baptism
- Infants are usually baptized within a few months of birth
- Conducted in church
- Godparents chosen as social allies
- **Darcy's godfather: Wickham's father?** The source relationship requires caution
- **Wickham's godfather: the late Mr. Darcy**, explicitly stated in Ch. 35

### Funerals
- Conducted in church
- Burial in the churchyard
- Black clothing and trimmings
- Only close relatives attend; women are often absent

## 7. The Social Functions of Religion

### Church as a Marriage Market
- Sunday is the only regular gathering of everyone in the neighborhood
- A likely place to first see newcomers such as Bingley or Darcy
- **The Bennet sisters might become excited by seeing new soldiers on Sunday**

### Display of Rank
- **Family pews**—estate families occupy separate pews
- Seating position reflects social hierarchy
- The Bennets have a pew as a minor landed family
- Darcy occupies the foremost pew in Pemberley parish

### Charitable Duty
- Landed families are obliged to relieve the poor
- Darcy: “liberal man, did much good among the poor” (Ch. 43)
- Lady Catherine: “scolded them into harmony and plenty” (Ch. 30)—authoritarian charity

## 10. Religion's Influence on the Characters

### Direct Influence
- **Collins:** his entire profession
- **Wickham:** refusing ordination is the decisive break with Darcy
- **Mary:** her identity

### Indirect Influence
- **The standard for every character's moral judgment**
- The sanctity of marriage
- The distinction between hypocrisy and truth
- The duty of charity
