# Mr. Wickham / George Wickham

## Basic Information
- **Original names**: Mr. Wickham, George Wickham
- **Korean rendering**: Mr. Wickham / George Wickham
- **Age**: The same age as Darcy, about 28
- **Family**: Son of the steward of Darcy’s father; raised as a favorite of the elder Mr. Darcy
- **Occupation**: Militia officer → ensign in the regular army, a commission purchased by Darcy
- **Residence**: Meryton with the militia → Brighton → a regiment in the North near Newcastle
- **First appearance**: chunk_09 (Chapter 15)

## Character Profile
The most dangerous figure in the novel. His handsome appearance, charming conversation, and flawless social manners win everyone’s immediate favor at a first meeting. Even Elizabeth concedes that “his appearance was greatly in his favour; he had all the best part of beauty, a fine countenance, a good figure, and very pleasing address.” Yet the charm is wholly superficial. Beneath it lies a liar, gambler, libertine, and seducer who preys on underage girls.

Wickham’s narrative function is twofold. First, he is both the catalyst that decisively fixes Elizabeth’s “prejudice” and the central means by which that prejudice is broken. Second, he is a mirror that reveals Darcy’s past and present, but also a distorting mirror. Elizabeth accepts Wickham’s false account of Darcy without question, solidifying her prejudice; Darcy’s letter reveals the truth and shatters it. Through his elopement with Lydia, Wickham brings the novel’s crisis to its peak and prompts Darcy’s heroic intervention.

## Development by Event

### Act I: A Charming Entrance (Chapters 15–17)
- **EVT-013** He first meets Elizabeth and Jane on a street in Meryton, appearing as a militia officer. When he encounters Darcy, both men change color, hinting at a past conflict.
- **EVT-014** He gives Elizabeth a defamatory account of Darcy, claiming Darcy withheld the living promised to him by Darcy’s father. He poses as noble, saying his reverence is too great to dishonor the elder Darcy’s memory through a public confrontation. **In fact, he voluntarily surrendered the living for three thousand pounds and squandered the money.**

### Act II: His True Nature Revealed (Chapters 18–40)
- **EVT-022** He abruptly redirects his courtship to Miss King, an heiress of ten thousand pounds, and immediately withdraws his attention from Elizabeth. Money rather than feeling is revealed as his motive.
- **EVT-029** Darcy’s letter exposes Wickham’s true character: his surrender of the living, his dissipation, and his attempted seduction of Georgiana Darcy, then fifteen, for her thirty-thousand-pound fortune. This is the turning point at which Elizabeth recognizes Wickham’s lies.
- **EVT-033** Before Lydia leaves for Brighton, Wickham and Elizabeth meet for the last time. Wickham is disconcerted when Elizabeth says Darcy improves on closer acquaintance.

### Act III: Lydia’s Elopement and a Compelled Marriage (Chapters 46–52)
- **EVT-038** He elopes with Lydia to London and lives with her without intending marriage, placing the entire Bennet family in danger of social ruin.
- **EVT-040** Darcy’s secret intervention clears all Wickham’s debts, purchases him an ensign’s commission, and provides Lydia’s settlement on condition that they marry. Wickham accepts money and agrees to the marriage.

### Conclusion
- **EVT-049** He transfers to a northern regiment near Newcastle and lives in an unstable marriage with Lydia. Whenever they need money, they write to Elizabeth and Jane. Darcy bars Wickham from Pemberley but continues to welcome Lydia.

## Core Relationships
- **Darcy**: Childhood companion → betrayal → enmity; exploits the elder Darcy’s affection, and the attempted seduction of Georgiana permanently destroys the relationship
- **Elizabeth**: Wins her favor through charm → implants prejudice through a false narrative → leaves her disillusioned when the truth emerges
- **Lydia**: Seduces the fifteen-year-old and elopes with her; has no intention of marriage until Darcy’s money compels it
- **Georgiana Darcy**: Attempts to seduce her at fifteen in pursuit of her £30,000 fortune
- **The Bennet family**: The man responsible for bringing the entire family to the brink of social ruin

## Translation Notes
- Wickham’s speech is outwardly that of a perfect gentleman. Keep it smooth and elegant on the surface while preserving a double tone in which his hypocrisy becomes visible on rereading.
- Words of sincerity that Wickham applies to himself, including “openness” and “frankness,” are all devices for disguising falsehood.
- Darcy’s account of Wickham in the letter should convey restrained anger and factual coolness, in a style closer to a legal statement than emotional exaggeration.
- Translate “living” as the ecclesiastical living—a parish and income granted to a clergyman. It resembles a benefice, but “living” was the more common term in contemporary usage.
