# *Pride and Prejudice* — Encyclopedia of Regency Life

> This document explains the life, customs, and institutions of the Regency era (1811–1820, or more broadly 1795–1830) that translators and adapters must understand to answer **“Why would they act that way?”**, even when those details do not appear directly in the text.
>
> Each entry identifies its connection to the novel under `[Connection to the novel]`.

---

## 1. The Structure of the Day — A Routine Completely Unlike Today’s

### The Meal System

| Meal | Time | Contents | Modern Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Breakfast** | 9–10 a.m. | Tea, toast, bread, butter, cold meat; a light meal | Breakfast |
| **(No lunch)** | — | In the Regency, “lunch” either did not exist or meant an informal snack | — |
| **Dinner** | 3:30–5 p.m. | The **principal meal** of the day; a formal meal of two or three courses | Modern lunch and dinner combined |
| **Tea** | 6–8 p.m. | Tea + light cakes; time for socializing | Afternoon snack |
| **Supper** | 9–10 p.m. | A light cold meal, also served after a ball | Late-night snack |

> **[Connection to the novel]** Collins urges everyone to be “quick” and not keep Lady Catherine waiting because the dinner hour (3:30–4 p.m.) was strictly observed. Elizabeth intends to leave Netherfield at three in the afternoon (Ch.7) so that she can be home before dinner.

### The Servant-Calling System

| Device/Role | Function | Example in the Novel |
|---|---|---|
| **Bell** (bell pull) | Pulling a cord in a room rang that room’s bell in the servants’ hall | “Ring the bell, Kitty, for Hill” (Ch.49) |
| **Footman** | Opened doors, delivered messages, attended carriages | Delivers a letter to Jane (Ch.7) |
| **Butler** | Supervised male servants; managed wine and silverware | “met by the butler” (Ch.49) |
| **Housekeeper** | Supervised female servants and all domestic work | Mrs. Reynolds (Pemberley), Hill (Longbourn), Nicholls (Netherfield) |

### Changing Clothes (Dressing)

People changed clothes **at least two or three times a day**:
- Morning: morning dress (simple indoor clothing)
- Walking/calling: walking dress + bonnet + pelisse in winter
- Evening/dinner: evening dress (more revealing and ornate)
- Ball: ball gown + shoe roses + gloves

> **[Connection to the novel]** “At five o’clock the two ladies retired to dress” (Ch.8)—they spend an hour and a half dressing before dinner. When Collins says, “Do not make yourself uneasy about your apparel” (Ch.29) and tells them to dress simply, he means that they should preserve the difference in rank before Lady Catherine.

## 2. Travel and Communication — The Logic of a Slow World

### Typical Travel Times

| Means | Speed | Example Distance | Application in the Novel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **On foot** | 3 mph | Longbourn → Meryton: 1 mile = 20 min / Longbourn → Netherfield: 3 miles = 1 hour | Elizabeth’s walk (Ch.7) |
| **Carriage (ordinary)** | 6–8 mph | Longbourn → Hunsford: 24 miles = 3–4 hours | Elizabeth’s journey to Kent (Ch.27) |
| **Post** | 8–10 mph | London → Brighton: about 55 miles = 5–6 hours | Wickham and Lydia’s escape route |
| **Express post-chaise** | 10–12 mph | London → Longbourn: about 25 miles = 2–3 hours | Lady Catherine’s full-speed visit (Ch.56) |
| **Walking + carriage** | — | Hunsford → Rosings: 0.5 mile = 10 min on foot | Collins’s daily calls |

### The Postal System

| Type | Speed | Cost | Example in the Novel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ordinary post (Penny Post)** | Same day within London / 1–3 days between regions | Paid by recipient, usually 1–2 shillings | Miss Bingley’s and Collins’s letters |
| **Express** | Dedicated mounted messenger; a few hours | Very expensive, 5–10 shillings or more | Colonel Forster’s elopement report; Mr. Gardiner’s marriage settlement |
| **Cross post** | Provincial post that did not pass through London | Slower | Why Jane’s letter was “mis-sent” (Ch.46) |

> **[Connection to the novel]** Jane’s two letters arrive together (Ch.46) because the first was mis-sent. Since postage was **paid by the recipient**, writing many letters imposed an expense on the other person. Miss Bingley’s promise of an “unreserved correspondence” therefore signals a great favor—or would, if it were not hypocritical.

## 3. The Legal and Social Process of Marriage

### Steps Toward Marriage

```
1. Courtship
   - Repeated dances at balls → calling → walking out
   - Speaking without a formal introduction was rude
   
2. Proposal
   - Required the father’s prior permission or subsequent approval
   - Collins receives Mrs. Bennet’s prior permission (Ch.19)
   - Darcy receives Mr. Bennet’s approval afterward (Ch.59)

3. Engagement
   - Could be kept secret before the formal announcement
   - Breaking an engagement was a serious social scandal

4. Banns or Licence
   - Banns: announced in the parish church on three consecutive Sundays; free
   - Common Licence: issued by a bishop; one-week wait; fee required
   - Special Licence: issued by the archbishop; immediate and valid anywhere; very expensive
   
5. Wedding
   - Held in the bride’s parish church in the morning
   - The couple departed immediately for a honeymoon or the husband’s residence
```

> **[Connection to the novel]** Wickham and Lydia claim to be going to Gretna Green in Scotland because marriage under the age of twenty-one required parental consent in England but **not in Scotland**. In reality, they hide in London and do not intend to marry. Mrs. Bennet’s demand for a “Special licence” (Ch.59) is ostentation induced by Darcy’s wealth.

### The Social Position of Unmarried Women

```
[Married woman]       → “Mrs.”  = absorbed into her husband’s house, property, and rank
[Unmarried woman]     → “Miss” = lives in her father’s house; almost no independent income
[After father’s death]→ must depend on siblings/relatives or become a governess
[After a scandal]     → permanently expelled from society; “come upon the town” = become a prostitute
```

> **[Connection to the novel]** Charlotte accepts Collins because she is twenty-seven, has no fortune, and is plain: marriage is “the only honourable provision” by which a well-bred young woman can escape poverty (Ch.22). If Lydia does not marry Wickham, she will “come upon the town”—social death.

## 4. Fine Distinctions in the Class System

### Social Hierarchy (Highest → Lowest)

```
[Royalty]   The Royal Family
   │
[Nobility]  Nobility / Peerage
   │        Duke > Marquess > Earl > Viscount > Baron
   │        ※ Colonel Fitzwilliam’s father = an Earl
   │        ※ Darcy is not a peer—he belongs to the highest gentry
   │
[Titled rank] Baronet / Knight
   │        ※ Sir William Lucas = Knight (non-hereditary)
   │        ※ Sir Lewis de Bourgh = Baronet (hereditary)
   │
[Gentry]    Landowning class
   │        ※ Darcy (£10,000 a year), Mr. Bennet (£2,000), Bingley (inherited wealth but no estate)
   │
[Professions] Clergymen, lawyers, soldiers, physicians
   │        ※ Collins (clergyman), Fitzwilliam (soldier), Jones (apothecary)
   │
[Trade]     Wealth accumulated through commerce
   │        ※ Mr. Gardiner; Bingley’s late father
   │        ※ The very mention of “trade” carries class disparagement
   │
[Servants]
   │
[Poor]      Cottagers / The Poor
```

### The Stigma of “Trade”

The Bingley sisters mock Mr. Gardiner’s residence in Cheapside because working in **trade** did not suit the dignity of the gentry. The irony is that the Bingley family itself made its money through commerce: “a circumstance more deeply impressed on their memories than that their brother’s fortune and their own had been acquired by trade” (Ch.4).

> **[Connection to the novel]** Darcy’s willing reception of the Gardiners (EVT-036-001) is revolutionary: a member of the highest gentry welcomes people of the merchant class on his own estate and even grants them permission to fish. He transcends class prejudice.

## 5. Women’s “Accomplishments” — A Résumé for the Marriage Market

### Expected Accomplishments

| Accomplishment | Importance | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| **Piano/harp** | Highest | Musical skill equaled marriage value; Lady Catherine lectures women to “practise” |
| **Singing** | High | Singing to the piano; Mary’s failure (Ch.18) |
| **Drawing/sketching** | High | Watercolors, pastels; Georgiana’s crayon drawings |
| **Dancing** | High | Having no partner at a ball was humiliating |
| **Modern languages (French/Italian)** | Medium | French phrases inserted into letters and conversation |
| **Needlework** | Basic | Netting a purse, decorating screens |
| **Reading** | Emphasized only by Darcy | “improvement of her mind by extensive reading”—absent from Miss Bingley’s list |
| **Having a governess** | Respectability | Lady Catherine: “No governess! How was that possible?” |

> **[Connection to the novel]** When Elizabeth says during the accomplishments debate (Ch.8) that she has never seen such a woman, she exposes the standard as **unrealistic**. Darcy’s addition of “reading” reveals his true standard: he values **inner depth** over outward arts.

## 6. Medicine and Health — Apothecary vs. Physician

### The Medical System

| Profession | Role | Cost | Patients |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Physician** | University educated; diagnosed only; did not dispense medicine personally | Very expensive | Upper class |
| **Surgeon** | Performed operations and treatment | Medium | Upper middle class |
| **Apothecary** | Made house calls, diagnosed, and dispensed medicine; effectively the local family doctor | Affordable | Lower middle class + countryside |

> **[Connection to the novel]** Mr. Jones, an **apothecary**, attends Jane because the Bennets are middling gentry. Miss Bingley’s claim that they should call an “eminent physician” from London is both (1) ostentation and (2) class consciousness that looks down on the apothecary.

### “Sea-bathing”

This was not mere recreation but a **medical prescription**. Physicians recommended it as treatment for nervous exhaustion, depression, and many other ailments. It was the reason coastal resorts such as Brighton existed.

> **[Connection to the novel]** “A little sea-bathing would set me up for ever” (Ch.41, Mrs. Bennet)—her plan to cure her nerves with sea-bathing was perfectly conventional at the time.

## 7. The Military System — What the Militia Really Was

### Militia vs. Regular Army

| Category | Militia | Regulars |
|---|---|---|
| **Duty** | Domestic security/defense | Combat, including overseas deployment |
| **Stationing** | Rotated between regions, moving each winter | Fixed garrisons |
| **Soldiers** | Compulsory levy or volunteers | Volunteers + officers who purchased commissions |
| **Officers** | Relatively inexpensive commissions | Purchase system—rank bought with money |
| **Social standing** | A mixture of gentlemen’s sons and adventurers | More organized but exposed to overseas danger |
| **Social role** | Joined balls and society in the area of stationing | — |

> **[Connection to the novel]** The militia’s stationing at Meryton lights the fuse of the novel. They remain “all winter” and become objects of fascination for the neighborhood’s young women. Wickham’s transfer from the militia to an ensigncy in the regular army means that Darcy **purchased** the commission for him—effectively an exile.

## 8. Letter-Writing Customs — Material Details

### The Physical Form of a Letter

| Item | Explanation | Example in the Novel |
|---|---|---|
| **Paper** | Fine hot-pressed paper, folded and sealed | Miss Bingley’s letter: “elegant, little, hot-pressed paper” |
| **Seal** | Closed with sealing wax, sometimes impressed with a family crest | — |
| **Envelope** | Usually there was none; the letter was folded, addressed, and sealed | Darcy’s letter: “an envelope containing two sheets”—exceptionally uses an envelope |
| **Writing implement** | Quill pen; had to be regularly sharpened or “mended” with a knife | Miss Bingley volunteers to mend Darcy’s pen (Ch.10) |
| **Ink** | Iron-gall ink; slow to dry, so blotting sand was sprinkled over it | — |
| **Cost** | Paid by recipient; no prepaid stamps; charge based on number of sheets | Darcy’s densely written two sheets = high cost + evidence of urgency |

### Letter Etiquette

- Courtesy required an **immediate** reply. Delay signaled indifference.
- Miss Bingley’s failure to answer Jane for four weeks (Ch.26) is **deliberate coldness**.
- Promising an “unreserved correspondence”—frequent, frank exchange of letters—signaled deep friendship, exposing Miss Bingley’s hypocrisy.

## 9. The Reality of Money — What Could It Buy?

### Standard of Living by Annual Income

| Annual Income | Standard of Living | What It Allowed | Character |
|---|---|---|---|
| **£10,000** | Highest gentry | Great estate + London townhouse + several carriages + 30 or more servants | Darcy |
| **£4–5,000** | Upper gentry | Could rent an estate + carriage + 10 or more servants | Bingley |
| **£2,000** | Middling gentry | Country house + one carriage (horses shared with farm) + 5–8 servants | Mr. Bennet |
| **£300–500** | Clergyman/professional | Parsonage + 2–3 servants + frugal life | Collins |
| **£100** | Lower rank | Lodgings + no servant + extreme frugality | Lydia’s annual allowance |
| **£50 or less** | Poverty | Independent life impossible | Each Bennet sister’s share after their mother’s death |

### Examples of Purchasing Power (Based on 1811)

| Item | Price | Very Rough Modern Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| One servant’s annual wage | £10–25 | About KRW 1–2.5 million |
| One horse | £20–50 | About KRW 2–5 million |
| One carriage | £50–200 | About KRW 5–20 million |
| One dress | £2–10 | About KRW 200,000–1 million |
| One loaf of bread | About 6 pence | About KRW 5,000 |
| Purchase of an ensigncy | £400–500 | About KRW 40–50 million |
| One fireplace at Rosings | £800 | About KRW 80 million (!!) |

> **[Connection to the novel]** Mr. Bennet says, “Wickham’s a fool if he takes her with a farthing less than ten thousand pounds.” Ten thousand pounds is roughly KRW 1–1.5 billion today. Wickham’s debts + commission + settlement could plausibly total this amount, which Mr. Gardiner could not afford—indirect evidence that Darcy paid it.

## 10. The Social Season — The Rhythm of “Town” and “Country”

### The Annual Social Calendar

| Period | Place | Activity |
|---|---|---|
| **January–June** | **London (Town)** | The Season: balls, opera, court presentations, marriage market |
| **June–August** | Coastal resorts such as Brighton, or the country | The Season ends; London is “empty” |
| **August–December** | **Country** | Shooting (begins in August), harvest, Christmas |
| **September 29** | — | Michaelmas; date for renewing leases and employment |

> **[Connection to the novel]** Bingley’s move before Michaelmas (Ch.1) aligns with the start of the country season. Miss Bingley’s claim that summer London is empty and offers nothing to do (Lydia’s recollection, Ch.51) reflects the end of the social season. Darcy’s presence at Pemberley in summer is normal.

## 11. Religion and the Parish — The Meaning of Sunday

### Sunday Obligations

- Every family attended the **parish church**; in practice, attendance was compulsory.
- Hearing the clergyman’s sermon was the heart of Sunday observance.
- Balls and social events were not held on Sundays.

> **[Connection to the novel]** “On Sunday, after morning service, the separation... took place” (Ch.12)—Jane and Elizabeth leave Netherfield after Sunday morning worship. When Collins says “any other clergyman is engaged to do the duty of the day” (Ch.13), he means that he found another clergyman to preach in his place on Sunday.

### Architectural Styles

| Building | Style | Visual Essentials |
|---|---|---|
| **Pemberley** | Georgian/Palladian stone; “handsome stone building” | Symmetry, restrained elegance, harmony with natural landscape |
| **Rosings** | Same style but more ostentatious; “handsome modern building” | Display through expensive windows; decorative |
| **Netherfield** | Gentry-scale country house | Large enough to hold a ball |

| **Lucas Lodge** | Small to medium, one mile from Meryton | Suitable for small parties |

| Feature | Women | Men |
|---|---|---|
