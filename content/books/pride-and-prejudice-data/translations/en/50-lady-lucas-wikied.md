# Lady Lucas

## Basic Information
- **Original name:** Lady Lucas
- **Korean rendering:** Lady Lucas
- **Family:** wife of Sir William Lucas; mother of Charlotte, Maria, and several other children
- **Residence:** Lucas Lodge, Meryton
- **First appearance:** chunk_01–02 (around Chap. 5)

## Character Introduction
The wife of Sir William Lucas and another pillar of Meryton society. Paired with Sir William’s sociability, she represents the practical mother concerned with household affairs and her children’s marriages. As Mrs. Bennet’s chief competitor and point of comparison, she maintains a rivalry in which the two women constantly measure their children’s marriage prospects beneath a surface friendship. Though her role is small, she functions as a mirror for Mrs. Bennet’s jealousy and anger.

Her narrative function is chiefly to catalyze Mrs. Bennet’s reactions. She appears when she comes to Longbourn with the news that Charlotte will marry Collins and when she trades predictions with Mrs. Bennet at the Netherfield ball about a marriage between Bingley and Jane. As Austen describes her, she is “too good-natured a woman to be a very successful rival”—so amiable that she is inept even at rivalry.

## Development by Event
- **EVT-003:** after the Meryton assembly, she and Mrs. Bennet review the evening at the Lucas home, commenting together on Darcy’s rudeness, Bingley’s kindness, and other matters.
- **EVT-016-003:** at the Netherfield ball, she listens as Mrs. Bennet publicly predicts Jane and Bingley’s marriage. Seated beside her, Lady Lucas agrees, serving as a supporting player in the decisive scene Darcy overhears and later uses as grounds for separating Bingley from Jane.
- **EVT-020:** after Charlotte becomes engaged to Collins, Lady Lucas visits the Bennets and takes pride in her eldest daughter’s marriage. The visit directly ignites Mrs. Bennet’s jealousy and anger.

## Core Relationships
- **Charlotte, her eldest daughter:** greatly relieved that her formerly unmarried twenty-seven-year-old daughter will marry; she takes pride in the economic security of the Hunsford living
- **Mrs. Bennet:** a rival in the marriage competition beneath their outward friendship; their relative standing shifts with news of their children’s marriages
- **Sir William Lucas:** her husband; she is the more retiring partner beside his courtly boasts and goodwill

## Translation Notes
- Because her husband is a knight, “Lady Lucas” is a non-hereditary courtesy title. Keep in mind that her rank differs from Lady Catherine’s aristocratic status.
- In her conversations with Mrs. Bennet, preserve the double tone: outward sweetness with an edge of competition underneath.
- Mothers’ public boasting about their children’s marriages formed part of contemporary social convention. Render this unfamiliar context naturally for modern readers.
