# *Pride and Prejudice* — Chapter-by-Chapter Translation Guide

> For each of the 61 chapters, this guide organizes the **summary, characters present, location, time, key lines, mistranslation risks, and reference setting sheets**.
> A translator working on any chapter should be able to obtain all necessary context by opening **this document alone**.
>
> **Legend:**
> - 🔑 = A key line important to the novel as a whole (requires special care in translation)
> - ⚠️ = Expression at risk of mistranslation
> - 📎 = Setting sheet to consult

## Volume I (Chapters 1–23)

### Chapter 1 — News of Bingley + the Bennets' First Conversation

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | News arrives that a wealthy young man is moving into Netherfield. Mrs Bennet demands that her husband visit him; Mr Bennet evades her with dry sarcasm. |
| **Location** | Longbourn drawing room |
| **Characters** | Mr Bennet, Mrs Bennet (conversation only; their daughters are mentioned) |
| **EVT** | EVT-001 |
| **🔑 Key lines** | “It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune must be in want of a wife.” — The opening establishes the irony of the entire novel: surface and actual meaning are inverted. |
| | “You want to tell me, and I have no objection to hearing it.” — Mr Bennet's sardonic concession |
| | “I must throw in a good word for my little Lizzy.” — The first sign of favoritism |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risks** | “mean understanding” = a **poor/limited** intellect (not “vicious”) / “fancied herself nervous” = **called herself** nervous (not actual momentary anxiety) / “business of her life” = the **business** of her life, a managerial metaphor |
| **📎 References** | Period Lexicon (nervous, mean), Nuance List (NUA-001, NUA-002) |

### Chapter 2 — Mr Bennet Reveals His Secret Visit

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | Mr Bennet belatedly reveals that he has already visited Bingley. The family rejoices. |
| **Location** | Longbourn drawing room |
| **Characters** | The entire Bennet family |
| **EVT** | EVT-002 |
| **🔑 Key line** | “I am the tallest.” — Lydia's first line; the first sign of her confidence and lack of judgment |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risk** | “stout” = in period usage, **bold/strong** (not the modern “fat”) |

### Chapter 3 — The Meryton Assembly: “tolerable”

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | At the Meryton assembly ball, Bingley's party of five enters. Darcy initially attracts attention, then earns general dislike through his proud conduct. He insults Elizabeth as “tolerable.” Bingley dances with Jane twice. |
| **Location** | Meryton assembly rooms |
| **Characters** | Nearly everyone (Wickham has not yet appeared) |
| **EVT** | EVT-003 |
| **🔑 Key line** | “She is tolerable; but not handsome enough to tempt me; and I am in no humour at present to give consequence to young ladies who are slighted by other men.” — The novel's point of departure, containing three layers of pride |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risks** | “tolerable” = passable/fair (not praise) / “to tempt” = to attract him, premised on his own desirability / “give consequence” = treat as important / “discovered to be proud” = not literally “discovered,” but **judged/proclaimed** proud, with irony in the snap verdict / “His character was decided” = his **reputation**, not his personality, was settled |
| **📎 References** | Period Lexicon (tolerable, consequence, character), Nuance List (NUA-005, NUA-006), Recurring Motifs (“tolerable”) |

### Chapter 4 — Jane and Elizabeth Assess Bingley and Darcy

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | The sisters review the ball. Jane praises Bingley. Bingley's and Darcy's dispositions are contrasted. The narrator explains the Bingley family background: trade origins and an inheritance of nearly £100,000. |
| **Location** | Longbourn (the sisters' room) |
| **Characters** | Jane, Elizabeth; the narrator explains Bingley's and Darcy's backgrounds |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risks** | “not been of age two years” = has not been an adult (21) for two years → about 23 / “the liberty of a manor” = not merely a house, but **manorial rights** such as hunting privileges / “acquired by trade” = wealth **earned** through commerce; “trade” bears a class stigma |
| **📎 References** | Institutions and Economic Terms (manor, trade), Complete Group List (meaning of trade) |

### Chapter 5 — Defining “Pride vs Vanity”

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | The Lucas family and the Bennets discuss the ball. Charlotte states her pragmatic theory of marriage. Mary defines “pride vs vanity.” A young Lucas fantasizes about keeping foxhounds. |
| **Location** | Longbourn (the Lucases visit) |
| **Characters** | Elizabeth, Charlotte, Mrs Bennet, Mary, Sir William Lucas |
| **🔑 Key lines** | “I could easily forgive his pride, if he had not mortified mine.” — The first disclosure that Elizabeth has pride of her own |
| | “Pride relates more to our opinion of ourselves; vanity to what we would have others think of us.” — The title's defining distinction. Mary is pedantic, but the content is accurate. |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risk** | “mortified” = **wounded** her pride (stronger than merely embarrassing her) |
| **📎 References** | Recurring Motifs (pride, vanity), Period Lexicon (pride vs vanity pair) |

### Chapter 6 — Darcy's “fine eyes” + Elizabeth Refuses to Dance at Lucas Lodge

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | Darcy finds himself increasingly attracted to Elizabeth and acknowledges her “fine eyes.” At Lucas Lodge, Sir William tries to arrange a dance, but Elizabeth refuses. Miss Bingley uses the phrase “fine eyes” to needle Darcy. |
| **Location** | Lucas Lodge |
| **Characters** | Darcy, Elizabeth, Miss Bingley, Sir William Lucas, Charlotte |
| **EVT** | EVT-004, EVT-005 |
| **🔑 Key lines** | “Miss Elizabeth Bennet.” — The moment Darcy first tells Miss Bingley Elizabeth's name |
| | “Mr. Darcy is all politeness.” — Elizabeth's sarcasm. Surface: how very polite. Actual force: mockery. |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risk** | Free indirect discourse: “Mr. Darcy had at first scarcely allowed her to be pretty” — the narrator conveys Darcy's mind in his own idiom. “Allowed” means he **permitted himself to admit** it. |
| **📎 References** | Nuance List (NUA-008), Recurring Motifs (“fine eyes”), Forms-of-Address Guide (Miss Eliza vs Miss Elizabeth) |

### Chapter 7 — Jane Falls Ill + Elizabeth's Walk

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | The Bennets' financial situation is explained, with the first mention of the entail. The militia arrives. Miss Bingley's dinner invitation leads Mrs Bennet to send Jane on horseback in hopes of rain; Jane becomes ill. Elizabeth walks three miles to nurse her. |
| **Location** | Longbourn → Netherfield |
| **Characters** | The Bennet family, Bingley's party |
| **EVT** | EVT-006, EVT-007 |
| **🔑 Key line** | Mrs Bennet: “you had better go on horseback, because it seems likely to rain” — The strategy's crucial sentence. Surface: observation. Actual meaning: hope. |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risks** | “entailed, in default of heirs male” = an entail that takes effect **if there is no male heir** / “apothecary” = a druggist who also serves as the local medical practitioner (not merely a pharmacist) / “petticoat, six inches deep in mud” = six inches of mud on the petticoat, visual evidence of violating class codes |
| **📎 References** | Institutions and Economic Terms (Entail), Period Lexicon (apothecary), Regency Encyclopedia (medical system), Scene Map (SCENE 03) |

### Chapter 8 — The Accomplishment Debate

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | At dinner at Netherfield, Miss Bingley and Mrs Hurst criticize Elizabeth behind her back. Darcy says her “fine eyes” were “brightened by exercise.” The sisters mock her uncle in Cheapside. Elizabeth, Darcy, and Miss Bingley debate the “accomplished woman.” |
| **Location** | Netherfield dining room → drawing room |
| **Characters** | Elizabeth, Darcy, Miss Bingley, Mrs Hurst, Mr Bingley, Mr Hurst |
| **EVT** | EVT-007-001 |
| **🔑 Key lines** | “Not at all; they were brightened by the exercise.” — Darcy's brief, decisive counterattack, directly defeating Miss Bingley's attack on Elizabeth's “fine eyes” |
| | “to all this she must yet add something more substantial, in the improvement of her mind by extensive reading.” — Darcy's true standard concerns the mind, not appearance. |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risks** | “playing high” = gambling for **high stakes** / “ragout” = French stew and a class signal, versus a “plain dish” of English simplicity / “Cheapside” = not merely a place name, but a **code of class contempt** / “accomplished” = cultivated, not the modern “successful” |
| **📎 References** | Object Dictionary (ragout, loo), Period Lexicon (accomplished), Nuance List (NUA-010, NUA-011) |

### Chapter 9 — Mrs Bennet Disgraces Herself at Netherfield

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | Mrs Bennet, Kitty, and Lydia visit Netherfield. Mrs Bennet boasts, gossips, and attacks Darcy, humiliating Elizabeth. A debate on poetry and love follows. Lydia presses Bingley to keep his promise of a ball. |
| **Location** | Netherfield breakfast room → drawing room |
| **Characters** | Mrs Bennet, Lydia, Kitty, Elizabeth, Darcy, Bingley, Miss Bingley |
| **EVT** | EVT-008 |
| **🔑 Key lines** | Darcy: “I have been used to consider poetry as the food of love.” / Elizabeth: “of a fine, stout, healthy love it may. But if it be only a slight, thin sort of inclination, I am convinced that one good sonnet will starve it entirely away.” — A celebrated intellectual duel |

| **⚠️ Mistranslation risks** | “fancied she had gained a complete victory” = she **imagined** it; “fancied” is the signal / “stout” = **robust** (not fat) |
| **📎 References** | Nuance List (NUA-012), Forms-of-Address Guide (Mrs Bennet's speech) |

### Chapter 10 — The Reel + “bewitched” + Miss Bingley's Walking Scheme

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | Darcy writes a letter while Miss Bingley interrupts. Bingley and Darcy debate character. Darcy proposes a reel; Elizabeth refuses; he is “bewitched.” Miss Bingley invites Elizabeth to walk with her to attract Darcy's attention, and Darcy analyzes their “two motives.” |
| **Location** | Netherfield drawing room |
| **Characters** | Darcy, Elizabeth, Miss Bingley, Bingley, Mr Hurst |
| **EVT** | EVT-009 |
| **🔑 Key line** | “He had never been so bewitched by any woman as he was by her. He really believed that, were it not for the inferiority of her connections, he should be in some danger.” — The narrator exposes Darcy's mind. “bewitched” (enchantment) + “danger” define love as a threat. |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risks** | “bewitched” = **enchanted as if under a spell**, stronger than merely attracted / “precipitance” = haste, in Darcy's criticism of Bingley's irresolution / “mend a pen” = **trim** a quill, not repair it |
| **📎 References** | Object Dictionary (mend a pen, piquet, reel), Scene Map (SCENE 04), Nuance List (NUA-008) |

### Chapter 11 — Confessing Faults + “wilfully to misunderstand”

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | Jane begins to recover; Bingley tends her attentively. The party reads and plays cards in the evening. Miss Bingley's attempts to approach Darcy fail. Elizabeth and Darcy discuss their respective faults. Darcy senses the “danger” of his interest and becomes guarded. |
| **Location** | Netherfield drawing room |
| **Characters** | Darcy, Elizabeth, Bingley, Jane, Miss Bingley |
| **EVT** | EVT-009-001, EVT-009-002 |
| **🔑 Key lines** | “My good opinion once lost is lost for ever.” — A double foreshadowing: (1) the basis of the Wickham affair; (2) Elizabeth later uses this confession as a **weapon**. |
| | “And yours is wilfully to misunderstand them.” — A prophecy that precisely states the novel's central theme |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risks** | “wilfully” = **deliberately**, not by mistake but through chosen misunderstanding / “resentful” = inclined to hold a grudge, in Darcy's self-analysis |
| **📎 References** | Nuance List (NUA-013, NUA-014), Recurring Motifs (pride, “implacable resentment”) |

### Chapter 13 — Collins's Letter and Visit

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | Collins's letter is read aloud: an offer of reconciliation, flattery of Lady Catherine, and an “olive branch.” Collins arrives at four o'clock on Monday, 18 November. He praises Longbourn's furniture, with the implication that it will one day be his. |
| **Location** | Longbourn |
| **Characters** | The entire Bennet family, Mr Collins |
| **EVT** | EVT-011 |
| **🔑 Key lines** | “olive branch” — A gesture of reconciliation, reused in Chapter 57 to mean **offspring/pregnancy** |
| | “No, my dear; I think not. I have great hopes of finding him quite the reverse.” — Mr Bennet hopes Collins will prove a fool. |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risks** | “demean myself” = in period usage, **conduct myself**; the modern meaning, to lower oneself, is the exact opposite / “condescension” = **favor shown by stooping in rank**, not simply arrogance / “rectory” = the parsonage and the associated income as a whole |
| **📎 References** | Period Lexicon (condescension, demean), Institutions and Economic Terms (Entail, Patronage, living), Epistolary Collection (L-01) |

### Chapter 14 — Collins's Grandiloquence + Fordyce's *Sermons*

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | Collins praises Lady Catherine and describes Rosings, including an £800 chimney-piece. He introduces Miss de Bourgh. He reads from Fordyce's *Sermons* until Lydia interrupts, then switches to backgammon. |
| **Location** | Longbourn dining room → drawing room |
| **Characters** | Collins, Mr Bennet, the Bennet sisters, Mrs Bennet |
| **🔑 Key line** | “They arise chiefly from what is passing at the time” — Collins answers Mr Bennet's question about whether his compliments are improvised or planned: the perfection of hypocrisy without self-awareness. |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risks** | “Fordyce's Sermons” = a real book published in 1766; annotation needed / “protested that he never read novels” = he **never** reads novels, a clergyman's moral pose |
| **📎 References** | Object Dictionary (Fordyce's, backgammon), Forms-of-Address Guide (Collins's grandiloquent style) |

### Chapter 15 — Collins Changes Targets + Wickham Appears

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Summary** | Collins's background reveals poor education, conceit, and servility. Prompted by Mrs Bennet, he changes his intended bride from Jane to Elizabeth. During a walk in Meryton, Wickham first appears. Darcy and Wickham meet in the street and change color. |
| **Location** | Longbourn → the streets of Meryton |
| **Characters** | Collins, Mrs Bennet, Elizabeth, Lydia, Kitty, Wickham, Denny, Darcy, Bingley |
| **EVT** | EVT-012, EVT-013 |
| **🔑 Key lines** | “This was his plan of amends—of atonement—for inheriting their father's estate” — Narratorial irony: Collins rationalizes his proposal as **compensation**. |
| | “Both changed colour, one looked white, the other red.” — The visual contrast when Darcy and Wickham meet; the text does not specify who turns white and who red. |
| **⚠️ Mistranslation risks** | “wanting only regimentals to make him completely charming” = lacking only a uniform to be **completely** charming; “wanting” = **lacking** / “prepossession” = prejudice or prior bias, in Mrs Bennet's Chapter 15 usage |
| **📎 References** | Period Lexicon (wanting), Complete Unspecified-Character List (#22, “the stranger” = Wickham) |

### Chapter 16 — Wickham's False Testimony

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
