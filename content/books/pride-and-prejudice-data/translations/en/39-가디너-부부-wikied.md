## Basic Information
- **Original names:** Mr. Gardiner, Mrs. Gardiner, and Mr. Edward Gardiner
- **Korean rendering:** Mr. Gardiner / Mrs. Gardiner / the Gardiners
- **Relationship:** Mrs. Bennet's brother, elder or younger, and his wife
- **Occupation:** Mr. Gardiner works in trade in Cheapside, London
- **Residence:** Gracechurch Street, London
- **First appearance:** chunk_16, Chap. 25

## Character Introduction
They are the wisest and most sensible couple in the novel. Despite belonging to a London merchant family, they possess far more intelligence and polished manners than the gentry-born Bennets. The arrangement itself constitutes Austen's critique of class: true “gentility” is determined by character and judgment, not birth.

Mrs. Gardiner serves as both aunt and mentor to Elizabeth. At decisive moments in the narrative, she offers wise counsel and takes action: warning Elizabeth early about Wickham, proposing the Derbyshire tour, and revealing Darcy's secret intervention. Mr. Gardiner acts as the effective head of the family during Lydia's elopement, taking charge of the search and negotiations in London. Even after Mr. Bennet returns home in despair, Mr. Gardiner remains and manages the negotiations with Wickham, in fact financed by Darcy.

## Development by Event

### Act I: Wise Counsel, Ch. 25–27
- **EVT-024:** while caring for Jane in London, the Gardiners offer cautious advice about Bingley. In correspondence with Elizabeth, Mrs. Gardiner urges caution toward Wickham: “an interesting young man, but without fortune.”

### Act II: Derbyshire Tour, Ch. 42–44
- **EVT-034:** they take Elizabeth on a tour of Derbyshire and suggest visiting Pemberley, making possible the journey that becomes the turning point of her life.
- **EVT-036:** they encounter Darcy at Pemberley. When Darcy asks to be introduced and treats them courteously, Mrs. Gardiner is surprised: “And this is the man who is proud?”

### Act III: Lydia's Crisis and the Intervention, Ch. 46–52
- **EVT-040:** after Lydia's elopement, Mr. Gardiner directs the search in London. The Gardiners' home becomes the crisis-response headquarters. Darcy supplies the money, but Mr. Gardiner is the nominal negotiating party.
- **EVT-042:** Mrs. Gardiner writes Elizabeth and reveals the full extent of Darcy's secret intervention: finding Wickham, paying his debts, purchasing his ensigncy, and providing the settlement. **This letter decisively confirms Elizabeth's gratitude and love.**
- **EVT-048:** at the end, the Gardiners frequently visit Pemberley as Elizabeth and Darcy's closest relatives. Darcy sincerely respects them.

## Core Relationships
- **Elizabeth:** aunt and uncle as well as mentors; the adults Elizabeth trusts most
- **Darcy:** initially views them through class prejudice as “the uncle in Cheapside,” but genuinely respects them after meeting them; they become his closest relatives by marriage
- **Jane:** they care for her during her London stay and offer emotional support regarding Bingley
- **Mrs. Bennet:** sibling relationship; Mrs. Gardiner has far better judgment
- **Wickham:** Mr. Gardiner negotiates with him over Lydia and learns his true nature

## Translation Notes
- Cheapside and Gracechurch Street are commercial districts of London. Scenes in which Miss Bingley mocks them must convey the class implications.
- The Gardiners' dialogue and letters should have a formal, sensible tone. Their intellectual difference from the Bennets must emerge through the style of their speech.
- “uncle in Cheapside” is a recurring class-based slur in the work; use a consistent translation.
- Mrs. Gardiner's final letter (EVT-042) is narratively crucial. Preserve both the clarity of its information and its emotional resonance.
