# Elizabeth Bennet / Lizzy

## Basic Information
- **Original names:** Elizabeth Bennet, Lizzy, Eliza, Miss Eliza Bennet
- **Korean rendering:** Elizabeth Bennet / Lizzy
- **Age:** 20 (“not one-and-twenty” in the novel)
- **Family:** second daughter of the Bennet family
- **Residence:** Longbourn → Pemberley
- **First appearance:** chunk_01 (Chap. 1)

## Character Introduction
The second of the Bennets’ five daughters. Clever and witty, she is her father’s favorite. Her keen powers of observation and lively, playful disposition lead her to delight in the ridiculous. Yet this very discernment also draws her into the trap of prejudice. She embodies the “Prejudice” in the novel’s title, *Pride and Prejudice*.

## Development by Event

### Act I: The Formation of Prejudice (Chaps. 1–17)
- **EVT-003:** at the Meryton assembly, Darcy insults her as “tolerable; but not handsome enough to tempt me.” Rather than taking it heavily, she retells the slight to her friends with amusement and turns it into material for her wit.
- **EVT-005:** at Lucas Lodge, she refuses Darcy’s invitation to dance. This establishes their paradoxical relational pattern: the more she rejects him, the more he is drawn to her.
- **EVT-007:** she walks three miles to nurse Jane at Netherfield. Her intellectual exchanges with Darcy begin.
- **EVT-008:** she is mortified by her mother’s embarrassing visit to Netherfield, yet remains unbowed before Darcy.
- **EVT-014:** she believes Wickham’s false account in its entirety and becomes convinced that Darcy is “dishonest and inhuman.” **The decisive point at which her prejudice hardens.**
- **EVT-015:** she realizes that Collins has marked her out as the object of his proposal.

### Act II: Confrontation and Rupture (Chaps. 18–27)
- **EVT-016:** while dancing with Darcy at the Netherfield ball, she confronts him directly over Wickham. She is deeply ashamed by her family’s collective disgrace.
- **EVT-017:** she refuses Collins’s proposal at least five times, defining herself as “a rational creature speaking the truth from her heart.”
- **EVT-020:** she is deeply disappointed when Charlotte accepts Collins’s proposal and concludes that she can never again place genuine trust in her.
- **EVT-022:** when Wickham turns his attention to Miss King, Elizabeth calmly recognizes that she was never truly in love with him. Only wounded vanity remains.

### Act III: The Turning Point (Chaps. 28–40)
- **EVT-025:** at her first meeting with Lady Catherine, Elizabeth stands her ground without intimidation and becomes convinced that Wickham’s description was accurate, reinforcing her prejudice.
- **EVT-027:** Colonel Fitzwilliam confirms that Darcy was chiefly responsible for separating Bingley and Jane. Her anger reaches its highest point.
- **EVT-028:** she vehemently rejects Darcy’s first proposal: “the last man in the world whom I could ever be prevailed on to marry.” She gives three reasons: the insulting manner of his proposal, his separation of Bingley and Jane, and his mistreatment of Wickham.
- **EVT-029–030:** after reading Darcy’s letter, she discovers Wickham’s true nature. **“Till this moment, I never knew myself.”** It is both the recognition of her prejudice and a crisis of identity. She judges herself to have been “blind, partial, prejudiced, absurd.”

### Act IV: Reappraisal and Love (Chaps. 41–54)
- **EVT-032:** she tries and fails to persuade her father to prevent Lydia from going to Brighton. She acknowledges that Darcy’s criticism of her family was justified.
- **EVT-033:** in her final meeting with Wickham, she directly perceives his hypocrisy. She remarks pointedly that “Darcy improves upon acquaintance.”
- **EVT-035:** during her visit to Pemberley, she rediscovers Darcy’s taste and character through his estate. “And of this place, I might have been mistress!”
- **EVT-036:** she unexpectedly meets Darcy again and wonders at his completely altered behavior: “Why is he so altered?”
- **EVT-038:** she breaks down before Darcy upon hearing of Lydia’s flight and reproaches herself for keeping the truth about Wickham private.
- **EVT-039:** after Darcy leaves, she names her love for the first time: **“never had she so honestly felt that she could have loved him, as now, when all love must be vain.”**
- **EVT-041:** she now understands that he is exactly the man who, in disposition and talents, would most suit her. Her love is fully recognized, but appears hopeless.
- **EVT-042:** Mrs. Gardiner’s letter reveals Darcy’s secret intervention. Elizabeth’s gratitude and love deepen.

### Act V: Fulfillment (Chaps. 55–61)
- **EVT-045:** she firmly refuses Lady Catherine’s demand with “I will make no promise of the kind.” It amounts to an indirect declaration of love.
- **EVT-046:** she accepts Darcy’s second proposal, acknowledging that her feelings have undergone so material a change. The judgment that he was “the last man in the world” is completely reversed.
- **EVT-048:** she marries Darcy and becomes mistress of Pemberley.

## Core Relationships
- **Darcy:** aversion → prejudice → self-knowledge → respect → love → marriage
- **Jane:** her closest sister and confidante
- **Wickham:** attraction → trust → disillusionment → contempt
- **Charlotte:** close friendship → conflict over marriage → estrangement → partial reconciliation
- **Her father:** his favorite daughter and the only family member capable of intellectual exchange with him
- **Her mother:** a source of shame and an uncontrollable presence

## Translation Notes
- The early “lively, playful disposition” matures subtly into “ease and liveliness” later in the novel; the tonal progression should be preserved.
- The word “prejudice” itself is rarely used directly in the novel, yet it permeates all of Elizabeth’s judgments.
- Her descriptions of Darcy shift gradually from “disagreeable” through “improved on acquaintance,” “gentle sensation,” and “gratitude” to “love.” The translated vocabulary should preserve this gradient.
