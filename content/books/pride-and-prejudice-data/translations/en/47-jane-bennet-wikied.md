# Jane Bennet

## Basic Information
- **Original names:** Jane Bennet, Miss Bennet
- **Korean rendering:** Jane Bennet
- **Age:** about 22; older than Elizabeth and the eldest of five sisters
- **Family:** eldest daughter of the Bennet family
- **Residence:** Longbourn → the Netherfield area → London, staying with the Gardiners → Netherfield
- **First appearance:** chunk_01 (Chap. 1)

## Character Introduction
The eldest and most beautiful of the Bennets’ five daughters. Even her mother declares that no one is as pretty as Jane, and she immediately catches Bingley’s eye at the Meryton assembly. Gentle and benevolent in character as well as beautiful, she tries to see a person’s virtues before their faults. Even Elizabeth observes that Jane’s judgments are too generous.

Yet this goodness is also a weakness. Because she expresses her feelings with restraint, she fails to show Bingley the full extent of her affection. This becomes the basis for Darcy’s mistaken conclusion that Jane does not care for Bingley. After their separation, Jane suffers quietly in London without uttering a word of complaint, displaying the height of the emotional restraint demanded of women in the Regency era. She is the purest character in the novel and the emotional motive that provokes Elizabeth’s anger and action.

## Development by Event

### Act I: Meeting Bingley (Chaps. 1–12)
- **EVT-003:** she and Bingley confirm their mutual attraction by dancing together twice at the Meryton assembly. She alone among Bingley’s party receives the praise “angel.”
- **EVT-006:** as part of her mother’s scheme, she rides to Netherfield in the rain and catches a cold. Her prolonged stay at Netherfield gives her more opportunities to spend time with Bingley.
- **EVT-010:** she returns home after recovering. Her relationship with Bingley has deepened, but there has been no formal advance.

### Act II: Separation and Pain (Chaps. 18–27)
- **EVT-019:** Bingley abruptly leaves for London. Miss Bingley’s cold letter implies that he will not return, yet Jane tries not to doubt Miss Bingley’s goodwill.
- **EVT-021:** while staying with the Gardiners in London, she calls on Miss Bingley but is received coldly. Only then does she realize that Bingley is in London but has not sought her out. She resigns herself: “I will no longer deceive myself. He may have once liked me, but he does not now.”
- **EVT-023:** she tells Elizabeth that she has forgotten Bingley, though she has not forgotten him completely.

### Act III: Reunion and Marriage (Chaps. 43–61)
- **EVT-037:** news arrives that Bingley has returned to Netherfield. Jane pretends composure, but is inwardly shaken.
- **EVT-043:** Bingley begins visiting Longbourn. Color returns to Jane’s face, and her feelings revive.
- **EVT-044:** Bingley proposes. Jane declares herself “the happiest creature in the world” and confides to Elizabeth that she is so happy it cannot be real.
- **EVT-048:** she marries Bingley. After a year at Netherfield, they purchase an estate in Derbyshire and move near Pemberley.

## Core Relationships
- **Bingley:** mutual love at first sight, followed by separation, reunion, and marriage
- **Elizabeth:** her closest sister and sole confidante; they complement one another—Jane’s generosity corrects Elizabeth’s prejudice, while Elizabeth’s acuity corrects Jane’s innocence
- **Miss Bingley, Caroline:** an outward friendship, though Caroline uses and despises Jane; Jane tries to believe in her goodwill to the end
- **Darcy:** an indirectly antagonistic relationship, as Darcy leads the effort to separate her from Bingley; they reconcile by the conclusion
- **Her mother:** her mother’s greatest pride and the central object of her marriage project

## Translation Notes
- Jane always speaks gently and with restraint, and almost never condemns anyone. Preserve this consistent mildness in translation.
- “Miss Bennet” refers to Jane as the eldest daughter, while Elizabeth is distinguished as “Miss Eliza Bennet.” Observe the rules of social address.
- In phrases that express emotional restraint, such as “she bore it with fortitude” and “she tried to smile,” reflect the gap between inner pain and outward composure.
- Capture the subtle brightening of Jane’s tone when she reunites with Bingley.
