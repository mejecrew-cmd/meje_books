# *Pride and Prejudice* — Nuance List (Main-Text Translation Guide)

> This document tracks, chunk by chunk, **narration and dialogue whose surface meaning differs from its actual meaning**.
> Each entry follows the sequence `[Type]` → `Original` → `Surface meaning` → `Actual meaning/hidden nuance` → `Translation guidance`.
>
> **Type classification:**
> - **[Narratorial irony]** — Narration in which Austen's narrator mocks a character
> - **[Free indirect discourse]** — Sentences blending the narrator's voice with a character's thoughts
> - **[Subtext]** — Dialogue whose surface meaning differs from its real intention
> - **[Dramatic irony]** — Scenes shaped by information known to the reader but not the character
> - **[Double meaning]** — One word operating in two senses at once
> - **[Silence/absence]** — Cases where what is not said, or what is missing, carries meaning

## chunk_01 (Chaps. 1–6)

### NUA-001 [Narratorial irony] The opening — Key to the whole novel
> “It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune must be in want of a wife.”

- **Surface:** A wealthy bachelor naturally wants a wife.
- **Actual:** When a wealthy bachelor appears, **mothers with daughters** want him as a son-in-law. The subject is reversed. The inflation of “universally acknowledged” signals irony.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the elevated “truth universally acknowledged” for the irony to work. Paraphrasing it as casual common knowledge destroys the irony.

### NUA-002 [Narratorial irony] Introducing Mrs Bennet
> “She was a woman of mean understanding, little information, and uncertain temper. When she was discontented, she fancied herself nervous. The business of her life was to get her daughters married: its solace was visiting and news.”

- **Surface:** Objective characterization.
- **Actual:** “mean understanding” means poor intellect; “fancied herself nervous” means that when dissatisfied she **called herself** nervous, not that she truly had weak nerves. “business” and “solace” frame her life like **the management of a company**.
- **Translation guidance:** Do not render “fancied herself nervous” as an actual nervous collapse. Preserve the self-delusion: “imagined herself to have weak nerves.”

### NUA-003 [Subtext] Mr Bennet's first counterattack
> “You want to tell me, and I have no objection to hearing it.”

- **Surface:** I am willing to hear it.
- **Actual:** A **disguised concession**: “You are desperate to tell me, so I will let you.” It expresses fatigue and resignation, not interest, plus intellectual superiority in seeing through his wife's excitement.
- **Translation guidance:** Use a tone of indifferent concession: “You want to tell me, and I do not mind hearing it.”

### NUA-004 [Subtext] Mr Bennet conceals his favouritism toward Lizzy
> “I must throw in a good word for my little Lizzy.”

- **Surface:** I should say something good about Lizzy too.
- **Actual:** Recommending his daughter to Bingley is itself a joke intended to tease his wife. Yet “my little Lizzy” naturally reveals his preference; his wife instantly counters that “Lizzy is not a bit better than the others.”
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the warmth of “I must put in a word for our little Lizzy.”

### NUA-005 [Narratorial irony] Darcy's reversal of popularity
> “he was looked at with great admiration for about half the evening, till his manners gave a disgust which turned the tide of his popularity; for he was discovered to be proud”

- **Surface:** Admired for half the evening, then disliked.
- **Actual:** “discovered to be proud” is ironic. His character was not truly “discovered” at one ball; he was **summarily judged**. The narrator mocks the neighbourhood's rash judgment.
- **Translation guidance:** Prefer a quasi-judicial phrase such as “he was pronounced proud” over a neutral “was found to be proud.”

### NUA-006 [Subtext] “tolerable” — The novel's point of departure
> “She is tolerable; but not handsome enough to tempt me; and I am in no humour at present to give consequence to young ladies who are slighted by other men.”

- **Surface:** She is acceptable but not attractive enough to interest me.
- **Three layers of actual nuance:**
  1. “tolerable” = “bearable” — not praise but **the lowest grade of indifference**
  2. “to tempt me” assumes that he is a prize worth tempting — **pride**
  3. “slighted by other men” means there is no reason to notice a woman ignored by other men — Elizabeth is treated as **surplus**
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve all three layers: “She is tolerable, but not handsome enough to tempt me, and I am not disposed to give importance to young ladies whom other men have slighted.”

### NUA-007 [Narratorial irony] Charlotte's theory of marriage — Foreshadowing
> “Happiness in marriage is entirely a matter of chance.”

- **Surface:** Marital happiness is pure luck.
- **Actual:** Elizabeth and the reader take it lightly at this point, but it precisely foreshadows Charlotte's acceptance of Collins in EVT-020. She means it seriously.
- **Translation guidance:** Keep it light enough on first reading and grave on rereading: “Happiness in marriage is entirely a matter of chance.”

### NUA-008 [Free indirect discourse] Darcy's attraction — A secret known only to the reader
> “Mr. Darcy had at first scarcely allowed her to be pretty: he had looked at her without admiration at the ball; and when they next met, he looked at her only to criticise. But no sooner had he made it clear to himself and his friends that she had hardly a good feature in her face, than he began to find it was rendered uncommonly intelligent by the beautiful expression of her dark eyes.”

- **Surface:** A report of Darcy's thoughts.
- **Actual:** Free indirect discourse conveys Darcy's interiority in his own tone. “scarcely allowed her to be pretty” means he would not **permit himself** to find her pretty; he is attracted and resisting. The movement from “only to criticise” to “to find” reveals **the defeat of his will**.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the blurred boundary between narratorial observation and Darcy's mind, especially the pivot “no sooner had he made it clear to himself... than he began to find.”

## chunk_02 (Chaps. 7–9)

### NUA-009 [Subtext] Mrs Bennet's scheme
> “No, my dear, you had better go on horseback, because it seems likely to rain; and then you must stay all night.”

- **Surface:** Ride because it looks likely to rain.
- **Actual:** A **deliberate scheme** to have Jane caught by the rain and kept at Netherfield. “it seems likely to rain” is not observation but **hope**. It succeeds and Jane becomes ill.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the surface casualness and let readers discover the scheme: “No, my dear, you had better go on horseback; it looks like rain, and then you must stay the night.”

### NUA-010 [Subtext + silence] Darcy's “Not at all”
> (Miss Bingley) “I am afraid, Mr. Darcy, that this adventure has rather affected your admiration of her fine eyes.”
> (Darcy) “Not at all; they were brightened by the exercise.”

- **Surface:** Not at all; exercise made them brighter.
- **Actual:** He meets Miss Bingley's attack **head-on**, publicly affirms Elizabeth's beauty, and neutralizes the attempt to check him. “A short pause followed this speech”: Miss Bingley's loss for words is meaningful **silence**.
- **Translation guidance:** Keep Darcy's answer brief and decisive; never expand it. Preserve the pause that follows.

### NUA-011 [Subtext] Darcy's class judgment
> “But it must very materially lessen their chance of marrying men of any consideration in the world.”

- **Surface:** Low connections reduce their marriage prospects.
- **Actual:** When Bingley says an uncle in Cheapside does not matter, Darcy declares the **cold social reality**. It returns in his proposal as “inferiority of connections,” planting the seed of **self-contradiction**.
- **Translation guidance:** Make it sound like a verdict: “But it must materially lessen their chance of marrying men of any standing.”

### NUA-012 [Narratorial irony] Mrs Bennet's “victory”
> “Mrs. Bennet, who fancied she had gained a complete victory over him, continued her triumph.”

- **Surface:** She feels she has completely defeated Darcy.
- **Actual:** “fancied” means **deluded herself**. She reads Darcy's silent withdrawal as victory; in fact, she has deepened his contempt.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve “fancied”: “Mrs Bennet, imagining that she had gained a complete victory over him, continued triumphantly.”

## chunk_03 (Chaps. 10–13)

### NUA-013 [Subtext] Darcy confesses his flaw — Double foreshadowing
> “My temper I dare not vouch for. It is, I believe, too little yielding... My good opinion once lost is lost for ever.”

- **Surface:** My temper is unyielding, and once I lose a good opinion, it is lost forever.
- **Double foreshadowing:**
  1. **Wickham:** Explains Darcy's implacable attitude toward him
  2. **Elizabeth's weapon:** In EVT-016 she uses this confession as **evidence** for Wickham: “I remember his boasting of the implacability of his resentments”
- **Translation guidance:** Make the line memorable because it is quoted twice later: “My good opinion once lost is lost for ever.”

### NUA-014 [Subtext + double meaning] “Your defect is wilfully to misunderstand them”
> (Darcy) “And yours is wilfully to misunderstand them.”

- **Surface:** Your flaw is deliberately misunderstanding others.
- **Actual:** A prophecy that identifies the novel's theme, Prejudice. Darcy already sees Elizabeth's central flaw; in EVT-030 she recognizes that he was right.
- **Translation guidance:** “wilfully” is crucial: a **chosen misunderstanding**, not an accidental error.

### NUA-015 [Free indirect discourse] Darcy's inner danger
> “He began to feel the danger of paying Elizabeth too much attention.”

- **Surface:** He felt it dangerous to pay Elizabeth too much attention.
- **Actual:** “danger” is the key word. He defines attraction as **danger**, a threat under his class standards. It directly causes his deliberate distancing in EVT-010.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve “danger”; do not soften it to burden or inconvenience.

## chunk_04 (Chaps. 14–17)

### NUA-016 [Subtext] Wickham's rhetoric — Subtle manipulation
> “I have no right to give my opinion as to his being agreeable or otherwise. I am not qualified to form one. I have known him too long and too well to be a fair judge.”

- **Surface:** I have no right or qualification to judge him because I know him too well.
- **Actual:** An **attack disguised as humility**. He pretends to withhold judgment while “too long and too well” implies that **he knows Darcy best**, planting the impression that he possesses a terrible secret.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the simulated modesty. Making the attack explicit destroys the manipulation's subtlety.

### NUA-017 [Dramatic irony] Elizabeth's certainty
> “She remembered that he had boasted one day, at Netherfield, of the implacability of his resentments... His disposition must be dreadful.”

- **Surface:** She recalls Darcy boasting that his resentments are implacable.
- **Actual:** Elizabeth uses NUA-013 as **evidence supporting Wickham**. First-time readers are deceived with her; on rereading the irony erupts, because Darcy's honest self-analysis becomes **a weapon against him**.
- **Translation guidance:** Convey her certainty, while keeping Darcy's original line consistent so a rereader can recognize the irony.

## chunk_05 (Chaps. 18–19)

### NUA-018 [Subtext] The ball dance — Multilayered conversation
> (Elizabeth) “It is your turn to say something now, Mr. Darcy. I talked about the dance, and you ought to make some kind of remark on the size of the room, or the number of couples.”

- **Surface:** It is your turn; say something about the room or couples.
- **Actual:** She mocks the **rules of conversation**, satirizing empty social talk while attacking Darcy's silence. Darcy responds that whatever she wishes him to say shall be said, showing **compliant humour** she did not expect.
- **Translation guidance:** Contrast Elizabeth's provocative lightness with Darcy's unexpected compliance.

### NUA-019 [Dramatic irony + subtext] Raising Wickham
> (Elizabeth) “When you met us there the other day, we had just been forming a new acquaintance.”

- **Surface:** We had just been making a new acquaintance that day.
- **Actual:** Elizabeth **deliberately** raises Wickham to test Darcy. “A deeper shade of hauteur overspread his features.” She reads the reaction as **evidence of guilt**, but it is actually **justified anger** at Wickham.
- **Translation guidance:** Render “hauteur” as pride or haughtiness while maintaining Elizabeth's viewpoint; the translator must still understand its true cause.

### NUA-020 [Subtext] The contradiction in Collins's proposal
> “Before I am run away with by my feelings on this subject, perhaps it will be advisable for me to state my reasons for marrying.”

- **Surface:** Before emotion carries me away, I should state my reasons.
- **Actual:** “run away with by my feelings” reveals that **there is no feeling at all**. A person in love does not enumerate reasons. Of his three reasons, Lady Catherine's recommendation matters most; even his marriage is an instruction from his patroness.
- **Translation guidance:** Never shorten Collins's verbosity. Its length embodies his foolishness; preserve the irony of “before I am carried away by my feelings.”

## chunk_06 (Chaps. 20–23)

### NUA-021 [Subtext — celebrated scene] Mr Bennet's finest line
> “An unhappy alternative is before you, Elizabeth. Your mother will never see you again if you do not marry Mr. Collins, and I will never see you again if you do.”

- **Surface:** If you refuse Collins your mother will never see you again; if you accept him, I will not.
- **Actual:** (1) He neutralizes his wife's logic by **reversing it in the same form**; (2) declares his support for Elizabeth through wit; (3) indirectly exposes the emptiness of his wife's threat.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the antithetical symmetry and rhythm: “if you do not... your mother; if you do... I.”

### NUA-022 [Narratorial irony] Charlotte's cold calculation
> “Without thinking highly either of men or of matrimony, marriage had always been her object: it was the only honourable provision for well-educated young women of small fortune, and, however uncertain of giving happiness, must be their pleasantest preservative from want.”

- **Surface:** Though she thinks little of men or matrimony, marriage has always been her aim.
- **Actual:** The narrator intervenes in **social criticism**. “honourable provision” means a respectable means of support; “preservative from want” makes marriage an **economic survival strategy**. This is an age's problem, not merely Charlotte's.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the cold metaphor of “preservative”: “the pleasantest preservative against poverty.”

## chunk_07-08 (Chaps. 24–32)

### NUA-023 [Free indirect discourse] Elizabeth's self-deception
> “She was watchful enough to see it all, but she could see it and write of it without material pain. Her heart had been but slightly touched, and her vanity was satisfied with believing that she would have been his only choice, had fortune permitted it.”

- **Surface:** She calmly accepts Wickham's move toward Miss King.
- **Actual:** “without material pain” quotes Elizabeth's **self-rationalization** through FID. “vanity was satisfied” exposes vanity, not love, as the real feeling; she recognizes this in EVT-030.
- **Translation guidance:** Use “without any considerable pain,” whose lightness itself signals self-deception.

### NUA-024 [Narratorial irony + silence] Darcy's repeated visits
> “But why Mr. Darcy came so often to the Parsonage it was more difficult to understand. It could not be for society, as he frequently sat there ten minutes together without opening his lips; and when he did speak, it seemed the effect of necessity rather than of choice.”

- **Surface:** It is hard to understand why Darcy visits so often.
- **Actual:** The narrator ironically pretends not to know **the obvious fact of love**. Ten silent minutes prove it is not sociability while hinting that the silence is **evidence of love**. Charlotte notices; Elizabeth dismisses it.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the puzzled narratorial tone and leave the true purpose to the reader.

## chunk_09 (Chaps. 33–34) — Proposal/rejection

### NUA-025 [Dramatic irony] Fitzwilliam's careless revelation
> “He congratulated himself on having lately saved a friend from the inconveniences of a most imprudent marriage.”

- **Surface:** He congratulates himself for saving a friend from an unsuitable marriage.
- **Actual:** Fitzwilliam does not know it refers to Bingley and Jane. He repeats Darcy's boast. “inconveniences” reduces marriage to Jane to an inconvenience; “imprudent” demotes her love to imprudence.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve Fitzwilliam's light tone against the blow it delivers to Elizabeth.

### NUA-026 [Subtext — the novel's greatest confession] Darcy's first proposal
> “In vain have I struggled. It will not do. My feelings will not be repressed. You must allow me to tell you how ardently I admire and love you.”

- **Surface:** A declaration of love.
- **Subtext:** “In vain have I struggled” makes love an **object of combat**; “It will not do” declares repression's **failure**. His confession begins as an admission of defeat, followed by the worse insult of dwelling on her inferiority and his degradation.
- **Translation guidance:** Give “In vain have I struggled” its painful force. The declaration must feel like love and **insult at once**; never soften “inferiority” or “degradation.”

### NUA-027 [Subtext] Elizabeth's rejection — Anger in formal dress
> “In such cases as this, it is, I believe, the established mode to express a sense of obligation for the sentiments avowed, however unequally they may be returned.”

- **Surface:** Convention requires gratitude in such a case.
- **Actual:** The **blade of formality**. By invoking “the established mode,” she announces that **she will not follow it**. “however unequally they may be returned” formally declares that his feelings will **never be returned**.
- **Translation guidance:** Keep the formal register while exposing the edge of rejection. Removing the formality destroys the line's power.

### NUA-028 [Subtext] “not in a gentlemanlike manner”
> “had you behaved in a more gentlemanlike manner”

- **Surface:** Had you behaved more like a gentleman.
- **Actual:** For a man of the period, “not gentlemanlike” is **an insult of the highest order**, directly negating the gentlemanly identity grounded in his birth, education, and property. “She saw him start at this.” The phrase becomes the central shock that changes Darcy.
- **Translation guidance:** Keep “ungentlemanlike,” not a softened “impolite.”

## chunk_10 (Chaps. 35–40) — Letter and recognition

### NUA-029 [Free indirect discourse — the novel's finest psychology] Recognizing prejudice
> “She grew absolutely ashamed of herself. Of neither Darcy nor Wickham could she think, without feeling that she had been blind, partial, prejudiced, absurd.”

- **Surface:** Shame.
- **Actual:** A four-word climax: blind → partial → prejudiced → absurd. “Prejudice” in the title is applied to **Elizabeth herself**. “Of neither Darcy nor Wickham” means a double recognition that she misjudged both.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the rising intensity: “blind, partial, prejudiced, absurd.”

### NUA-030 [Free indirect discourse] “Till this moment, I never knew myself”
> “How despicably have I acted! I, who have prided myself on my discernment!... vanity, not love, has been my folly. Pleased with the preference of one, and offended by the neglect of the other, on the very beginning of our acquaintance, I have courted prepossession and ignorance, and driven reason away... Till this moment, I never knew myself.”

- **Surface:** Self-reflection.
- **Four layers:**
  1. “prided myself” — Elizabeth also possessed **pride**
  2. “vanity, not love” — her feeling for Wickham was vanity, not love
  3. “Pleased with the preference of one...” — she merely enjoyed Wickham's attention and resented Darcy's neglect, misreading her own emotion
  4. “I never knew myself” — she prided herself on judging others but did not know **herself**
- **Translation guidance:** This is the novel's peak. Translate each sentence precisely and with dignity, especially the famous “Till this moment, I never knew myself.”

## chunk_12-13 (Chaps. 43–48) — Pemberley and the elopement

### NUA-031 [Free indirect discourse] Regret at Pemberley
> “And of this place, thought she, I might have been mistress! With these rooms I might have now been familiarly acquainted! Instead of viewing them as a stranger, I might have rejoiced in them as my own... But no — that could never be; my uncle and aunt would have been lost to me; I should not have been allowed to invite them.”

- **Surface:** Regret that she might have been mistress of the house.
- **Actual:** “This was a lucky recollection—it saved her from something like regret.” “something like regret” is **not regret but something resembling it**. She consoles herself by imagining that the Gardiners could not have been invited, rationalizing that it is not real regret.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve “something like regret”; never flatten it into outright regret.

### NUA-032 [Narratorial irony — dramatic] The timing of recognizing love
> “never had she so honestly felt that she could have loved him, as now, when all love must be vain.”

- **Surface:** Never had she felt so honestly that she could have loved him.
- **Actual:** The novel's cruelest irony: **she recognizes love at the moment love seems impossible**. “must be vain” means inevitably futile. Rereaders know that Darcy is at this very moment beginning his secret rescue.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the brutal contrast: “now, when all love must be vain.”

## chunk_14-15 (Chaps. 49–54) — Resolution

### NUA-033 [Subtext] Mr Bennet's self-mockery
> “Wickham's a fool if he takes her with a farthing less than ten thousand pounds: I should be sorry to think so ill of him, in the very beginning of our relationship.”

- **Surface:** Wickham is a fool if he takes Lydia for less than £10,000.
- **Actual:** Anger at the worst possible son-in-law wrapped in humour. “I should be sorry to think so ill of him” is ironic because he already thinks the worst; “the very beginning of our relationship” forecasts how unhappy the relationship will be.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve Mr Bennet's cynical wit; direct anger would erase it.

### NUA-034 [Free indirect discourse — hidden confession] Elizabeth's complete love
> “She began now to comprehend that he was exactly the man who, in disposition and talents, would most suit her. His understanding and temper, though unlike her own, would have answered all her wishes. It was an union that must have been to the advantage of both.”

- **Surface:** She begins to understand that he suits her best.
- **Actual:** “unlike her own” is crucial: they suit each other **because they differ**. Her ease and liveliness would soften him, while she would learn from his judgment. Yet the ideal complementarity is expressed in the counterfactual “must have been,” as though already impossible.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the pain of the counterfactual: “would have been” / “might have.”

## chunk_16-17 (Chaps. 55–59) — Second proposal

### NUA-035 [Subtext] Lady Catherine as paradoxical catalyst
> (Lady C.) “Let me be rightly understood. This match, to which you have the presumption to aspire, can never take place.”
> (Elizabeth) “I will make no promise of the kind.”

- **Surface:** Promise not to marry him / I will make no such promise.
- **Actual:** Elizabeth's refusal to promise “not to” is an indirect declaration that **she may do so**. Darcy later explains that this taught him to hope: had she been absolutely against him, she would have told Lady Catherine openly.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the firmness of “I will make no promise of the kind” as a refusal of will.

### NUA-036 [Subtext] The second proposal — Contrast with the first
> “If your feelings are still what they were last April, tell me so at once. My affections and wishes are unchanged; but one word from you will silence me on this subject for ever.”

- **Surface:** If your feelings are unchanged, say so; one word will silence me forever.
- **Actual:** A decisive contrast with NUA-026: (1) “In vain have I struggled” becomes “My affections are unchanged,” replacing struggle with **acceptance**; (2) inferiority and degradation disappear; (3) “You must allow me” becomes “one word from you will silence me,” giving **decision-making power to her**.
- **Translation guidance:** Maximize the tonal difference. The feeling is the same, but its expression is transformed; humility and anxiety must be audible.

## chunk_18 (Chaps. 60–61) — Ending

### NUA-037 [Narratorial irony] Final irony
> “Happy for all her maternal feelings was the day on which Mrs. Bennet got rid of her two most deserving daughters.”

- **Surface:** The day Mrs Bennet married off her two finest daughters was happy.
- **Actual:** “got rid of” means **disposed of**. “most deserving daughters” are the most valuable daughters. Treating them as disposed stock completes the irony of Mrs Bennet's view of marriage.
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the cynicism of “got rid of,” not merely the neutral “married off.”

## Appendix: Austen's narrative techniques — Technical guide for translators

### A. Identifying Free Indirect Discourse

Austen's central technique: a character's first-person thought enters the narrator's third-person prose **without quotation marks**.

**Signals:**
1. Exclamations or questions appear inside third-person narration: “How despicably had she acted!”
2. After “she felt” or “she thought,” the prose gradually changes into the character's tone
3. The boundary between narrator and character is **ambiguous**

**Translation principle:** Deliberately preserve that ambiguity. Quotation marks or repeated “she thought” formulas destroy Austen's technique.

### B. Identifying narratorial irony

The narrator describes characters **outwardly neutrally but actually mockingly**.

**Signals:**
1. “fancied” / “imagined” / “flattered herself” = a signal of **delusion**
2. Excessively formal words applied to trivial objects = **inflated mockery**
3. “perhaps” / “it is possible” = the narrator pretending to reserve judgment while **certain**

**Translation principle:** Maintain a neutral narratorial tone while accurately translating mocking cue words such as “fancied.”

### C. Translating silence and absence

In Austen, **what is not said** often bears the most important meaning.

| Type of silence | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Darcy's silence (Netherfield) | Does not speak for ten minutes | Pain of love |
| Darcy's silence (Wickham topic) | Changes the subject | Pride refusing self-defence |
| Wickham's absence (Netherfield ball) | Does not attend | Cowardice (admits voluntary absence) |
| “A short pause followed” | After Miss Bingley's “fine eyes” attack | The attack backfires; Miss Bingley loses |
| Jane's silence (after Bingley leaves) | “the subject was never alluded to” | The wound is too deep to discuss |

**Translation principle:** Never reduce or omit “a pause,” “silence,” “said nothing,” or “made no answer.” These silences say more than dialogue.

### D. Letter styles — Differences among characters' epistolary voices

Letters compress a character's **education, personality, and intention**. Their formality and tone must differ sharply.

| Letter | Chapter | Stylistic traits | Translation guidance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Collins's first letter** (Ch. 13) | Excessive formality, concessions and parentheticals, repeated Lady Catherine, inflated figures such as “olive branch” | Use an excessively elevated register. Never shorten the sentences; length embodies foolishness |
| **Miss Bingley's letters** (Chs. 21, 24) | Surface excess of affection (“dearest friend,” “I depend on you”), actually a tool of separation; subtly inserts Bingley–Georgiana marriage hints | Preserve exaggerated intimacy so hypocrisy appears on rereading; retain the pretended caution of “dare to entertain” |
| **Darcy's letter** (Ch. 35) | Highest epistolary register; carefully nested complex, concessive, and conditional clauses; emotion leaks through restraint | Distinguish clearly from speech. Let emotion rise gradually in the Wickham section |
| **Jane's letters** (Ch. 46) | Calmness collapses: first letter composed → second confused (“I hardly know what I have written”); grammar and structure break down | Maximize the tonal difference and preserve the stop-start breathing of the second letter |
| **Mr Gardiner's letter** (Ch. 49) | Businesslike and concise; controlled emotion; precise legal/financial language | Use a practical business register with exact commitments |
| **Mrs Gardiner's letter** (Ch. 52) | Warmer and chattier; mixes information about Darcy with humorous implication: “If he had another motive, I am sure it would never disgrace him” | Cultivated, friendly epistolary prose; preserve the smile behind “another motive” |
| **Lydia's letters** (Chs. 42, 47) | Ignores grammar, overuses underlining/italics, “was obliged to leave off in a violent hurry”; long but empty | Voice of an immature teenager. Preserve emphasis and irresponsible haste; “lines under the words” hints at a secret-love code |
| **Collins's congratulatory/warning letter** (Ch. 57) | Reuses “olive branch” to mean “offspring”; warns that Lady Catherine will never tolerate the rumour, yet advises asking forgiveness | Maintain the same inflated formality as his first letter; annotate the changed meaning of “olive branch” |

### E. The social meaning of dance

Dance is not mere leisure but **a language of courtship, rejection, and power**.

| Dance action | Social meaning | Example in the novel |
|---|---|---|
| Dancing twice | Public expression of special interest | Bingley dances twice with Jane → the whole neighbourhood expects marriage |
| Refusing a dance | Public insult or expression of strong will | Elizabeth refuses Darcy (EVT-005); Darcy dances with no one (EVT-003) |
| Accepting an invitation | Social duty or acceptance of interest | Elizabeth accepts Darcy “without knowing what she did” (Ch. 18) — unconscious acceptance |
| Reserving the first two dances | Claims that evening's partner in advance | Collins reserves Elizabeth's first two (EVT-015), blocking Wickham |
| Boulanger | Final dance involving all guests | Mrs Bennet lists Bingley's partners and ends with “and the Boulanger” |
| Reel | Lively Scottish dance | Darcy provocatively proposes it to Elizabeth (EVT-009) |

**Translation principle:** Do not translate every “dance” identically. Distinguish “dance,” “ask for a dance,” and “be someone's partner” by context. “stand up with” is a period idiom meaning “dance with.”
