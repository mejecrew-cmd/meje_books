# *Pride and Prejudice* — Estimated Household-Staff Structures

> ⚠️ **This document consists of estimates and creative reconstruction.** The original novel explicitly identifies only some servants. The rest are reasoned estimates based on the standard staff of Regency-era English gentry households and the income of each family in the novel. A translation should use only servants explicitly named in the original; this document may also serve as a reference for adaptation or dramatization.

---

| Household | Annual Income | Standard Number of Servants | Servants Explicitly Mentioned |

## 2. Rosings — Lady Catherine’s Estate

Similar in scale to Pemberley, with the following differences:

| Estimated Feature | Basis |
|---|---|
| **Possibly more servants** | Lady Catherine’s ostentatious disposition |
| **Mrs. Jenkinson** (companion) | **Explicitly mentioned**—companion and governess to Anne de Bourgh; monitors her meals |
| **Lady’s Maid** (for Miss de Bourgh) | Estimated; attends to an invalid mistress |
| **Lady’s Maid** (for Lady Catherine) | Separate servant—“her waiting-woman was in it” in Ch.56, waiting in the carriage; **explicitly mentioned** |
| **Private Coachman** | A four-horse carriage and use of post horses require an experienced coachman |
| **Music Teacher** | Estimated from Lady Catherine’s recurring habit of recommending musical instruction |

### Estimated Total Staff: **30–45**

## 4. Longbourn — The Bennet Household

The original describes this household in the greatest detail. Its staff is typical of the middling gentry:

| Position | Number | Explicitly Mentioned / Estimated | Role in the Novel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hill (Mrs. Hill)** | 1 | **Explicitly mentioned**—Ch.49 (“My dear Hill”), Ch.13 | Housekeeper; trusted by Mrs. Bennet |
| **Butler** | 1 | **Explicitly mentioned**—Ch.49 (“met by the butler”) | Waits at table |
| **Cook** | 1 | Estimated; Mrs. Hill may fill both roles | |
| **Sarah** | 1 | **Explicitly mentioned**—Ch.55 (“Sarah, come to Miss Bennet”) | Maid; assists with dressing |
| **Housemaid** (besides Sarah) | 1–2 | Estimated | |
| **Kitchen Maid** | 1 | Estimated | |
| **Footman** | 1 | **Explicitly mentioned**—Ch.7 (“entrance of the footman with a note”) | Letters and doors |
| **Coachman** | 1 | **Explicitly implied** through references to the carriage and horses | Drives the carriage; horses shared with the farm |
| **Stable Boy** | 1 | Estimated | |
| **Farm Laborers** | 2–3 | **Implied**—the horses are “wanted in the farm much oftener” | Farm work |
| **Laundress** | 1 | Estimated; the work may be contracted out | |
| **Lady’s Maid** | Estimated **none** | Shared by the five sisters or absent | A sign of limited means |

### Estimated Total Staff: **5–8** indoor servants, with farm laborers counted separately

> **Translation Note:** The Bennet sisters probably have **no personal lady’s maids**. Mrs. Bennet therefore dresses their hair herself, or the sisters help one another. This is the background to the bustle of preparing for a ball—“Sarah, never mind Miss Lizzy’s hair” (Ch.55).

## 7. Carriage Staff for Travel from Hunsford to Rosings

When the Collinses go to Rosings:
- Lady Catherine sends a carriage: “Her Ladyship’s carriage is regularly ordered for us” (Ch.28).
- Collins therefore has **no carriage of his own**—at most, he travels on foot or keeps a gig.

## 8. Wickham and Lydia After Their Marriage — A Northern Regular-Army Posting

The newly married household of a junior officer:

| Staff | Note |
|---|---|
| **One maid-of-all-work** | Performs every domestic task alone, a sign of poverty |
| No one else | A consequence of Wickham’s debts and Lydia’s extravagance |

> **Translation Note:** This explains why Lydia constantly asks her sisters for money after her marriage: “they were always moving from place to place in quest of a cheap situation” (Ch.61).

## Use in Translation and Adaptation

| Context | Use |
|---|---|
| **Translation** | Use only servants explicitly mentioned in the original; this document is for background understanding |
| **Dramatization** | Visually distinguish each household through the number and ranks of its servants |
| **Novel Adaptation** | Use the absence of personal lady’s maids in the Bennet household to emphasize the sisters’ intimacy |
| **Derivative Works** | Portray class through the contrast between the scale of Pemberley’s and Longbourn’s staffs |
