# Charlotte Lucas

## Basic Information
- **Original names:** Charlotte Lucas; Mrs. Collins after marriage
- **Korean rendering:** Charlotte Lucas / Mrs. Collins after marriage
- **Age:** 27, “she was now about seven and twenty”
- **Family:** the Lucas family. Her father, Sir William Lucas, is a knight and former Meryton tradesman
- **Residence:** Lucas Lodge → Hunsford Parsonage after marriage
- **First appearance:** chunk_02, Chap. 3

## Character Introduction
Elizabeth's closest friend and the most pragmatic woman in the novel. Intelligent and sensible, Charlotte coolly recognizes her social position as an unmarried woman of twenty-seven who is neither beautiful nor wealthy. Her view that “Happiness in marriage is entirely a matter of chance” directly opposes Elizabeth's romantic idealism.

Her decision to accept Collins's proposal is one of the novel's central points of debate. Charlotte neither loves nor respects him, but chooses marriage as a practical means of securing a “comfortable home.” Elizabeth is deeply disappointed, yet Charlotte's life at Hunsford shows how she manages the marriage through her own strategies: she encourages Collins to spend time in the garden and secures a room of her own to preserve a minimum of independent space.

## Development by Event

### Decision to Marry, Chap. 22
- **EVT-020:** Collins proposes to Charlotte three days after Elizabeth rejects him, and Charlotte accepts immediately. She tells Elizabeth, “I am not romantic. I ask only a comfortable home. Considering Mr. Collins's character, connections, and situation in life, I am convinced my chance of happiness with him is as fair as most people can boast.” **Elizabeth is deeply disappointed and decides that there can never again be real confidence between them.**

### Life at Hunsford, Ch. 28–30
- **EVT-025:** Elizabeth visits Hunsford Parsonage and observes Charlotte's practical management of life with Collins. Charlotte encourages him to work in the garden while she spends her time on household tasks and reading. She pragmatically prefers not to hear the foolish things he says.
- **EVT-026:** she attends dinner at Rosings Park and performs her social obligations toward Lady Catherine without difficulty.

## Core Relationships
- **Elizabeth:** her closest friend, though the marriage to Collins fractures their friendship; they partly reconcile during the Hunsford visit
- **Collins, husband:** a practical marriage without affection; she manages his foolishness and secures space of her own
- **Lady Catherine:** neighbor and her husband's patron; Charlotte faithfully performs her social duties
- **Lucas family:** Sir William is pleased by his daughter's marriage; her brother and sister Maria visit Hunsford

## Translation Notes
- Charlotte's dialogue is concise and practical, without emotional exaggeration. Use a dry tone clearly distinct from both Elizabeth's wit and Mrs. Bennet's exclamations.
- The word “comfortable” in “comfortable home” means more than physical comfort: it denotes the best social security a woman of the period could expect. A rendering such as “a secure home” may be appropriate.
- Charlotte's self-defense when accepting the marriage is neither servile nor defiant, but composed. That restraint makes a deeper impression on the reader.
- The change in address from “Charlotte Lucas” to “Mrs. Collins” reflects the social meaning of marriage within the narrative.
