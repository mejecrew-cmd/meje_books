# *Pride and Prejudice* — Dictionary of Body Language and Etiquette

> A guide to Regency-era physical manners, etiquette, and movement—the definitive standard for screen and stage direction.
> A wrong gesture destroys the credibility of a period drama.

---

## 1. Greetings

### Bow (A Man’s Greeting)

#### Degree of Formality

| Type | Movement | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Slight bow** | Incline the head slightly, 4–5 degrees | Everyday greeting between social equals |
| **Bow from the neck** | Incline only the neck, 10–15 degrees | Standard greeting |
| **Bow from the waist** | Bend at the waist, 30–45 degrees | Formal; for a noblewoman or superior |
| **Deep bow** | 60 degrees or more, with one foot slightly behind | Highest formality; royalty or a first meeting |

#### Examples from the Novel
- **Darcy → Collins** at the Ch.18 ball: a “slight bow,” deliberately condescending
- **Collins → everyone:** Deep formality, signaling his hypocrisy
- **Wickham → Darcy** in the street in Ch.15: “touched his hat,” barely acknowledging him

#### Translation Notes
- “Bowed” should be visualized as a **physical action**, not reduced to “greeted.”
- Degree of formality signals the relationship.

### Curtsey (A Woman’s Greeting)

#### Types

| Type | Movement | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Bob curtsey** | Bend the knees lightly | Everyday |
| **Half curtsey** | Bend the knees further and lower the head | Standard |
| **Full court curtsey** | Place one foot far behind and bend the knees deeply | Court or highest formality |

#### Details of the Movement
- **Hands:** Lightly hold the sides of the dress or leave them naturally at rest
- **Eyes:** Gaze slightly downward, more so with greater formality
- **Duration:** Hold for one to two seconds

#### Examples from the Novel
- **Elizabeth → Darcy** in Ch.30: “merely courtesied to him, without saying a word”—**a bare curtsey + silence signals coldness**
- **Maria Lucas → Lady Catherine:** A deep curtsey, with nervousness

### Handshake—**Almost Never Used**

- In the Regency period, a handshake signaled **intimacy** and was not a routine greeting as it is today.
- Never used at a first meeting.
- In Ch.61, Miss Bingley “even shook hands” with Elizabeth—**a sign of concession**.

## 2. Conduct When Addressing Someone

### Formal Introduction After a First Meeting

```
[Rules]
- A lower-ranking person is introduced to a higher-ranking person
- A man is introduced to a woman; the woman holds the social choice
- Speaking without an introduction is rude

[Procedure]
1. An intermediary stands between the two people
2. “Mr. Darcy, may I introduce Miss Elizabeth Bennet?”
   - The higher-ranking person’s name comes first
3. Both greet each other: bow + curtsey
4. Brief conversation may follow

[Examples from the Novel]
- Collins introduces himself to Darcy in Ch.18—rude; Darcy responds with contempt
- A master of ceremonies may introduce people at a ball
```

### Hat Manners

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Greeting a woman | **Raise the hat:** touch + lift |
| Entering indoors | **Remove the hat** |
| Meeting an acquaintance | “Touched his hat”—Wickham and Darcy in Ch.15 |
| Speaking with a woman | Raise the hat at the start, then hold it or tuck it under the arm |

## 3. Manners at a Ball

### Entrance

```
[Order]
1. Leave outerwear, hats, and gloves in the cloakroom
2. Wait briefly in the antechamber
3. Announce arrival to the master of ceremonies: “Mr. and Mrs. Bennet, with their daughters”
4. Enter the main hall
5. Greet the host and hostess
```

### Procedure for Requesting a Dance

```
[A Man Approaching a Woman]
1. They must have been formally introduced
2. Approach, bow, and say: “May I have the honour of the next dance, Miss Bennet?”
3. If she accepts, stand beside her and converse until the music begins
4. When the music begins, move together to the dance line
```

### Rules for Refusal

```
[Rules]
- If a woman refuses one man, convention forbids her from dancing that set with anyone
- Polite reasons: “I am engaged” or “I am too tired”
- An explicit refusal is a social insult

[Examples from the Novel]
- Ch.6: Elizabeth refuses Darcy—“I have not the least intention of dancing”
   = an insulting refusal, which instead fascinates Darcy
- Ch.18: Elizabeth accepts Darcy’s request “without knowing what she did”
   = an unconscious acceptance
```

### Dance Movements

#### Country Dance (The Most Common)

```
[Formation]
- Two lines, one of men and one of women, face each other
- One couple begins at the end and gradually progresses down the line

[Movements]
1. Face each other and greet: bow + curtsey
2. Join hands and turn once
3. Four steps forward, then four back
4. Do-si-do—circle back-to-back
5. Repeat with the next couple down the line
6. One set lasts 30 minutes to an hour through repeated figures
```

#### How Hands Are Held

```
[Formal—two people]
- Palms face each other and only the fingers touch lightly
- Never interlace fingers
- Gloves are worn, blocking skin contact

[More Formal—fingertips only]
- Hold only the index and middle fingers lightly
- Used at a formal ball

[Meaning in the Novel]
- Fingertips only = distance
- Full palm contact = intimacy
- How Darcy and Elizabeth hold hands in their Ch.18 dance carries visual meaning
```

### Reel (Scottish)

- Faster and livelier
- Four or more dancers
- Many turning figures
- The dance Darcy proposes to Elizabeth in Ch.10—**a lively dance makes the proposal provocative**

### Quadrille (Formal Dance for Four Couples)

- Four couples arranged in a square
- Complex steps
- Became popular in Britain around 1815; only just arriving at the novel’s date

### Boulanger (Final Dance)

- Last piece of the ball
- Everyone arranged in a circle
- Lively and sociable

## 4. Table Manners

### Seating Arrangement

```
[Formal Dinner]

[Host]                              [Hostess]
   ↓                                   ↓
   ━━━━━━━ Dining Table ━━━━━━━

   Beside the hostess = principal guest
   Beside the host = second-ranking guest
   Bottom of the table = lowest rank or relatives

[Examples from the Novel]
- Ch.29 at Rosings: Collins sits at the bottom, the lowest seat
- Supper at the Ch.18 ball: Elizabeth and Darcy sit opposite one another—deliberate placement
```

### Serving Food (à la française)

```
[Method]
1. Place every dish on the table at once
2. The host begins carving the principal dish
3. Each diner serves themselves from nearby dishes
4. Ask a neighbor for distant items:
   “Could I trouble you for the salt?”
5. The meal lasts one to two hours
```

### Basic Manners

| Action | Manner |
|---|---|
| Napkin | On the lap, not tucked into clothing |
| Fork | **Left hand**, in the British style |
| Knife | Right hand |
| Cutting food | Cut only one bite at a time |
| Food in the mouth | **Do not speak** |
| Wine | Ritual of drinking with a neighbor: “I drink to you” |
| Leaving during the meal | Extremely rude |

### After Dessert

```
1. When the hostess rises, every woman rises
2. The women withdraw to the drawing room
3. The men remain at the table for port and political conversation
4. After 30 minutes to an hour, the men join the women for tea or coffee
```

## 5. Drawing-Room Manners

### Seating Arrangement

```
[Standard Arrangement]
- Hostess: beside the tea table and teapot
- Female guests: near the hostess
- Men: at liberty to stand or sit
- Elderly people: armchairs near the fireplace

[Activities]
- Drinking tea
- Card games
- Music at the piano
- Embroidery and sewing
- Reading, possible but socially disengaged
- Walking in the garden
```

### Pouring Tea

```
[The Hostess’s Ritual]
1. In some households, put milk into the small cup first
2. Pour tea
3. The guest adds their own sugar
4. Pass cup and saucer to the guest

[Guest Manners]
- Hold the saucer in the left hand and cup in the right
- Sip
- Make no loud noise
```

## 6. Calling Manners

### Morning Call

```
[Rules]
- A “morning call” actually takes place between 11 a.m. and 3 p.m.
- Stay only 15–30 minutes; a long visit is rude
- Receive callers in the drawing room, never a bedroom or dining room

[Procedure]
1. On arrival, present a visiting card or be announced in person
2. Be shown into the drawing room
3. The hostess enters and greets the caller
4. Brief conversation: weather, health, gossip
5. Tea is offered and accepted or declined
6. After a short time, rise, take leave, and depart

[Meaning of the Visiting Card]
- “I have called”—notification of the visit
- A folded corner means the caller came in person rather than sending a servant
- Creates an obligation to return the call within a week
```

### Application in the Novel
- Ch.3: Bingley returns Mr. Bennet’s call and stays ten minutes—formal
- Ch.55: Bingley stays an hour—**a sign of intimacy**
- Ch.55: Miss Bingley pays a brief, cold return call—**maintaining distance**

## 7. Entering and Leaving a Carriage

### Carriage Manners

```
[Order of Boarding]
1. Highest-ranking woman first
2. Other women
3. Men last, or on the box/horseback

[Seats]
- Forward seat, facing the direction of travel = seat of honor
- Back seat, facing backward = lower-ranking seat

[Alighting]
1. A man gets down first
2. He offers his hand to assist a woman
3. She takes his hand while stepping down—a rare opportunity for physical contact
```

### Meaning in the Novel

- When leaving Pemberley in Ch.43, “Mr. Darcy handed the ladies into the carriage”—**Darcy’s hand** touches Elizabeth’s or Mrs. Gardiner’s hand.
- In an age of very little physical contact, **carriage assistance is a legitimate moment of intimacy**.

## 8. Writing, Folding, and Sealing Letters

### Posture When Writing

```
[At the Desk]
1. Place the writing paper at an angle
2. Hold the quill in the right hand near the inkpot
3. Use the left hand to hold down the paper
4. One sheet takes about 20–30 minutes

[Wrong Posture = Poor Manners]
```

### Folding a Letter

```
[Traditional Method—No Envelope]
1. Write on one side, leaving the other blank
2. Fold horizontally into thirds
3. Fold vertically again
4. Write the address on the outside
5. Seal with wax and a stamp, using the family seal if available

[Example from the Novel]
- Darcy’s letter in Ch.35: “two sheets of letter paper, written quite through, in a very close hand”
- Darcy unusually uses an **envelope**: “envelope containing two sheets”
- This signals secrecy + refinement
```

### Receiving a Letter

```
[Manners]
- Do not read it instantly; pause briefly to show emotional control
- Read a private letter when alone
- If reading before family, read only briefly
- Reply promptly as a matter of courtesy
```

### Application in the Novel
- Ch.21, Miss Bingley’s letter: Jane goes upstairs to read it alone—**she senses bad news**
- Ch.46, Jane’s letter: Elizabeth reads it immediately—**an emergency**

## 9. Bedroom and Private-Space Etiquette

### Rules of Entry

```
[Who May Visit]
- Bedroom: family only (sisters and parents)
- A boudoir with a writing desk: close female friends
- Study: family + close friends

[Forbidden]
- An unmarried man and woman alone together in a room = scandal
- A member of the opposite sex in the bedroom = absolutely forbidden

[Examples from the Novel]
- Darcy + Elizabeth alone together in the drawing room (Chs.32, 34) = already a serious breach of formality
- The looks exchanged by Miss Bingley, Mrs. Hurst, and others when Darcy enters the drawing room
```

### Changing Clothes

```
[With a Lady’s Maid]
1. Unfasten the gown
2. Unlace the corset
3. Change the petticoat
4. Put on the new gown
5. Dress the hair
6. Change the shoes

[Without a Maid]
- Sisters help one another (the Bennet sisters)
- “Sarah, never mind Miss Lizzy’s hair” (Ch.55)—there is no time to spare
```

## 10. Use of the Hands and Arms

### Walking Arm in Arm

```
[Rules]
- The woman stands on the man’s right
- She rests her hand on his arm rather than gripping it
- A sign of intimacy, but still within the bounds of formality

[In the Novel]
- Ch.43, Pemberley: Mrs. Gardiner–Elizabeth → Darcy–Mrs. Gardiner → Darcy–Elizabeth
  = changes of partner = a meaningful moment
```

### Kissing the Hand—**Almost Never Done**

- Hand-kissing is rare in England; it is a Continental custom.
- If a character such as Wickham attempts it, the gesture comes across as **oily and affected**.

### Use of the Fan (Women)

```
[The Language of the Fan—Often Unconscious, but Still Meaningful]
- Opening and gently waving it: composure
- Fanning rapidly: tension/excitement
- Hiding the face behind it: embarrassment
- Closing it: a signal that the conversation is over

[A Possible Scene from the Novel]
- Miss Bingley’s fan at a ball = a device for watching Darcy
```

## 11. Manners of the Gaze

### Rules of Looking

```
[Permitted]
- Meet the other speaker’s eyes for one to three seconds at a time
- Receive the hostess’s or host’s gaze

[Forbidden]
- Staring openly, especially at a woman
- Looking someone up and down = extremely rude
- Watching what another person is eating during a meal

[Example from the Novel]
- Darcy often watches Elizabeth—Elizabeth notices in Ch.10
- “She hardly knew how to suppose that she could be an object of admiration”
- It remains unclear whether his gaze is rude or admiring
```

### Smiling

```
[Degrees of Formality]
- A slight upward turn of the lips = standard
- A smile that shows the teeth = highly intimate
- Loud laughter = rude, especially for a woman

[Elizabeth’s Distinctiveness]
- “lively, playful disposition, which delighted in anything ridiculous”
- Her frequent laughter makes her more animated than most women and constitutes a subtle breach of the social code
```

## 12. Distance (Personal Space)

### Standard Distance

```
[Ordinary Conversation]
- 1–1.5 metres (more than an arm’s length)
- Any closer = intimate

[Formal Greeting]
- 2 metres (formal distance)
- Step back, then approach to bow

[Dancing]
- Partners draw close only when joining hands
- Between figures, remain at least one step apart

[In the Novel]
- Darcy brings his chair closer to Elizabeth (Ch.32), then draws away again
- Emotional eruption → self-command
```

## 13. Frequency of Physical Contact (Low)

### Permitted Contact

| Contact | Situation |
|---|---|
| Handshake | A sign of intimacy (rare) |
| Taking an arm | While walking |
| Carriage assistance | Taking a hand while alighting |
| Dancing | Joining hands |
| Family | Sisters embracing or kissing |

### Moments of Physical Contact in the Novel (Rare)

- Darcy + Elizabeth: **the dance (Ch.18)** + **carriage assistance (Ch.43)** + **the walk during the second proposal (Ch.58)**
- There is almost no other contact, making every touch **emotionally explosive**.

## 14. Expressions of Grief and Anger (Restrained)

### Emotional Control in Polite Society

```
[Permitted]
- A small sigh
- Turning the head away
- Silence
- Leaving the room

[To Be Restrained]
- Loud weeping (only when alone)
- Shouting in anger
- Exaggerated gestures

[Breaches in the Novel]
- Mrs. Bennet’s attacks of nerves = lack of restraint
- Lydia’s excitement = breach of manners
```

### Scenes of Genuine Emotional Eruption (Restraint Breaking)

- Ch.34, Darcy: “walked with quick steps across the room”—an eruption of anger
- Ch.34, Elizabeth: after rejecting him, she “cried for half an hour”—but only when alone
- Ch.46, Elizabeth: she weeps before Darcy—the crisis destroys her self-command

## 15. Screen and Stage Direction Checklist

### Check in Every Scene

- [ ] Are the gestures of greeting appropriate to the level of formality?
- [ ] Does the seating arrangement reflect rank?
- [ ] Is the frequency of physical contact appropriate to the period?
- [ ] Are the manners of the gaze accurate?
- [ ] Is a lady’s maid present while a woman dresses or undresses?
- [ ] Is the order of boarding the carriage correct?
- [ ] Are letters folded and sealed correctly?
- [ ] Are the dance movements historically accurate?
- [ ] Does the seating at the table follow formal convention?
- [ ] Is emotional expression restrained?

### Common Errors in Modern Dramas

- ❌ Handshakes are far too frequent
- ❌ There is too much physical contact (a modern sensibility)
- ❌ Voices and laughter are too loud
- ❌ Gazes are too direct
- ❌ People stand too close together
- ❌ Changing clothes happens too quickly and without a lady’s maid

## 16. Character-Specific Body Language

### Elizabeth—Active + Slightly Transgressive

- Walks quickly
- Occasionally laughs loudly
- Uses animated gestures
- Meets others’ eyes directly, even challengingly

### Darcy—Restraint + Silence

- Moves slowly
- Allows long silences
- Holds a deep, lingering gaze
- Barely moves his hands, even while speaking

### Mrs. Bennet—Exaggeration

- Fans herself frequently
- Sighs loudly
- Gestures extravagantly
- During an attack of “nerves,” reclines in a chair and hides her face behind her fan

### Mr. Bennet—A Posture of Detachment

- Sits deep in his chair
- Rarely looks up from his book
- Sometimes turns his back on family conversation

### Lydia—Lack of Restraint

- Runs about (“flew about the house”)
- Laughs loudly
- Disregards manners

### Lady Catherine—Domination

- Holds herself rigidly upright
- Stares directly to exert pressure
- Uses commanding gestures

### Collins—Excess

- Repeats deep bows
- Rubs his hands together in flattery
- Inclines his head frequently

### Wickham—Seductive Manners

- Smiles gently
- Stands at an intimate distance, breaking convention
- Holds the gaze for a long time, seductively

## 17. Body-Language Guide to Key Scenes

### Ch.34: The Proposal

```
[Darcy’s Movement]
- Enters the drawing room, confident but tense
- Sits → rises → paces about the room
- Approaches Elizabeth “in an agitated manner”
- Speaks the proposal with heightened intensity: “dwelt on with a warmth”
- After her rejection, leans against the mantelpiece to absorb the shock
- Finally crosses the room with quick steps

[Elizabeth’s Movement]
- Remains seated in surprise
- Does not rise, maintaining her posture
- Keeps that posture even while refusing him
- Flushes with anger
- Collapses only after Darcy leaves
```

### Ch.46: News of the Elopement

```
[Elizabeth]
- Springs from her chair with the letter in her hand
- Rushes toward the door
- Encounters Darcy and stops
- Her legs give way, and she sits again
- Covers her face with a handkerchief
- Sobs

[Darcy]
- Is startled as he enters
- Approaches, then moves to summon a servant
- Attempts to comfort her
- Paces the room as he forms a private resolution
- Gives her one final look, then leaves
```

### Ch.58: The Second Proposal

```
[Both]
- Find themselves alone during a walk
- Elizabeth speaks first to express her gratitude
- Darcy stops
- They face one another
- They resume walking slowly as they talk
- They do not hold hands, maintaining decorum
- Their gazes, however, deepen
- At the end, they may naturally join hands as they walk home
```
