# *Pride and Prejudice* — Epistolary Collection

> This document catalogs **every letter, note, and written message** in the novel by sender, recipient, date, location, style, and narrative function.
> Letters are a central narrative device in Austen’s fiction, and each letter’s style reveals the sender’s character.

## Classification of Epistolary Styles

### Type 1: The Collins Letter (L-01, L-06, L-18, L-19)
```
Characteristics:
- A single sentence runs five to eight lines
- Semicolons and parenthetical clauses are nested to excess
- Contradictory vanity and humility in phrases such as “I flatter myself” and “I venture to say”
- Lady Catherine is mentioned in every paragraph
- Repeated use of “olive branch,” shifting in meaning from reconciliation to pregnancy

Translation principles:
- Never shorten the length. Length equals foolishness
- Use an excessively formal register: “I venture to pride myself...” / “Presumptuous though it may be...”
- Preserve the alternation between humility and vanity within the same letter
```

### Type 2: The Darcy Letter (L-10)
```
Characteristics:
- Highly refined complex sentences, precisely built from concessive clause → conditional clause → main clause
- Emotional restraint gradually collapses (Bingley section = cool → Wickham section = agitated)
- “Be not alarmed, madam”—the opening sentence creates tension for the reader
- Self-defense tempered by restraint: he does not blame the recipient
- Ends with “God bless you”—the letter’s sole open display of emotion

Translation principles:
- Use a distinctly epistolary register rather than conversational speech: “I trust that you will...” / “I hereby make known that...”
- Maximize the tonal change between the Bingley section (Part 1) and Wickham section (Part 2)
- Preserve the hesitation immediately before revealing pain in “I must now mention a circumstance which I would wish to forget myself”
```

### Type 3: The Miss Bingley Letter (L-04, L-05)
```
Characteristics:
- Exaggerated rhetoric of affection: “dearest friend,” “I depend on you”
- Wraps the essential act of division in gentle expressions
- “dare to entertain the hope”—pretends humility while twisting the knife
- A duality that is formal while feigning intimacy

Translation principles:
- Preserve the exaggerated intimacy: “To my dearest friend”
- Let the hypocrisy emerge upon rereading: sincere to a first-time reader, calculating to a returning reader
```

### Type 4: The Jane Letter (L-03, L-07–09, L-11–12)
```
Characteristics:
- Normally calm, restrained, and minimizing
- L-11 → L-12: a sudden shift from composure to confusion
- “I hardly know what I have written” in L-12 = an admission that she has lost control
- “I take up my pen again to do, what I have just told you I would not” = writes again after saying she would not

Translation principles:
- Maximize the tonal difference between L-11 and L-12
- Preserve L-12’s broken, intermittently resumed breath
```

### Type 5: The Gardiner Family Letter (L-13–14, L-17, L-21)
```
Mr. Gardiner (L-13–14):
- Businesslike, concise, and exact in legal and financial vocabulary
- Restrained emotion; dry even in “I hope it will give you satisfaction”

Mrs. Gardiner (L-17):
- Warmer and more conversational than her husband
- Artfully mixes humor and implication: “If he had another motive, I am sure it would never disgrace him”
- A narrative letter that unfolds the story chronologically

Translation principles:
- Mr. Gardiner: practical business register—“I hereby inform you...”
- Mrs. Gardiner: warm but cultivated conversational register—“It seems to me...”
```

### Type 6: The Lydia Letter (L-15, L-16)
```
Characteristics:
- Ignores grammar and overuses underlining/italics
- “was obliged to leave off in a violent hurry”—breaks off abruptly
- Empty of substance but long (the one sent to Kitty is “full of lines under the words”)
- L-15, the elopement letter: “Don’t be alarmed” → immediately followed by alarming news

Translation principles:
- Preserve the irresponsible letter-writing voice of a teenager
- Retain underlining and emphasis through **typographic marking** such as italics, underlining, or bold
- Use an em dash to express the abrupt break—
```
