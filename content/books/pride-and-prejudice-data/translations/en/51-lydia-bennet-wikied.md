# Lydia Bennet

## Basic Information
- **Original names:** Lydia Bennet; Mrs. Wickham after marriage
- **Korean rendering:** Lydia Bennet / Mrs. Wickham after marriage
- **Age:** 15; the youngest of five sisters, “not yet sixteen”
- **Family:** fifth daughter of the Bennet family
- **Residence:** Longbourn → Brighton → London, while in flight → Wickham’s northern regiment at Newcastle
- **First appearance:** chunk_04 (around Chap. 6)

## Character Introduction
The youngest Bennet daughter and the central figure in the crisis of the novel’s latter half. Tall and well grown, she looks more mature than her age and has been raised without restraint under her mother’s favoritism. She has no intellectual interests and cares only for the society of officers, balls, and shopping. Loud in speech and uninhibited in behavior, she flaunts herself without hesitation even before her elder sisters.

Lydia’s unruliness results from the combination of her father’s neglect, her mother’s encouragement, and the absence of guidance among the sisters. At the young age of fifteen, she is sent to Brighton with no social judgment whatsoever and yields to Wickham’s seduction without resistance. Her flight and cohabitation threaten the social ruin of the entire Bennet family, and the crisis is barely resolved through Darcy’s secret intervention. Most strikingly, Lydia shows no remorse even afterward. Instead, she parades her superiority over her sisters as “the youngest who married first.”

## Development by Event

### Act I: Unruliness (Chaps. 6–41)
- **EVT-032:** Mrs. Forster invites her to Brighton, and the trip is settled. Elizabeth warns her father of the danger, but he dismisses the warning. Lydia’s motive is simple: the officers are going to Brighton, so she will go too.

### Act II: Flight and Crisis (Chaps. 46–51)
- **EVT-038:** she runs away with Wickham to London and lives with him without marriage. In the letter she leaves for Mrs. Forster, she boasts that she will become “Mrs. Wickham,” unaware that Wickham has no intention of marrying her.
- **EVT-040:** Darcy’s secret intervention—paying Wickham’s debts, purchasing him a commission, and providing a settlement—brings about a compelled marriage. Lydia visits Longbourn and shows off as “Mrs. Wickham” without the slightest shame. She displays her wedding ring and demands precedence over her sisters. She carelessly lets slip that Darcy attended the wedding, providing the clue that reveals his secret intervention.

### Conclusion
- **EVT-049:** the couple move to Wickham’s northern regiment at Newcastle. Their marriage is unstable, and whenever they need money Lydia writes to Jane and Elizabeth. “Their love was quickly extinguished and replaced by indifference toward one another.”

## Core Relationships
- **Wickham:** she runs away with him, drawn by his charm; their relationship cools rapidly after marriage
- **Her mother, Mrs. Bennet:** the favored youngest daughter; her mother does the most to encourage Lydia’s unruliness
- **Her father, Mr. Bennet:** Lydia is the greatest consequence of his neglect; after her flight, he blames himself
- **Kitty:** her closest sister; Kitty imitates Lydia
- **Elizabeth / Jane:** she ignores her elder sisters’ attempts to stop her and asks them for money after marriage

## Translation Notes
- Lydia speaks loudly, rapidly, and lightly. Her speech is full of exclamations and almost devoid of reflection. Render colloquial exclamations such as “Lord!” and “La!” naturally.
- In her return to Longbourn after the flight, preserve the dual impression of shamelessness and the immaturity of a fifteen-year-old girl.
- When Lydia proudly calls herself “Mrs. Wickham,” reflect the gap between marriage’s social status and the reality of her marriage.
- Lydia’s letters have unstable spelling and grammar. The translation may convey this through a slightly immature written style.
