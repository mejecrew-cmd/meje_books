# *Pride and Prejudice* — Recurring Motif Tracker

> This document tracks how the same words, expressions, and images are **repeated throughout the novel while their meanings change**.
> A translator must render the same source term consistently so that readers can recognize the repetition and variation.

---

## 1. Tracking Repeated Key Words

### “pride” — One Half of the Novel’s Title

| # | Location | Speaker/Context | Usage | Referent | Shift in Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ch.3 | Narrator (local opinion) | “he was the proudest, most disagreeable man” | Darcy | Negative — arrogance |
| 2 | Ch.5 | Charlotte | “His pride does not offend me so much” | Darcy | Tolerable — “he has reason enough for it” |
| 3 | Ch.5 | Elizabeth | “I could easily forgive his pride, if he had not mortified **mine**” | Elizabeth herself | First revelation that **Elizabeth also has pride** |
| 4 | Ch.5 | Mary | “Pride relates more to our opinion of ourselves; vanity to what we would have others think” | Definition of the concepts | Announces the theme of the entire novel |
| 5 | Ch.11 | Darcy | “pride—where there is a real superiority of mind—pride will be always under good regulation” | Darcy himself | Self-defense — pride as a virtue |
| 6 | Ch.16 | Wickham | “His pride never deserts him; but with the rich he is liberal-minded” | Darcy | Complex — pride can sometimes function as a virtue |
| 7 | Ch.34 | Darcy | “Could you expect me to rejoice in the inferiority of your connections?” | Darcy | An eruption of class pride — the contradiction in his proposal |
| 8 | Ch.34 | Elizabeth | “had not your pride been hurt by my honest confession” | Elizabeth | Direct attack on Darcy’s pride |
| 9 | Ch.36 | Elizabeth | “I, who have **prided** myself on my discernment” | Elizabeth herself | **Recognition of her own pride** — the novel’s turning point |
| 10 | Ch.58 | Darcy | “I have been a selfish being all my life, in practice, though not in principle... my pride” | Darcy | Acknowledges pride as a fault |

> **Translation principle**: Translate “pride” consistently with the same equivalent for **arrogant pride**. The same word must also be used when it applies to Elizabeth in #3 and #9, or the contrast will not work.

### “prejudice” — The Other Half of the Novel’s Title

| # | Location | Speaker/Context | Usage | Referent |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ch.5 | Narrator | “a strong prejudice against everything he might say” | Elizabeth → Darcy |
| 2 | Ch.18 | Elizabeth | “And never allow yourself to be blinded by prejudice?” | A question to Darcy |
| 3 | Ch.36 | Elizabeth | “blind, partial, **prejudiced**, absurd” | **Elizabeth herself** |
| 4 | Ch.40 | Elizabeth | “the misfortune of speaking with bitterness is a most natural consequence of the **prejudices** I had been encouraging” | Elizabeth herself |
| 5 | Ch.43 | Narrator | “family **prejudice**” (Mr. Gardiner’s interpretation of Mrs. Reynolds’s praise of Darcy) | A servant’s bias toward her master |

> **Translation principle**: Translate “prejudice” consistently as **prejudice/bias**. The irony depends on Elizabeth asking Darcy in #2 whether he has been “blinded by prejudice,” only to apply the term to herself in #3.

### “tolerable” — The Word That Sets the Novel in Motion

| # | Location | Speaker | Object | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ch.3 | **Darcy** | Elizabeth | “She is **tolerable**; but not handsome enough to tempt me” — **the initiating insult** |
| 2 | Ch.5 | Narrator | Sir William Lucas’s fortune | “a **tolerable** fortune” — ordinary usage |
| 3 | Ch.7 | Jane (letter) | Her own condition | “a **tolerable** answer” — feeling “fairly well” |
| 4 | Ch.22 | Narrator | Charlotte’s feelings | “**tolerably** composed” — pretending to be calm |
| 5 | Ch.26 | Elizabeth (letter) | The Wickham affair | “with **tolerable** firmness” — only just managing firmness |

> **Translation principle**: The “tolerable” in #1 is one of the most famous single words in the novel. Every later occurrence should unconsciously recall #1 for the reader. Use a consistent equivalent such as **“passable”** or **“fairly acceptable.”**

### “disguise” — A Keyword in Darcy’s Identity

| # | Location | Speaker | Context | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ch.34 | **Darcy** | Retort after his proposal is rejected | “**Disguise** of every sort is my abhorrence” — hatred of concealment and affectation |
| 2 | Ch.35 | Darcy (letter) | Confession that he separated Bingley and Jane | “this concealment, this **disguise**, was beneath me” — regrets concealing Jane’s presence |
| 3 | Ch.36 | Elizabeth (thought) | Reassessment of Wickham | Discovers “**disguise**” in Wickham’s conduct — Wickham is the true master of disguise |

> **Translation principle**: Render “disguise” consistently as **“disguise”** or **“affectation.”** Preserve the triangular progression: Darcy’s principle in #1 → admission of his own contradiction in #2 → transfer to Wickham in #3.

### “appearance” vs. “reality” — Surface and Substance

| # | Location | Context | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ch.10 | Darcy | “Nothing is more deceitful than the **appearance** of humility” — humility’s appearance is deceptive |
| 2 | Ch.16 | Wickham | “the world is blinded by his fortune... sees him only as he chooses to be seen” — control of appearances |
| 3 | Ch.36 | Elizabeth | “His countenance, voice, and manner, had established him at once in the possession of every virtue” — Wickham’s appearance functions as **proof** of virtue |
| 4 | Ch.40 | Elizabeth | “One has got all the goodness, and the other all the **appearance** of it” — **the key line** |

> **Translation principle**: #4 summarizes the entire novel in one sentence: “One has all the real goodness, and the other only its **appearance**.” Render “appearance” consistently with a word meaning **outward appearance**.

### “fine eyes” — The Repeated Sign of Darcy’s Attraction

| # | Location | Speaker | Context |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ch.6 | **Darcy → Miss Bingley** | “Miss Elizabeth Bennet” — **the first time he says her name** |
| 2 | Ch.8 | **Miss Bingley → Darcy** | “this adventure has rather affected your admiration of her **fine eyes**” — an attempt to check him |
| 3 | Ch.8 | **Darcy** | “Not at all; they were brightened by the exercise” — brushes aside the check |
| 4 | Ch.10 | **Miss Bingley** | “do let the portraits of your uncle and aunt Philips... what painter could do justice to those beautiful **eyes**?” — mockery |
| 5 | Ch.10 | **Darcy** | “their colour and shape, and the eyelashes, so remarkably fine, might be copied” — answers the mockery in earnest |
| 6 | Ch.18 | Narrator | After the Wickham affair, Miss Bingley offers “witticisms on **fine eyes**” — after the ball |

> **Translation principle**: Translate “fine eyes” consistently as **“beautiful eyes.”** The phrase is both Miss Bingley’s recurring weapon of attack and the repeated sign of Darcy’s attraction.

### “last man in the world” → Reversal

| # | Location | Speaker | Original | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ch.34 | Elizabeth | “**the last man in the world** whom I could ever be prevailed on to marry” | Absolute rejection |
| 2 | Ch.50 | Elizabeth (thought) | “proposals which she had proudly spurned only four months ago would now have been gladly and gratefully received” | **Complete reversal** |

> **Translation principle**: #1 must be memorable for the reversal in #2 to work: “Of all the men in the world, you are the very last whose proposal I could accept.”

## 2. Recurring Images and Situational Patterns

### Walking — An Expression of Freedom and Will

| Location | Walker(s) | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Ch.7 | Elizabeth (a three-mile walk) | Defies convention, shows independence, and sparks Darcy’s attraction |
| Ch.33 | Elizabeth (walking at Rosings) | Three “accidental” encounters with Darcy — she seeks freedom but cannot escape him |
| Ch.36 | Elizabeth (a two-hour walk) | Physical expression of recognizing her prejudice — she thinks as she walks |
| Ch.43 | Elizabeth (the grounds at Pemberley) | Reunion with Darcy — walking becomes the setting for repairing the relationship |
| Ch.58 | Elizabeth + Darcy (a Longbourn lane) | Second proposal — walking becomes the setting for union |

> **Translation principle**: The repetition of “walking” is deliberate. Use one consistent equivalent for each occurrence, while distinguishing the three-mile journey as “went on foot.”

### Letter — A Medium for Truth and Falsehood

| Letter | Truthfulness | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| Mr. Collins’s letters | Hypocritical sincerity | Expose the sender’s foolishness |
| Miss Bingley’s letters | Artful falsehood | Carry out the separation |
| Darcy’s letter | **Painful truth** | The turning point of the entire novel |
| Jane’s letters (the elopement) | Distraught truth | Convey the crisis |
| Mrs. Gardiner’s letter | Revealed truth | Exposes secret mediation |

### Ball/Dance — A Testing Ground for Relationships

| Ball | Key Event | Change in Relationship |
|---|---|---|
| Meryton assembly (Ch.3) | The “tolerable” insult | Antipathy begins |
| Lucas Lodge party (Ch.6) | Elizabeth refuses to dance | Paradoxical attraction |
| Netherfield ball (Ch.18) | Dance + confrontation + humiliation | Every conflict erupts |
| (No later balls) | — | The relationship moves into private spaces: letters and walks |

### Window/Prospect — Observation and Discovery

| Location | Observer | What Is Seen | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ch.3 | The Bennet sisters | Bingley from an upstairs window (blue coat, black horse) | The limits of first impressions — only the exterior is visible |
| Ch.28 | Maria Lucas | Miss de Bourgh’s phaeton from the window | Awe before authority |
| Ch.32 | Mr. Collins | Watches carriages from the window of the room facing the road | A surveillance system built on flattery |
| Ch.43 | Elizabeth | The view of the grounds from Pemberley’s windows | “every window there were beauties” — discovers Darcy’s taste |

## 3. Required Consistency Table for Translation (Final)

| Repeated Source Term | Standard Rendering | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| pride | pride / arrogance | The novel’s title; applies to both Darcy and Elizabeth |
| prejudice | prejudice | The novel’s title; applies chiefly to Elizabeth |
| vanity | vanity | Must remain distinct from pride (Mary’s definition in Ch.5) |
| tolerable | passable / fairly acceptable | The pivotal word in Ch.3 |
| disguise | disguise / affectation | Darcy’s principle + Wickham’s true nature |
| appearance | outward appearance | Contrasted with reality/substance |
| fine eyes | beautiful eyes | The recurring sign of Darcy’s attraction |
| sensible | sensible / discerning | Not “sensitive” |
| condescension | gracious lowering of rank | Not “conceit” |
| civil | civil / polite | Minimum courtesy, contrasted with “amiable” |
| amiable | amiable / lovable | The highest praise |
| consequence | social standing / importance | Not merely “result” |
| establishment | marriage / setting up a household | Not “facility” |
| connexion | connections / relations | Not a physical “connection” |
| fortune | wealth / dowry | Not “luck” |
| living | clerical benefice | Not everyday “life” in a clerical context |
| wanting | lacking | Not “desiring” |
| nervous | weak-nerved / afflicted in the nerves | Not merely “tense” |
| address | manner / bearing / courtship | Distinguish by context |
| design | intention / plan | Not graphic “design” |
