# Mr. Bennet

## Basic Information
- **Original name:** Mr. Bennet
- **Korean rendering:** Mr. Bennet
- **Age:** middle-aged, estimated to be in his early fifties
- **Income:** £2,000 a year; the estate is subject to an entail
- **Family:** landed gentry; owns the Longbourn estate, which the entail destines for the male heir, Collins
- **Residence:** Longbourn, Hertfordshire
- **First appearance:** chunk_01 (Chap. 1)

## Character Introduction
Head of the Bennet family and the figure who opens the novel’s first scene. He possesses a keen intellect and sardonic humor, but applies them only to observation and ridicule rather than to solving his family’s problems. As a young man, attracted by beauty, he married a woman who was intellectually ill suited to him. After losing respect for his wife early in the marriage, he chose a life withdrawn into his library. Though the father of five daughters, he is a neglectful head of household who takes no active part in their education or conduct.

He favors Elizabeth as the only family member capable of intellectual exchange, yet dismisses even her plea to prevent Lydia from going to Brighton as though it were a nuisance. Lydia’s flight is the direct consequence of Mr. Bennet’s neglect, and only then does he acknowledge his failure. Even this repentance does not last. Austen remarks that the force of such a dreadful lesson fades with time. He is one of the characters she judges most coldly.

## Development by Event

### Act I: The Sardonic Observer (Chaps. 1–2)
- **EVT-002:** when his wife badgers him to visit Bingley, he teases her by pretending he has not already done so. The scene completely reveals the Bennet marriage: tormenting his wife is his only amusement.

### Act II: The Consequences of Neglect (Chaps. 18–41)
- **EVT-018:** during Collins’s proposal to Elizabeth, Mr. Bennet takes Elizabeth’s side and ends the conflict with a joke: whichever parent she refuses, one of them will never speak to her again. It is his typical pattern of escaping conflict with his wife through humor.
- **EVT-032:** Elizabeth warns him of the danger of Lydia’s trip to Brighton, but he dismisses her, saying that if Lydia makes a fool of herself they will have the pleasure of laughing at her. **This decision directly causes Lydia’s flight.**

### Act III: Repentance and Its Limits (Chaps. 46–50)
- **EVT-047:** after hearing of Lydia’s flight, he goes to London in search of her but fails. On returning home, he admits, “It has been my own doing. Lizzy, you were right.” Yet the repentance is not permanent: it might have been better had he borne shame and misery to the end, but after three days his customary humor returns.

## Core Relationships
- **Elizabeth:** his favored second daughter and the only family member capable of intellectual conversation with him
- **Mrs. Bennet:** an impulsive youthful marriage → loss of respect → an object of ridicule; the failed marriage spreads dysfunction throughout the household
- **Lydia:** the greatest victim of his neglect; the absence of education and control leads directly to her flight
- **Collins:** heir under the entail; Mr. Bennet despises him but ultimately makes no alternative provision for the estate
- **Jane / Bingley:** gives his consent to Bingley’s proposal, approving him as a good young man well suited to Jane

## Translation Notes
- Almost all of Mr. Bennet’s speech is built from irony and satire. Its surface meaning often differs from its actual intent, so preserve the double nuance.
- The challenge is to retain the dry wit of lines such as “You may as well call it impertinence at once.”
- “Entail” is a contemporary legal institution; a brief translator’s note may be needed.
- When Austen’s narrator describes Mr. Bennet, preserve the dual tone of sympathy and criticism.
