# Pride and Prejudice — Adaptation and Screenwriting Guide

> ⚠️ **This document supports creative adaptation.** It identifies what may be changed when adapting the work across media, periods, and viewpoints—and what must never be altered.

---

## 1. Elements That Must Never Change: the Essence of the Novel

### Core Framework—Changing It Destroys the Work's Identity

| Element | Reason |
|---|---|
| **First proposal → rejection → letter → self-recognition** sequence | The novel's structural spine |
| **Darcy's two-proposal structure** | Transformation is the central theme |
| **Wickham's lie → exposure** | The mechanism of prejudice |
| **Elizabeth's self-recognition** (“I never knew myself”) | The thematic climax |
| **Pemberley visit → reversal of Elizabeth's view of Darcy** | Character discovered through space |
| **Lydia's elopement → Darcy's secret intervention** | Love proven through action |
| **Double-marriage ending** | The genre promise |

### Elements That Require Care

| Element | Caution |
|---|---|
| **Class consciousness** | Modernization can erase motivation; adapt it to the chosen period |
| **The economic urgency of marriage** | Risk of reducing the story to a simple romance through a modern lens |
| **Social constraints on women** | Weakening them also weakens the conflict |
| **Collins's absurdity** | Risk of reducing him to a mere comic character |

## 2. Adaptation Guide by Medium

### A. Film (90–150 minutes)

#### Compression Strategy
- **Volume I (Ch. 1–23)** = 30–40 minutes, one-third of the whole
- **Volume II (Ch. 24–42)** = 30–40 minutes
- **Volume III (Ch. 43–61)** = 30–50 minutes

#### Keep as Priorities
- Meryton assembly (EVT-003)
- First proposal (EVT-028)
- The letter (EVT-029)
- Walk of self-recognition (EVT-030)
- Pemberley (EVT-035, EVT-036)
- Elopement (EVT-038)
- Second proposal (EVT-046)

#### May Be Compressed or Omitted
- The details of Collins's proposal (EVT-017)—keep it brief
- Some social scenes, including parts of Ch. 5–6
- Some Gardiner appearances
- Some scenes involving Mary and Kitty

#### Visual Emphasis
- First view of Pemberley (Ch. 43)
- The enclosed space of the Hunsford proposal
- The splendor of the balls

### B. Miniseries (5–6 hours)

#### The 1995 BBC Model: Six Episodes, About Five Hours
- **Almost every chapter can be included**
- Free indirect discourse can be rendered in voice-over
- Darcy's unseen actions can be added on screen, including the London search

#### Add or Expand as Priorities
- EVT-039-001–005, Darcy's secret intervention—show it on screen
- EVT-019-001, the negotiation to separate Bingley and Jane—show it on screen
- Details of the characters' daily routines

### C. Stage Play (2 hours 30 minutes–3 hours)

#### Compression and Symbolization
- **Revolving stage or minimalist set**
- Adjust the cast size; minor roles may be combined
- Compress events through a chorus or narration

#### Essential Scenes
- Meryton assembly as the opening
- Hunsford proposal as the Act I climax
- Pemberley at the start of Act II
- The elopement crisis
- Second proposal as the finale

### D. Musical

#### Scenes Suited to Songs
- “It is a truth universally acknowledged” as the opening ensemble
- Mary's song as comedy
- The ball as an ensemble dance
- “I never knew myself” as Elizabeth's solo
- “In vain have I struggled” as Darcy's solo
- “All love must be vain” as Elizabeth's solo
- Second proposal as a duet
- Wedding as the finale

### E. Webtoon or Comic

#### Paneling Strategy
- First impression = large panel
- Dialogue = sequence of small panels
- Proposal = full-page close-ups of facial expressions

#### Visual Motifs
- Darcy's gaze, repeated
- The letter as a designed physical object
- Pemberley landscape in wide panels

## 3. Adaptation by Period

### A. Period-Faithful Adaptation: 1811
- Use every setting sheet as written
- Reproduce costume, architecture, and manners accurately
- **Barriers for modern audiences:** the class system, the urgency of marriage, and forms of address

### B. Move to the Victorian Era: 1850s–1860s
- Costume: crinolines and greater formality
- Marriage law: different conditions, before the Married Women's Property Act 1882
- Darcy: could be an earl or higher
- Wickham: sent to a colonial army

### C. Move to the Edwardian Era: 1900s
- Costume: hobble skirts; motorcars appear
- Ball → country-house party
- Darcy: lord of the estate in its final generation

### D. 1920s–1930s
- Modernism
- Costume: flapper versus tradition
- Motorcars and telephones appear
- Marriage matters less, requiring a different conflict

### E. Contemporary Korean Adaptation: Example

#### Conversion Matrix
| Original | Contemporary Korean Version |
|---|---|
| Bennet family | Middle-class self-employed household with five daughters |
| Entail | Another form of financial crisis, such as business failure |
| Darcy | Heir to a conglomerate or a wealthy Gangnam family |
| Bingley | Darcy's close friend |
| Wickham | Charming fraudster |
| Collins | Religion versus contemporary Korea—lawyer, professor, or insurance agent? |
| Lady Catherine | Darcy's mother or elder maternal aunt |
| Ball | Club party, wedding, or corporate party |
| Military | A workplace or another attractive outside group |

#### Essentials to Preserve
- The trap of first impressions
- Revelation through a letter or message
- The effect of family on love
- The moment of self-recognition
- The two-confession structure

#### Risks of Modernization
- **Weaker urgency around marriage** → needs another urgent stake: career, family, visa, and so on
- **Weaker class difference** → needs another divide: education, hometown, or family background
- **Changed means of communication** → KakaoTalk or another messenger replaces the letter

### F. Science-Fiction Adaptation
- Families on space colonies
- Darcy = heir to a great fleet
- Wickham = space pirate
- Entail = inheritance of the family's planetary fief

### G. Fantasy Adaptation
- Magical families
- Darcy = member of a powerful magical house
- Marriage = union of hereditary magic
- Ball = magical rite

## 4. Adaptation by Viewpoint

### A. Darcy's Viewpoint: Retelling
- **Advantage:** exposes Darcy's entire inner life and shows the secret intervention directly
- **Risk:** destroys the mystery and weakens Elizabeth's process of discovery
- **Precedent:** works such as *Mr. Darcy's Diary*

### B. Wickham's Viewpoint
- Antihero narrative or self-justification
- Wickham's motives in the Georgiana affair and Lydia's elopement
- **Risk:** he can too easily become a charming narrator; moral balance is required

### C. Charlotte's Viewpoint
- The realist's view of marriage
- Details of daily life at Hunsford
- Married life with Collins

### D. Mary's Viewpoint
- The viewpoint of the “invisible third daughter”
- Observer of her sisters
- Escape into music and books

### E. Georgiana's Viewpoint
- Suitable for a light novel
- Recovery from trauma and the process of accepting Elizabeth as a sister

### F. Servant's Viewpoint: Mrs. Reynolds and Others
- *Downton Abbey* style
- Observe “what happens upstairs” from below

### G. Bingley's Viewpoint
- The viewpoint of an indecisive friendship
- His dependence on Darcy
- A year in which he cannot forget Jane

## 5. Adaptation by Genre

### A. Heightened Comedy
- Expand the parts of Collins, Mrs. Bennet, and Lady Catherine
- Emphasize Wickham's ridiculous side
- Emphasize Mr. Bennet's wit

### B. Heightened Drama or Melodrama
- Detail Darcy and Elizabeth's emotions
- The pain of Jane and Bingley's year apart
- Lydia's tragic dimension

### C. Heightened Mystery
- Reveal Wickham's identity more slowly
- Turn Darcy's secret intervention into a detective plot
- Use the timing of the letter to create suspense

### D. Horror or Gothic: Experimental
- Make Pemberley frightening
- Make Wickham a true villain
- Make Lady Catherine threatening

### E. Action or Thriller
- Turn the search for Wickham into an action sequence
- Add a duel, which never occurs in the novel but Mrs. Bennet fears
- Emphasize the escape plot

## 6. Adaptation Options for Key Scenes

### Ch. 34: First Proposal

#### Traditional Version
- A dark drawing room; the two are alone
- Darcy's explosive confession
- Elizabeth's anger
- A fierce confrontation

#### Modernization Option 1: Workplace
- Confession in a corporate office
- Conflict of the same intensity

#### Modernization Option 2: KakaoTalk
- Long message → negative reply
- The phone screen supplies the visuals

#### Screen Version
- Mostly close-ups
- Alternating eyelines between the two
- No music

### Ch. 43: First View of Pemberley

#### Traditional Version
- First reveal of the main house from the carriage
- Music swells

#### Modernization
- First visit to a Gangnam penthouse
- Or arrival at a country villa

#### Screen Version
- Wide shot → close-up
- Darcy's portrait, or photograph

### Ch. 46: News of the Elopement

#### Traditional Version
- Two letters and Darcy's arrival
- Elizabeth collapses

#### Modernization
- A phone call with Darcy beside her by chance
- Exposure on social media

## 7. Changes That Must Be Avoided

### Risks in Character Transformation
- ❌ **Make Elizabeth like Darcy from the beginning**—destroys the theme of prejudice
- ❌ **Make Darcy kind from the beginning**—destroys the theme of transformation
- ❌ **Make Wickham sympathetic**—moral ambiguity obscures the theme
- ❌ **Make Collins serious**—weakens the satire
- ❌ **Make Lydia solely a tragic victim**—weakens the theme of irresponsibility

### Risks in Structural Transformation
- ❌ **Darcy proposes once and is accepted**—negates the work's core
- ❌ **Self-recognition without the letter**—removes the most important device
- ❌ **Omit the Pemberley visit**—removes the device of character discovery
- ❌ **Omit Lydia's crisis**—removes the proof of Darcy's transformation

### Risks in Tonal Transformation
- ❌ **Too dark**—loses Austen's wit
- ❌ **Too light**—loses the social criticism
- ❌ **Too passionate**—loses the restrained emotion

## 8. Guide to Tone by Medium

### Screen-Time Allocation for a 120-Minute Film

| Section | Chapters | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Introduction | Ch. 1–3 | 10–12 minutes |
| Inciting movement | Ch. 4–17 | 25–30 minutes |
| Development | Ch. 18–34 | 30–35 minutes |
| **First proposal** | Ch. 34 | 5–7 minutes, with dedicated weight |
| Crisis | Ch. 35–46 | 25–30 minutes |
| Climax and resolution | Ch. 47–58 | 15–20 minutes |
| Conclusion | Ch. 59–61 | 5–10 minutes |

### Stage-Act Allocation: Three Acts
- **Act I:** Ch. 1–19, through Collins's proposal
- **Act II:** Ch. 20–46, through the news of the elopement
- **Act III:** Ch. 47–61

## 9. Checklist for Moving the Story to a New Era

```
[ ] Is there still a compelling reason marriage is urgent?
[ ] Does the difference in class or status still matter?
[ ] Is there a system in which first impressions can be wrong?
[ ] Does secret information still have value? (letter or message)
[ ] Can family still be a burden?
[ ] Can appearance and reality still differ?
[ ] Is a moment of self-recognition possible?
[ ] Can love be proven through action?
```

If all eight answers are YES, a strong adaptation is possible.

---

## 10. Core Messages of the Work: Unchangeable

1. **First impressions are often wrong.** Pride and prejudice exist on both sides.
2. **Marriage is both a social and economic act and an act of love.** The two come into conflict.
3. **Self-recognition is the most difficult realization.** “Till this moment, I never knew myself.”
4. **Love is proven by action, not appearance.** Darcy's secret intervention demonstrates this.
5. **Family does not determine whom one loves, but it influences the decision.**
6. **Time reveals appearance and reality.** “One has all the goodness, the other all the appearance of it.”

Any adaptation that preserves these six messages remains *Pride and Prejudice*.
