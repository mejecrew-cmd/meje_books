# Mrs. Bennet

## Basic Information
- **Original name**: Mrs. Bennet
- **Korean rendering**: Mrs. Bennet
- **Maiden name**: Gardiner; daughter of a Meryton attorney
- **Age**: Middle-aged
- **Family**: Mr. Bennet’s wife; her brother Mr. Gardiner is a London merchant
- **Residence**: Longbourn
- **First appearance**: chunk_01 (Chapter 1)

## Character Profile
Appearing immediately after the novel’s opening sentence, she supplies much of its comic energy. The “business of her life” is to marry off her daughters, and in pursuit of that goal she charges ahead without regard for either dignity or strategy. She is intellectually limited and nervously excitable, and tries to control her family by wielding her “poor nerves” as a weapon. In reality, these complaints are merely a device for attracting her husband’s attention, as Mr. Bennet understands perfectly.

Mrs. Bennet is not merely a ridiculous figure. Beneath all her conduct lies the fear that the entail may leave her daughters homeless after their father’s death. In light of this existential fear, her obsession with their marriages is also a rational survival strategy within the social structure of the period. Yet her methods are so blatant and indiscriminate that they paradoxically obstruct the very marriages she pursues. Her public predictions and boasting at the Netherfield ball directly contribute to Darcy’s decision to separate Bingley and Jane.

## Development by Event

### Act I: The Marriage Project (Chapters 1–12)
- **EVT-001** She reacts immediately to the news that a wealthy single man has taken Netherfield Park and presses her husband to visit him. She is certain that Bingley will marry one of her five daughters.
- **EVT-006** She deliberately sends Jane to Netherfield on horseback rather than by carriage, hoping the rain will give her a cold and prolong her stay. The scheme succeeds, but Elizabeth condemns it.
- **EVT-008** During a visit to Netherfield, she openly predicts a Bingley–Jane marriage and seriously damages the family’s dignity in Darcy’s presence. **This conduct contributes to Darcy’s motive for separating them.**

### Act II: The Collins Commotion (Chapters 18–23)
- **EVT-016** At the Netherfield ball, she loudly broadcasts the expected Bingley–Jane marriage. Darcy listens closely and watches with grave attention.
- **EVT-040** She falls into hysterics at the news of Lydia’s elopement, refusing to leave her room, wailing, and fearing Wickham will be killed in a duel if captured. The moment she hears of the marriage, however, she becomes joyful and immerses herself in preparing the trousseau.

## Core Relationships
- **Mr. Bennet**: An unequal marriage defined by their intellectual disparity; she fails to recognize her husband’s mockery
- **Jane**: The daughter of whom she is proudest and the principal object of investment in her marriage project
- **Elizabeth**: A daughter she does not favor; “Lizzy is not a bit better than the others, and I am sure she is not half so handsome as Jane, nor half so good-humoured as Lydia”
- **Lydia**: Her favorite youngest child; she permits and even encourages Lydia’s reckless behavior
- **Collins**: Initially hostile to him as heir under the entail, then delighted by the prospect of his marrying Elizabeth
- **Bingley**: Her first choice for a son-in-law, valued chiefly as the possessor of four or five thousand pounds a year

## Translation Notes
- Mrs. Bennet’s speech is marked by exclamations, repetition, and exaggeration. Reproduce patterns such as “Oh! my dear Mr. Bennet,” “I am sure,” and “How can you be so tiresome” consistently.
- “Nerves” is not merely ordinary nervousness, but a period term associated with female nervous disorder or hysteria; choose among “nerves,” “nervous complaint,” and related expressions according to context.
- The narrator’s tone toward Mrs. Bennet is cool observation rather than sympathy: “a woman of mean understanding, little information, and uncertain temper.”
- Mrs. Bennet repeatedly rages against the entail. Understanding the historical injustice of the institution is essential to reproducing the nuance accurately.
