# *Pride and Prejudice* — Forms of Address and Tone Translation Guide

> This document tracks **who speaks to whom, using which form of address and tone**, and **how those choices change with events**.
> It also catalogues period metaphors, sarcasm, biblical and literary allusions, and other expressions **that are easily mistranslated without cultural context**.

---

## Part 1: System of forms of address

### 1-1. Basic hierarchy

| Form | Addressee | Formality | Translation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Your Ladyship** | Lady Catherine and other titled women | Highest — third-person deference | “Your Ladyship” |
| **Your Lordship** | Earls and other male peers | Highest | “Your Lordship” / “my lord” |
| **Lady + first name** | A titled woman | High | “Lady Catherine” |
| **Sir + first name** | A knight or baronet | High | “Sir William” |
| **Mr. / Mrs.** | Untitled gentry | Middle — standard | “Mr.” / “Mrs.” |
| **Miss + surname** | Unmarried woman (eldest daughter) | Middle | “Miss Bennet” (= Jane) |
| **Miss + first name** | Unmarried woman (younger daughter) | Middle | “Miss Elizabeth” |
| **First name only** | Intimate relationship | Low — informal | “Lizzy” / “Caroline” |

### 1-2. “Miss Bennet” vs “Miss Elizabeth Bennet” — A crucial distinction

| Form | Referent | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Miss Bennet** | Jane (eldest daughter) | Only the family's **eldest daughter** is “Miss + surname” |
| **Miss Elizabeth Bennet** | Elizabeth (second daughter) | Younger daughters must be distinguished by **first name** |
| **Miss Eliza Bennet** | Elizabeth | Used chiefly by Miss Bingley — carries **subtle condescension** |
| **Miss de Bourgh** | Anne de Bourgh | As an only daughter, “Miss + surname” is sufficient |
| **Miss Darcy** | Georgiana | With her brother unmarried, she is called “Miss Darcy” |

> **Translation point:** Determine from context whether “Miss Bennet” refers to Jane or Elizabeth. When Darcy addresses Elizabeth directly as “Miss Bennet,” he is not **giving her the status of the eldest daughter**; “Miss Bennet” is a natural direct address to his interlocutor. In letters, “Miss Elizabeth Bennet” distinguishes her precisely.

## Part 2: Character-specific address patterns and event-driven change

### 2-1. Darcy → Elizabeth: A gradation of address

| Point | Address | Context | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chap. 3 (EVT-003) | “she” (third person, to Bingley) | “She is tolerable” | **Objectification** — not worth naming |
| Chap. 6 (EVT-004) | “Miss Elizabeth Bennet” (to Miss Bingley) | “Miss Elizabeth Bennet” | He names her for the first time — **interest begins** |
| Chap. 10–11 | “Miss Bennet” (direct address) | During the Netherfield stay | Standard formality — distance remains |
| Chap. 34 (EVT-028) | “Miss Bennet” → “you” | Proposal scene | Formality collapses during the proposal and “you” becomes frequent |
| Chap. 35 (letter) | “Miss Elizabeth” / “you” | Letter body | Epistolary formality mixed with emotional “you” |
| Chap. 43 (EVT-036) | “Miss Bennet” (gentle tone) | Reunion at Pemberley | Same address, but a **completely different tone** |
| Chap. 58 (EVT-046) | “you” → “Elizabeth” (inferred) | After the second proposal | Shifts to first-name address after engagement |

### 2-2. Elizabeth → Darcy: Change in address

| Point | Address | Context | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Chap. 3–18 | “Mr. Darcy” (formal + cold) | Entire first section | Standard formality with a cold tone |
| Chap. 18 (EVT-016) | “Mr. Darcy” (provocative) | During their dance | Same address, **sarcastic tone** |
| Chap. 34 (EVT-028) | “Mr. Darcy” (angry) | Rejection | “You are mistaken, Mr. Darcy” — anger wrapped in formality |
| Chap. 43 (EVT-036) | “Mr. Darcy” (flustered) | Reunion at Pemberley | Same address, now carrying **shyness and confusion** |
| Chap. 58 (EVT-046) | “Mr. Darcy” → transition | Acceptance of second proposal | Gradually softens as she expresses gratitude |

### 2-3. Miss Bingley's strategy of address — Subtle aggression

| Target | Address | Nuance |
|---|---|---|
| Elizabeth (surface friendliness) | “Miss Eliza Bennet” / “Eliza” / “Miss Eliza” | **“Eliza” is not merely a diminutive but a subtle demotion.** Elizabeth's family uses “Lizzy,” while formal acquaintances use “Elizabeth.” “Eliza” sits between them, feigning intimacy while belittling her. |
| Elizabeth (behind her back) | “Miss Elizabeth Bennet” / “she” | Uses the full formal name to create distance while criticizing her |
| Darcy (flattery) | “Mr. Darcy” | Always maintains formality and displays respect |
| Jane (surface friendship) | “dear Jane” / “dearest friend” | Exaggerated affection in letters — the language of hypocrisy |

### 2-4. Address within the family

| Speaker → target | Address | Nuance |
|---|---|---|
| Mrs Bennet → husband | “Mr. Bennet” / “my dear Mr. Bennet” | Always formal. “my dear” is habitual rhetoric, not sincere affection |
| Mr Bennet → wife | “Mrs. Bennet” / “my dear” | Ironic distancing. “my dear” carries irony |
| Mrs Bennet → daughters | “Lizzy” / “Jane” / “Lydia, my love” | Only Lydia gets “my love” — linguistic evidence of favouritism |
| Mr Bennet → Elizabeth | “Lizzy” / “my little Lizzy” / “child” | Affectionate diminutives for his favourite daughter |
| Jane ↔ Elizabeth | “dear Lizzy” / “dearest Jane” | Sincere forms of affection, contrasting with Miss Bingley's hypocritical “dearest” |

### 2-5. Lady Catherine's power through address

| Target | Address | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Collins | “Mr. Collins” | Standard, but with a **downward tone** — she treats the clergyman as a subordinate |
| Elizabeth | “Miss Bennet” | Formal, but used **like an interrogation** |
| Herself (third person) | “Lady Catherine” / “her Ladyship” | Used by Collins and naturally accepted by her |
| Darcy | “Darcy” / “nephew” | An aunt's familiarity plus authority |
| Charlotte | “Mrs. Collins” | Standard, but she treats her only as an object of orders and interference |

## Part 3: Speech types — Class and character embedded in style

### 3-1. Character-specific speech traits

#### Mr. Darcy — Formal + concise + declarative

```
Early traits:
- Short, definite sentences with few modifiers.
- “I certainly shall not.” (Chap. 3)
- “She is tolerable.” (Chap. 3)
- “Not at all.” (EVT-007, responding to Miss Bingley's attack on the “fine eyes”)
→ Translation: Polite but brief and dry; restrained formality that reveals no emotion.

Proposal scene (EVT-028):
- His sentences suddenly grow long and emotion erupts. “In vain have I struggled.”
- Yet the register remains formal. “You must allow me to tell you...”
→ Translation: Preserve formality, but lengthen the sentences and let the emotional tremor show.

Letter (EVT-029):
- His most elevated register: complex sentences with nested concessive and conditional clauses.
- “Be not alarmed, madam, on receiving this letter...”
→ Translation: A clearly epistolary style, distinct from speech, at the level of “I beg that you...”

Later section (after EVT-036):
- Still formal, but now softened. “Will you do me the honour...”
→ Translation: Maintain politeness while warming the tone. The same formal question now carries sincerity.
```

#### Elizabeth Bennet — Wit + irony + observation

```
Core traits:
- Conversational wit that controls the exchange. She twists rather than attacks directly.
- “I could easily forgive his pride, if he had not mortified mine.” (Chap. 5)
- “There is a stubbornness about me that never can bear to be frightened.” (Chap. 31)
→ Translation: Intelligent, lively speech. Polite where socially required, but never stiff.

Anger scene (EVT-028):
- Wit disappears and turns into a direct attack.
- “You could not have made me the offer of your hand in any possible way
   that would have tempted me to accept it.”
→ Translation: Formal, cold, and decisive. The absence of wit is itself the signal of anger.

After self-recognition (EVT-030 onward):
- Self-deprecation and reflection enter her tone.
- “How despicably have I acted!”
→ Translation: Self-criticism still borrows the form of wit: “How contemptibly I have behaved.”

Later section (after EVT-046):
- Her wit returns, now mixed with tenderness.
- “You know enough of my frankness to believe me capable of that.” (Chap. 58)
→ Translation: Early aggressive wit has matured into self-mocking humour.
```

#### Mrs. Bennet — Exaggeration + repetition + illogic

```
Core traits:
- Overuses exclamations (Oh! / Lord! / Good Lord!)
- Repeats the same point in different forms
- Changes subject without logical connection
- “Oh, my dear Mr. Bennet... you have no compassion on my poor nerves.”
→ Translation: Preserve exclamations without overloading them.
   Keep the logical leaps between sentences; do not polish them smooth.

Excitement scene (EVT-040, news of Lydia's marriage):
- “My dear, dear Lydia! She will be married! I shall see her again!
   She will be married at sixteen!”
→ Translation: A volley of short exclamations. Zero shame, pure excitement. Preserve the scene's unpleasantness.
```

#### Mr. Bennet — Cynicism + paradox + a single thrust

```
Core traits:
- Pretends to agree with his wife while conveying the opposite
- Compresses sharp satire into one sentence
- “You mistake me, my dear. I have a high respect for your nerves.
   They are my old friends.” (Chap. 1)
- “An unhappy alternative is before you, Elizabeth. Your mother will
   never see you again if you do not marry Mr. Collins, and I will
   never see you again if you do.” (EVT-018)
→ Translation: Keep the blade of the irony: a concealed dagger beneath surface courtesy.
```

#### Mr. Collins — Verbosity + flattery + self-contradiction

```
Core traits:
- Abnormally long sentences overloaded with semicolons, dashes, and parenthetical clauses
- Speaks of humility while revealing vanity
- Honorific language explodes whenever Lady Catherine is mentioned
- “I flatter myself that my present overtures of good-will are highly
   commendable...” (Chap. 13 letter)
- In his proposal, he lists three reasons for marrying; the third is “Lady Catherine's recommendation”
→ Translation: Never shorten the verbosity. Its length embodies his foolishness.
   Excessively formal phrases such as “I venture to flatter myself” are appropriate.
```

#### Lady Catherine — Commands + interrogation + assertion

```
Core traits:
- Her questions are interrogations. She expects answers but permits no contradiction.
- Presents her opinions as facts
- “Why did not you all learn? You ought all to have learned.” (Chap. 29)
- “I will not be interrupted!” (Chap. 56)
→ Translation: Not crude informality, but overwhelming authority within elevated formality.
   Use commanding constructions such as “Surely you ought...” and “You shall...”

Special tone in EVT-045 (Longbourn visit):
- Threat and contempt contained within formality
- “The shades of Pemberley to be thus polluted!”
→ Translation: “That Pemberley's dignity should be defiled in this fashion!”
   Wrap the height of fury in aristocratic formality.
```

## Part 4: Sarcasm, irony, and metaphor

### 4-1. Ironies running through the novel

#### Opening sentence
> “It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune must be in want of a wife.”

- **Surface:** It is a universal truth that a wealthy bachelor must want a wife.
- **Actual meaning:** When a wealthy bachelor arrives, **mothers with daughters** want him as a son-in-law. The grammatical subject has been reversed.
- **Translation point:** Preserve the inflated formality of “a truth universally acknowledged.” Explaining it away destroys the irony.

#### “tolerable” (Chap. 3)
> “She is tolerable; but not handsome enough to tempt me.”

- **tolerable:** “bearable” / “passable” — not praise but **the lowest degree of indifference**.
- **Translation options:** “She is passable” / “She will do” / “She is tolerable” — never “rather pretty,” which erases the deliberate slight.
- **to tempt me:** Presupposes arrogantly that he is a prize worth tempting.

#### “the last man in the world” (EVT-028, Chap. 34)
> “you were the last man in the world whom I could ever be prevailed on to marry.”

- **Superlative refusal:** Of every man in the world, he is the very last she would consent to marry.
- **Narrative irony:** The declaration is completely reversed later in the novel.
- **Translation:** Preserve the absolute tone of “the last man in the world.”

#### “Till this moment, I never knew myself” (EVT-030, Chap. 36)
> “How despicably have I acted! I, who have prided myself on my discernment!”

- **Dismantling of the self:** The moment Elizabeth realizes that the “Prejudice” of the title was her own.
- **Double irony:** “I, who have prided myself on my discernment” joins pride and prejudice in one sentence.
- **Translation:** The nuance of “I did not know myself” is crucial: not merely failed self-knowledge, but recognition of self-deception.

### 4-2. Character-specific sarcasm and satire

#### Mr. Bennet's barbs

| Original | Target | Interpretation | Translation key |
|---|---|---|---|
| “I have a high respect for your nerves. They are my old friends.” (Ch. 1) | Wife | Wraps twenty years of fatigue with her nerves in “respect” | Preserve the blade of irony |
| “For what do we live, but to make sport for our neighbours, and laugh at them in our turn?” (Ch. 57) | The world | A cynical philosophy of life's purpose | The line condenses his character |
| “I admire the activity of your benevolence” (Ch. 7, to Mary) | Mary | Mocks Mary's pedantic moralizing | A barb like “How energetically benevolent you are” |
| “Next to being married, a girl likes to be crossed in love a little now and then” (Ch. 24) | Jane (indirectly) | Makes a joke of his daughter's heartbreak | Cruel wit at the boundary of affection and cynicism |
| “Wickham's a fool if he takes her with a farthing less than ten thousand pounds” (Ch. 49) | Wickham/Lydia | Bitter self-mockery about the worst possible son-in-law | Anger transformed into humour |

#### Elizabeth's sarcasm

| Original | Target | Interpretation | Translation key |
|---|---|---|---|
| “I could easily forgive his pride, if he had not mortified mine.” (Ch. 5) | Darcy | She could forgive his pride had he not wounded hers | “pride” operates twice |
| “Mr. Darcy is all politeness.” (Ch. 6) | Darcy | Surface: how polite. Actual: sarcasm | Irony conveyed almost entirely by tone |
| “I wonder who first discovered the efficacy of poetry in driving away love!” (Ch. 9) | Darcy | Counters Darcy's claim that poetry feeds love | Keep the witty lightness |
| “One has got all the goodness, and the other all the appearance of it.” (Ch. 40) | Darcy/Wickham | Reassesses them after reading the letter | Condenses the novel's theme into one sentence |
| “What are men to rocks and mountains?” (Ch. 27) | Men in general | Declares her escape into nature after disappointment | Exaggerated enthusiasm as reaction to disappointment |

#### Miss Bingley's venom

| Original | Target | Interpretation | Translation key |
|---|---|---|---|
| “She really looked almost wild.” (Ch. 8) | Elizabeth | Contempt for her mud-spattered walk | “almost savage” — condemnation of a breach of upper-class codes |
| “an abominable sort of conceited independence” (Ch. 8) | Elizabeth | Defines walking alone as contemptible, conceited independence | Key phrase of class contempt |
| “I am afraid, Mr. Darcy, that this adventure has rather affected your admiration of her fine eyes.” (Ch. 8) | Darcy/Elizabeth | Sarcastically checks Darcy's interest | “fine eyes” becomes a repeated weapon |
| “your mother-in-law... always at Pemberley with you” (Ch. 6) | Darcy | Threatens him with Mrs Bennet as mother-in-law | Mockery that backfires on Darcy |

#### Lady Catherine's insolence

| Original | Target | Interpretation | Translation key |
|---|---|---|---|
| “You give your opinion very decidedly for so young a person.” (Ch. 29) | Elizabeth | Someone of your age and station is remarkably opinionated | A command disguised as observation |
| “The shades of Pemberley to be thus polluted!” (Ch. 56) | Elizabeth | That Pemberley's grounds should be defiled so | “polluted” means desecration, not mere contamination |
| “Are the shades of Pemberley to be thus polluted?” (Ch. 56) | Elizabeth | A rhetorical question requiring no answer | The height of aristocratic fury |
| “I was told... that Miss Elizabeth Bennet would, in all likelihood, be soon afterwards united to my nephew.” (Ch. 56) | Elizabeth | Begins as hearsay but aims to interrogate | Pretends to convey information while applying pressure |

### 4-3. Biblical/literary allusions and period metaphors

| Expression | Source/background | Use in the novel | Translation treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| **“olive branch”** | Genesis 8:11 (Noah's dove) | A “gesture of reconciliation” in Collins's letter (Ch. 13), and “offspring/pregnancy” in another Collins letter (Ch. 57, Psalm 128) | **Same expression, two meanings** — translate by context as “offer of peace” or “children/offspring” |
| **“Fordyce's Sermons”** | James Fordyce's *Sermons to Young Women* (1766) | Collins attempts to read it instead of a novel; Lydia interrupts after three pages | Byword for conservative female instruction; a note is needed |
| **“St. James's”** | St James's Palace, London, centre of royal society | Sir Lucas boasts of his knighthood and court attendance | Translate as “St James's Palace” and explain its court-society meaning |
| **“Cheapside”** | Commercial district in the City of London | The Bingley sisters mock Uncle Gardiner's residence | Not merely a place name but **a code of class contempt**; explain in a note |
| **“natural”** (multiple senses) | Eighteenth-century English | “natural beauty” = natural beauty / “natural daughter” = illegitimate daughter / “nothing could be more natural” = entirely understandable | Translate differently by context. Pemberley's “natural beauty” is naturalistic aesthetics rejecting artifice |
| **“coming out”** | Debut in society | Lady Catherine criticizes all five daughters being “out” | Entirely different from the modern sense: translate as “entering society” |
| **“sensibility” vs “sense”** | Eighteenth-century debate over sensibility | Jane's “great strength of feeling, a composure of temper” | sense = reason/judgment; sensibility = emotional sensitivity/feeling. Also the title of another Austen novel |
| **“picturesque”** | Eighteenth-century aesthetic movement | Elizabeth sarcastically calls Miss Bingley's party a “charming group... The picturesque would be spoilt” (Ch. 10) | Ironically applies landscape aesthetics to human relations |
| **“the appearance of humility”** (Ch. 10) | Darcy's maxim | “Nothing is more deceitful than the appearance of humility.” | The appearance of humility is most deceptive; accurately diagnoses Bingley's self-deprecation |

### 4-4. Recurring motifs — Meaning shifts in the same word

| Word | Early meaning | Turning point | Later meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| **pride** | Darcy's flaw (Elizabeth's viewpoint) | EVT-030 (recognition) | Also Elizabeth's flaw: “I, who have prided myself...” |
| **prejudice** | Hardly used directly | EVT-030 | “blind, partial, prejudiced, absurd” — Elizabeth applies it to herself |
| **tolerable** | Darcy's slight against Elizabeth (Ch. 3) | — | Also used normally of Jane's “tolerable” health (Ch. 7); readers remember the first use |
| **disguise** | “disguise of every sort is my abhorrence” (Darcy, Ch. 34) | EVT-029 letter | Darcy's honesty is both virtue and defect, contrasted with Wickham's “disguise” |
| **appearance** | Wickham's “appearance of goodness” | EVT-030 | “One has got all the goodness, and the other all the appearance of it” — appearance separated from reality |
| **impropriety** | Darcy criticizes the Bennets' “want of propriety” | EVT-029 letter | Elizabeth accepts the justice of the criticism — painful assent |

## Part 5: Translation consistency checklist

### 5-1. Terms that must remain consistent

| Original | Standard translation | Caution |
|---|---|---|
| Mr. Darcy | Mr Darcy | Do not vary the spelling of Darcy |
| Pemberley | Pemberley | Do not mix variants |
| Netherfield | Netherfield | |
| Entail | entail / strict settlement | Do not alternate loosely with “inheritance law” |
| Settlement | marriage settlement | Do not use “compensation” or senses from unrelated contexts |
| Living | clerical living / benefice | Never translate as ordinary “life” or “livelihood” in a clerical context |
| Express | express messenger / express letter | Avoid modern parcel-delivery meanings |
| Accomplishment | accomplishment / cultivated skill | In context, a qualification of an “accomplished woman” |

### 5-2. Consistency of address

| Situation | Rule |
|---|---|
| Miss Bennet = Jane | Strictly observe the eldest-daughter rule |
| Miss Elizabeth Bennet = Elizabeth | Include the first name for younger daughters |
| Miss Bingley's “Eliza” | Keep “Eliza” to preserve the subtle demotion of the deliberate familiar form |
| Family “Lizzy” | Keep “Lizzy” as a mark of intimacy |
| Darcy's “Miss Bennet” | Direct address to his interlocutor; confirm from context that it does not refer to Jane |

### 5-3. Tone turning points — The same address in a different tone

| Character | Address | Early tone | Turning point | Later tone |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Elizabeth → “Mr. Darcy” | Cold + sarcastic | EVT-030 (recognition) | Flustered + respectful + shy |
| Darcy → “Miss Bennet” | Restrained + distant | EVT-036 (Pemberley) | Gentle + considerate |
| Mrs Bennet → “Mr. Darcy” | “the most disagreeable man in the world” | EVT-047 (engagement news) | “my charming son-in-law!” |
| Mrs Bennet → “Mr. Wickham” | Indifference | EVT-040 (marriage news) | “dear Wickham!” |
