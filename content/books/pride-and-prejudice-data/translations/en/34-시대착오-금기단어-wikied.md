# Pride and Prejudice — Dictionary of Anachronistic and Prohibited Terms

> Modern vocabulary that must **never be used in Korean translation**, together with acceptable alternatives.
> These words instantly destroy the credibility of a period drama.
> For a work set in the 1810s, use only Korean or Sino-Korean expressions that existed before 1810.

---

## 1. Absolute Taboos: Loanwords and Translated Coinages

### A. Direct English Imports and Loanwords (Absolutely Prohibited)

| Prohibited Term | Period of Adoption | Recommended Alternative |
|---|---|---|

| **cool** | 1990s | “composed” / “detached” |

| **love** as an English loanword | Loanword | Native Korean “love” |
| **date** | 20th century | “meeting” / “rendezvous” / “appointment to walk” |
| **café** | 20th century in Korea | “tea house”—almost absent from the novel; “tea gathering” is closer |

| **sofa** | 20th century in Korea | “long chair” / “easy chair” |
| **veranda** | Loanword | “balcony” is also risky; avoid “terrace” as well |

| Term | Risk | Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| **feminist** | A late-19th-century term | “one who argues for women's rights,” though no character in the novel is a feminist in the modern sense |
| **independent woman** | Slightly modern | “self-reliant woman” / “woman who decides for herself” |
| **free will** | Acceptable | Preserve as is |

### B. Recommended Forms of Address by Period

```text
[Use of Sino-Korean Titles in an 1810s Korean Register]

Men:
- -ssi (氏): standard, e.g. “Mr. Darcy”
- -gong (公): highly formal
- -gyeong (卿): title of nobility
- seonsaeng (先生): a learned person

Women:
- bu-in (夫人): married woman
- yang (孃): unmarried woman
- -ssi (氏): neutral
- manim: a high-ranking married woman
- yeongbu-in (令夫人): noble lady
- assi: unmarried woman of a genteel family

Family:
- honoured father / honoured mother: respectful
- father / mother: ordinary
- elder brother / elder sister: respectful sibling forms
- older sister: intimate between sisters
- older brother used by a sister: somewhat modern and therefore risky
```

### C. **Risky Forms of Address** (Anachronistic)

| Form | Risk |
|---|---|
| **oppa** | Possible in ordinary speech, but slightly modern in formal contexts; use an older respectful brother-form |
| **eonni** | Possible between sisters; if it sounds too modern, use the respectful elder-sibling form |
| **jagiya** (“honey”) | Absolutely prohibited |
| **yeobo** | Possible between spouses; if it sounds too modern, use “you” |
| **dangsin** | Possible between spouses or in formal address |

## 4. Vocabulary for Period Atmosphere

### A. Dignified Negative Expressions

```text
[Modern]                         [Recommended for Period Drama]
annoyed                     →   “troubled” / “disturbed at heart” / “displeased”

offended                    →   “hurt at heart” / “wounded in pride”
dislike                     →   “be reluctant” / “dislike” / “be troubled by”
```

## 5. Character-Specific Vocabulary in Korean

### A. Darcy: Restraint and Formality

```text
[Prohibited]
- Friendly colloquial speech
- Loanwords other than necessary English proper forms
- Frequent exclamations

[Recommended]
- Formal declarative endings
- Rich but controlled Sino-Korean vocabulary
- Short, precise sentences
- Restraint in metaphor and poetic expression

Translation example:
Original: “She is tolerable; but not handsome enough to tempt me.”
Translation sense: “She is passable. Yet she is not beautiful enough to tempt me.”
(Not: “She's okay, but I'm not really into her.”)
```

### B. Elizabeth: Lively Formality

```text
[Prohibited]
- Vulgar expressions
- Excessive formality

[Recommended]
- Soft formal endings
- Witty figures of speech
- A touch of satire

Translation example:
Original: “I could easily forgive his pride, if he had not mortified mine.”
Translation sense: “I could easily forgive his pride—had he not wounded my own.”
(Not: “I could let the arrogance slide, but him messing with my pride is so annoying.”)
```

### C. Collins: Excessive Formality

```text
[Recommended]
- Excessively deferential endings
- Excessive Sino-Korean diction, such as “presumptuous though I may be” and “I venture to pride myself”
- Long and verbose sentences

Translation example:
Original: “Allow me, by the way, to observe...”
Translation sense: “If I may be so bold as humbly to observe...”
```

### D. Mrs. Bennet: Excited Everyday Speech

```text
[Recommended]
- Frequent exclamations: “Good heavens!”, “My word!”, “Oh!”
- Sequences of short sentences
- Repetition and exaggeration

Translation example:
Original: “Oh, my dear Mr. Bennet!”
Translation sense: “Oh, my dear Mr. Bennet!” in an excited Korean register
```

### E. Lydia: Vulgar, but within the Period

```text
[Recommended]
- Lively everyday speech in an 1810s Korean register
- Exclamations equivalent to “Oh!” and “Indeed!”
- Never use modern coinages

Example:
Original: “I'm not afraid; for though I'm the youngest, I'm the tallest.”
Translation sense: “I am not afraid! I may be the youngest, but I am the tallest!”
(Not modern slang equivalent to: “I'm not shook—I'm the tallest, so I'm the best.”)
```

### F. Wickham: Charming Formality

```text
[Recommended]
- Smooth formal speech
- Sweet metaphors
- A slight excess of polish

Translation example:
Original: “His behaviour to myself has been scandalous.”
Translation sense: “His conduct toward me has been nothing short of disgraceful.”
```

### G. Lady Catherine: Imperious Formality

```text
[Recommended]
- Imperative endings equivalent to “you shall” and “you must”
- Questions that function as interrogations
- Sino-Korean and authoritative vocabulary

Translation example:
Original: “You give your opinion very decidedly for so young a person.”
Translation sense: “For one so young, you deliver your opinions very decisively.”
(Formal sarcasm)
```

## 6. Expressions Frequently Mistranslated

### A. The Trap in Translating “you”

```text
English “you” maps to many Korean forms of address.

[Determine Formality and Intimacy]
English second person → Korean title or form of address

Husband → wife: formal “you” / spousal address
Lover → lover: “you” / “thou”
Friend → friend: formal “thou” / name
Servant → master: “sir” / “madam,” without a second-person pronoun
Master → servant: familiar “you” / name
Noble → commoner: “thou” / “you”
Commoner → noble: “Your Ladyship” / “Your Honour”

[Application in the Novel]
Darcy → Elizabeth, early: formal “you”
Darcy → Elizabeth, late: “you,” with deepened feeling
Elizabeth → Darcy: “you” / “thou” according to register
```

### B. Handling Tense

```text
[English Simple Past → Korean]

“He bowed.”
→ “He bowed.” (plain)
→ “He made a bow.” (elevated)
→ “He offered a formal bow.” (formal and character-specific)

[Compound Tense]
“She had read the letter.”
→ “She had already finished reading the letter.”
→ “It was the case that she had already read the letter.”
```

### C. Degrees of Politeness

```text
English “Would you...?” → Korean levels

Highly formal: “Would you be so kind as to...?”
Standard formal: “Would you please...?”
Everyday formal: “If you would...”
Intimate: “Will you...?”

[Application in the Novel]
- Darcy–Elizabeth, formal: “Would you be so kind as to...?”
- Collins, excessive: “I most earnestly and humbly beseech you to...”
- Mrs. Bennet, everyday: “Please do...” / colloquially formal equivalent
```

## 7. Review Checklist

### Reread after Completing the Translation

- [ ] Are there any **loanwords or modern coinages** such as stress, cool, date, or café?
- [ ] Are there any **modern Korean slang terms** such as sseom, mildang, or inssa?
- [ ] Are there any **modern social concepts** such as companies, commuting, or vacations?
- [ ] Is each **character's voice** consistent?
- [ ] Does the **degree of formality** suit each character?
- [ ] Are the **forms of address** appropriate to the period?
- [ ] Is the **Sino-Korean vocabulary** appropriate—not too much or too little?
- [ ] Is the transliteration of **unavoidable loanwords** consistent?

## 8. Conclusion

### Golden Rules for Translating Period Drama into Korean

1. **Use only Korean or Sino-Korean vocabulary that existed before the period**
2. **Maintain differentiated vocabulary for each character**
3. **Keep the degree of formality consistent**
4. **Prohibit all modern loanwords and coinages**
5. **Prohibit modern social concepts**

### Self-Censorship Question

> “Would this expression sound natural to a person in 1810s Joseon or Britain?”

If the answer is “awkward,” choose another expression.
