# Pride and Prejudice — Complete List of Indefinite References

> This document tracks **entities referred to only by pronouns, demonstratives, or idiomatic expressions rather than proper names** in the novel.
> It catalogues expressions whose referents cannot be identified without context—such as “this gentleman,” “that lady,” “his friend,” and “a certain person”—together with their **actual referents, locations by chapter or section, and classifications**.
>
> Use it to decide whether a pronoun should be made explicit or preserved in translation.

## D. Blanks (----): Austen's Deliberate Anonymization

Austen **deliberately replaces certain proper names with blanks (----)**. This contemporary novelistic convention creates an impression that the story concerns real people and places.

| # | Original | Referent | Translation Guidance |
|---|---|---|---|
| 50 | "the ----shire" | The county of the militia regiment | Preserve the blank as “----shire”; do not specify a county |
| 51 | "General ----'s regiment" | The general's name | Preserve as “General ----'s regiment” |
| 52 | "---- Street" | Wickham and Lydia's hiding-place address | Preserve as “---- Street” |
| 53 | "Lord ----" | Colonel Fitzwilliam's father, an earl | Preserve as “Lord ----” |

**Translation principle:** Do not fill in or conjecture the blanks. The blanks are Austen's stylistic choice and part of a game with the reader—the convention of pretending that the story is true.

## E. “it” / “this” / “that”: Key Pronoun Referents

The most frequent source of confusion in translation is determining **what “it” refers to**.

| # | Original | Referent of “it” | Location | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 54 | "It is a truth universally acknowledged" | The entire proposition that follows | Ch.1 | Anticipatory “it”; the extraposed subject is “that a single man...” |
| 55 | "if he had not mortified mine" | mine = my pride | Ch.5 | “My pride” contrasted with “his pride”; Elizabeth has pride too |
| 56 | "I have not the smallest hope. It is every way horrible!" | Lydia's entire situation | Ch.46 | “it” means the possibility that Lydia will not be found and will not marry |
| 57 | "It will not do." | The attempt to suppress his feelings | Ch.34 | The first sentence of Darcy's proposal; “it” is his effort to repress his feelings |
| 58 | "This was a lucky recollection — it saved her from something like regret" | The thought that the Gardiners could not have been invited | Ch.43 | “it” is Elizabeth's realization that marrying Darcy would have separated her from her aunt and uncle |
| 59 | "It taught me to hope" | The report of Lady Catherine's visit | Ch.58 | “it” is Elizabeth's refusal to promise her aunt that she would reject an engagement |
| 60 | "If you will thank me, let it be for yourself alone" | The object of gratitude | Ch.58 | “it” is gratitude for rescuing Lydia; Darcy admits that he acted “for you alone,” not for her family |
