# *Pride and Prejudice* — Scene and Set Layouts

> For each key scene, this document extracts and organizes the **spatial arrangement, visual information, lighting, weather, character positions, sound, and sensory details** from the novel.
> It ensures accuracy in translated scene descriptions and provides a basis for set construction in adaptations.

---

## SCENE 01: Longbourn Drawing Room—News of Bingley (Ch.1)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Presumably the drawing room. Only Mr. and Mrs. Bennet are present. |
| **Time** | Daytime—“one day” |
| **Atmosphere** | Ordinary marital conversation. Mr. Bennet presumably has a book or newspaper; Mrs. Bennet, her needlework. |
| **Movement** | Mrs. Bennet talks almost entirely on her own while Mr. Bennet scarcely moves. Finally, “he left the room, fatigued with the raptures of his wife.” |
| **Key Visual** | The contrast between Mr. Bennet’s blank expression and Mrs. Bennet’s excitement |

## SCENE 02: Meryton Assembly Room—The Ball (Ch.3)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Public assembly room: dancing on one side, conversation and spectators on the other |
| **Time** | Evening into night; candlelight |
| **People** | The whole neighborhood—dozens of people. Bingley’s party of five enters: Mr. Bingley, two sisters, the elder sister’s husband, and another young man. |
| **Sound** | Band music, dancing feet, chatter |
| **Key Arrangement** | Darcy paces in a corner and dances with no one. Elizabeth sits without a partner for two dances. Bingley comes over to encourage Darcy; Elizabeth is **close enough to overhear** their conversation. |
| **Key Visual** | Darcy’s isolation vs. Bingley’s animation. Elizabeth’s expression at “She is tolerable”: insulted, but able to turn it into amusement |
| **Costume** | Bingley’s sisters: “fine women, with an air of decided fashion.” Darcy: “fine, tall person, handsome features, noble mien” |

## SCENE 03: Morning at Netherfield—Elizabeth Arrives (Chs.7–8)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Netherfield breakfast parlour; everyone is at breakfast |
| **Time** | Morning |
| **Weather** | Heavy rain the previous night; muddy fields in the morning |
| **Elizabeth’s Condition** | “weary ancles, dirty stockings, and a face glowing with the warmth of exercise”; six inches of mud on her petticoat; disordered hair |
| **Reactions** | Miss Bingley + Mrs. Hurst: contempt, later calling her “almost wild” / Bingley: approval, “remarkably well” / Darcy: “divided between admiration and doubt” / Hurst: thinks only of breakfast |
| **Key Visual** | Muddy Elizabeth’s glowing face vs. the cold looks of the immaculate Bingley sisters |

## SCENE 04: Evening at Netherfield—The Accomplishment Debate + The Reel (Chs.8, 10)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Netherfield drawing room: fireplace, piano, card tables, sofas |
| **Lighting** | Candlelight, after dinner |
| **Arrangement—Ch.8** | Mr. and Mrs. Hurst, Miss Bingley, and Bingley at the loo table. Elizabeth declines and reads, then moves beside the card table to observe, leading to the debate with Darcy about accomplishments. |
| **Arrangement—Ch.10** | Darcy writes a letter while Miss Bingley interrupts. Bingley and Hurst play piquet. Elizabeth sews. Miss Bingley plays the piano, then a Scotch air, prompting Darcy’s proposal of a reel. |
| **Sound** | Piano music, the scratch of a pen, Miss Bingley’s endless flattery |
| **Key Visual** | The moments when Miss Bingley notices Darcy’s gaze repeatedly returning to Elizabeth |

## SCENE 05: Evening at the Philipses’—Wickham’s Account (Ch.16)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | The Philipses’ drawing room. Two card tables: whist for Collins and the adults; lottery tickets for the young people |
| **Time** | Evening; candlelight |
| **Sensory Detail** | The smell of Uncle Philips’s port; coffee and muffins |
| **Key Arrangement** | Wickham sits beside Elizabeth. Lydia is absorbed in the lottery and cannot monopolize him, allowing Wickham and Elizabeth a long conversation. |
| **Atmosphere** | Warm food from the kitchen + sounds of cards + Wickham’s low voice |
| **Key Visual** | Wickham’s handsome face; the other officers beside him look like “broad-faced stuffy uncle Philips” |

## SCENE 06: The Netherfield Ball (Ch.18)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Netherfield ballroom, with a separate supper room |
| **Time** | Evening to late night; white soup, supper, cards; the Bennets remain until last |
| **Lighting** | Extensive, brilliant candlelight |
| **People** | Many officers—a “cluster of red coats”—neighborhood families, and Bingley’s party. Wickham is **absent**. |
| **Key Scene Arrangements** | (1) Collins and Elizabeth’s first two dances, “dances of mortification,” with Collins moving the wrong way; (2) Darcy and Elizabeth dance and converse from facing lines, followed by the “hauteur” moment and Sir William’s interruption; (3) at supper, Elizabeth and Darcy sit **opposite** each other, and Darcy hears Mrs. Bennet’s prophecy; (4) everyone listens as Mary sings at the piano; (5) while waiting to leave, Miss Bingley and Mrs. Hurst are exhausted, while Bingley and Jane stand apart talking. |
| **Key Visual** | The most crowded and splendid scene, containing both Darcy’s solitude and Elizabeth’s shame |

## SCENE 07: Longbourn Breakfast Room—Collins Proposes (Ch.19)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Breakfast room, immediately after breakfast |
| **Movement** | Collins asks Mrs. Bennet for a “private audience” → Mrs. Bennet and Kitty leave → Elizabeth remains alone → Collins delivers his long speech → Elizabeth tries to escape → Collins follows |
| **Elizabeth’s Action** | She hides her feelings through “incessant employment,” pretending to concentrate on her needlework. |
| **Key Visual** | Mrs. Bennet **loitering outside the door to eavesdrop**: she “dawdled about in the vestibule to watch.” |

## SCENE 08: Longbourn Library—“Your Mother... I...” (Ch.20)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Library, Mr. Bennet’s private domain, surrounded by books |
| **Arrangement** | Mr. Bennet looks up from his book at his wife with “calm unconcern.” |
| **Position of the Key Speech** | Elizabeth is summoned and stands in the library between her parents to hear the verdict. |
| **Atmosphere** | A space ruled by Mr. Bennet’s cynical humor; only here is he entirely himself |

## SCENE 09: Grand Dinner at Rosings—First Meeting with Lady Catherine (Ch.29)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Rosings dining room → drawing room. The entrance hall has “fine proportion and finished ornaments.” |
| **Scale** | “all the servants, and all the articles of plate”; an enormous dinner |
| **Crossing the Park** | About half a mile on foot from Hunsford: “every park has its beauty” |
| **Arrangement** | Lady Catherine occupies the center. Collins sits at the foot of the table. Elizabeth sits between Charlotte and Miss de Bourgh, who says nothing. |
| **Visuals** | Lady Catherine: “tall, large woman, strongly-marked features.” Miss de Bourgh: “pale and sickly... insignificant.” Mrs. Jenkinson sets a screen before Miss de Bourgh to shield her. |
| **Sound** | Lady Catherine’s ceaseless monologue + Collins’s agreement + Sir William’s awed silence |

## SCENE 10: Hunsford Parsonage—The First Proposal (Ch.34)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Parsonage parlour. Elizabeth is alone; the rest of the household has gone to Rosings. |
| **Time** | Evening; one candle or otherwise minimal lighting |
| **Weather** | Not stated; Elizabeth is low enough in strength to stay home with a headache |
| **Darcy’s Movement** | The door opens and he enters alone → asks after her health → sits, rises, and “walked about the room” → approaches Elizabeth “in an agitated manner” → proposes → leans against the mantelpiece to hear her rejection → “walked with quick steps” across the room → exits |
| **Elizabeth** | Remains seated and does not rise → “grew more angry every moment” after refusing him → after Darcy leaves, “sat down and cried for half an hour” |
| **Key Visual** | In the dark room, firelight catches Darcy’s face, “pale with anger,” against Elizabeth’s flushed cheeks. |
| **Sound** | Only their voices, Darcy’s footsteps, the shutting door, and the opening of the front door |

## SCENE 11: Morning in Rosings Park—The Letter (Ch.35)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Walk beside the park paling → gate |
| **Time** | Early morning, spring |
| **Weather** | “pleasantness of the morning”—a clear spring morning |
| **Movement** | Elizabeth looks into the park from the gate → a gentleman approaches from the grove → Darcy holds out a letter → Elizabeth takes it “instinctively” → Darcy gives a slight greeting and disappears into the grove |
| **Key Visual** | “a look of haughty composure”—restrained hurt in Darcy’s face; the hand offering the letter; his back disappearing among the leaves |
| **Afterward** | Elizabeth begins reading as she walks: “two sheets of letter paper, written quite through, in a very close hand” |

## SCENE 12: First View of Pemberley (Ch.43)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Approach** | The carriage enters through the lodge and passes through “a beautiful wood stretching over a wide extent,” climbing half a mile. |
| **First View** | From the hilltop, **Pemberley House** across the valley: “a large, handsome stone building, standing well on rising ground, and backed by a ridge of high woody hills; and in front a stream of some natural importance was swelled into greater, but without any artificial appearance” |
| **Key Visual** | Landscaping that maximizes natural beauty: “neither formal nor falsely adorned,” the opposite of Rosings’s ostentatious splendor |
| **Interior** | Dining parlour: “large, well-proportioned.” Furnishings: “neither gaudy nor uselessly fine—with less of splendour, and more real elegance, than the furniture of Rosings” |
| **Gallery** | Large portraits. Elizabeth stands long before Darcy’s full-size portrait: “such a smile over the face, as she remembered to have sometimes seen, when he looked at her” |
| **Garden Reunion** | Walking across the lawn toward the river, “the owner of it himself suddenly came forward from the road which led behind it to the stables.” They meet at twenty yards and both blush deeply. |

## SCENE 13: Lambton Inn—News of the Elopement (Ch.46)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Inn sitting room. Elizabeth reads alone. |
| **Time** | Morning; her aunt and uncle are out walking |
| **Movement** | Elizabeth reads and springs up → runs to the door → the door opens and **Darcy appears** → startled by her pale face and haste → she sits and weeps → Darcy paces, frowning → comforts her gently → leaves after “one serious parting look” |
| **Key Visual** | Her trembling hand clutching the letter; Darcy’s pacing shadow; “I have not an instant to lose” |
| **Sound** | Elizabeth’s sobs, Darcy’s cautious steps, the closing door |

## SCENE 14: Longbourn Wilderness—Confrontation with Lady Catherine (Ch.56)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | The Longbourn garden’s artificial wilderness + decorative hermitage |
| **Approach** | Lady Catherine arrives in a chaise-and-four → Mrs. Bennet receives her → Lady Catherine draws Elizabeth into the garden |
| **Time** | Morning |
| **Arrangement** | Gravel walk → the two confront each other alone in a copse. The carriage waits at the entrance, with the maid inside. |
| **Key Visual** | Lady Catherine’s imposing size vs. Elizabeth’s resolute face; leaf shadows between them |
| **Sound** | Footsteps on gravel, Lady Catherine’s commanding voice, birdsong in ironic peace |

## SCENE 15: Longbourn Walk—The Second Proposal (Ch.58)

| Item | Evidence from the Text |
|---|---|
| **Space** | Country path near Longbourn, toward Lucas Lodge |
| **Time** | Morning, autumn |
| **Movement** | Five set out—Bingley, Jane, Elizabeth, Kitty, Darcy → Bingley and Jane fall behind → Kitty turns off at Lucas Lodge → **Elizabeth and Darcy are alone** |
| **Landscape** | Autumn country road, leaves, fields: “They walked on without knowing in what direction” |
| **Key Visual** | Their profiles as they walk side by side. Elizabeth cannot see his expression—“she could not look, she could listen”—so emotion is carried only by voice. |
| **Atmosphere** | The opposite of the first proposal’s dark, tense interior: bright, open outdoors and a free choice |
