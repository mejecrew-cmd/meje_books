# *Pride and Prejudice* — Narrator Guide

> Austen's narrator is **not a transparent camera, but a personality with opinions**.
> The narrator observes, judges, and mocks the characters, and sometimes blends her own voice with a character's thoughts.
> This document tracks the narrator's **identity, techniques, shifts in viewpoint, and uses of free indirect discourse**.
> It is the most difficult area of translation and the one in which translators most often fail.

---

## 1. The narrator's identity

### Who is this narrator?

- **Third-person omniscient narration** — but **selectively omniscient**: knowing everything while showing **only part of it**
- **Gender unspecified** — but rich in feminine sensibility and knowledge of society (not to be equated with Austen herself)
- **An insider to the period** — naturally understands the rules of Regency society
- **An ironist** — outwardly neutral but actually mocking in the portrayal of characters
- **A moral judge** — not wholly neutral. The narrator's **values** appear in whom she portrays favourably and whom negatively

### What the narrator does not do

- Does not **address the reader directly** (exception: the final “Be that as it may” in Ch. 46, the sole metacomment)
- Does not **announce the future directly** (plants foreshadowing, but never says “as will later be revealed”)
- Does not **openly condemn characters** (uses a mocking tone and lets readers judge for themselves)

## 2. The narrator's three modes

### Mode A: Omniscient Narration

The narrator describes **from outside the characters**, objectively, without entering their thoughts.

```
Example: “Mr. Bennet was so odd a mixture of quick parts, sarcastic humour,
reserve, and caprice, that the experience of three-and-twenty years had
been insufficient to make his wife understand his character.” (Ch. 1)

Traits:
- The observations and judgments clearly belong to the narrator
- “quick parts” and “sarcastic humour” are the narrator's assessments
- “insufficient” carries the narrator's humour

Translation principles:
- Preserve the narrator's tone in a natural third-person narrative style
- “Mr Bennet was such an odd mixture...” — the tone of an observing narrator
```

### Mode B: Limited Point of View

The narrator adheres to **one character's viewpoint**, conveying only what that character sees and feels.

```
Example: “Elizabeth was surprised, but said not a word.” (Ch. 32)

Traits:
- Accesses Elizabeth's interiority, but not Darcy's
- The reader must **interpret** Darcy's actions alongside Elizabeth
- Most of the novel follows Elizabeth's viewpoint

Exceptional viewpoint shifts:
- Ch. 6: Darcy's viewpoint (“He began to wish to know more of her”)
- Ch. 12: Darcy's viewpoint (“Elizabeth had been at Netherfield long enough”)
- Ch. 22: Charlotte's viewpoint (“Charlotte herself was tolerably composed”)
- Ch. 35 letter: Darcy's first-person epistolary voice
- End of Ch. 44: the Gardiners' viewpoint (“it was evident that he was very much in love”)

Translation principles:
- Recognize the exact moment of a viewpoint shift and move into that character's tone
- In Elizabeth's viewpoint, describe only Darcy's behaviour; render motives as conjecture
```

### Mode C: Free Indirect Discourse (FID) — The most difficult

A character's **first-person thought appears without quotation marks** inside the narrator's third-person prose.

```
Example: “How despicably have I acted! I, who have prided myself on my
discernment!” (Ch. 36)

Analysis:
- “How despicably have I acted!” is Elizabeth's interior monologue
- It has no quotation marks and connects naturally to the surrounding third-person narration
- “she grew absolutely ashamed of herself” (narrator) →
  “Of neither Darcy nor Wickham could she think...” (narrator + Elizabeth) →
  “How despicably have I acted!” (Elizabeth's direct thought)

Translation principles:
- Do not add quotation marks. The blurred boundary is central to Austen's technique
- Do not overinsert “she thought”
- Let narration and inner monologue flow naturally into one another
```

## 3. Complete list of free indirect discourse locations

### Identification criteria

1. **Exclamations/questions** appear within third-person narration without quotation marks
2. A character's tone continues after **“she felt” / “she thought”**
3. The boundary between narrator and character is **ambiguous**
4. **Present tense** intrudes into past-tense narration

### Major FID locations

| Chapter | Focal character | Opening text | Type | Difficulty |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ch. 1 | Mrs Bennet | “The business of her life was to get her daughters married” | Narratorial summary reflecting Mrs Bennet's own self-conception | Medium |
| Ch. 6 | **Darcy** | “Mr. Darcy had at first scarcely allowed her to be pretty” | Darcy's interiority in his own tone; “allowed” means permission from himself | **High** |
| Ch. 6 | Elizabeth | “Of this she was perfectly unaware: to her he was only the man who...” | Narrator views Elizabeth's ignorance | Medium |
| Ch. 8 | Elizabeth | “Elizabeth was so much caught by what passed, as to leave her very little attention for her book” | Slides into Elizabeth's viewpoint | Medium |
| Ch. 10 | **Darcy** | “He really believed that, were it not for the inferiority of her connections, he should be in some danger” | Darcy's mind; “danger” is his word choice | **High** |
| Ch. 12 | **Darcy** | “Elizabeth had been at Netherfield long enough. She attracted him more than he liked” | Rare passage from Darcy's viewpoint | **High** |
| Ch. 15 | Collins | “He thought too well of himself to comprehend on what motive his cousin could refuse him” | Narrator quotes Collins's vanity | Medium |
| Ch. 22 | **Charlotte** | “Without thinking highly either of men or of matrimony, marriage had always been her object” | Narrator conveys Charlotte's cool self-analysis | **High** |

| Ch. 61 | Narrator (irony) | “Happy for all her maternal feelings was the day on which Mrs. Bennet got rid of her two most deserving daughters” | Narratorial irony quoting and mocking Mrs Bennet's viewpoint | **High** |

## 4. The narrator's irony patterns

### Pattern A: “fancied” / “imagined” / “flattered herself” — Signals of delusion

When the narrator uses these words, they signal that **the character is mistaken**.

| Location | Original | Mistaken character | Reality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ch. 1 | “she fancied herself nervous” | Mrs Bennet | Not weak nerves, but dissatisfaction |
| Ch. 9 | “Mrs. Bennet, who fancied she had gained a complete victory” | Mrs Bennet | A defeat that deepened Darcy's contempt |
| Ch. 17 | “Mr. Collins might never make the offer, and, till he did, it was useless to quarrel” | Elizabeth | Collins will certainly propose |
| Ch. 19 | “He had fully proposed being engaged by Wickham” | Elizabeth | Wickham does not come |

**Translation principle:** Render “fancied” as **“imagined” / “supposed” / “deluded herself into thinking”**. A neutral “thought” erases the signal of delusion.

### Pattern B: Elevated formality applied to trivial objects — Mockery

The narrator applies elevated vocabulary to trivial actions for **comic ridicule**.

| Location | Original | Object | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ch. 1 | “The business of her life” | Mrs Bennet's marriage project | Mocks it with the managerial word “business” |
| Ch. 15 | “This was his plan of amends—of atonement—for inheriting” | Collins's motive for proposing | Mocks it with the religious word “atonement” |
| Ch. 22 | “the pure and disinterested desire of an establishment” | Charlotte's motive for marriage | “pure and disinterested” is not false; that fact is itself ironic |

### Pattern C: Double negatives/minimization — Actually strong affirmation

| Original | Surface | Actual |
|---|---|---|
| “not unpleasing” | not displeasing | **fairly pleasant** |
| “not without satisfaction” | not lacking satisfaction | **considerably satisfied** |
| “something like regret” | something resembling regret | **regret she cannot acknowledge** |
| “She could not be insensible to the compliment” | could not be insensitive to the compliment | **she was clearly pleased** |

## 5. Narrator vs character: Whose judgment is it?

The most important question in translation is: **Does this evaluation belong to the narrator or the character?**

### Checklist for identifying the judging subject

| Signal | Subject | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Past tense + third person + evaluative adjective | **Narrator** | “She was a woman of mean understanding” |
| “she felt” / “it seemed to her” | Transition from **character → narrator** | “Elizabeth felt that they had entirely mistaken his character” |
| Exclamation without quotation marks | **Character** (FID) | “How despicably have I acted!” |
| “perhaps” / “it is possible” | **Narrator's ironic reservation** | “Perhaps he had been civil only because he felt himself at ease” |
| “to be sure” / “indeed” / “certainly” | **Depends on context** | Ironic in the narrator's voice; emphatic in a character's |

### Crucially ambiguous cases

#### Case 1: Ch. 43 “to be mistress of Pemberley might be something!”
- **Surface:** She might have been mistress of Pemberley!
- **Whose judgment?** It is **Elizabeth's**, because “thought she” immediately precedes it. Yet the exclamation point may carry a **touch** of narratorial irony.
- **Translation:** Render Elizabeth's candid exclamation, then balance it with her self-correction, “But no—that could never be.”

#### Case 2: Ch. 44 “it was evident that he was very much in love with her”
- **Surface:** It was clear that he was deeply in love with her.
- **Whose judgment?** The viewpoint has shifted to **the Gardiners**. This is not Elizabeth's judgment, but the Gardiners' observational conclusion.
- **Translation:** Carry the nuance of “it was clear to the Gardiners.”

#### Case 3: Ch. 50 “no such happy marriage could now teach the admiring multitude what connubial felicity really was”
- **Surface:** Such a happy marriage could no longer teach the world what true marital happiness was.
- **Whose judgment?** **The narrator's** irony. “admiring multitude” is already mocking, while also reflecting Elizabeth's despair.
- **Translation:** Use the narrator's lightly distanced, ironic tone.

## 6. The novel's sole metacomment (Ch. 46)

> “If gratitude and esteem are good foundations of affection, Elizabeth's change of sentiment will be neither improbable nor faulty. But if otherwise, if the regard springing from such sources is unreasonable or unnatural, in comparison of what is so often described as arising on a first interview with its object, and even before two words have been exchanged, nothing can be said in her defence, except that she had given somewhat of a trial to the latter method, in her partiality for Wickham, and that its ill success might, perhaps, authorize her to seek the other less interesting mode of attachment.”

This is the only moment in the novel when **the narrator addresses the reader directly**.

- **Function:** A **pre-emptive rebuttal** to the potential criticism that Elizabeth's change of feeling is unrealistic
- **Tone:** Light humour (“less interesting mode of attachment”)
- **Wickham reference:** “her partiality for Wickham” is offered as the failed case of **love at first sight (the latter method)**
- **Translation guidance:** Preserve the paragraph's **essayistic tone**. This is not a character's story but the narrator's **critical intervention**.

## 8. Final principles for translators

### The three prohibitions in translating the narrator

1. **Do not explicate the narrator's irony**
   - ❌ “Mrs Bennet foolishly deluded herself that she had won.”
   - ✅ “Mrs Bennet, imagining that she had gained a complete victory, continued triumphantly.”
   - (Readers must recognize the delusion for themselves.)

2. **Do not add quotation marks to free indirect discourse**
   - ❌ “She thought, ‘How despicably have I acted!’”
   - ✅ “How despicably had she acted! She, who had prided herself on her discernment!”
   - (The boundary between narration and interiority must remain blurred.)

3. **Do not explain the narrator's humour or wit**
   - ❌ “(This is ironic.)”
   - ✅ Reproduce the original tone and leave the judgment to the reader.
