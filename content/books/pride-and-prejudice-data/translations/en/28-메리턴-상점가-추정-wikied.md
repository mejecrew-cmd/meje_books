# *Pride and Prejudice* — Meryton High Street (Inferred/Created)

> ⚠️ **This document is inferential and creative.** Meryton is often mentioned in the novel but rarely described in detail; only such places as the Philipses' house, Clarke's library, and a milliner's shop are specified. This reconstruction combines those fragments with the standard form of a Regency-era English country market town.

---

## Meryton Overview

### Explicit Information in the Novel
- One mile from Longbourn
- A “market town”
- Headquarters of the militia
- Home of Mr. and Mrs. Philips
- Clarke's library, frequented by officers
- A milliner's shop, often visited by the Bennet sisters

### Inferred Population
- **Approximately 800–1,500 people**, standard for a country market town
- Permanent residents plus roughly 100–200 militia during their winter posting

## 1. Principal Shops (Inferred)

### Clarke's Circulating Library ★
**Explicit text (Ch. 7): “she sees them now very often standing in Clarke's library”**

- **A favorite haunt of the officers**
- More than a book-lending business: a **social space**
- Newspapers and magazines available
- Open to women
- Subscription membership, one or two guineas a year
- Proprietor: Mr. Clarke, a fictional but explicitly named person

### Modiste / Milliner
**Explicit text (Ch. 7): “to a milliner's shop just over the way”**

- Opposite the Philipses' house
- Often visited by the Bennet sisters, especially Lydia and Kitty
- **A place where women meet and gossip**
- Carries some of the latest London fashions
- Sells bonnets (£1–3), ribbons, and lace

### Mr. Jones's Apothecary
**Explicit text (Ch. 7): “Mr. Jones came”**

- Serves as both apothecary and local doctor
- Prescribes, compounds medicines, and makes house calls
- Herbs, pills, syrups, and ointments
- One or two assistants on duty

### Mr. Philips's Attorney's Office
- Faces a street in Meryton
- Ground floor: office
- Upper two or three floors: residence
- Handles local disputes, wills, and contracts

## 5. Meryton's Daily Rhythm (Inferred)

### Weekdays
- **5–6 a.m.:** baker and butcher begin work
- **8–9 a.m.:** the street comes alive; servants shop
- **10 a.m.–noon:** women walk, shop, and pay calls
- **Lunchtime:** shops briefly quiet
- **2–4 p.m.:** activity returns; last outings before dinner
- **After 5 p.m.:** dinner hour; the street grows quiet
- **After 7 p.m.:** party guests travel and the inn grows lively

### Market Day (Inferred Once a Week)
- Livestock market: cattle, sheep, pigs
- Produce market
- Farmers arrive by cart from nearby districts
- The square fills completely

### Sundays
- All shops closed
- Church services in the morning and afternoon
- Activities other than walking are discouraged

## 8. Meryton Compared with Gracechurch Street, London

| Item | Meryton | Gracechurch Street |
|---|---|---|
| Scale | Around 1,000 people | A central commercial district in the City of London |
| Social unit | “Four-and-twenty families,” as Mrs. Bennet boasts | Anonymous city life |
| Speed of gossip | Immediate; everyone knows everyone | Slower; neighbors are strangers |
| Miss Bingley's assessment | At least a place where people live | “Cheapside,” spoken with contempt |

> **Translation point:** The Bingley sisters look down on Meryton and look down still further on Gracechurch Street; a double hierarchy is at work.
