# Mr. Bingley / Charles Bingley

## Basic Information
- **Original names:** Mr. Bingley, Charles Bingley
- **Korean rendering:** Mr. Bingley / Charles Bingley
- **Age:** estimated to be about 22–23
- **Income:** £4,000–5,000 a year from inherited wealth; he has not yet purchased an estate
- **Family:** from a northern commercial family whose fortune was made in his father’s generation; sisters Caroline and Louisa, Mrs. Hurst
- **Residence:** Netherfield Park, leased → London → Netherfield → an estate in Derbyshire
- **First appearance:** chunk_01 (Chap. 1, introduced indirectly); chunk_02 (Chap. 3, in person)

## Character Introduction
A wealthy unmarried young man who appears in Meryton after leasing Netherfield Park. “Good-looking and gentlemanlike,” with “a pleasant countenance, and easy, unaffected manners,” he wins everyone’s favor from the first ball. Social by nature in complete contrast to Darcy, he mixes easily with everyone and dances with pleasure. Yet this cheerful character is also linked to a lack of independence in judgment.

Bingley’s central weakness is that he yields too readily to the influence of those around him, especially Darcy and his sisters, instead of trusting his own judgment. Though sincerely in love with Jane, he withdraws to London and tries to give her up under Darcy’s opposition and his sisters’ manipulation. Only after Darcy withdraws his interference does Bingley return to Longbourn and propose, revealing both his goodness of heart and his irresolute will. Bingley’s “modesty,” paired with Darcy’s “pride,” shows that both too much and too little self-confidence can be faults.

## Development by Event

### Act I: Meeting Jane (Chaps. 1–12)
- **EVT-001:** news that he has leased Netherfield Park excites Meryton society and becomes Mrs. Bennet’s chief concern.
- **EVT-003:** at the Meryton assembly, he dances twice with Jane and calls her the most beautiful woman he has ever seen.
- **EVT-006:** he receives the ill Jane at Netherfield and worries about her sincerely. His affection for her deepens.
- **EVT-010:** Jane recovers and returns home. Their relationship has advanced, but not to a formal proposal.

### Act II: Separation and Parting (Chaps. 18–27)
- **EVT-019:** persuaded by Darcy and aided by his sisters’ conspiracy, he leaves for London. Though Bingley finds it difficult to leave Jane, he yields to Darcy’s claim that Jane does not love him and does not return to Netherfield.
- **EVT-021:** Jane visits in London, but Miss Bingley conceals the visit and does not inform him, as inferred from the text. He remains unaware that Jane is in London.

### Act III: Return and Marriage (Chaps. 43–61)
- **EVT-037:** after Darcy ceases his interference, Bingley returns to Netherfield and visits Longbourn with Darcy.
- **EVT-043:** he visits Longbourn frequently and resumes his relationship with Jane, quickly overcoming their earlier awkwardness.
- **EVT-044:** he proposes to Jane and is accepted, then asks Mr. Bennet for consent.
- **EVT-048:** he marries Jane. After a year at Netherfield, they purchase an estate in Derbyshire and move near Pemberley.

## Core Relationships
- **Jane:** love at first sight, followed by separation, reunion, and marriage; both are good and gentle, making them the most innocent couple
- **Darcy:** his closest friend and mentor, but Bingley depends too heavily on Darcy’s judgment and lacks autonomy; Darcy drives both the separation and its reversal
- **Caroline, Miss Bingley:** his younger sister; seeks to marry Darcy and cooperates in separating Bingley and Jane
- **Mrs. Hurst, Louisa:** his married sister; acts alongside Caroline

## Translation Notes
- Bingley always speaks brightly and simply, without elaborate rhetoric. Contrast him clearly with Darcy’s elevated style.
- In “easy, unaffected manners,” “easy” means relaxed and natural, not simple or effortless.
- Adjectives such as “amiable,” “good-natured,” and “yielding” recur in descriptions of Bingley. Keep their tone consistent.
- In passages showing Bingley’s irresolution, preserve a tone of regret rather than blame; Austen does not portray him harshly.
