# *Pride and Prejudice* — Dictionary of Objects and Class Symbols

> This document classifies the objects, props, food, and games appearing in the novel as **class signals**.
> Translation must reflect not merely their dictionary meanings but the **wealth, rank, and character** they imply.

---

## 1. Carriages — A Barometer of Wealth and Rank

The carriage was the most immediately legible **sign of wealth** in the period. Its type, number of horses, and whether it was owned or hired directly communicated class.

### Carriage Hierarchy (Highest → Lowest)

| Carriage | Original | Horses | Class Signal | User in the Novel | Appearance |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Four-horse carriage** | Chaise and four | 4 | Highest—an intimidating mode of travel for great aristocrats | Bingley when moving in; Lady Catherine visiting Longbourn | chunk_01, 16 |
| **Barouche** | Barouche(-box) | 4 | Upper—large luxury carriage; offering someone the seat beside the driver on the box was a favor | Lady Catherine | chunk_10 |
| **Curricle** | Curricle | 2 | Upper—light, fast, and elegant; a young gentleman’s sports car | Darcy, Bingley | chunk_12 |
| **Phaeton** | Phaeton | 2 | Upper-middle—small open carriage; also available as a lady’s low phaeton | Miss de Bourgh | chunk_08, 15 |
| **Chaise** | Chaise | 2 | Middle—covered traveling carriage; ordinary means of long-distance travel | Elizabeth traveling to Hunsford | chunk_07 |
| **Post-chaise** | Post-chaise / Travelling post | Changed by stage | Middle—traveled by changing post-horses; quite expensive | Lady Catherine’s criticism; ordinary travel | chunk_10 |
| **Hackney coach** | Hackney-coach | 1 | Lower—a commercial London taxi that provided anonymity | Wickham and Lydia concealing their escape route | chunk_13 |

### Translation Notes
- When **“chaise and four”** appears, it is not merely a “carriage” but an **ostentatious display of wealth**. It heightens the neighborhood’s expectations at Bingley’s first appearance.
- **Lady Catherine arriving at Longbourn with post-horses** conveys urgency and intimidation: she spared no expense and traveled at full speed.
- **Hackney coach**: Wickham and Lydia change vehicles to break the trail—the nuance is “they switched to a taxi and disappeared.”
- Whether a carriage is **owned** or **hired** also matters. The Bennets own a carriage, but the horses must be used on the farm, so it is not often available—why Jane has to ride on horseback.

## 2. Card Games — Metaphors for Character

Choosing a card game was not mere leisure. It revealed a character’s **intelligence, sociability, and appetite for gambling**.

### Card-Game Character Matrix

| Game | Original | Players | Character | Players in the Novel | Metaphorical Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Vingt-un** | Vingt-un | Many | Fast rounds, intuitive luck | Bingley, Jane | Simple, pure characters—their shared preference suggests similarity |
| **Commerce** | Commerce | Many | Card exchange, requires strategy | Comparison | More calculating than vingt-un; Bingley and Jane’s preference for vingt-un emphasizes their simplicity |
| **Loo** | Loo | Many | High stakes, strongly associated with gambling | The Hursts, Bingley’s party | The party’s luxurious amusement; Elizabeth refuses because they are “playing high” |
| **Whist** | Whist | 4 | Intricate strategy, partnership | Collins at the Philipses; older society | Forerunner of bridge; Collins joins without knowing the game and loses every hand, exposing incompetence |
| **Piquet** | Piquet | 2 | Intimate contest, advanced strategy | Bingley, Hurst | A two-player game; they play separately while Darcy writes his letter |
| **Quadrille** | Quadrille | 4 | French origin, elegance | Lady Catherine, Collins | Evening amusement at Rosings; Elizabeth’s designation as “the fourth at quadrille” represents her presumed fate as Collins’s wife |
| **Cassino** | Cassino | 2–4 | Light matching game | Miss de Bourgh | An undemanding game suited to her sickliness |
| **Backgammon** | Backgammon | 2 | Dice + strategy | Mr. Bennet, Collins | Mr. Bennet’s **means of escape** from Collins’s verbosity |
| **Lottery tickets** | Lottery tickets | Many | Simple drawing, speculative | Lydia | The lowest form of amusement, symbolizing Lydia’s vulgarity and excitement |

### Translation Notes
- Whether to transliterate or explain a game’s name depends on context. For example, “Vingt-un” is best rendered as a transliteration plus the number “21.”
- **Collins losing every hand at whist** condenses his flattery, ostentation, and incompetence into one sentence: he has lost five shillings, but thanks to Lady Catherine it means nothing.
- **Lydia’s excitement over lottery tickets** symbolizes her speculative, impulsive nature. She yields her conversation time with Wickham to Elizabeth only because of the game.

## 3. Food & Drink — The Taste of Class

| Food/Drink | Original | Class Signal | Context in the Novel | Translation Note |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ragout** | Ragout | Upper—a French stew | Mr. Hurst is speechless when Elizabeth prefers a “plain dish.” The Bingley sisters’ sophisticated food culture vs. Bennet simplicity | Retain “ragout”; French cuisine signals the upper class |
| **Venison** | Venison / Haunch | Highest—only great landowners could hunt deer | Mrs. Bennet prepares it for Bingley and Darcy; rare meat available only to estate owners | “Haunch of venison/venison”—symbol of landed privilege |
| **Hothouse fruit** | Grapes, nectarines, peaches | Highest—required a hothouse in the British climate | Served in Pemberley’s drawing room, naturally revealing Darcy’s immense wealth | “Pyramid of hothouse fruit”—effortless abundance rather than deliberate display |
| **Port wine** | Port wine | Lower-middle—a common man’s drink | Described through the smell emitted by Uncle Philips, contrasting his worldly middle-class presence with Wickham’s polish | Appears only as an odor—a tool of class comparison |
| **White soup** | White soup | Upper-middle—essential ball refreshment | Bingley says he will send invitations as soon as Nicholls has made enough white soup | A prop establishing the formality of the ball |

## 4. Clothing & Accessories — Markers of Status

| Item | Original | Class/Functional Signal | Context in the Novel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Red coats** | Red coats | Militia officers’ uniform | Elizabeth searches for Wickham among the “red coats” at the ball; visual emblem of Lydia and Kitty’s officer mania |
| **Livery** | Livery | Uniform marking a servant’s household | Elizabeth instantly recognizes Darcy’s party from the curricle’s livery; visually transmits family identity |
| **Shoe roses** | Shoe-roses | Ball-preparation accessory | Rain prevents a personal purchase, so they are obtained through a proxy; signals anticipation and preparation |
| **Parasol** | Parasol | Essential accessory for an upper-class woman | Lydia mentions buying a new one in Brighton; symbol of fashion and consumption |
| **Nightcap + powdering gown** | Nightcap and powdering gown | Indoor wear | Mr. Bennet jokes that these alone could serve as his legacy; symbol of financial irresponsibility |

## 5. Music & Arts — Measures of Accomplishment

| Item | Original | Meaning | Context in the Novel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pianoforte** | Pianoforte | A lady’s central accomplishment | Skill directly affected marriage-market value. Elizabeth plays “tolerably,” Mary practices hard without talent, and Georgiana excels. Lady Catherine insults Elizabeth by telling her to practise in the housekeeper’s room. |
| **Pastels/crayons** | Crayons | A woman’s artistic accomplishment | Georgiana’s pastel drawings are displayed at Pemberley; Elizabeth examines them with more interest than the oil paintings. |
| **Netting a purse** | Netting a purse | A woman’s needlework accomplishment | Darcy and Miss Bingley cite it as an example of something that is not a “true accomplishment,” distinguishing superficial from substantive culture. |

### The Standard for an “Accomplished Woman” — The Central Debate of Chapter 8

| Speaker | Standard of Accomplishment |
|---|---|
| **Bingley** | “Painting tables, covering screens, and netting purses—every young woman can do them” (low standard) |
| **Miss Bingley** | “Music, singing, drawing, dancing, modern languages + a certain something in gait, voice, and expression” (external standard) |
| **Darcy** | All the above + **“improvement of her mind by extensive reading”** (internal standard) |
| **Elizabeth** | “I have never seen such a woman. I have never seen half of it together” (satirical dismantling) |

> Translate “accomplished” consistently, but in this debate its nuance may be closer to **“fully formed”** than simply “cultivated.”

## 6. Letters & Communication — Speed Equals Urgency

| Means | Original | Speed/Cost | Use in the Novel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ordinary post** | Post / Letter | Several days, inexpensive | Most correspondence: Miss Bingley’s and Collins’s letters, among others |
| **Express** | Express | A few hours, expensive | Used only for emergencies: news of Lydia’s elopement (Colonel Forster → Longbourn); Mr. Gardiner’s notice of the marriage settlement |
| **Quill pen** | Pen (quill) | — | The task of sharpening a quill with a knife, “mending a pen”; Miss Bingley volunteers to impress Darcy |

### Translation Notes
- The appearance of **“express” is itself an emergency signal**. “An express arrived” carries the urgency of “an emergency dispatch came at midnight.”
- Darcy’s letter (Ch.35) is a **long letter densely covering two sheets**. Translate it in a refined epistolary register clearly distinct from spoken conversation.

## 7. Summary: Narrative Functions of Objects

| Function | Object | Operation |
|---|---|---|
| **Measure of wealth** | Carriage types, hothouse fruit, glazing, fireplace cost | Ownership and use reveal wealth instantly |
| **Metaphor for character** | Choice of card game | Vingt-un = purity; loo = luxury; whist = strategy; lottery = vulgarity |
| **Boundary of class** | Ragout vs. plain dish, smell of port, livery | Expresses the cultural break between upper and middle classes through sight, smell, and taste |
| **Standard of accomplishment** | Pianoforte, drawing, netting a purse, reading | Checklist determining value in the marriage market |
| **Signal of urgency** | Express | Its appearance itself announces a coming crisis |
| **Instrument of escape** | Hackney coach, post-chaise | Switching transportation to break pursuit |
| **Symbol of authority** | Chaise and four, glazing/window tax, right of presentation to a living | Visual and institutional display of wealth + power |
