# *Pride and Prejudice* — Map of Institutional and Economic Terms

> This document systematically organizes the **legal, economic, and social institutions** that drive the novel’s plot.
> Without understanding these terms, a translator cannot convey the characters’ motives or the true nature of their crises.

---

## 1. The Economics of Marriage — Why Everyone’s Life Depends on It

### Core Structure: Fortune → Settlement → Pin-money

```
[Bride’s side]                          [Groom’s side]
Fortune (dowry/property)                Estate / Income
    │                                      │
    └──→ Marriage Articles ←───────────────┘
              │
              ├──→ Settlement
              │      An annuity secured for the widow after her husband’s death
              │
              ├──→ Pin-money
              │      A fixed annual sum the wife can spend without her husband’s interference
              │
              └──→ Jointure
                     A specific form of settlement providing a widow’s income
```

### Application in the Novel

| Character | Fortune/Property | Marriage Settlement | Translation Key |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Each Bennet sister** | About £1,000 (one fifth of their mother’s £5,000 after her death) | — | Invested in “4 per cents”; about £40 annual interest—far too little for independence |
| **Lydia–Wickham** | Lydia’s £1,000 portion + £100 a year during her father’s lifetime | Darcy pays Wickham’s additional expenses | Mr. Bennet marvels that it was settled for so little; Darcy actually bears most of the cost |
| **Georgiana Darcy** | £30,000 | — | Wickham’s real reason for seducing Georgiana |
| **Miss de Bourgh** | The entire Rosings estate | — | A marriage to Darcy would mean “the union of the two estates” |
| **Miss King** | £10,000 inherited from her grandfather | — | Why Wickham turns away from Elizabeth |

### Translation Guide to Terms

| Original | Recommended Rendering | Never Translate As | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fortune | property / fortune / dowry | luck | In a marriage context it always means property |
| Settlement | marriage settlement / property settlement | settlement in a place, mere agreement | A legally binding property contract |
| Pin-money | personal allowance / wife’s allowance | pin money literally | An official expense specified in the marriage contract |
| 4 per cents | the 4 per cent government funds | simply four percent | Proper name for British government securities |
| Portion | share / inheritance portion | mere part | A legal allocation from an estate |

## 2. Entail — The Novel’s Central Engine of Conflict

### Structure

```
[Estate owner: Mr. Bennet]
    │
    │  Under the entail,
    │  daughters cannot inherit
    │
    └──→ [Legal heir: Mr. Collins]
              │
              ├── On Mr. Bennet’s death → inherits the whole Longbourn estate
              ├── Mrs. Bennet + five daughters → must leave
              └── Alternative: a son could have joined in cutting off the entail, but
                         five daughters in succession → impossible to break
```

### Narrative Function of the Entail by Event

| Event | Role of the Entail |
|---|---|
| EVT-011 (Collins arrives) | An abstract law is embodied as a person—“I intend to make amends to my amiable cousins” |
| EVT-017 (Collins proposes) | Elizabeth’s refusal = rejecting the entail’s supposed “compensation” |
| EVT-020 (Charlotte accepts) | Charlotte’s acceptance makes her Longbourn’s future mistress, provoking Mrs. Bennet’s hostility |
| Ch.50 | Mr. Bennet’s regret: he assumed a son would be born and therefore saved nothing |

### Translation Notes
- Translate **“entail” with the same term every time**. Do not alternate among “restriction on estate inheritance,” “law of limited succession,” and other formulations.
- When Mrs. Bennet says, “I cannot bear to hear that mentioned,” “that” means the entail. Preserve her evasive refusal to name it directly.

## 3. The Economics of the Church — How a Parish Clergyman Earned a Living

### Core Structure

```
[Patron]
    │
    ├──[Patronage]──────────────→ Appointment of a parish clergyman
    │
    ├──[Presentation of the living]→ Grants the income of a particular parish
    │
    └──[Advowson]───────────────→ Permanent right of appointment attached to an estate
    
[Parish clergyman: Rector/Vicar]
    │
    ├── Income 1: Tithes—taxes/produce paid by parishioners
    ├── Income 2: Glebe land—farmland attached to the parsonage
    └── Income 3: Fees—for baptisms, marriages, funerals, and so on

[Taking orders]
    └── After university, a man had to be ordained by a bishop to become a clergyman
```

### Application in the Novel

| Character/Event | Role of Clerical Economics |
|---|---|
| **Collins** | Gains the Hunsford living through Lady Catherine’s patronage. Claims that tithes must be negotiated “without displeasing his patroness,” revealing the economic basis of his flattery. |
| **The essence of the Wickham–Darcy conflict** | The elder Darcy promises Wickham the best living in his will. Wickham resolves against taking orders, accepts £3,000 instead, and squanders it. Three years later, when the incumbent dies, he demands the living after all and is refused. |
| **Darcy’s power** | Holds the patronage of several parishes. Colonel Fitzwilliam has no such power, which is Charlotte’s hidden reason for trying to match Elizabeth with Darcy. |

### Translation Guide to Terms

| Original | Recommended Rendering | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Living | clerical living / benefice / parish income | Not ordinary “life,” but the official income from a parish |
| Patronage | patronage / right of presentation | An aristocrat’s right to appoint a clergyman |
| Presentation | presentation to a living / parish appointment | The act of “presenting” a clergyman to a particular parish |
| Taking orders | take holy orders / enter the Church | Formal ordination by a bishop |
| Incumbent | incumbent / current clergyman | The person presently holding the living |
| Rector | parish rector | A full rector receiving all tithes; a vicar received only part |
| Tithes | tithes | Taxes or produce paid by parishioners; the clergyman’s main income |

## 4. The Economics of the Army — How an Officer Obtained His Rank

### Core Structure

```
[Militia]                              [Regular Army]
  │                                      │
  ├── Domestic security/defense           ├── Active service, including overseas deployment
  ├── Rotating regional stations          ├── Fixed garrisons
  ├── Officer commissions relatively cheap├── Purchase system for commissions
  │                                      │     └── Rank bought and sold for money
  └── Wickham’s unit before elopement      └── Wickham’s unit afterward, purchased by Darcy

[Rank hierarchy, low → high]
  Ensign → Lieutenant → Captain → Colonel
  
  ※ An ensigncy was the lowest commissioned infantry rank
  ※ This is the commission Darcy purchases for Wickham
```

### Application in the Novel

| Term | Context in the Novel | Translation Note |
|---|---|---|
| **Militia regiment** | Stationed at Meryton for the winter; object of Lydia and Kitty’s frenzy; officers’ red coats are its visual symbol | Use one consistent term for militia regiment |
| **Commission** | The officer’s position itself; “accepted a commission” = became an officer | commission / appointment as an officer |
| **Commission purchased** | The officer’s post is bought with money; Darcy buys Wickham’s ensigncy | purchase of a commission / payment for appointment |
| **Ensigncy** | Lowest regular-army officer rank; cheapest commission | ensigncy / infantry ensign’s commission |
| **Colonel** | Regimental commander; Colonel Forster and Colonel Fitzwilliam | Both are colonels but have entirely different characters |

## 5. Social Institutions — Rules of the Marriage Market

### Core Terms

| Original | Translation | Explanation | Context in the Novel |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Assembly** | assembly / public local ball | Public social event where local families danced; choice of partners sent social signals | The Meryton assembly (EVT-003) starts everything |
| **Ball** | formal ball | More formal than an assembly; hosted at a private house | Netherfield ball (EVT-016) |
| **Coming out** | coming out / social debut | A young daughter becomes eligible to attend balls as an adult | Lady Catherine criticizes all five daughters for being “out” |
| **Accomplishment** | accomplishment / cultivated skill | Music, drawing, dancing, languages, and other skills increasing marriage-market value | The accomplishments debate in Ch.8; see Object Dictionary §5 |
| **Special licence** | special marriage licence | Issued by the Archbishop of Canterbury; marriage permitted at any time and place; very expensive | Mrs. Bennet demands one in excitement over Darcy’s wealth |
| **Banns** | marriage banns | Marriage after three consecutive weekly announcements in the parish church; ordinary procedure | Rarely mentioned directly, but contrasted with a special licence |

```

│                                               │
│  Estate ──→ Annual Income                     │
│      │                                        │
│      ├──[Entail]──→ Inheritance by male heir only
│      │                                        │
│      ├──[Manor]──→ Attached hunting, fishing, and other rights
│      │                                        │
│      └──[Patronage]──→ Right to appoint parish clergy

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ MARRIAGE ECONOMY ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

```

### How This Structure Drives the Plot

| Institution | Plot Engine |
|---|---|
| **Entail** | Why marriage is urgent for the Bennet sisters; why Collins exists; Charlotte’s motive for marrying |
| **Fortune/Settlement** | Why Wickham targets Georgiana (£30,000); the financial terms of Lydia’s marriage; the basis for Collins insulting Elizabeth by saying her fortune is too small for another chance |
| **Patronage/Living** | Core of the Darcy–Wickham conflict; economic basis for Collins’s flattery of Lady Catherine |
| **Commission/Ensigncy** | Darcy’s method of “disposing of” Wickham—buys him an ensigncy and sends him north, effectively exiling him |
| **Trade** | The Bingleys’ complex about wealth derived from commerce; the Bingley sisters’ contempt for Mr. Gardiner because he lives in Cheapside; Darcy’s willing acceptance of the Gardiners proves his transformation |

## 7. Reference Table for Monetary Values in the Novel

| Amount in the Novel | Meaning | Very Rough Modern Equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| £10,000 a year | Darcy’s income—highest gentry / near-aristocratic | About KRW 1–1.5 billion per year |
| £4–5,000 a year | Bingley’s income—upper gentry | About KRW 400–700 million per year |
| £2,000 a year | Mr. Bennet’s income—middling gentry | About KRW 200–300 million per year |
| £100,000 | Bingley’s total inherited fortune | About KRW 10–15 billion |
| £30,000 | Georgiana’s fortune | About KRW 3–5 billion |
| £10,000 | Miss King’s inheritance | About KRW 1–1.5 billion |
| £5,000 | Mrs. Bennet’s fortune, divided among five daughters | About KRW 500–700 million in total |
| £3,000 | Sum Wickham receives instead of the living | About KRW 300–500 million |
| £1,000 | Legacy the elder Darcy leaves Wickham | About KRW 100–150 million |
| £100 a year | Mr. Bennet’s annual allowance to Lydia | About KRW 10–15 million a year |

> **Caution**: Conversions vary enormously by period and method. These figures are rough purchasing-power references, not exact scholarly conversions. Retain the original amounts in translation and explain the context in notes where appropriate.
