## Estimated Translation Difficulty

| Item | Assessment | Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Vocabulary** | Many period terms (★★★★☆) | More than twenty-five words whose period meanings differ from modern usage, including *tolerable*, *civility*, and *agreeable* |
| **Syntax** | Long and complex (★★★★☆) | Multi-clause sentences frequently exceed five lines; FID makes the speaker difficult to identify |
| **Cultural distance** | Great (★★★★☆) | Many institutions, including entails, purchased commissions, and the social season, have no direct equivalent in modern South Korea |
| **Technical terminology** | Limited (★★☆☆☆) | Mostly everyday vocabulary, but with period-specific meanings |
| **Identifying the narrator** | Very difficult (★★★★★) | The boundary between FID and direct narration is ambiguous; misidentifying it can reverse the meaning in translation |

## Special Considerations

1. **The title is the theme:** Pride and prejudice are embodied separately by the two protagonists, then exchanged and resolved. Consistent translation of these two terms forms the backbone of the entire translation.
2. **The narrator's presence:** Austen's narrator is an ironist who is “invisible, but always smiling.” This is the translator's greatest trap.
3. **The narrative function of letters:** Darcy's letter in Chapter 35 is the physical and semantic center of the work. The quality of its translation governs the whole.
4. **The subtlety of class language:** A five-step gradation among adjectives of favor—*tolerable* → *civil* → *agreeable* → *amiable* → *charming*.
5. **Meryton as a model town:** Because the novel offers few concrete details, translation and adaptation require reconstruction of a standard Regency country market town.
