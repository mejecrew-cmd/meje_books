# *Pride and Prejudice* — Menus and Food Culture (Estimated/Creative)

> ⚠️ **This document consists of estimates and creative reconstruction.** It reconstructs plausible tables by combining foods explicitly mentioned in the novel—ragout, venison, hothouse fruit, white soup, and port—with standard Regency-era diets and each household’s financial means.

---

## Basic Structure of Regency-Era Meals

### Courses at a Formal Dinner

| Course | Contents |
|---|---|
| **First Course** | Soup + fish + meat, all placed on the table at once |
| **Second Course** | Poultry + vegetables + pastry, all placed at once |
| **Dessert** | Fruit + nuts + small cakes, arranged on a separately reset table |

> **Service:** “à la française”—all dishes were spread across the table at once, and diners helped themselves to what was nearby.

### Level by Household
- **Pemberley/Rosings:** Full formal courses and elaborate decoration at every meal
- **Netherfield:** Formal courses reflecting Bingley’s uncomplicated tastes
- **Longbourn:** Simple on ordinary days, formal when entertaining
- **Hunsford:** Frugal, with fewer courses
- **Wickham and Lydia:** Extremely frugal

## 11. Seasonal Ingredients

### Autumn (the novel begins, September–November)
- Beginning of the game season: pheasant, partridge, hare
- English-grown apples, pears, and plums
- Pumpkins and cabbages

### Winter (December–February)
- Preserved meats: salted and smoked
- Winter vegetables: turnips, cabbages, onions
- Preserved fruit: jams and pickles
- **Venison**—game season

### Spring (March–May)—The Hunsford Period
- Spring vegetables: asparagus and peas
- Lamb
- New-laid eggs

### Summer (June–August)—The Pemberley Period
- Abundant fresh vegetables
- **Hothouse fruit:** grapes, peaches, nectarines
- Abundant fish
- Strawberries and raspberries

## 12. Table Etiquette (Reference for Translation and Adaptation)

| Item | Rule |
|---|---|
| **Seating** | The hostess sits at the end of the table, with the guest of honor on her right |
| **Serving** | All food is placed on the table at once; each diner helps themselves to nearby dishes |
| **Ladies withdraw first** | After dessert, the ladies retire to the drawing room while the gentlemen drink port |
| **Rejoining** | About an hour later, the gentlemen join the ladies in the drawing room for tea |
| **Collins’s excess** | Praising every course is behavior lacking in refinement |
| **Mr. Hurst’s rudeness** | He cares only about food and stops speaking to Elizabeth when she says she prefers a “plain dish” |
