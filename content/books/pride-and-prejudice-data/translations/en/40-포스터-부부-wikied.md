## Basic Information
- **Original names:** Colonel Forster, Mrs. Forster, and Harriet Forster
- **Korean rendering:** Colonel Forster / Mrs. Forster / Harriet Forster
- **Position:** Colonel Forster commands the militia regiment stationed at Meryton
- **Residence:** Meryton → Brighton as the militia moves
- **First appearance:** chunk_22, around Chap. 39

## Character Introduction
They are the commander of the militia stationed at Meryton and his young wife. Mrs. Forster, Harriet, is a young and frivolous woman close to Lydia's age, and the two rapidly become friends. Her invitation for Lydia to accompany her to Brighton directly enables Lydia's elopement. After the flight, Colonel Forster testifies that Wickham was “not a man to be trusted as his reputation suggested” and conveys information about the escape to the Bennet family.

## Development by Event

### Brighton Episode
- **EVT-032:** Mrs. Forster invites Lydia to Brighton. Elizabeth warns her father of the danger, but Mr. Bennet dismisses her, saying Lydia will be safe under Colonel Forster's supervision.
- **EVT-038:** Lydia flees with Wickham. In a letter left for Mrs. Forster, she boasts that she will become Mrs. Wickham. Colonel Forster pursues Wickham and reports the situation to the Bennets. He also reveals that Wickham had a poor reputation within the regiment because of gambling debts and immoral conduct.

## Core Relationships
- **Lydia:** Mrs. Forster is Lydia's Brighton friend. She should act as a supervisor, but her own recklessness makes her fail.
- **The Bennets:** indirect responsibility for Lydia's elopement through the Brighton invitation and failure of supervision
- **Wickham:** Colonel Forster reveals Wickham's bad reputation after the flight

## Translation Notes
- Translate “Colonel” with the established term for that rank. Distinguish the militia, a local-defense organization, from the regular army.
- Recognize the structural problem: Mrs. Forster's youth and recklessness encourage Lydia's self-indulgence.
- Mrs. Forster and Lydia's intimacy is evident when Lydia calls her “Harriet” in the letter.
