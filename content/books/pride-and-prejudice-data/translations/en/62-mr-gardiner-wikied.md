# Mr. Gardiner / Edward Gardiner

## Basic Information
- **Original names**: Mr. Gardiner, Mr. Edward Gardiner
- **Korean rendering**: Mr. Gardiner / Edward Gardiner
- **Relationship**: Mrs. Bennet’s younger brother; maternal uncle to Elizabeth, Jane, Lydia, and their sisters
- **Occupation**: Engaged in commerce and trade in Cheapside, London
- **Residence**: Gracechurch Street, London
- **First appearance**: chunk_07 (Chapter 25)

## Character Profile
Though Mrs. Bennet’s younger brother, he possesses a character entirely opposite to his sister’s. He is “a sensible, gentlemanlike man, greatly superior to his sister, as well by nature as education.” Despite the supposedly “low” station of a Cheapside merchant, his actual cultivation and judgment surpass those of Mr. Bennet, the Bingley sisters, and Lady Catherine, all members of the gentry. At their first meeting even Darcy is surprised that a merchant can possess such dignity, and immediately revises his prejudice.

During Lydia’s elopement, Gardiner assumes the practical role of head of the family and directs the search and negotiations in London. He formally faces Wickham as the party to the settlement, though it is later revealed that Darcy provided the funds. With his consent, Gardiner thus serves as the “mask” concealing Darcy’s secret intervention, demonstrating both his good judgment and his trust in Darcy. He is one of Austen’s rare sympathetic portraits of the commercial class.

## Development by Event

### Before the Lydia Crisis: Introduction and Assistance
- **EVT-024** While visiting his sister’s family, he takes Jane back to London and cares for her. During the Christmas visit, he calmly settles the Bennet family’s disputes.
- **EVT-034** He travels through Derbyshire with his wife and Elizabeth. When the itinerary is shortened from the Lake District to Derbyshire, a visit to Pemberley becomes possible. He accompanies Elizabeth on the journey that transforms her life.
- **EVT-036** He meets Darcy in person at Pemberley and is greatly surprised when Darcy first asks to be introduced to the Gardiners and treats them courteously.
- **EVT-036-001** Darcy gives him permission to fish and even provides tackle. Gardiner judges Darcy “completely different from what I had supposed,” serving as a witness to Darcy’s rehabilitation in Elizabeth’s eyes.

### Lydia’s Elopement and the Intervention (Chapters 46–52)
- **EVT-038** On hearing of the elopement, he arrives at Longbourn immediately with Mr. Bennet and calmly assumes command.
- **EVT-039-005** (Circumstantial) He receives the terms of the settlement from Darcy and agrees to serve as the nominal agent, allowing it to appear that he has rescued the Bennet family.
- **EVT-040** Gardiner’s letter announces the marriage settlement between Wickham and Lydia. The family at Longbourn believes Gardiner has spent a large sum and vows lifelong gratitude. Mr. Bennet resolves to repay the debt, saying, “My brother-in-law has done it all.”
- **EVT-040-002** He arranges Lydia and Wickham’s wedding at his London home and permits Darcy to attend.

### Conclusion (Chapter 61)
- **EVT-048** After Elizabeth and Darcy marry, the Gardiners become their most beloved relatives.
- **EVT-049** “The Darcys always remained on the most intimate terms with the Gardiners, and felt the sincerest gratitude toward the persons who had brought Elizabeth to Derbyshire.”

## Core Relationships
- **Elizabeth**: Her maternal uncle and a dependable adult; the structure positions her uncle, rather than her father, as her model of a discerning grown man
- **Darcy**: Class prejudice at first → mutual respect → closest relative after the marriage; their friendship forms the emotional foundation of the ending
- **Mrs. Bennet**: His sister; the intellectual and emotional gulf between them is so great that he treats her as someone beyond control
- **Mr. Bennet**: His brother-in-law; during the Lydia crisis, Gardiner fills the place Mr. Bennet has failed to occupy
- **Wickham**: The face-to-face negotiator in Lydia’s case; the mask through which Darcy’s terms are conveyed to Wickham

## Translation Notes
- In “sensible, gentlemanlike,” render “gentlemanlike” as “gentlemanly” or “dignified.” Austen’s central point is that this adjective applies to a man in trade.
- “Cheapside” is a marker of class prejudice in Miss Bingley’s mockery that Jane’s uncles live there. Transliterate the place name while making its commercial character clear from context.
- Gardiner’s letter in EVT-040 uses the formal business tone of a merchant: dry and efficient, distinctly unlike Mr. Bennet’s irony or Collins’s exaggeration.
- “Brother Gardiner” denotes a brother-in-law. The translation must distinguish among brother-in-law and maternal uncle according to the speaker.
