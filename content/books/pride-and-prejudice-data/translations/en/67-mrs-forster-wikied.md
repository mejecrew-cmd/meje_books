# Mrs. Forster / Harriet

## Basic Information
- **Original names**: Mrs. Forster, Harriet
- **Korean rendering**: Mrs. Forster / Harriet
- **Age**: Very young, approximately Lydia’s age
- **Relationship**: The recently married wife of Colonel Forster
- **Residence**: Meryton → Brighton with the militia’s transfer
- **First appearance**: chunk_05–06 (around Chapters 18–21, as her friendship with Lydia develops)

## Character Profile
Colonel Forster’s young wife. Nearly the same age as Lydia and Kitty, she is essentially a peer and friend of Lydia’s generation. Although she possesses the social status of a married woman, she utterly fails to serve as a discerning elder, and that failure creates the narrative crisis. She is the person who invites Lydia to Brighton and is nominally responsible for restraining Lydia’s recklessness there, yet instead becomes a companion in her amusements and an accomplice.

Mrs. Forster is not malicious, but she represents Austen’s satirical type of “a girl married too young,” conveying the message that marriage does not confer judgment. Lydia’s playful letter left for her when she elopes—“Dear Harriet, I am going to Gretna Green with Wickham! How surprised you will be!”—reveals that their relationship was not supervisor and ward, but accomplice and accomplice.

## Development by Event
- **EVT-032** She invites Lydia to Brighton and sends Mr. Bennet a formal invitation requesting Lydia’s company. Elizabeth judges the invitation “a calamity for Lydia” and tries unsuccessfully to persuade her father.
- **EVT-038** She is the first to discover Lydia’s elopement. After receiving Lydia’s playful farewell letter, she immediately informs her husband. The letter’s contents are sent to Longbourn, formally notifying the family of the crisis. Yet she shows almost no self-reproach for her failure of supervision, revealing a lack of mature reflection.

## Core Relationships
- **Lydia**: Her “best friend” in Brighton; a structural failure in which the supposed supervisor descends to the level of a peer
- **Colonel Forster**: Her older husband, who fails to restrain his wife’s recklessness
- **The Bennet family**: Entrusted with Lydia’s protection, but fails to fulfill her responsibility of supervision

## Translation Notes
- Lydia’s intimate use of “Harriet” for Mrs. Forster may conflict with surname-based honorific conventions in translation. If transliterated directly, the context must still reveal their relationship as equals.
- Lydia’s teasing tone in the farewell letter approaches the frivolity of “Ha! ha! ha!” The translation should make readers feel that Mrs. Forster was the sort of friend to receive such a letter.
- Mrs. Forster’s youth and thoughtlessness are objects of structural criticism. Preserve the contrast between her age and poor judgment rather than rendering her merely as a “young wife.”
