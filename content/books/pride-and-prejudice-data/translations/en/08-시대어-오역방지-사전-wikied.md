# *Pride and Prejudice* — Historical-Language Mistranslation Prevention Glossary

> The English of Austen's era (the Regency, around the 1810s) contains many words that have **the same spelling as today but a different meaning**.
> This document catalogues **words that become mistranslations when rendered according to their modern meanings**, helping translators avoid serious errors.
>
> **Risk classification:**
> - **[Danger]** — The period meaning is the opposite of, or entirely different from, the modern meaning. A mistranslation reverses the whole sentence.
> - **[Caution]** — The meanings partly overlap, but the essential nuance differs.
> - **[Reference]** — An obsolete usage that must be understood for a natural translation.

---

## A. [Danger] — Opposite or entirely different from the modern meaning

### 1. sensible
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | (1) Having good sense; judicious / (2) aware of something |
| **Modern English meaning** | Sensitive ← **not the intended meaning** |
| **Example in the novel** | "a sensible, intelligent young woman" (Ch. 5, Charlotte) = a **judicious and intelligent** woman |
| | "I am very sensible of the honour" (Ch. 19, Elizabeth) = I am **fully aware of** the honour |
| **Translation** | “judicious” / “sensible” / “fully aware of” |
| **Mistranslation example** | ~~“a sensitive young woman”~~ → creates an entirely different characterization |

### 2. condescension / condescend
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | A person of high rank **stooping to bestow a favour**. A term of praise. |
| **Modern English meaning** | A patronizing, superior attitude ← **the opposite meaning** |
| **Example in the novel** | "such affability and condescension" (Ch. 14, Collins praising Lady Catherine) = Lady Catherine's **gracious willingness to lower herself** |
| | "She had even condescended to advise him to marry" (Ch. 14) = she had **personally stooped** to advise him to marry |
| **Translation** | “graciously stooped to” / “deigned to” / “personally” |
| **Mistranslation example** | ~~“such arrogance and patronizing behaviour”~~ → turns Collins's flattery into criticism |
| **Caution** | Austen also uses the word **ironically**. Collins means it as sincere praise, while the reader finds it ridiculous; the translation must preserve this duality. |

### 3. nervous / nerves
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | Having **weak nerves** / suffering from a nervous disorder (a medical concept) |
| **Modern English meaning** | Tense, anxious ← a different nuance |
| **Example in the novel** | "You have no compassion on my poor nerves." (Ch. 1, Mrs Bennet) = you have no compassion for my **poor weakened nerves** |
| | "she fancied herself nervous" (Ch. 1, narrator) = she imagined herself to have **weak nerves** |
| **Translation** | “weak nerves” / “nervous disorder” — not merely “anxious” |
| **Key point** | Mrs Bennet's “nerves” are a medical self-diagnosis (in fact an affectation), not simple anxiety. |

### 4. nice
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | Precise, fastidious, capable of subtle discrimination |
| **Modern English meaning** | Pleasant, kind ← almost unrelated |
| **Example in the novel** | Though rarely used directly, "a nice distinction" = a **fine/subtle** distinction |
| **Translation** | “precise” / “fastidious” / “subtle” |

### 5. wanting
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | **Lacking**, deficient |
| **Modern English meaning** | Desiring ← entirely different |
| **Example in the novel** | "No one admitted to the privilege of hearing you can think anything wanting." (Ch. 31, Darcy) = no one who hears you can think **anything lacking** |
| **Translation** | “lacking” / “deficient” |
| **Mistranslation example** | ~~“can think that anything is desired”~~ |

### 6. address (noun)
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | (1) Manner of speaking, bearing, or approach / (2) a postal address / (3) courtship |
| **Modern English meaning** | Predominantly a location or postal address |
| **Example in the novel** | "very pleasing address" (Ch. 15, Wickham) = a very pleasing **manner/address** |
| | "my addresses" (Ch. 20, Collins) = my **courtship/advances** |
| | "Jane had written the direction remarkably ill" (Ch. 46) = Jane had written the **address** very illegibly (here direction = address) |
| **Translation** | According to context: “manner” / “bearing” / “courtship” / “address” |

### 7. direction
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | The **address or destination** written on a letter |
| **Modern English meaning** | Orientation; instruction |
| **Example in the novel** | "the wished-for direction" (Ch. 52, Wickham's address) = the longed-for **address** |
| **Translation** | “address” / “whereabouts” |

## B. [Caution] — A different essential nuance

### 8. handsome
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | (1) A person: good-looking/beautiful, for either sex / (2) an action or sum: **generous, liberal** / (3) a building: imposing |
| **Difference from modern usage** | Modern usage applies it mostly to male appearance |
| **Example in the novel** | "a handsome young man" (Bingley) = good-looking / "handsome modern building" (Rosings) = an **imposing** building / "a very handsome thought" (Collins praising Darcy) = a **fine/generous** thought |
| **Translation** | Person: “handsome/beautiful” / building: “imposing/fine” / action: “fine/generous” |

### 9. civil / civility
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | Polite, but only **minimally courteous**; it need not be sincere |
| **Difference from modern usage** | Similar today, but Austen clearly distinguishes “civil” from “warm/cordial” |
| **Example in the novel** | "with cold civility" (Ch. 46, Elizabeth to Darcy) = with **cold politeness** |
| | "civil disdain" (Ch. 18, Miss Bingley) = **polite disdain** — an intentional oxymoron |
| **Translation** | “civil/polite”; where insincerity matters, “formal” or “perfunctory” |
| **Scale** | civil (minimum) < polite (standard) < courteous < warm < cordial (sincere) |

### 10. consequence
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | **Social importance, standing, or status** |
| **Modern English meaning** | Result, outcome |
| **Example in the novel** | "give consequence to young ladies who are slighted by other men" (Ch. 3, Darcy) = make young ladies whom other men slight **feel important** |
| | "a man of consequence" = a man **of standing/importance** |
| **Translation** | “standing” / “importance” / “status,” according to context |

### 11. intelligence
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | **News, information** |
| **Modern English meaning** | Intellectual ability |
| **Example in the novel** | "the great intelligence" (Ch. 30, Collins announcing Darcy's arrival) = this **momentous news** |
| | "fresher intelligence of her former friends" (Ch. 25) = **more recent news** of her former friends |
| **Translation** | “news” / “information” / “tidings” |

### 12. design
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | **Intention, plan, or scheme** |
| **Modern English meaning** | Visual or functional design |
| **Example in the novel** | "Is that his design in settling here?" (Ch. 1, Mr Bennet) = is that his **intention** in settling here? |
| | "without any design" (Ch. 22, Jane) = without any **scheme/ulterior motive** |
| **Translation** | “intention” / “plan” / “ulterior motive” |

### 13. countenance
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | (1) **Face, complexion, expression** / (2) to support or approve |
| **Modern English meaning** | Face (now uncommon) |
| **Example in the novel** | "a fine countenance" (Wickham) = a fine **face/appearance** |
| | "Bingley could keep his countenance" (Ch. 9) = Bingley could **keep a straight face** |
| **Translation** | “expression” / “complexion” / “appearance” |

### 14. character
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | (1) Personality / (2) **reputation, public estimation** — both senses may operate together |
| **Example in the novel** | "His character was decided." (Ch. 3) = his **reputation was settled** (= he was judged proud) |
| | "Wickham's character" = Wickham's **true nature/reputation** (reversed later in the novel) |
| **Translation** | Distinguish “personality” from “reputation” by context; where both operate, use a term meaning “the kind of man he is” |

### 15. establishment
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | (1) **A settled home through marriage** / (2) an independent basis for life in general |
| **Example in the novel** | "what an establishment it would be for one of them" (Ch. 1) = what a fine **settlement/home** it would provide for one daughter |
| | "the pure and disinterested desire of an establishment" (Ch. 22, Charlotte) = the pure desire to secure **a settled home** |
| **Translation** | “settlement” / “a home of her own” / “an established household” — not “facility” |

### 16. connexion(s) / connection(s)
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | **Relations, family ties, social connections** |
| **Modern English meaning** | Connection in a general sense |
| **Example in the novel** | "low connexions" (Darcy) = **low relations/connections** (= a merchant uncle and an attorney uncle) |
| | "the connexion was a most unhappy one" = that **family connection by marriage** was a very unfortunate one |
| **Translation** | “connections” / “relations” / “family ties” |

### 17. amiable
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | **Lovable, pleasing, good-natured** — stronger praise than modern “amicable” |
| **Example in the novel** | "she is one of the most tractable creatures... a very great favourite" (Ch. 33, Elizabeth's assessment of Georgiana) |
| | "the most amiable man of my acquaintance" (Ch. 24, Jane on Bingley) = the most **lovable** man I know |
| **Translation** | “lovable” / “good-natured” / “pleasing” |

### 18. agreeable
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning in Austen's era** | **A pleasant person; enjoyable company** |
| **Example in the novel** | "If he had been so very agreeable, he would have talked to Mrs. Long" (Ch. 5, Mrs Bennet) = if he had been so **pleasant**, he would have spoken to Mrs Long |
| **Translation** | “pleasant” / “agreeable” / “good company” |
| **Scale** | disagreeable < tolerable < agreeable < amiable |

## C. [Reference] — Obsolete usages

### 19. complaisance / complaisant
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | A **willing, accommodating disposition** intended to please others |
| **Do not confuse with** | “complacency” (self-satisfaction) |
| **Example in the novel** | "his complaisance" (Ch. 6) = his **obliging manner** |

### 20. propriety / impropriety
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | **Proper conduct, decorum, behaviour befitting one's station** — not merely abstract appropriateness, but compliance with social norms |
| **Example in the novel** | "want of propriety" (Darcy's letter criticizing the Bennet family) = a **lack of decorum** |
| **Translation** | “decorum” / “propriety” / “respectability” |

### 21. scheme
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | A **plan**, more neutral than the modern negative sense of “plot” |
| **Example in the novel** | "No scheme could have been more agreeable" (Ch. 27, travel plan) = no **plan** could have been more pleasant |
| **Translation** | “plan” / “arrangement” — not necessarily negative |

### 22. apprehension
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | (1) **Understanding, perception** / (2) anxiety — Austen uses sense (1) more often |
| **Example in the novel** | "I had no apprehension of his feeling a serious attachment" (Ch. 35, Darcy's letter) = I **did not perceive** that he felt a serious attachment |
| **Translation** | “perception” / “understanding”; use “anxiety” only where that sense applies |

### 23. complaint
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | (1) A grievance / (2) **an illness or ailment** |
| **Example in the novel** | "nervous complaints" (Mrs Bennet) = **nervous ailments** |
| **Translation** | Check the context; for Mrs Bennet it always means “ailment” |

### 24. prospect
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | (1) A literal **view or vista** / (2) a future prospect |
| **Example in the novel** | "a charming prospect over that gravel walk" (Ch. 9) = a charming **view** beyond the gravel walk |
| **Translation** | In a building/window context, “view/vista”; in a future context, “prospect/possibility” |

### 25. natural
| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| **Meaning** | (1) Natural / (2) naturalistic (landscaping) / (3) **illegitimate** (rare) / (4) innate |
| **Example in the novel** | "natural beauty" (Pemberley landscaping) = beauty preserving a **naturalistic aesthetic**, not artificial |
| | "a sort of natural self-consequence" (Lydia) = a kind of **innate** self-importance |
| **Translation** | Distinguish by context. Pemberley's “natural” is a term from **naturalistic aesthetics**. |

## D. Scale of evaluative adjectives — Preserve intensity in translation

The adjectives Austen uses to describe people form a **precise scale**:

### Positive scale (weak → strong)
```
tolerable (barely acceptable, so-so) ← lowest — Darcy's assessment of Elizabeth
  ↓
civil (polite) ← the ceremonial minimum
  ↓
agreeable (pleasant; good company)
  ↓
pleasing (likeable)
  ↓
amiable (lovable, good-natured)
  ↓
charming
  ↓
delightful (giving pleasure)
  ↓
excellent ← highest
```

### Negative scale (weak → strong)
```
not very agreeable (not particularly pleasant) ← British understatement
  ↓
disagreeable
  ↓
unpleasant (more direct)
  ↓
ill-natured
  ↓
proud ← applied to Darcy
  ↓
insolent
  ↓
abominable
  ↓
despicable ← strongest — Elizabeth's self-condemnation
```

### Translation principles
- Preserve **the same differences in intensity** in the target language.
- Rendering both “tolerable” and “agreeable” as “fine” destroys the scale.
- Rendering both “civil” and “amiable” as “kind” erases the difference in relational warmth.

## E. British understatement — The most frequent source of mistranslation

Austen uses **double negatives, minimization, and understatement** extremely often. Literal translation erases the nuance.

| Original pattern | Surface meaning | Actual meaning | Translation guidance |
|---|---|---|---|
| "not unpleasing" | not displeasing | **fairly pleasant** | Resolve the double negative but keep the force mild: “not bad” |
| "not without" | not lacking | **definitely present** | “there certainly was...” |
| "she could not help" | could not refrain from | **she did it** (strong impulse) | Preserve the negative construction where natural: “could not help...” |
| "I dare say" | I venture to say | **probably so** (light conjecture) | “probably” / “I suppose” |
| "I am sure" | certainly | **I think** (often a habitual flourish, not certainty) | In Mrs Bennet's speech, use emphatic but groundless certainty: “I am certain...” |
| "something like regret" | something resembling regret | regret she is **reluctant to acknowledge** | Keep “something like regret”; do not state flatly that it is regret |
| "not entirely hopeless" | not completely without hope | **there is a little hope** | “not entirely without hope” |
| "I fancy" | I imagine | **it seems to me** (light conjecture) | “I think” / “it seems” |
| "by no means" | by no method | **absolutely not** (strong negation) | “by no means” / “certainly not” |
| "tolerable/tolerably" | bearable | **only just acceptable** (not praise) | “so-so” / “passable” |

## F. Period nuances of verbs

| Verb | Meaning in Austen's era | Modern meaning | Example in the novel | Translation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **to own** | To acknowledge, admit | To possess | "I own I was surprised" = I **admit** I was surprised | “admit” / “acknowledge” |
| **to engage** | (1) to promise or arrange (2) to employ (3) to occupy the attention | Primarily to become betrothed | "she was engaged elsewhere" = she had **another engagement** | Check context |
| **to want** | To **lack** / need | To desire | "want of propriety" = **lack** of decorum | “lack” / “absence” |
| **to wait on** | To **visit formally** | To wait / serve | "to wait on Mr. Bingley" = to **call on** Mr Bingley | “call on” / “pay one's respects to” |
| **to make out** | To **understand, comprehend** | To discern / kiss passionately | "I cannot make him out" = I **cannot understand him** | “understand” / “make sense of” |
| **to recollect** | To **call consciously to mind** | Now largely synonymous with remember | In Austen, it carries the nuance of deliberately recalling | “recollect” / “call to mind” |
| **to flatter oneself** | To **pride oneself** (often mistakenly) | To compliment oneself | "I flatter myself that..." = **I venture to pride myself that...** | “pride oneself” — often ironic |
| **to trifle with** | To **play with; treat lightly** | — | "You are too generous to trifle with me" = you are not base enough to **play with my feelings** | “play with” / “toy with” |

## G. Pairs that must never be confused in translation

| Pair | Difference | Result of confusion |
|---|---|---|
| **pride** vs **vanity** | pride = one's opinion of oneself; vanity = what one wishes others to think (Mary's definition, Ch. 5) | Destroys a central distinction in the novel's title and theme |
| **sense** vs **sensibility** | sense = reason/judgment; sensibility = emotional sensitivity/feeling | Erases the contrast between Jane (sensibility) and Elizabeth (sense) |
| **civil** vs **polite** vs **amiable** | civil = minimum courtesy; polite = standard courtesy; amiable = sincere liking | Erases differences in relational warmth |
| **fortune** vs **income** | fortune = total capital; income = annual revenue | Misreads economic scale: Bingley's £100,000 versus £4,000–5,000 a year |
| **trade** vs **profession** | trade = commerce (lower status); profession = learned profession (higher status) | Erases a subtle distinction in class prejudice |
| **living** vs **life** | living = clerical benefice/parish income; life = existence | Reverses the heart of the Darcy–Wickham conflict |
| **interesting** | In Austen: engaging or moving, stronger than modern casual usage | Translating “most interesting” as merely “fun” weakens the emotional force |
