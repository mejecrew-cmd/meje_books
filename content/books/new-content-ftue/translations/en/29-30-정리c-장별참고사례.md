# 30. Summary C: Chapter-by-Chapter Reference Cases

*This Summary C is what each chapter in the main text refers to as “Appendix D.” Whenever the main text directs you to “Appendix D,” come here.*

> Of the reference content for each chapter, only the few core examples directly tied to that chapter’s argument remain beside the main text; the rest are collected here by chapter. Each entry has been refined and ends with “What to examine,” so it can be read alongside the chapter’s point. The organizational note at the start of each chapter explains the categories and how to read them. When the same work appears in several chapters, it remains in each because the focus differs.

## Chapter 1. Games Aren’t Cool

These examples extend Chapter 1’s argument beyond games: familiarity blinds users and creators alike, and the contest is decided by the first judgment, before the first input. They are grouped into five areas—video and board games; film and content; publishing, comics, and music; mobile apps and services; and offline life. Read each example beside the four mechanisms from the main text—the expert’s curse, invisible design, New Content relativity, and the curse of genre—and the conclusion of “designing the first judgment.” Ask which mechanism each case demonstrates.

**Video and Board Games**

- Portal: Valve’s 2007 first-person puzzle game makes the opening sequence of test chambers the tutorial itself. Players internalize the rules through play rather than coercion, and even the guiding voice, GLaDOS, is integrated into the world. What to examine: when the teaching section is indistinguishable from the main game, each chamber shows how the user’s first judgment begins with “fun,” not “study.”
- Dark Souls / Getting Over It: Dark Souls (2011) confronts the player with its first demon boss while poorly equipped, then lets them escape through a door on the left, obtain a proper weapon, and return. Getting Over It (2017) makes a fall that can erase all progress after a single mistake central to its identity. Both deliberately break the familiar expectation of a “friendly tutorial.” What to examine: alongside the main text’s scope condition—that unfriendliness becomes an admission ticket rather than a flaw when targeting core users—observe which users read this first experience as a challenge.
- Mega Man (Rockman), Cut Man stage: In the first Mega Man (1987), the cursor on the stage-select screen defaults to this introductory area. Enemies in narrow passages and the placement of ladders let players practice movement, jumping, and shooting without coercion. What to examine: the invisible tutorial that teaches through enemies and terrain instead of instructions, and how one default on a selection screen predetermines the path of the first experience.
- The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild, Great Plateau: The 2017 game turns its starting region into a miniature version of the full game. Players must gain four essential abilities from four shrines to receive the paraglider and leave, but the game tells them only where to go, not how to get there. What to examine: the devices that keep a closed starting zone from feeling like a compulsory training camp, and how freedom of route coexists with clarity of purpose.
- Half-Life 2, Ravenholm: Shortly after players acquire the gravity gun, this area fills the stage with explosive barrels, saw blades, and zombies. Their hands learn to throw the environment as a weapon without explanation. What to examine: when a new mechanic is taught through stage arrangement rather than text, how every prop serves as a practice problem.
- Gloomhaven: Jaws of the Lion / Pandemic’s introductory design: Jaws of the Lion, a companion game to Gloomhaven, turns its first five scenarios into lessons that open the rules gradually. Rather than making players finish the rulebook, it starts the first session immediately and teaches through play. Pandemic lowers the burden of the first session with an introductory setup that uses fewer cards to determine difficulty. What to examine: the same prescription works in board games—reduce explanation in the rulebook and reorder the experience to bring the first session forward.
- Arcade attract mode (demo screen): While no coin is inserted, the machine repeatedly shows a play demonstration and high-score table to stop passersby. What to examine: an ancestor of the “playable ad,” placing a first judgment through display alone before interaction begins.
- Steam Next Fest: Steam’s recurring event gathers demos of unreleased games and makes them free to play for several days. Developers can observe reactions to the first few minutes and wishlist trends before release. What to examine: when the first judgment moves ahead of the purchase, the demo’s opening screen effectively becomes the store page, and the demo’s first five minutes are graded before the main game.

**Film and Content**

- Up, the “Married Life” opening: The 2009 film compresses Carl and Ellie’s life together into roughly four and a half minutes of music and montage without a line of dialogue, capturing the audience’s emotions in the opening alone. What to examine: a model of first-impression design that makes viewers feel without explanation, placing emotion before information.
- WALL·E’s opening: The 2008 film proceeds for roughly its first twenty minutes almost without dialogue, like a silent film, conveying both the world of a devastated Earth and the protagonist’s character through movement, sound, and direction. What to examine: how far first-impression design can go by removing explanation and simply showing, and what fills the resulting space without boredom.
- Raiders of the Lost Ark (Indiana Jones), cold open: The 1981 film imprints its protagonist through action alone—crossing temple traps, escaping a rolling boulder, and losing the artifact to his rival Belloq—without explaining who he is. What to examine: how action without explanation designs the first judgment, and how even failure becomes part of a character’s appeal.
- Hollywood’s “first ten minutes rule” and cold-open convention: The industry belief that a film is lost if its opening does not capture the audience is so established that screenwriting manuals teach the placement of events in the first ten minutes separately. What to examine: an entire industry sharing a deadline for the first judgment. Only the length of the deadline differs from the design of a game’s first session; the structure is the same.
- Television cold opens (Breaking Bad and others): A pre-title scene draws viewers in before they can change the channel. Breaking Bad (2008) expanded such openings into independent short stories, as with the crashed RV in the desert and Walter standing in his underwear. What to examine: when the opening scene favors the hook over information delivery, what it deliberately leaves unexplained.
- Netflix autoplay and Skip Intro: Autoplaying the next episode and the Skip Intro button have solidified, through interface design, a viewing habit that treats waiting as taboo. What to examine: the process by which one service’s convenience feature becomes a new baseline every other piece of content must pass—New Content relativity taking shape.
- Netflix title-thumbnail personalization: Netflix has described in its official technology blog a system that maintains several thumbnails for the same title and selects different images according to viewing history, because a single first-impression image can determine the decision to watch. What to examine: personalization of the first judgment, presenting a different first screen for the same content according to the viewer.
- YouTube ads and the five-second skip: As ads skippable after five seconds became standard, advertising grammar itself reportedly reorganized around placing the brand and hook within the first five seconds. What to examine: the moment viewers gain the right to leave, the design of the first seconds changes the grammar of an entire genre.

**Publishing, Comics, and Music**

- The opening-line “hook” in fiction—for example, Moby-Dick’s “Call me Ishmael”: The craft tradition of capturing readers in the first line is so long that famous opening sentences are discussed as a category of their own. What to examine: readers close a book for different reasons at each stage from cover to opening line and from opening line to first chapter. Follow that far and the example meets Chapter 3’s funnel reading.
- Pop hooks and shortened intros: Designing the first seconds to catch the ear is an old craft, but intros are said to have grown shorter and choruses to have moved forward in the age of streaming and short-form video. What to examine: the causal chain by which a medium’s counting method—the standard for registering a stream—changes the design of the content’s opening.
- Weekly Shonen Jump-style reader surveys: Readers submit their favorite series each week; rankings influence both placement within the magazine and whether serialization continues, and low-ranked works are often canceled. Former editors have said that discontinuation may be considered after as little as three weeks. What to examine: in a structure where the response to the first installment determines survival, what creators pour into Episode 1 reveals the full weight of a first impression.
- Free first webtoon episode + vertical scrolling: Making the first episode free reduces entry cost to zero, while familiar vertical scrolling eliminates the need to learn how to read it. What to examine: the design borrows familiarity with price (“free”) and operation (“scroll”) at once, reducing friction in the first judgment from both sides.
- Web novels, “Episode 1 abandonment,” and fast openings (regression, possession, reincarnation, cathartic payoff): Capturing readers in the opening of the first episode is treated as standard craft. Familiar premises such as returning to the past, inhabiting another body, or reincarnation let the story skip explanation and bring gratifying developments forward. What to examine: the compression of an opening by borrowing the reader’s prior experience—genre grammar—which leads directly to Chapter 5’s discussion of borrowing.

**Mobile, Apps, and Services**

- TikTok’s “Continue as guest”: The app shows videos first and requests login only when interaction requires it, moving the cost of entry back and the first enjoyment forward. (For Duolingo’s delayed registration, see the Chapter 2 references.) What to examine: merely changing the order of entry steps can turn the first judgment from “a hassle” into “fun.”
- Ad pretesting for hypercasual games: In the mobile hypercasual industry, developers are said to produce several ad videos before completing a game, measure the cost per install, and develop only concepts that receive a strong response into full products. What to examine: an extreme case in which the first judgment—one image in an ad—is graded before the product, showing that the design of the comparison set discussed in the main text has already hardened into an industry process.

**Offline and Everyday Life**

- A play’s opening scene: The first image and first sound are carefully constructed to command the audience the moment the curtain rises. What to examine: even a medium with neither Skip nor Pause treats its first moment as decisive. A first impression determines the attitude of even an audience that cannot escape.
- A new employee’s first day of onboarding: In human resources, the welcome on the first day and the readiness of equipment and workspace are widely understood to affect retention and engagement. What to examine: organizations design first experiences too, and small arrangements such as who shares lunch with the new employee shape the first judgment.
- Apple unboxing (OOBE): It is often said in the industry that even the air gap is designed so the lid descends slowly under controlled pressure. What to examine: design that attends to the first impression of “the moment of opening” before the product itself, moving the start of the first experience ahead of the product screen.
- “Plug and play” appliances: A design that works as soon as it is connected and removes the need to read a manual brings invisible design into everyday life. What to examine: recall an appliance you began using without instructions and verify the paradox that the better something works, the less anyone notices.
- The IKEA effect: People value furniture they assemble themselves more highly than equivalent finished products; in one experiment, participants assigned a higher price to objects they had built. What to examine: a user’s first act of touching can lead to attachment, supporting the shift of a first screen from “showing” to “letting them touch.”
- The 0.1-second first-impression experiment: In a 2006 study by psychologists Janine Willis and Alexander Todorov, people judged qualities such as trustworthiness and attractiveness after seeing an unfamiliar face for only 0.1 seconds, and longer exposure changed those judgments very little. What to examine: if the first judgment is closer to a reflex than deliberation, the first screen should be designed not as an argument but as an impression legible at a glance.
- Perfume and cosmetics on a department store’s ground floor: Placing perfumes and cosmetics on the entrance floor is a long-standing industry convention. Their scent and brightness are said to define the impression of the entire store at the moment a shopper enters. What to examine: spaces have first screens too, and deciding what occupies the first point of contact is a design problem in every industry.

## Chapter 2. FTUE Is Not a Tutorial

These examples show that the six terms separated in Chapter 2 appear in the same arrangement outside games: within the whole called UX, FTUE—the first encounter—and NUX—adoption—are distinct, while OOBE, tutorials, and onboarding are smaller stages and formats within them. The cases are grouped into video games; film and content; board games and play; mobile apps and services; and offline life. For each, pair it with the three units in the main text—device setup, first play, and one month later—and ask where “making it usable,” “recognizing what it is,” and “turning it into a habit” divide.

**Video Games**

- Portal: The opening test chambers are both tutorial and part of the main game, so the control-learning segment and core-fun segment flow together without a boundary. What to examine: an extreme case that erases the distinction that a tutorial is merely one format within FTUE by uniting the format and the experience in one body.
- Half-Life 2, Black Mesa East: Alyx has the player play catch with the robot Dog, passing a disabled Combine Rollermine back and forth. Through play, the hands learn the gravity gun’s pull and push without arrows. What to examine: implicit learning in which the tutorial is absorbed into play with a character rather than presented as separate instructions, revealing the division between explicit and implicit learning.
- Animal Crossing: In early Animal Crossing games, shortly after moving into town, Tom Nook uses the player’s debt as a reason to put them to work in his shop. Free play opens only after errands such as changing clothes, planting saplings, and greeting neighbors are complete. What to examine: the compulsory tutorial that reveals the product’s identity and the adoption habit of returning daily to see the town are clearly different stages—the boundary between tutorial and NUX.
- Overwatch Practice Range: A practice range and modes against AI are separated from main matchmaking, placing the stage for testing hero abilities apart from the first real match. What to examine: having a teaching format—the practice range—does not take responsibility for the first live experience. What happens in the first Quick Play match is the remaining half of FTUE.

**Film and Content**

- Breaking Bad, Episode 1 cold open: Without explanation, the episode first throws out the image of Walter driving an RV through the desert in a gas mask and underwear while sirens approach. The series’ cold opens are often cited for emphasizing the hook over information delivery. What to examine: capturing the audience with a first impression and transmitting the world and its rules are separate designs—first impression and tutorial-like explanation are different work.
- Star Wars: Episode IV opening: The 1977 film gives only minimal context in a few lines of opening crawl, then drops the audience into the middle of an ongoing galactic civil war and lets the unfolding story fill in the rest. What to examine: explaining all the background and creating immediate immersion are different tasks; the first immersion can work even when explanation is postponed.
- A series pilot episode: Introducing the world, rules, and characters in Episode 1 and making viewers return every week are designed separately. What to examine: the design of the first encounter—the FTUE equivalent—and the design of adoption—the NUX equivalent—are handled with different techniques inside the same work.
- A novel’s prologue and Chapter 1: The section that lays out the background is distinct from the section that draws readers into the story. What to examine: add the common belief that many readers skip prologues, and the cost of placing an explanatory section first becomes visible.

**Board Games and Play**

- Gloomhaven: Jaws of the Lion / Pandemic introductory design: Studying the rulebook is only one format; designing a first session that remains enjoyable to the end is broader. What to examine: the distance between “the rules were taught” and “the first game was fun” has the same shape as the distance between tutorial completion rate and D1.
- “Learning game” rule variants: In a first session, scores may deliberately be ignored or the rules reduced, separating the educational segment from the fun of the full game. What to examine: setting aside one teaching session is the same design choice as placing explicit learning outside the main game.
- TRPG “Session Zero”: A customary meeting before the main adventure where participants align expectations, rules, and boundaries. What to examine: teaching the first input and agreeing on the entire first experience are different tasks; aligning expectations matters enough to become a separate stage.

**Mobile, Apps, and Services**

- The iPhone’s first-power-on “Hello” screen: It begins with a single greeting shown in several languages and leads users into language, account, and permission settings without a dense wall of instructions. What to examine: the OOBE segment that makes the device usable sits separately before the main experience, and designing that entry to be short and frictionless is work of its own.
- A new smartphone’s setup wizard: The OOBE segment for language, account, and permissions sits before the segment in which apps are enjoyed. What to examine: the length and blockages of this segment cause abandonment independently of the first fun, so OOBE should be evaluated by friction removal rather than fun.
- Apps that defer permission requests until needed: Instead of bundling notification and location requests at first entry, these apps ask for each permission at the moment it becomes necessary. What to examine: an OOBE-splitting design that shortens the entry line and reduces the distance to the first fun; “when to ask” matters as much as “what to ask.”
- Slackbot’s first guidance in Slack: New users are said to complete first steps such as profile setup through a conversation with Slackbot, performing the product’s core action—exchanging messages—inside the guidance itself. What to examine: when OOBE-like setup occurs within the product’s core grammar, teaching and first use overlap in a single action.
- Netflix profile creation and preference selection: Immediately after joining, users are reportedly asked to create a profile and select several titles they like before the main activity of viewing, providing material for the first recommendation screen. What to examine: making the service usable and turning binge-watching into a habit are separate designs in services too.

**Offline and Everyday Life**

- A pre-show announcement and the rising curtain: Operational guidance about seats and emergency exits—the OOBE equivalent—differs in character from the play’s first scene. What to examine: even within the same time and place, the tone and purpose of the two segments are completely different; the first experience begins only after the guidance ends.
- Driving school theory and road practice: Learning rules and developing embodied driving skill are divided into different stages. What to examine: a perfect theory score does not guarantee competence on the road, just as tutorial completion does not guarantee the first fun.
- Unboxing and installing an appliance—OOBE—and first use: Making something ready to use and using it well are separate. What to examine: recall the distance between the day the installer left and the day the appliance became part of daily life, and the difference between OOBE and adoption becomes tangible.
- Receiving the car key and starting the engine for the first time—the delivery procedure: The delivery stage that makes the car operable is distinct from the stage in which driving becomes embodied. What to examine: no matter how helpful the delivery procedure, the tension of the first drive remains; the two stages cannot be solved at once.
- IKEA assembly instructions: Stick figures, arrows, and symbols show the assembly sequence without words, asking for no language knowledge and allowing a first-time viewer to follow. What to examine: like Mario 1-1, this is a model of guidance that teaches through universal intuition, using arrangement and images rather than explicit prose to elicit the next action.
- A gym orientation: Equipment instructions and an InBody measurement immediately after registration merely make the facility usable. Whether that member returns the following week remains a separate issue. What to examine: the chapter’s D1 argument repeats offline—tutorial completion, or finishing orientation, does not guarantee adoption, or regular attendance.

## Chapter 3. What Does “First” Mean?

This collection checks the tool presented in Chapter 3—the funnel reading that divides “first” into eleven thresholds from T0, first word of mouth, through T10, first return, and identifies the stage where people leak—against cases from other media. It is grouped into video games and platforms, video and content, publishing/comics/music, mobile/apps/services, and offline/everyday life. For each case, pair the following questions with the chapter’s discussions of the funnel, time to value, and threshold rearrangement: Which threshold is operating here? What action constitutes the aha moment? What device rearranges the order of the thresholds?

**Video Games / Platforms**

- Outer Wilds: in this 2019 game, “understanding” accumulates instead of stats, and the first reward arrives as an insight when scattered clues suddenly lock together. What to examine: an aha moment can be a cognitive event rather than a system reward such as a level or item, along with the cost that such a moment is difficult to bring forward.
- Minecraft’s first night: while making the first pickaxe and building the first shelter before sunset, the user reaches the realization, “So this is what kind of game it is.” What to examine: the time pressure of day and night creates the first session’s objective on its own, allowing the system to manage time to value.
- Stardew Valley’s first harvest: planting seeds, waiting several in-game days, and finally harvesting the first crop remains a clear first achievement. What to examine: a case where the aha moment is designed to arrive several days later rather than within the first session, and the small rewards that sustain the user in between.
- It Takes Two: this 2021 two-player-only cooperative game reveals its “core value” only when the pair solves their first cooperative puzzle together. What to examine: a game whose aha moment requires another person adds the threshold of recruiting a partner to first entry (T3).
- Steam’s two-hour refund policy: Steam officially offers refunds regardless of reason when playtime is under two hours and the purchase was made within fourteen days, turning the first two hours after purchase into a de facto evaluation period. What to examine: if the first session is also a refund review, time to value becomes a variable directly tied to revenue.

**Video / Content**

- The *Save the Cat!* method: the screenwriting guide *Save the Cat!* specifies the structure of the first ten minutes by page count, establishing tone with an opening image on page one and introducing the catalyst around page twelve. What to examine: an approach to craft that manages the opening phase through coordinates—what happens on which page—rather than intuition, much like the eleven thresholds.
- Episode-one drop-off curves in streaming: a funnel in which viewers leave from episode to episode, where the key question is which episode loses the most people. What to examine: saying “At which episode does it break?” rather than “Viewership is low” reveals what to repair, using the same language as threshold-level diagnosis.
- Cold opens (*Breaking Bad*, *Lost*): a teaser that drops viewers into the middle of an event before the opening credits, binding them to the story before they change channels. What to examine: a rearrangement that postpones the explanatory section wholesale to accelerate first recognition (T4).
- Review embargoes: the film and game industries contractually control when reviews may be published, managing the time when first word of mouth (T0) is released. It is also widely observed that an embargo lasting until just before release can itself be read as a bad sign. What to examine: the pre-game phase (T0–T2) is a managed design target rather than luck, along with the backfire in which control becomes a negative signal.

**Publishing / Comics / Music**

- The hook of a novel’s first sentence and Chapter 1 drop-off: the point where a reader closes a book may be the cover, the first line, or the first chapter, and each stage has a different cause. What to examine: drop-off always occurs stage by stage regardless of medium, so prescriptions must also differ by stage.
- *Weekly Shōnen Jump* reader surveys and early cancellation: readers vote each week for the works they liked, and serials rise or fall on those rankings; reportedly, in some cases editors may assess cancellation as early as three weeks after a series debuts. What to examine: how a market in which opening-phase results immediately decide survival solidifies a structure that concentrates resources in the first installment.
- The web-novel first-episode “golden time” and cathartic opening: because readers may leave immediately if Episode 1 ends in frustration, standard practice is to preserve continued-reading rates by including satisfying developments and a small complete arc within the opening episodes. What to examine: the publishing version of shortening time to value by pulling first reward (T8) into Episode 1.
- Streaming’s thirty-second rule and shortened intros: once a play had to pass thirty seconds to count toward streams and royalties, intros reportedly shrank from around twenty seconds in the 1980s to roughly five, while more songs moved the chorus forward. What to examine: a measurement standard that changed the form of content, demonstrating that what we measure determines what we make.

**Mobile / Apps / Services**

- Playable ads: an advertising format that lets users directly control a miniature stage before installation, pulling first action (T5) and a taste of first reward (T8) ahead of installation (T3). What to examine: the chapter’s final argument—that thresholds are not a fixed ladder but design variables that can be rearranged—in its clearest form.

**Offline / Everyday Life**

- Sales pipeline stages: conversions are tracked stage by stage from lead to contract to identify where the largest drop occurs. What to examine: funnel thinking was a basic practice of sales management even before digital products, and only by dividing the stages can responsibility and prescriptions be separated.
- Test-driving a car: a procedure that puts the first action—the steering wheel—before the major decision to purchase. Dealerships standardize even the test-drive route and accompanying guidance to manage the first few minutes of impression. What to examine: in high-priced products, the same threshold rearrangement used in supermarket sampling becomes a process complete with route design.
- Apartment model homes: the convention of building a full-scale sample unit of a home that does not yet exist, bringing the first impression and expectation (T1–T2) of an unrealized product forward into a physical experience. What to examine: the device that creates expectation (T2) also becomes a promise, and a mismatch with the actual move-in experience can return as a dispute.
- The IKEA effect: experiments have reported that people assign greater attachment and value to objects they assembled themselves. What to examine: the achievement of first reward (T8) grows when users complete the first result with their own hands, so automatic progress that completes it for them can instead diminish the reward.

## Chapter 4. What Is Experience?

This collection examines Chapter 4’s two tools across several media: the signifier-signified checkpoint separating signs (experience imitations) from events (experiences), and the layered reading in which experience accumulates across the world, system, sensory, social, and business layers that collide with one another. It is grouped into video games; video, music, and stage; mobile, apps, and services; and offline and everyday life. For each case, pair the chapter’s checkpoint questions with these: What is the sign here, and is there an event behind it? If there is a collision, which layers are colliding?

**Video Games**

- Visual overload in Diablo IV boss fights: players have repeatedly pointed out situations where particles from their own and allied skills cover the screen, hiding the warning signs for boss attacks that actually need to be avoided. What to examine: a collision in which the sensory layer covers and breaks the system layer—the moment spectacle devours information.
- Half-Life’s opening without cutscenes: the story is told through the structure of corridors and scripted direction while the player almost never loses first-person control. What to examine: a case that avoids the collision itself by dissolving system-layer guidance into the world layer, and the conditions under which guidance does not break immersion.
- Bot matching in mobile competitive games: some mobile competitive games are said to fill early matches with bots disguised as people to grant winning streaks. The sign says “competition,” but the event of facing a real opponent is absent. What to examine: the deeper betrayal of forging the event rather than merely copying the sign, because the moment the deception is exposed, all prior victories collapse retroactively.
- Anonymous cooperation in Journey: the 2012 game Journey lets strangers travel together silently without usernames, chat, or scores. Even after removing almost every social sign, the event of walking together leaves a powerful emotion. What to examine: the exact opposite proof of the empty heart—experience exists when the event exists, even without a signifier.

**Video / Music / Stage**

- Inside Out: the 2015 film depicts Joy and Sadness as characters, while tying the control-console buttons in the mind to actual changes in Riley’s behavior and emotions according to who presses them. What to examine: a model of personification in which the sign of abstract emotion is clearly paired with the event—Riley’s change—and signifier and signified engage one another.
- Fake newspapers and news screens in films: props that look like real information but point to no event, leaving a sign without substance. What to examine: a passing prop can survive on the sign alone, along with the limit exposed when the audience pauses to inspect it.
- The radio transition in Once Upon a Time in Hollywood: music playing like a background score is revealed as sound inside the world the moment a character switches off the car radio. What to examine: the same music belongs to a different layer depending on whether it is sound inside the world or direction outside it, and direction can move across layer boundaries.
- Brecht’s alienation effect in epic theater: scene-title placards, exposed stage lighting, and direct address deliberately remind the audience “This is a play” and break immersion. What to examine: the same structure as a collision where the system layer breaks the world layer’s immersion, turned deliberately into a weapon.
- Chekhov’s gun: the dramatic principle that a gun hanging on the wall in Act One must eventually be fired says that a sign placed on stage must be repaid by an actual event. What to examine: a classical rule of the signifier-signified checkpoint—prepare the event behind the sign—and an invitation to ask the same question of every UI element.
- The Milli Vanilli scandal: the sign of people singing was perfect, but the actual singers were elsewhere. After a recording skipped during a performance exposed the split, their Grammy became the first ever revoked. What to examine: the scale of betrayal that returns when a split between sign and event is revealed, as a model of collapsed trust.
- Canned laughter in sitcoms: laying down a sign of amusement feels empty when there is no genuinely funny event, a point reflected in the declining use of audience-laughter tracks in comedy. What to examine: a sign alone does not create emotion, and audiences detect it first when the sign tries to replace the event.
- Frankenbiting in reality shows: fragments spoken at different times are spliced together to manufacture a statement that was never made, squeezing out a sign of authenticity. Trust collapses when the event is revealed as assembled. What to examine: the point where fabricating events through editing turns the sign-event checkpoint into an ethical issue.
- The MacGuffin: a device placed as a sign that drives the story even though its substance may remain empty. What to examine: a rare exception in which a mismatch between sign and event still works, along with the condition that makes it possible—the object sits where nobody inspects its substance.

**Mobile / Apps / Services**

- An app where a progress bar fills completely and nothing happens: without a reward event behind the sign of completion, the bar becomes an empty heart. What to examine: expectations accumulated while the bar fills default at the last segment; a promise created by a sign must be repaid with an event.
- Research on the labor illusion: an experiment on travel-search sites reportedly found greater satisfaction when people were made to wait while seeing which airlines were being searched than when they received results instantly. What to examine: a sign showing that an event is genuinely happening increases perceived value—the amplification produced when signifier and signified form a pair.

**Offline / Everyday Life**

- Active sound design in cars: synthesized engine sound, calculated from engine speed and throttle input, is played through cabin speakers to create a sign of power. Some drivers deliberately turn its intensity down to hear the actual exhaust. What to examine: when sign and actual output diverge, users begin to doubt the sign, so sensory-layer direction also needs inspection.
- The heavy tuning of a car-door closing sound: designers exploit the perception that a low, heavy sound signals luxury, constructing a sensory sign of solidity that is known to shape the quality felt during a first test drive. What to examine: when sign and actual quality pair, trust remains; when only the sound is good and quality diverges, the asymmetry eventually returns as betrayal.
- Placebo buttons: reports have said that many pedestrian crossing buttons in New York remain disconnected from signal systems, while a substantial number of elevator Close buttons do not operate for ordinary users because of regulations. Pressing supplies only a sign of control. What to examine: weigh the utility of a sign that reduces anxiety against the trust lost when its emptiness is exposed, and consider the ethics of the signifier.
- Open kitchens in restaurants: a layout that exposes cooking to customers. Research is known to have found that when customers and cooks can see each other, food ratings and satisfaction both rise. What to examine: the offline power of a sign paired with an event—the experience of the same food changes merely because the event, cooking, is made visible.

## Chapter 5. Experience Builds on Experience: Prior Analogous Experience

This collection examines Chapter 5’s tool across several media: dividing the prior analogous experiences users bring from other media into expectations, what to borrow, prices, and taboos, then checking whether we can keep the promises attached to borrowed shapes. It is grouped into video games; mobile, apps, and services; video, comics, and music; and offline and everyday life. Pair every case with the chapter’s borrowing checkpoint—borrow unchanged, borrow the shape only, or discard—and ask: Where did this familiarity come from? What does it promise? What betrayal follows if we break it?

**Video Games**

- Standard FPS controls in Doom (2016): the game accepts the WASD and mouse-look habits of people who have played other first-person shooters, erasing control explanations. What to examine: the basic form of borrowing, which reduces explanation cost to zero by retaining a genre standard, along with the limit that it erases nothing for someone who does not know that standard.
- Spec Ops: The Line: this 2012 game borrows the familiar grammar of an ordinary military shooter to establish expectations, then betrays them head-on in the white-phosphorus sequence to create shock. What to examine: borrowing as a tool of direction beyond saving explanation, where the shock of betrayal is proportional to the scale of the borrowed familiarity.
- Circle-to-confirm and X-to-cancel on Japanese PlayStation systems: this platform standard ran opposite to Western editions. When the PS5 standardized X-to-confirm even in Japan, the most deeply trained habits stumbled hardest. What to examine: habits established by a platform remain in the body regardless of the user’s will, making the moment a standard changes the most dangerous moment.
- Virtual joysticks in mobile games: a control method that draws a console controller’s stick on a touchscreen. It is free familiarity to someone who has used a controller, but a foreign language requiring separate study for someone who has never held one. What to examine: the same borrowing can be an asset or a burden depending on point of origin, so first ask who is the subject of “familiar.”

**Mobile / Apps / Services**

- Right swipe in a dating app: a gesture popularized by one app acquired the meaning “like, select,” allowing shopping, job-search, and language-learning apps to borrow the promise unchanged. What to examine: how quickly a gesture made by one product becomes an industry lingua franca, and how its original context—intuitive like-or-dislike judgment—is borrowed with it.
- Pull to refresh: a gesture often said to have spread from an early Twitter app propagated across apps and became a standard, turning into screen grammar everyone knows without remembering being taught. What to examine: the chapter’s rental metaphor—that most screen grammar was taught by somebody else—through one of the most deeply ingrained gestures.
- Skeuomorphism in early iOS: the Notes app’s yellow legal pad, iBooks’ wooden bookshelf, and Game Center’s green felt borrowed the forms of physical objects so people who had used them could immediately read the screen. What to examine: borrowing can originate from real-world objects rather than another app, and after everyone became familiar with screens, the borrowing aged into kitsch and was discarded.
- The floppy-disk Save icon: floppy disks disappeared long ago, yet the Save button keeps their shape. Even generations that have never seen the object inherit only the promise “this shape means Save.” What to examine: signs can outlive their original objects, and prior analogous experience accumulates through screen experience as well as physical experience.
- The hamburger menu: the three-line menu icon is standard among makers, but analyses have found that hiding a menu inside it lowers discovery and use among ordinary users. What to examine: the app-design version of the genre curse—the assumption that a promise shared only among makers is common knowledge for everyone.

**Video / Comics / Music**

- Tournament arcs in boys’ comics: the single-elimination structure moves through increasingly powerful opponents while displaying new characters and growth, so readers can predict the next development merely by seeing the bracket. What to examine: a structure that reduces explanation by borrowing the script in the reader’s mind—the predicted next scene—while the moment that prediction is slightly twisted produces the greatest fun.
- The promised opening of regression and villainess-transmigration stories: the protagonist uses foreknowledge of the original plot to avoid catastrophe, so readers begin the first scene with the script “The protagonist knows the future and will change fate.” What to examine: genre grammar becoming a bundle of prior analogous experiences that reduces the explanatory cost of Episode 1 almost to zero.
- Jeff Buckley’s “Hallelujah”: borrowing Leonard Cohen’s 1984 song, Buckley changed the verse selection and arrangement to create an interpretation evaluated alongside the original. What to examine: the balance of what is borrowed—the song—and what is newly given—the interpretation—at the boundary where borrowing a shape while changing its interior becomes creation rather than imitation.
- Genre conventions—the promised scenes of Westerns and film noir: they borrow audience expectations to reduce explanation, and deliberately diverging from them creates freshness. What to examine: conventions remain assets even at the moment they are broken, with the condition that the freshness works only for audiences who know the convention.
- The opening sequence after a cold open: viewers know in advance the order in which a strong first scene is followed by the title and opening, and prepare for the next scene according to that script. What to examine: when users already know what comes next, meeting the prediction becomes comfort in itself.
- The opening montage in Pixar’s Up: compressing a couple’s life into a little over four nearly wordless minutes creates in advance the emotional foundation through which the audience reads every action in the main story. What to examine: a work making its own prior experience for the main story to rely on—if there is no experience to borrow, it can be created within the opening minutes.
- Scream: Wes Craven’s 1996 horror film assumes the audience’s genre knowledge so thoroughly that its characters recite horror-movie rules aloud, then twists those expectations from the opening scene to create shock. What to examine: borrowed conventions remain assets even when twisted, along with the limit that the twist does not work for an audience unfamiliar with them.

**Offline / Everyday Life**

- Build-A-Bear Workshop: customers stuff a toy themselves, dress it, and even make a birth certificate, drawing out greater affection for something made with their own hands. What to examine: the construction of borrowing two experiences customers already know—playing with dolls and adoption paperwork—and weaving them into a single store journey.
- Dice, pieces, and moving across spaces: borrowing the basic grammar of board games that everyone knows lets players focus on one new rule. What to examine: the chapter’s principle “three familiar, one unfamiliar” as a long-standing fundamental of board-game design.
- Power symbols, Play triangles, and two-line Pause icons: promises that work across devices and can be read immediately even on an unfamiliar appliance. What to examine: symbols standardized across media and devices are the cheapest borrowing assets, so using one for another meaning is the most expensive mistake.
- Everyday conventions such as hot water on the left and cold on the right: violating a bodily ingrained promise makes even an everyday object confusing. What to examine: no matter how minor the violation, the user pays the cost each time, so the price of breaking convention is billed repeatedly.
- The QWERTY keyboard: a layout solidified in the typewriter era persists onto smartphone screens despite alternatives promoted as more efficient. Whether those alternatives truly were more efficient remains disputed after later rebuttals. What to examine: the strength of prior-experience inertia and what a new grammar lacks when it cannot defeat familiarity.
- Traffic-light color conventions: the promise “red means stop, green means go” crossed from roads to screens, so red error messages and green confirmation buttons are read fastest. What to examine: the largest archetype of a promise borrowed across media, and why users hesitate before a screen that violates it, such as a red confirmation button.

## Chapter 6. The User Is Not One Person: Eight Layers of Identity

This collection extends Chapter 6’s argument beyond games: a user is not one but eight overlapping layers within one person—customer, user, viewer, button presser, player, console and operator, character, and resident—and the first screen addresses them all at once. The examples are grouped into six areas: video games; board and tabletop games; video and content; performance and publishing; mobile, apps, and services; and offline and everyday life. Keep the chapter’s five visible layers—customer, viewer, button presser, player, and character—beside the three hidden ones—user, console and operator, and resident. For each example, identify which layer has awakened, which awoke incorrectly, and which never awoke.

**Video Games**

- Animal Crossing: in Nintendo’s life-simulation series, the same person switches roles from a player decorating a house and village, to a village resident living by store hours and seasonal events synchronized to real time, to a guest who visits a friend’s island and looks around without freely changing things. Animal Crossing: New Horizons (2020) adds the role of island representative, adjusting terrain and rules and awakening a layer close to the operator. What to examine: how one work gives different permissions and screens to the player, resident, and spectator layers, especially at the moment permissions decrease on somebody else’s island.
- Creative and Survival in Minecraft: in the same world, Creative mode offers infinite resources and no death, so blocks are viewed through an architect’s eyes. Survival mode adds hunger and monsters at night, so the same blocks are viewed through a survivor’s eyes. Changing one mode replaces the desire layer within the same person. What to examine: mode choice is effectively a choice of “which layer to enter as,” and different layers require different information and tension on the same terrain.
- Asynchronous cooperation in Death Stranding: in this 2019 game, the same person becomes a guest using ladders and bridges left by others, a helper who builds structures for others, and a builder who contributes materials to complete someone else’s unfinished structure. Because players connect only through “likes” without meeting directly, the burden of socializing does not intrude on role changes. What to examine: a design that deliberately lowers connection intensity so the guest and helper layers can remain alive simultaneously within one person.
- Multiplayer roles in Elden Ring and Dark Souls: the same person changes roles among a host asking for help, a cooperator summoned through a sign, and an invader entering another person’s world. Through floor messages visible to everyone, they may hint at traps and treasure or deceive with lies. Cooperation, hostility, and observation overlap within one world rather than separate modes. What to examine: when contradictory roles are permitted to one person, their entrances—summon signs and invasion items—are separated.

**Board Games / Tabletop**

- One player serving as game master in a tabletop RPG: at the same table, one person runs the world and makes rulings while the others live within it as characters. The game master occupies the console-and-operator view, while participants occupy the character view. At the next gathering, roles may switch, and yesterday’s operator becomes today’s character. What to examine: how differently the operator and character layers see information—the whole scenario versus the scene before their eyes—and how that asymmetry becomes a source of fun.
- Keep Talking and Nobody Explodes: this 2015 asymmetric cooperative game places people in the same room and game, but one becomes the defuser who sees only the bomb on screen while the others become experts who see only a paper manual. The defuser cannot see the manual and the experts cannot see the bomb, so two layers holding different information connect through speech alone. What to examine: an extreme case in which layer separation becomes the game’s skeleton, forcing cooperation by giving each layer only the information it needs.

**Video / Content**

- Family viewing programs: one living-room screen must give children something to watch while simultaneously giving the parents beside them reassurance and interest, or one of them changes the channel. Speak to only one side, and the other takes the remote. What to examine: the basic form of double address, where one scene sends different signals to two audiences when the viewer layer overlaps at the household level rather than within one person.
- Sesame Street’s co-viewing design: the program intentionally includes humor and parody to entertain parents while teaching children, encouraging them to watch together. This design is said to rest on the judgment that a parent watching beside a child improves the child’s learning. What to examine: retaining the guardian layer not as decoration but as a means of assisting the original goal, the child’s learning.
- Shrek’s dual-layer direction: on the same screen in the 2001 film, children see a fairy-tale adventure while parents read movie parodies and topical jokes passing above the children’s heads. The Magic Mirror introducing brides as “Bachelorette Number One, Two, and Three,” parodying dating shows, is one example. What to examine: the craft of stacking two messages without interference, so a joke legible to one layer passes without obstructing the other.
- Twitch Plays Pokémon: a 2014 Twitch stream in which commands typed by viewers in chat directly controlled the game. Hundreds of thousands of people controlled one character together and reportedly finished Pokémon Red in a little over two weeks. The instant a viewer typed one line, they became an operator; releasing the keyboard returned them to viewer. What to examine: how thin the border between watching and operating becomes when the threshold between viewer and button presser is lowered to one chat input.

**Performance / Publishing**

- The immersive performance Sleep No More: audience members wear masks and silently wander through what is described as nearly one hundred rooms. Following one actor up the stairs makes them more participant-like; staying to watch another scene leaves them as a spectator. Within an audience member who bought one ticket, spectator and participant layers divide according to personal choice. What to examine: the balance that lets people choose which layer to remain in while ensuring either choice produces a complete experience.
- The musical Hamilton: almost entirely sung, it lets a first-time listener follow the plot, while historical and literary references embedded in rapid rap lyrics become legible only to core fans after repeated listening. What to examine: placing a second reading for the core-fan layer inside the same lyrics without blocking comprehension for the light-audience layer.
- Norton Juster’s The Phantom Tollbooth: in this 1961 children’s novel, children read an adventure about a bored boy while adults read a layer of wordplay that literalizes idioms. A phrase such as “jumping to conclusions” becomes an actual island reached by jumping. What to examine: publishing’s double address, burying several reading layers within one text without blocking progress for readers at the lower layer.

**Mobile / Apps / Services**

- The 90–9–1 participation inequality: a rule of thumb for online communities in which most people only watch, some occasionally contribute, and a tiny number create directly, widely associated with usability researcher Jakob Nielsen’s 90–9–1 ratio. The exact ratio varies by community, but the slope is consistently observed. What to examine: the watching majority is a premise rather than a defect, giving grounds to design separately for people who only observe.
- Parental-consent gates in children’s apps: screens that excite children and consent or payment screens that reassure parents appear as different layers within one flow. The parental-verification step may deliberately be made difficult for children to solve, using multiplication or a long press on text. What to examine: a design that clearly changes the layer addressed by each screen, alongside a reverse design that deliberately blocks one layer—the child—from passing.
- Netflix Kids profiles: within the same account, a child profile uses a brighter, simpler interface and larger thumbnails while restricting access to settings; adult profiles remain unchanged. The profile-selection screen effectively asks, “Which layer are you entering as?” What to examine: how the simplest device for delegating layer separation to the user—the profile—became a standard way for one service to provide different interfaces to different layers.
- Wikipedia readers and editors: the Edit button is always visible on every article, yet most people read for life. The moment a person corrects one typo, the same person moves from reader to editor. Many articles can be edited without signing in to lower the threshold of the first edit. What to examine: exposing an entrance to the operator layer—Edit—at all times on the viewer layer’s screen—the article—while keeping it subdued enough not to interfere with reading.
- Switching between guest and host on Airbnb: with one account, a person becomes a guest renting accommodation while traveling, then switches to host mode when listing their own home and becomes an operator managing bookings and settlements. The app replaces the entire first-screen composition between the two modes. What to examine: mode switching that provides entirely different screens to the customer and console-and-operator layers within one person, and how deep the switch button is placed.

**Offline / Everyday Life**

- Presenter, attendee, and staff routes at a conference: in the same venue, presenters move to backstage waiting rooms, attendees to session rooms, and staff across the spaces between them. The same person may be a presenter in the morning and return as an attendee in the afternoon. What to examine: spatial design becoming layer design, with a single badge color labeling layers and dividing permissions offline.
- Karaoke: in one room, the same person becomes the star when holding the microphone, an audience member cheering while someone else sings, and an operator arranging the order while holding the song controller. The layers rotate every few minutes without confusion because the microphone, sofa, and controller serve as signs of the layer. What to examine: a layer-transition method that signals role change through the object held in the hand rather than through a screen.
- Parent-observation classes: the same lesson in the same classroom becomes a more tense-than-usual class for children and a chance for parents standing at the back to check whether their children are doing well. The teacher addresses the child and guardian layers simultaneously through one class. What to examine: what changes when another layer—the guardian—enters a screen that normally addresses only one—the student—and where the demands collide, such as advancing the lesson versus putting on a demonstration.

## Chapter 7. Extreme Beginners and False Beginners: Eight Types of Experiencers

This collection extends Chapter 7’s argument into media beyond games: there is no single kind of beginner. Beneath the broad divide between gamers and ordinary people, beginners split by what they do not know into eight branches—genre, title, platform, worldbuilding, economy, social, returning, and spectator—and false beginners carrying the wrong luggage are more difficult than extreme beginners arriving empty-handed. The cases are grouped into five branches: video games; board games; video, publishing, and content; mobile, apps, and services; and offline life. As you read each case, identify which of the eight beginners this person is and whether they arrived empty-handed or carrying the wrong luggage.

**Video Games**

- Dead Cells / Hades: Both roguelikes assume repeated death. Someone who knows the genre’s grammar reads the first death as a signal that “the main game starts here” and moves on to upgrades, while a newcomer reads the same death as failure and an ending. Hades (2020) attaches a reward to death itself by advancing conversation and story at the home base to which the player returns after dying. Examine which interpretation the first screen after death supports when the same screen is read in opposite ways by a genre beginner and a veteran.
- The first death in Dark Souls: Someone who knows Soulslike grammar reads death as a signal for learning. A newcomer reads the same death as “the game is unfair,” interpreting the same screen in the opposite way. Whether a person knows that the souls dropped on death can be recovered from that place divides the interpretations. Examine how, in one scene, the meaning of death itself must be taught to a genre beginner, while the same explanation is clutter to a veteran.
- The jump button in Devil May Cry: In the Western release of the first game (2001), jump was assigned to the triangle button, while sequels settled on X for jumping. Communities often recount that people whose hands were trained by later titles press the wrong button when returning to the original. The remastered HD Collection adjusted the first game’s mapping to match the sequels, this time misaligning the hands of people familiar with the old release. Habits diverge by edition even within one series. Examine how false beginners come not only from other works but from other editions of the same series, which is why “They are our fans, so they will know” does not hold.
- EVE Online’s abbreviation barrier: In the space MMO EVE Online, abbreviations such as MWD, AB, and BS fill chat and guidance, making the first screen an alien language to genre beginners. Communities have even created separate glossaries for newcomers. Examine how, when the terminology barrier is high, users eventually create their own unofficial interpreters—glossaries and mentor organizations—to fill the gap, though design could have paid that cost from the beginning.
- Animal Crossing: New Horizons: Even people who do not play games learn slowly through simple actions such as fishing and gathering, where failure costs almost nothing. Only a few tasks emerge each day, matching the pace of ordinary life rather than a gamer’s speed. Examine the shape of an entrance that takes ordinary people as its primary audience, and the dual structure that still holds gamers by stacking depth—customization and collection catalogs—behind it.
- Modern Controls in Street Fighter 6: The 2023 game first asks players to choose between Classic, with traditional command inputs, and Modern, which produces special moves with one button in exchange for certain adjustments. Genre beginners reach the fun of a match immediately through Modern, while veterans retain their habits through Classic. Examine how the branching prescription “Is this your first time?” is implemented at the control-system level in a fighting genre notorious for high entry barriers.

**Board Games**

- Twilight Imperium / Gloomhaven: Before heavyweight board games that take hours per session, even board-game veterans become title beginners under the weight of the rulebook. Large games in the Gloomhaven family therefore sometimes provide introductory scenarios that use only part of the rules. Examine whether the product itself acknowledges that even veterans are beginners before this work and provides a separate introductory entrance.
- Catan: The rules are light, but outcomes depend on resource trading and negotiation, so even among people who have read every rule, a first game differs completely between those new to trading and those familiar with it. Examine how grammar outside the rules—negotiation technique and a sense of market value—cannot be taught by the rulebook, and how that is effectively the social beginner’s first hurdle.

**Video, Publishing, and Content**

- A foreign-language film without subtitles: The same screen is a wall to a language beginner and simply dialogue to someone who knows the language. What someone knows upon arrival changes the difficulty of the content itself. Examine the shortest illustration of the premise behind this chapter’s entire classification: difficulty is not an attribute of content, but a relationship between content and experiencer.
- The opening of Mad Max: Fury Road: The 2015 film establishes only the premise of wars over water and oil in a short piece of narration, then throws the audience straight into a chase. A worldbuilding beginner can follow without knowing the earlier films, while fans receive an uncluttered introduction. Examine the sense of minimum length that compresses guidance for a worldbuilding beginner until it is short enough not to bore a veteran.
- Joining a long-running soap opera midway: Daytime serials that continue for decades place dialogue in every episode in which characters retell past events to one another. Even someone who missed yesterday can catch up with who is who within a few episodes. Examine the method of spreading guidance thinly across the entire work rather than confining it to one entrance, so a title beginner can join at any episode.
- “Previously on” recaps in serialized works: Seasonal dramas conventionally condense the preceding plot into about a minute at the start of a new episode. It is a light review for someone who watched last week and an answer to “What changed while I was gone?” for someone returning after months. Examine the standard device for removing a returning beginner’s first hurdle without touching the main work—the prototype to which returning-user guidance in games can refer.
- Introductory books and academic papers in specialist fields: Even on the same subject, an introductory book assumes the reader knows nothing, while a paper assumes familiarity with prior research. Assumptions about what the reader does not know reshape the vocabulary, introduction, and footnotes in their entirety. Examine how the location of the primary reader changes the form of the document itself, and why one book rarely satisfies both.

**Mobile, Apps, and Services**

- Duolingo’s placement test: At the start of a language course, Duolingo lets learners choose whether to begin at the beginning or take a proficiency assessment. Those who choose the assessment start at a point suited to the result. Examine a textbook implementation of branching: it separates a genre beginner, someone new to the language, from a returning beginner, someone who studied it before, with one question and applies the answer directly to the starting point.

**Offline and Everyday Life**

- Applause between movements at a classical concert: A first-time attendee tries to applaud whenever a movement ends, while someone who knows the convention waits for the entire piece to finish. One must read the number of movements in the program and watch the conductor lower their hands to know the end; that reading is itself a grammar shared among insiders. Examine a structure where well-intentioned applause becomes a marker that someone “arrived without knowing,” and whether one line of guidance can remove that burden in advance.
- A fast-food self-order kiosk: Even someone familiar with in-app payments pauses at a store kiosk when the first screen pours out the entire menu at once, unsure where to begin while a line forms behind them. The same act of ordering follows different screen grammar on a smartphone and a kiosk, turning a mobile expert into a platform beginner. Examine how the time pressure of a waiting line multiplies the beginner’s hurdle and why the same UI must be far more forgiving than a screen viewed alone.
- Onboarding an experienced hire: They are a veteran at the job but a beginner in the new company’s tools, procedures, and jargon. Repeating new-hire training is boring; skipping it leaves them blocked. This is why companies create separate onboarding for experienced hires. Examine a case where both “They have experience, so they will know” and “They are new, so teach everything” are wrong, and choosing what to skip and what alone to teach becomes the classification of the experiencer.
- A licensed driver starting a rental car for the first time: They are skilled at driving but new to that car’s ignition method and button layout, so they get lost in the parking lot trying to release the parking brake. The unfamiliar thing is not the skill but the layout. Examine how much of mastery is actually a habit tied to a particular arrangement, and why changing the arrangement makes veterans the first to get lost.
- A car with the turn-signal stalk on the other side: Depending on model and standard, a turn-signal stalk may sit on the left or right side of the wheel. It is commonly said that veteran drivers switching to an imported car or an overseas rental often move the wipers while trying to signal. Examine how one lever turns a decades-long driver into a platform beginner, and how the wrong answer is absorbed as one harmless sweep of the wipers rather than an accident, allowing the person to laugh and move on.
- A new TV with a different remote: Someone can be skilled at watching TV yet become a platform beginner the instant they cannot find the input button on a new remote. They knew every function; only the button’s name and position changed. Examine how a false beginner’s blockage is not a lack of ability but a mapping mismatch, and why the prescription is not education but one line of correspondence.
- Starbucks-style size terms: Store-specific names such as Tall, Grande, and Venti become a small alien language to a first-time visitor. At the counter, “Give me a large one” and “A Grande” need translation between them. Examine how the taboo against pouring out genre terminology without explanation operates identically at a café counter, and how one follow-up question from the staff substitutes for that translation.

## Chapter 8. Customers Within the Customer: From Persona to Immigration Candidate

This collection extends into cases inside and outside games the chapter’s argument that our customers are not one kind, but several people who lived in different neighborhoods and enter through different doors. A persona must be drawn not as a demographic portrait, but as an immigration-candidate card recording where someone lived and what they brought if it is to change first-screen decisions. In addition to the section’s central examples, cases where the same scene is read differently by origin, entrances separated by origin, and origin asked in the first question are grouped by medium from video games to offline life. Each item ends with “what to examine,” explaining what a designer should observe. There is one reading method: identify what different origins take differently from the same entrance, and which design decision that difference changed.

**Video Games**
- The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt: The first two games used Geralt’s amnesia to introduce the world and its people from the beginning for newcomers. The 2015 third game lets go of that device, beginning with Geralt’s memory restored. It places people and events recognizable only to those who know the novels and earlier games in the opening, while letting context flow through dialogue and recollection so that newcomers can follow. Examine how dual signals coexist at one entrance: beginners receive guidance from a blank slate, while existing fans are welcomed by names they know. Does our first screen also contain signals that two origins can each take away?
- Detroit: Become Human: This 2018 interactive drama from Quantic Dream accepts the viewing habits brought by people from film and television—the familiar act of following scenes and identifying with characters—and narrows most controls to choices within scenes and brief inputs. At the end of every chapter, a branching flowchart confirms how one’s choices divided the story. Examine the placement that makes the habits of non-game origins—watching and choosing—the first input, and where it puts branching confirmation, the new flavor available only in games.
- Kingdom Hearts: As platform-spanning side stories between main entries came to carry central lore, newcomers who had not played the earlier titles and side stories were often said to get lost from the opening of a main title. Examine it as a counterexample of “continuity lock” that excludes people who enter without an origin in prior knowledge. What does someone who missed earlier installments begin without knowing on our first screen?
- Street Fighter 6: This 2023 fighting game places Classic controls for series fans accustomed to command inputs beside Modern controls that produce special moves with one button. It separately welcomes fighting-game newcomers through World Tour, a single-player mode where they roam streets and learn to fight. Examine how the same game separates entrances through control systems and modes by origin, and the convergence path that ultimately brings every entrance into the same competitive world.
- Pokémon GO: Before the first screen of this 2016 location-based game stood both fans who raised Pokémon in childhood and people who did not play games but installed it as a reason to walk. Fans received a nostalgic signal from the first starter Pokémon, while walkers could begin with the familiar action of walking over a map. The two origins were said to remain in the same app for different reasons. Examine the structure in which two origins gathered at one entrance settle through different rewards, and whether our first action also works through the habits of a person not from games.

**Mobile, Apps, and Services**
- Spotify’s first entry: Immediately after signup, it asks someone to choose at least three favorite artists, then shows a first home screen filled not with emptiness but with recommendations based on those tastes. It asks in its first question which musical neighborhood this person lived in and turns the answer directly into a first-screen decision. Examine the flow where one question about origin actually changes the first screen, and how many fields in our persona change first-screen decisions.

**Film and Television**
- The post-credits scene in The Avengers (2012): After the film, a short scene says that to fight the Avengers is “to court death,” and Thanos turns and smiles. A comics reader knows that Thanos has long adored the entity Death and reads the implication of the smile; a new viewer sees only the appearance of a new villain. Examine how the thickness of the signal taken from the same scene differs by origin, while the treatment still supports the scene as complete for the person who does not know.
- The Harry Potter film adaptations: Book readers expect the realization of scenes they imagined, while viewers meeting the story first through film expect a new story. Two different expectations sit side by side in the same theater. Examine how the priority between reproducing the source and guiding newcomers reveals which origin was chosen as the primary immigration candidate.
- Dune (2021): Denis Villeneuve’s adaptation handles in one opening both the proper nouns brought by novel readers and the blank slate of new viewers. Terms flow throughout the screen, but the story itself can be followed through the universal structure of conflict among houses: newcomers receive atmosphere, while knowledgeable viewers receive depth. Examine what supports the same proper noun so that it welcomes one origin without becoming memorization for another.
- Top Gun: Maverick: The 2022 film forms a complete story for viewers who do not know the 1986 original, while layering separate signals for fans, including Goose’s son Rooster and old photographs. Examine how dual design crosses a gap of decades by giving fans additional rewards without blocking newcomers, and where fan signals are placed so they do not obstruct new viewers’ understanding.
- The first episode of HBO’s The Last of Us: The 2023 adaptation lengthens the daily life of Joel’s daughter Sarah, which the game passed over briefly. Viewers who did not play the game first become attached to the character, while game fans revisit a known ending from another angle. Examine how length and viewpoint were rearranged to fit the same opening to two origins, and what our opening lengthens and shortens for each origin.
- Game of Thrones: Readers of the novels watch direction and adaptation while knowing what comes next. Television-only viewers experience the developments for the first time. The same episode becomes a subject of comparison for one side and a shock for the other. Examine how, especially in episodes where the two origins’ reactions diverge, what counts as a spoiler and what counts as a reward reverses by origin.

**Animation**
- Demon Slayer: Kimetsu no Yaiba – The Movie: Mugen Train: The 2020 film continues directly after the first television season. Someone who watched the season arrives knowing Nezuko’s situation and the hierarchy of the Demon Slayer Corps, so the emotional line functions immediately. For someone seeing the work for the first time in a theater, the weight of relationships and tension remains blank in the same scenes. Examine the information gap on the same opening scene between origins that saw the previous installment and those that did not, and the judgment of how much guidance to add to bridge it.
- Arcane: This 2021 Netflix animation centers the story on two sisters so that viewers who have never played League of Legends can watch without prior knowledge, while giving game fans separate rewards in recognizing familiar champions and place names. Examine dual design that gives additional signals only to knowledgeable viewers without blocking those without an origin, and the line that keeps those rewards from becoming conditions for understanding the main plot.

**Music**
- K/DA’s “POP/STARS”: Riot created this virtual girl-group song with League of Legends champions during the 2018 World Championship. For game fans, it was the event of familiar champions taking the stage; for K-pop and pop listeners who did not know the game, it was simply a good new song. Two origins gathered at the same song through different entrances. Examine the condition under which one piece of content satisfies the differing expectations of two origins: completeness that makes neither side’s prior knowledge mandatory for appreciation.

**Publishing and IP Expansion**
- Film novelizations and game tie-in novels: This is a longstanding practice of moving readers who first encountered a work on screen into the entrance of prose. Readers turn pages already knowing the scenes and actors’ faces. Examine how expectations created in another medium—familiar scenes and voices—work on the first page of the new medium, and what mental image someone moving to our medium will bring.
- Drama and game adaptations of original webtoons: Readers accustomed to episodic rhythm and vertical scrolling move into another medium. They arrive knowing what comes next in the story, but must leave their familiar reading action behind. Examine how separating what original readers bring—the expectation of the next scene—from what they leave behind—the habit of reading—produces the fields of the immigration-candidate card directly.
- IP expansion from original web novels: Expectations collide when readers accustomed to cathartic developments and fast pacing are brought into video or games. Video slows the rhythm, while games insert control. Examine how changing media creates a new source of friction even in the same story, and whether it can be written in advance in the candidate card’s “what will bother them” field.
- Board games based on video games: Fans who first met a work digitally are welcomed by the initial setup of physical components. People must now handle rule processing that the screen performed for them, so the first experience has a different cost structure even within the same IP. Examine how someone who likes the same world gets stuck at a different place in initial setup when the medium changes.

**Offline and Everyday Life**
- A first consultation at a gym: A trainer first asks a new member about exercise history. The opening program differs depending on whether the person has never exercised, is returning after a break, or came from another form of exercise. Examine how one question about origin immediately changes the first program—the first screen—making it the smallest immigration-candidate card, and whether the first question in our onboarding changes decisions as much.

## Chapter 9. Primary and Adjacent Worlds: A World Must Be Able to Admit Residents

This collection extends the chapter’s argument into examples inside and outside games: worldbuilding should be measured not by the amount of setting, but by whether a person can enter and live there. Choose one primary world and several adjacent worlds, measure different immigration distances for every origin—near, far, and dangerous—and design the first screen as the entrance to a neighborhood rather than city hall. In addition to the section’s central cases, the remaining examples are grouped by medium from video games to offline life. Each item ends with “what to examine,” explaining what designers should observe. The reading method is simple: see whether each world enters through proper nouns and explanation or through mood and action, and what narrows the distance between the person’s former home and the new world.

**Video Games**
- Elden Ring / Dark Souls: FromSoftware’s games do not lay a thousand years of history over subtitles. They let world history flow through the placement of ruined villages, churches, and cities and through one-line item descriptions. In Elden Ring, the true history of the Golden Order appears only after gathering clues in hidden areas such as the Subterranean Shunning-Grounds beneath Leyndell. Examine how design that lets people learn by exploring rather than memorizing secures both depth and accessibility, and where our world’s settings are buried instead of on the first screen.
- The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild: The 2017 game sends an awakened Link into a broad landscape without a long setting explanation. The view before him and distant towers and shrines call his next steps. The neighborhood entrance’s impression arrives before proper nouns. Examine how the first screen gives a direction someone wants to visit instead of information, and how landmarks replace instructions.
- Halo / Mass Effect: These series have cosmic-scale settings, yet do not pour the proper nouns of races and factions onto the first screen. They let people learn the world one piece at a time from dialogue and records while moving through missions. Examine how the larger the total volume of setting, the more sparingly the entrance must reveal it, and count the proper nouns appearing in the first hour.
- The opening of Portal: Valve’s 2007 puzzle game lets an awakened test subject solve the first room directly while listening only to announcements from the AI GLaDOS. The player learns world and controls simultaneously through touch rather than memorizing rules. Examine this residential opening, which lets people live immediately instead of explaining on the first screen, and the translation performed by an in-world device—announcements—in place of tutorial copy.
- The opening of Hollow Knight: The 2017 game places the player, after brief control guidance, in Dirtmouth, a village with only empty houses and streetlamps, then invites them to descend through a well despite an elder’s warning. It does not explain who or why. The lonely impression at an empty neighborhood entrance conveys the weight of a fallen kingdom first. Examine when someone first feels “I want to know about this world” in an opening where mood precedes setting, and the order in which lore is released only after that curiosity arises.
- The opening of Disco Elysium: The 2019 game awakens an amnesiac detective in a ruined hotel room. Rather than listening to a world explanation, he gropes first through his own condition, learning the world at the player’s pace. Examine how setting clues are released one piece at a time as needed rather than all at once, and how the protagonist’s ignorance excuses the player’s ignorance.
- Kingdom Hearts / Xenosaga: Terms unique to the series—Heartless, Nobody, Keyblade, and others—accumulate over installments and are often cited as an entry barrier for newcomers. A name that is a mystery to deep fans becomes an entrance exam for a first-time visitor. Examine this reverse example of how a proper-noun bomb becomes a cost, and count how many names unnecessary for the moment appear first at our entrance.
- Minecraft: The game begins by placing someone in a field without explanation or a stated objective. They learn the world’s rules by hand while gathering wood and surviving the first night. Beside the survival world is a creative world for building freely, attached through the same block grammar, so a person familiar with one can walk into the other. Examine the structure in which primary and adjacent worlds share hand habits and shorten immigration distance, directly realizing the definition of a world as a way of life rather than a unit of setting.
- Fortnite: Beside its primary battle-royale world, it has attached Creative modes for making and playing, events centered on performance and socializing, and adjacent worlds such as LEGO Fortnite (2023). The same avatar and locker follow people into every world. Examine the distance arrangement where adjacent worlds remain close enough to the primary yet offer one new flavor, and how the shared household of an avatar reduces the burden of moving.

**Mobile, Apps, and Services**
- A first impression filled with examples instead of an empty screen: Collaboration tools and note apps fill first entry with sample documents and projects rather than emptiness, conveying through atmosphere “this is what people do here” instead of making someone memorize settings. Examine whether examples furnished like a model home before move-in guide the first action, and whether they are easy to remove once no longer needed.
- Instagram Reels: This vertical short-video section added in 2020 is often described as designed so that the hand habit of people who lived on TikTok—vertical swiping—works unchanged. It becomes a neighboring district within the same app for existing Instagram residents and a near migration for people from TikTok. Examine how importing another neighborhood’s hand habit shortens immigration distance, along with the cost to existing residents who feel that their neighborhood changed.

**Film and Animation**
- The opening of Spirited Away: The 2001 film follows Chihiro through a tunnel and an empty restaurant street without explanatory dialogue, until night falls and gods appear. The audience receives the other world through mood at the same pace as Chihiro. Examine an opening that makes the world felt first through scenery and light without proper nouns, and the structure in which the protagonist’s confusion carries the audience’s confusion.
- Episode 1 of Attack on Titan: It first shows peaceful daily life inside the walls, then drops the audience directly into the incident of a Colossal Titan breaking through. The world is experienced through an event, not taught as a setting lecture. Examine, in this in-medias-res opening without a preliminary background explanation, how a few minutes of daily life support the weight of the shock that follows.
- Episode 1 of Made in Abyss: It begins with daily life in Orth, the town at the edge of the enormous pit, and immediately lets the audience experience cave-raiding culture through action, living the world’s mood and rules without explanation. Examine how the first episode gives the impression of the neighborhood entrance before a setting lecture, and how the world’s central rules arrive naturally inside a character’s daily work.
- Mad Max: Fury Road: The 2015 film drops the audience directly into a chase after a few short lines of monologue. Art direction and character behavior, not explanatory dialogue, reveal the rules of a wasteland ruled by those who control water and oil. No knowledge of the previous three films is required. Examine what prevents the audience from getting lost even when explanation is reduced to an extreme, and how clear desires and a single-direction route replace a setting lecture.

**Literature and Narrative Technique**
- Tolkien’s The Silmarillion and The Hobbit: Even within the same world, The Silmarillion, which begins with genealogy and myth, and The Hobbit, which admits readers through one hobbit’s adventure, have different immigration distances. Depth is not the same as accessibility. Examine an arrangement that provides several entrances to the same world but makes the first a near migration, and ask whether we are putting our world’s Silmarillion on the first screen.
- In medias res: A narrative method used since epic poetry that drops readers into an event rather than laying down the background first, making them live in the world. Examine what holds readers when explanation is postponed, and how long tension in the event lets them tolerate the blanks in the setting.

**Music**
- Janelle Monáe’s Metropolis series: It builds the dystopian city of the android Cindi Mayweather one fragment at a time through songs and inter-song skits rather than a setting bible. Examine residential worldbuilding that lets the world seep into the background while someone enjoys the work, and the order that makes the song good before requiring the setting.

**Board Games and TRPGs**
- Gloomhaven’s first scenario: Although it is a large campaign board game released in 2017, it does not make players read the entire setting book. It lets them live in the world through one small first adventure. Examine the method of reducing rules and world to one session’s portion, and the restraint of carving a smaller first entrance for a larger world.
- A TRPG session zero: Before the main game, participants meet to align tone and mood, what they want, and what they wish to avoid. Before making anyone memorize the rulebook, they first see whether this is a place where people can live. Examine how agreeing on the neighborhood’s mood before moving in reduces abandonment, and whether our onboarding has a stage corresponding to session zero.

**Offline and Everyday Life**
- Disneyland’s “weenie,” the attention-drawing landmark: A term associated with Walt Disney, it refers to a distant landmark such as a castle that catches the eye and draws people inward. The scenery points the route without instructions. Examine design where landscape rather than names and maps creates movement, and what serves as the weenie on our first screen.
- Mood design in a museum’s first gallery: Instead of pouring out wall text, museums use the first room’s light and sound to convey what kind of place it is, reducing viewing fatigue. Examine the first-room arrangement that gives atmosphere before information, and whether our first screen is a room of wall text or a room of light.
- An IKEA showroom: Furniture is shown not through specification sheets and shelf display, but by furnishing entire living rooms and bedrooms. A person entering can bodily estimate, “This is what living here would be like.” Examine how displaying a way of life rather than specifications draws the imagination of moving in, and whether our first screen shows specs or a household.
- Inspecting a new neighborhood before deciding to move: People do not register as residents first. They look at the streets and people’s expressions to judge whether it is worth living there. Examine how the first screen is the object of that inspection and what appears within the first few seconds at our neighborhood entrance.

## Chapter 10. The Character Roster and the Sensory Roster

This collection examines whether the chapter’s argument also operates outside games: before a character is a picture filling the screen, it is an interpreter translating the world into human sensation, and each character calls different people through one of eight sensory clusters—protection and care, exploration and discovery, aspiration and display, play and reaction, collection and ownership, speed and mastery, connection and conversation, and safety and comfort. In addition to the section’s central cases, the remaining examples are grouped by medium from video games to offline life. Each ends with “what to examine,” describing what designers should observe. The reading method is simple: in each case, see which sensation, rather than ability or tier, makes people stop before the character.

**Video Games**
- Overwatch: In this 2016 team shooter, people viewing the same roster are drawn differently: some to a weighty tank who blocks the front with a shield, some to a damage hero who dives quickly looking for one decisive hit, and some to a support who keeps allies alive. Each role carries a different play sensation. The weight of aspiration and display, the thrill of speed and mastery, and the reward of protection and care call different people on the same screen. Examine whether characters on our roster divide the sensations among themselves or all wear different clothes over one sensation.
- Agent selection in Valorant: In Riot’s 2020 tactical shooter, agents divide into role groups such as Duelists and Sentinels. Someone who enjoys direct fights is drawn to Duelists; someone who enjoys laying traps, guarding the rear, and frustrating opponents is drawn to Sentinels. They choose a character through the grain of play, not tier. Examine how role-group names indicate behavioral grain rather than performance figures, and whether the first choice screen asks not “What is strongest?” but “How do I want to play?”
- Villagers in Animal Crossing: Hundreds of villagers with no combat stats at all divide only by eight personality types and speech patterns—lazy, peppy, snooty, and others—so every person finds different villagers to care for. The villager people keep talking to remains more memorable than a strong one. Examine how a favorite arises through connection and conversation alone even on a roster without an ability field, and how the number of personality types is the number of kinds of people the game can call.
- Villagers in Stardew Valley: In this 2016 farming game, relationships deepen through gifts and heart events independently of combat and farming ability. Each person chooses different villagers to befriend and marry. They are objects of relationship, not efficiency units. Examine how, when relationship content is separated from efficiency, people treat characters as partners rather than parts.
- Team Fortress 2 class silhouettes: The nine classes of this 2007 game were shaped with distinct bodies and poses so they can be distinguished by black silhouettes alone. Heavy’s large body looks impossible to knock over, while Scout’s thin legs look fast but weak, foreshadowing personality and play sensation. Valve is known to have made silhouette recognition a principle of its art direction. Examine whether appearance alone, without explanatory copy, reveals which sensation a character calls—speed and mastery or safety and comfort.
- Off the Hook (Pearl and Marina) in Splatoon: The host duo of Splatoon 2 (2017) pairs loud, direct Pearl with calm, technical Marina. Their opposite grains divide which one draws each fan. Eyes stop at different places on the same screen. Examine how placing the two side by side without ranking turns the split into preference rather than conflict.

**Animated Film**
- The emotion characters in Inside Out: In the 2015 film, Joy is yellow and star-shaped, Sadness blue and teardrop-shaped, and Fear thin like a single exposed nerve. The creators are known to have shaped color and form so each emotion reads immediately. Appearance is the heart each character touches. Examine whether our characters’ shape and color convey the intended sensation at a glance and whether personality remains when form is hidden.
- Carl and Russell in Up: The 2009 film shapes stubborn old Carl as a square and innocent boy Russell as a circle, letting the contrast of two forms make both readable without an ability explanation. Examine how shape language is the cheapest means of translating personality.
- Minions / Totoro: Without a line explaining ability, softness and playfulness immediately touch protection and care and play and reaction. Examine what remains to attract people when specifications are removed from a character introduction.

**Literature**
- Winnie-the-Pooh’s friends: Laid-back Pooh, timid Piglet, bouncy Tigger, and gloomy Eeyore beckon different readers through different temperaments in the same forest. They are remembered by personality, not performance. Examine how the spectrum of temperaments becomes the spectrum of readers and how broad the temperament range of our roster is.

**Live-Action Television and Drama**
- Favorite-character splits in ensemble casts: Even while watching the same drama, viewers’ hearts remain with different characters. It is common for a supporting character, rather than the protagonist promoted by producers, to create a phenomenon. Examine how popularity follows character grain rather than screen time or setting, and how the person before whom viewers stop becomes the measured value of the sensory roster.
- Sesame Street: This children’s program, begun in 1969, divides sensations among puppets on one stage. Elmo carries connection and conversation through eye contact with young viewers. Cookie Monster carries play and reaction that erupts when touched. Big Bird’s large, clumsy nature inspires protection and care. Children of different ages and temperaments each meet their interpreter. Examine how to place several interpreters in one world without letting their sensations overlap.
- Pengsoo: This penguin trainee from EBS’s Giant Peng TV (2019) began on a children’s channel but is often said to have gained an adult office-worker fandom through a personality that speaks without inhibition. Examine how a character’s sensation, not the target field in a planning document, determines whom it calls, and what signal appears when the intended origin and the origin that actually gathers diverge.

**Music**
- Favorite-member splits in idol groups: Even watching the same stage, different people are drawn to vocals, dance, kindness, or stage command, dividing their preferred members. Popularity of the group as a whole and each member’s fandom run separately. Examine how different charms, not a ranking table, call different people, and how the prototype of the character roster already exists onstage.

**Manga and Comic Books**
- Character popularity polls in boys’ manga: In reader polls for weekly serialized manga, the strongest character does not necessarily come first. The character that touched people does, and protagonists are not rarely surpassed by supporting characters. Examine this rare device that numerically demonstrates performance and popularity as separate axes, and whether our game can measure them separately.

**Webtoons**
- Webtoons driven by characterization: The first episode lets readers meet a person to love through expression, atmosphere, and speech habits rather than stats or world explanation. Examine which sensation appears first in the opening episode and whether attraction precedes information.

**Theme Parks and Spatial Experiences**
- Character greetings in Disney parks: Visitors choose favorite characters to meet around the park. Lines form before popular characters, but these lines are made by each person’s heart, not a tier chart. Examine how the meeting is designed before transaction and how the first point of contact is “going to find” rather than “drawing.”

**Character IP and Merchandise**
- Sanrio’s My Melody and Kuromi: My Melody’s gentle loveliness and Kuromi’s mischievous edge call different fans through opposite grains. Both are known to compete near the top of annual character rankings. Examine IP operation that makes divergent popularity read as a difference in grain rather than superiority.
- Kakao Friends’ Ryan: This maneless male lion joined in 2016 and is known to have quickly become the flagship through one quiet, dependable grain without an ability setting. While Apeach carries play and reaction on the same roster, Ryan carries safety and comfort. Examine the division where characters within one IP take different sensations and call different people, and the flow through which those sensations call for content in stickers and merchandise.

**Offline and Everyday Life**
- Everland’s panda Fu Bao: Born in Korea in 2020, the panda is often said to have inspired phenomenon-level attachment not through tricks or rarity tier but through widely shared scenes of connection with keepers. People stopped before scenes of receiving and giving care, not before an ability. Examine the path through which protection-and-care sensation grows into attachment through care scenes, and whether a character game’s first screen should show a specification chart or a moment of care.

**General Apps**
- Microsoft Office’s Clippy: Introduced in Office 97, this paperclip assistant aimed for connection and conversation but became disliked by interrupting work with unsolicited interference. It was disabled by default in early-2000s releases and eventually disappeared. Examine this counterexample: merely placing a character does not make it an interpreter. If the character’s sensation and actual behavior diverge, the interpreter becomes noise.

## Chapter 11. The User's State: Distraction and the Price of Experience

This collection extends the chapter's claim that experience is not given but paid for: the first screen charges users seven costs—attention, time, opportunity, social burden, failure, personal information, and the psychology of monetization—and those prices rise and fall with the user's state rather than following a fixed list price. The cases are grouped by medium, and each entry ends with an “Observation” describing what a designer should watch. There is one way to read them: look at what cost each case charges and when, and whether it charges before giving or after.

**Video Games**
- Flappy Bird and Doodle Jump-style one-thumb games: Flappy Bird (2013) uses only a single tap that makes the bird rise once, while Doodle Jump (2009) moves with a single tilting action. By reducing input to one action for a distracted, one-handed user holding a subway strap, they let “just one more round” roll through every short gap. Observation: whether our first action remains possible with one hand amid noise; the number of inputs is the number of lines on the attention bill.
- Slot machines' “near misses” and winning presentations: gambling research discusses how a near miss that stops just short of a win and a presentation that sounds like victory despite returning less than the stake encourage continued play. This is the source form of the excitement borrowed by gacha. Observation: the same presentation reads as excitement to someone from a gaming background but as a warning—“this place runs on chance and money”—to a newcomer; the price of excitement differs by origin.
- Super Mario Bros. 1-1: the 1985 game places a Goomba in the player's path immediately after the start without a line of text, letting the player learn to jump on their own before reaching a complex section. The level structure pays the attention cost that explanatory text would have charged. Observation: embedding instruction in the screen's structure reduces the cost almost to zero; watch how the arrangement teaches only one action at a time.

**Live-Action Film**
- The line of commercials and trailers before a movie: a time cost charged first to people who came for the feature, and the longer it is, the more expensive it feels to viewers who have already paid for a ticket. Observation: an added charge feels more expensive to someone who has already paid another cost; measure how many minutes of “line before the feature” our first screen contains.
- The cold opens of Breaking Bad and Lost: Breaking Bad (2008) begins its first episode with a man in his underwear driving an RV carrying bodies through the desert. Lost (2004) begins with its protagonist awakening in a jungle without explaining the crash. They raise a question before explaining who and why, avoiding time and attention charges before giving. Observation: how delaying explanation and putting an event first turns costs into postpayment.

**Live-Action TV / Drama**
- Netflix's “Skip Intro” and automatic next episode: they treat waiting and repeated clicks as costs and remove them from the viewer's hands. The Skip Intro button was reportedly introduced around 2017, and the next episode begins automatically after the credits. Observation: design that reduces friction on the assumption that viewers will look away; also note the accompanying criticism that automatic continuation holds viewers for too long.
- Ad-supported free viewing: content looks free, but the viewer pays a time cost in advertising. The less visible the cost is, the more readily people step inside. Observation: visible and felt costs are different; identify which costs on our first screen are visible and which are hidden.
- YouTube's “Skip after 5 seconds” ads: instead of forcing the full time cost of an ad, they charge a fixed five-second rate and leave the rest to the viewer's choice. Skipped ads are reportedly tied to a system in which the advertiser is not charged. Observation: when a cost cannot be avoided, announcing its upper bound in advance lowers its perceived price; compare waiting whose end is known with waiting whose end is unknown.

**Music**
- Shortened song intros in the streaming era: because distracted listeners skip a song within seconds, musicians first cut the time cost of the introduction. Reportedly, intros that averaged around twenty seconds in the 1980s have recently shortened to around five, and roughly a quarter of streaming songs are skipped in the first five seconds. Observation: cut the introduction so listeners do not leave before hearing the value; the first five seconds are the most expensive checkout counter.

**Board Games**
- Heavy board games such as Gloomhaven: the thick rulebook of this 2017 title can charge attention and time costs all at once before the first play. The rulebook therefore uses the first scenario, Black Barrow, as an example, teaches only the rules needed for that session, and recommends that one person lead the game. Observation: teach only what the first play needs instead of everything in advance; this is a concrete technique for converting a lump-sum cost into installments.
- Beginner-friendly progression that adds one task per turn: it slices requirements thin so even a distracted first-time player decides only one thing at once. Observation: the principle of “ask for only one thing at a time” takes the same form on the table.

**Real-World Workflows**
- Sites that block browsing until sign-up: they charge a personal-information cost to someone who might merely come to like the service. Charge before giving, and people calculate first. Observation: can we read drop-off before the sign-up barrier as “excessive cost” rather than “lack of interest”?
- Immediate assistance at a store entrance versus letting people browse first: when an employee follows someone as soon as they enter, a person who only wanted to look around feels burdened and leaves. Social costs are priced in everyday spaces too. Observation: whether the greetings, notifications, and conversation on our first screen are hospitality or burden; the user's state makes that judgment.

**General Apps**
- Apps whose product bundles and discount timers cover the screen from day one: they impose a psychological monetization cost by making users decide “Will this place cost money?” before enjoying anything. Observation: the moment a payment offer precedes the experience of value, the calculation of trust begins.
- The anecdote of the “$300 million button” in guest checkout: as told by UX expert Jared Spool, a large online retailer required sign-up just before checkout, then added a “Continue without registering” button and saw completed purchases rise enough to add roughly $300 million in annual revenue. Observation: the personal-information cost is most expensive just before payment; moving the timing of the same charge changes its price.
- Amazon's one-click ordering: it collects and stores the recurring costs of entering an address and payment method once, then completes future orders with one press. The feature was patented in 1999, and the patent reportedly expired in 2017. Observation: where on our screen could a design collect a repeated time and attention cost only once?

**Devices / Consumer-Electronics UX**
- Long initial-setup wizards for smart TVs and devices: they line up language, region, remote pairing, Wi-Fi, terms, account login, and updates, charging time and attention before the device can be used. If even one step fails, returning to the beginning makes the process more frustrating. Products that at least provide “Skip” or “Later” for account login and similar steps reduce the entry cost. Observation: distinguish costs that cannot be deferred, such as essential setup, from those that can, such as account and app login; package the bill into one sheet.

**Theme Parks / Spatial Experiences**
- Posted wait times at Disney parks: attractions display estimated waits at their entrances and fill the queue with things to see and advance presentation. It is often said that the posted time is set slightly longer than the actual one to leave the feeling that “we got on sooner than expected.” Observation: if a time cost cannot be removed, disclose it in advance and place content inside the wait to lower its perceived price; this principle can be copied onto a loading screen.

**Offline / Everyday Life**
- Costco sample stations: they let customers taste before buying. This is a prototype of postpayment that puts value in the customer's hand before collecting a charge, and samples are often said to raise sales of the product substantially. Observation: whether our first screen presents the checkout before the sample; what is the “one bite” we will give first?

**Literature**
- The in medias res openings of the Odyssey and Iliad: rather than explaining everything ab ovo, from the egg, they pull the reader directly into the central situation. The Iliad begins not at the start of the Trojan War but with the quarrel between Achilles and Agamemnon, filling in the missing background later through recollection. Observation: the ancient prototype of an opening that delays background explanation and begins with action; epics were already collecting costs through postpayment thousands of years ago.

**Comics**
- The battle for the first chapter in Weekly Shonen Jump: reader surveys rank a series from immediately after its debut, and a failure to hold readers within the first few chapters is known to lead to early cancellation. Editors and creators therefore place their greatest effort into the first chapter. Observation: the first chapter is where a newcomer assigns the most expensive first-impression cost; watch what it shows and what it defers.

## Chapter 12. Types of Experience by Content

This collection brings together cases from many media to test two claims: a feature list and an experience list are not the same, and people's experiences through content divide into ten types—sensation, understanding, choice, achievement, ownership, relationship, world, ritual, display, and discovery—so people open the same content in pursuit of different experiences. The cases are grouped by medium, and each entry ends with an “Observation” describing what a designer should watch. There is one way to read them: count how many of the ten cells each piece of content fills, and which cells people enter through.

**Video Games**
- Minecraft: in the same world, one person becomes absorbed in building, another in survival and exploration, and another in researching redstone circuits. Separate Creative and Survival modes formally recognize that split. Observation: choosing a mode is the question “Which experience did you come to undergo?”; examine what must be separated for one world to hold several experiences.
- Stardew Valley: in this 2016 game, Grandpa's Evaluation in year three awards points in different cells, including total earnings, museum collection, skills, friendships with villagers, and restoration of the Community Center. Whether a player pursues only farming, collecting, or relationships, they can reach the same recognition. Observation: the evaluation system itself is a rare case of counting several experience types equally; examine which types our own evaluation table counts exclusively.
- The Sims: home decoration (ownership), human relationships (relationship), and creating a life story (world and choice) divide a sandbox without a victory condition into as many experiences as there are people. Observation: when there is no victory condition, what each person came to do becomes most clearly visible.
- Death Stranding: this 2019 game centers delivery and foregrounds asynchronous cooperation, in which roads, bridges, and zip-lines placed by one person are shared by others. It drives the game through connection (relationship), not combat (achievement). Observation: a game still works when the genre's default experience is removed and another type is cast as the lead.
- EVE Online: in the space MMO running since 2003, mining, manufacturing and trade, exploration, piracy, and combat all function as professions. Combat is not the only means of contribution; the whole economy divides into different experiences for different people. Observation: when every experience type has its own profession, one world can admit several kinds of people at once.
- Final Fantasy XIV: the same game has separate noncombat jobs, Disciples of the Hand (crafting) and Disciples of the Land (gathering). There is room for people who stay through crafting, gathering, housing, and glamour without fighting. Observation: examine a design that formalizes the place of noncombat players at the job-system level.
- Pokémon GO: in this location-based game released in 2016, one person opens the app to complete the Pokédex and collect differently colored variants (ownership), another for raids and battles (achievement), and another for walking and gatherings (relationship and ritual). Observation: whether the overlapping experiences in one app are separated enough not to interfere with one another, and whether the first screen blocks entry through any experience.
- Gran Turismo: racing (achievement) appears to be central, but beginning with GT Sport (2017), Scapes photo mode, livery, and tuning gave a separate group ways to customize cars (sensation and ownership), photograph them, and show them (display). Observation: even within the racing genre, experiences outside competition form a substantial layer.

**Animated Film**
- Toy Story: the story centers relationship as different characters assign different meanings to the same toys. Its audience also divides between children who see adventure and adults who see friendship and loss. Observation: how to layer one screen so it arrives as a different experience depending on the viewer.
- Spirited Away: children see the 2001 film as an adventure through the world of spirits (discovery and world), while adults read it as an allegory of labor, money, and growth (understanding). Observation: layering the same scene so it gives two audience groups different experience types at once.
- Pixar's Inside Out / Up: children enjoy colorful adventures and characters (sensation and discovery), while adults receive the acceptance of sadness and the emotions of loss (world and understanding). Observation: a composition that keeps the two layers of experience from obscuring each other.

**Literature**
- The Little Prince: children read the same text as an adventure across planets (discovery), while adults read the relationship and loss of taming and farewell. One book arrives as different experiences for different readers. Observation: the thickness of a text that gives each layer a different experience without excluding any readership.

**Live-Action TV / Drama**
- Diverging motives for watching the same IP: one person watches for narrative, another for character relationships, and another for the accuracy of the world-building. Observation: viewing reactions and fan activities—clip edits, setting guides, character analysis—are free data showing the distribution of experience types.

**Music**
- Different listening through the same playlist: on Spotify, one person lets the same set of songs play for concentration or background (sensation and ritual), another digs into it to discover new artists (discovery), and another shares a collaborative list they built together (relationship and display). Observation: one recording divides into several experiences according to how it is heard; one feature, playback, does not determine the experience.

**Board Games**
- Catan: collecting resources (ownership), extending roads (achievement), and negotiation (relationship) overlap in one game, giving different pleasures to different people. Observation: overlapping experiences interlock rather than remain separate—you must negotiate to collect resources.
- Descent / Gloomhaven: achievement-oriented combat players and story-oriented players coexist at the same table. Observation: even people playing together under the same rules undergo different experiences; one table is an intersection of many experiences.

**Theme Parks / Spatial Experiences**
- Disneyland: in the same park, one person buys the achievement of riding attractions, another a relationship with characters, and another time spent dwelling in atmosphere and world. Observation: a single admission ticket becomes a ticket to different experiences for different people, and the circulation routes are designed not to block those divisions.

**General Apps / Fandom**
- Instagram: one person opens the same app for display (curating a feed), another for relationship (messages), and another for discovery (Explore). Observation: each bottom tab is effectively divided into an entrance for an experience type.
- LEGO / LEGO building apps: the tactile pleasure of making (sensation), ownership of the finished object, and exhibition and display all fit inside one activity. Observation: the feature is only “assembly,” yet it carries three experiences; it is a model of one feature filling several experience cells.
- YouTube: one person opens the same app to learn through lectures and documentaries (understanding), another to see updates from subscribed channels (relationship), another for background sound before sleep (sensation and ritual), and another for an unexpected video brought by the algorithm (discovery). Observation: the first screen, the Home feed, is assembled as a different experience entrance for each person; identify whose entrance our first screen is.
- Strava: although it records running and cycling, one GPS-recording feature overlaps personal records (achievement), exploring new routes (discovery), segment rankings and Kudos (display and relationship), and exercise at the same time every day (ritual). Observation: a model of one feature filling several experience cells, and a criterion for choosing which feature to put first in the first thirty minutes.

**Offline / Everyday Life**
- Watching baseball at the stadium: one person watches the same game through strategy and statistics (understanding), another enjoys the atmosphere of chants and food (sensation and ritual), another treats it as time with companions (relationship), and another collects uniforms and merchandise (ownership and display). Observation: one piece of content, the game, divides into several experiences in the stands; the assumption that “fun is competition” is only half true even at the ballpark.
- Hiking: one person climbs the same mountain to certify reaching the summit (achievement and display), another for scenery and air (sensation), another to talk with a companion (relationship), and another as a rhythm of walking the same route every week (ritual). Observation: the content, the mountain, is one, yet there are as many reasons to climb as people, and the entrance tells none of them they are wrong.
- Book clubs: the same book is an experience of understanding and world when read alone, relationship when discussed together, and display through reviews and proof of reading. Observation: activities around content, rather than the content alone, change the experience type; consider which layers of activity can surround our game.

## Chapter 13. What Buttons Symbolize: Dissecting Simulated Experience

This collection shows that the chapter's dissection of the sign on the screen, the signifier, from the real event it points to, the signified, works the same way beyond games. A sign is genuine only when an event stands behind it; an eventless sign is exposed faster the more spectacular it is; and the size of the presentation must match the size of the event. The cases are grouped by medium, and each ends with an “Observation” describing what a designer should watch. There are two questions for reading: what experience does this sign point to, and does that experience actually happen?

**Video Games**
- Diablo III's presentation for legendary items: in the 2012 game, when a legendary item drops, a weighty sound rings out, an orange beam rises from the floor, and a star appears on the minimap, making the moment impossible to miss. Early after release, however, the “rarity” represented by the sign often conflicted with actual performance, making the beam close to an empty sign. It is often said that sign and event aligned only after the loot system was substantially reworked. Observation: whether the weight of the event is honestly reflected in the size of the sign; the order of fixing the event, the value of the drop, before enlarging the sign.
- RPG level numbers and experience bars: a rising number points to growth, but without the event of crossing a wall once impassable, only the number rises empty. Observation: what the user becomes able to do when a level rises; whether number and event are paired.
- Peggle's “Extreme Fever” finale: in the 2007 game, when the last orange peg is struck, time slows, the screen follows the ball, Beethoven's “Ode to Joy” erupts, and rainbows and fireworks cross the screen. The presentation is extravagantly exaggerated yet not false, because a real event—clearing the stage—must precede it. Observation: a large event must come first for a large presentation to read as a large cheer; exaggeration itself is not a sin.
- PlayStation's “easy platinum” trophies: the highest-tier trophy originally signified accomplishing something difficult. As mass-produced games that award it after a few button presses and a few minutes flooded the market, the sign lost weight. Major trophy-tracking sites reportedly began excluding such games from rankings, and the platform went as far as removing them from the store and issuing guidelines warning that repeat-offender accounts would be penalized. Observation: a badge issued without the event of qualification reduces the value of everyone bearing that badge; the issuance standard is the value of the sign.
- The craft of game “juice”: screen shake, particles, and impact effects dress actions with satisfaction, but the craft's guidance is known to say that presentation should echo the game's core play. Observation: industry craft itself states this chapter's conclusion—presentation is a mirror that enlarges a real event, not a stage that invents an absent one.

**Live-Action Film**
- Chekhov's gun: Anton Chekhov wrote that if a gun hangs on the wall in the first act, it must later be fired; if it will not be fired, it should not hang there. Observation: a story itself demands that an event follow any sign placed before us; count the guns hanging on our screen that never fire.
- The horror “cat scare”: tense music foretells a threat, but only something harmless, such as a bird crashing through a window, appears. The Resident Evil series uses this sign by deceiving once with a false cue and then releasing the real threat. Observation: repeated false promises of an event make audiences stop trusting the sign itself, though an intentional betrayal that deceives once and then delivers the real thing works within the genre's agreement.
- The lawsuit over the Drive (2011) trailer: it is often recounted that a viewer who went to the theater after seeing a trailer cut like a chase-action film demanded a refund and an end to the advertising when the feature proved closer to a taciturn drama, even filing suit against the distributor. Observation: a trailer is also a sign pointing to the event of the feature; when the promised event and the actual product conflict, the cost returns as refund demands and reputational damage.

**Literature**
- Exaggerated blurbs on covers and belly bands: if a sign such as “the book of a lifetime” is not matched by the event of the text, readers doubt the publisher's next recommendation too. Observation: trust in a sign is diminished at the publisher level, not only one book at a time.
- Macbeth's soliloquy: Macbeth calls life a tale told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, signifying nothing. Observation: one line compresses what remains when the signs of loud sound and passion have no event of meaning behind them.

**Live-Action TV / Drama**
- Exaggerated captions and sound effects in reality shows: tense music and giant captions inflate an uneventful scene. Observation: viewers notice the presentation the moment the sign becomes larger than the event; frequent inflation reduces the value of truly large scenes.
- Sitcom laugh tracks: beginning in the 1950s, sound engineer Charley Douglass used a “Laff Box” of prerecorded laughter, adding and removing it scene by scene to place a sign saying “this is the funny part.” But when a scene was not funny, the track sounded more awkward. As viewers gradually stopped trusting the sign, its use declined. Observation: directing a response through a sign backfires when it conflicts with the event; audience response is made by the event, not the sign.

**Music**
- Excessive Auto-Tune and crowd-chorus effects: they imitate the event of emotion through sound processing, but when the song contains no such emotion, the effect floats free. Observation: postproduction can enlarge only an emotion that already exists; an absent event cannot be created in the mix.
- Milli Vanilli: the sign of singers on stage was perfect, but the event of singing was performed by others. A lip-synced recording skipped onstage and exposed them, and their 1990 Best New Artist Grammy was reportedly rescinded for the first time in the award's history. Observation: when only the sign, the appearance of performance, exists and the event, actual singing, is empty, even the applause already received is annulled after exposure.

**Comics**
- Speed lines and written sound effects: even if signs that something major is happening fill the panel, exaggeration alone remains when the actual event is small. Observation: matching the size of the sign to the size of the event is equally required in panel presentation.

**General Apps**
- Empty social-media clones: they have Like buttons but no people exchanging likes. They copy the sign while leaving the event empty. Observation: when a feature, the sign, is copied from a competitor, the people and activity that made it work, the event, do not follow.
- LinkedIn skill endorsements: unlike the profile-strength bar, which works because it is tied to real benefits, endorsements that anyone can give anyone with one click accumulate even from people who have never worked together, blurring what they prove. Observation: even within one service, trust divides between signs with and without the event of verification.
- Red-dot notifications with no news: some apps place a red dot on an icon when there is nothing to tap. When the sign “a new event exists” repeats without an event, users soon learn to ignore the dot entirely. Such designs for capturing attention are also discussed as dark patterns. Observation: the speed at which a sign's currency loses value; count the dots and badges on our screen printed without events.
- Fake close buttons in ads: some ads draw an X that leads to the advertiser's page rather than closing, or make it deliberately too small to press. The moment the sign “close” betrays the event of closing, users doubt the screen's other signs too. Observation: one sign's betrayal spreads into a cost of trust for the whole screen.

**History / General Cases**
- Potemkin villages: the story says that along Catherine II's inspection route, only handsome village facades were erected, dismantled after the procession passed, and rebuilt on the next riverbank, though many consider the anecdote itself exaggerated. Observation: regardless of its historical truth, the phrase became a universal name for an empty facade because the structure of a sign without substance is so common.

**Offline / Everyday Life**
- Nonfunctioning pedestrian buttons: a 2004 New York City report said that more than 2,500 of the city's 3,250 pedestrian buttons remained disconnected after signal automation. Some elevator Close buttons are often said to share the same fate. They are placebo buttons that accept the sign of pressing without the event of changing the signal. Observation: a false sign left to soothe the need for control works until exposed; after exposure, the sign of buttons as a whole is doubted.
- Restaurant signs claiming “the original”: when several restaurants in one alley all hang “original” signs, no sign proves the event of being first. Anyone can hang the sign, while the event happened at only one establishment. Observation: the more common unverifiable signs become, the more the value of the entire sign falls; the alley shares the structure of “easy platinum.”

## Chapter 14. The Harmony of Surprise and Familiarity: Four Principles and Flow

This collection gathers cases from beyond games that repeat the chapter's claims: four principles make the first screen readable—intuitiveness, familiarity, consistency, and a low barrier to entry; the mixture rule is “make one unfamiliar and three familiar”; and novelty should be placed in the result beyond the hand rather than in the controls beneath it. The cases are grouped by medium, and each ends with an “Observation” describing what a designer should watch. There is one way to read them: count what is familiar, which single thing is new, and whether novelty is concentrated in one place or scattered.

**Video Games**
- Super Mario Bros. 1-1: without a line of text, the 1985 game teaches players within their first few steps to avoid and stomp a Goomba, hit a question block, and distinguish a mushroom from an enemy. Miyamoto is often quoted in substance as saying that the moment players discover for themselves what to do, the game becomes theirs. Observation: the archetype of an explanation-free opening that teaches through flow; the amount is cut so only one new thing appears on each screen.
- Half-Life's opening tram: the 1998 game does not begin with a cutscene that takes control away. It places players on a slow monorail, lets them look around, and teaches through their hands that doors respond when approached. The environment itself teaches without guidance text. Observation: beginning through experience instead of explanation; keeping familiar first-person controls and concentrating novelty in the world.
- Resident Evil 4: rather than asking players to choose difficulty before starting, the 2005 game is known to read combat performance through a hidden score and adjust enemy aggression, durability, and drops in real time—tighter when a player performs well, looser when they struggle. Observation: adaptive difficulty that lets the game adjust without asking users to declare their skill; removing the wall of a first choice before newcomers.
- Animal Crossing: there is no game over or failure screen, and Tom Nook waits without pressing players even if they do not repay their debt. After short tutorial tasks, it releases them to play their own way without a fixed answer. Observation: undefeated onboarding that does not turn failure into a verdict; rhythm and attachment can grow without game over.
- Portal: the 2007 game preserves the familiar first-person-shooter gestures of WASD movement and mouse aiming, concentrating novelty into one thing, a portal gun connecting two places. Early test chambers let players fire one portal and then connect two, learning rules one piece at a time through their hands. Observation: a textbook mixture of “make three familiar and one unfamiliar”; novelty is placed beyond the hand, in space, not beneath it, in the controls.
- QWOP / Getting Over It: counterexamples that deliberately break familiar controls and pile up only novelty. The unfamiliarity is their identity, but it is a wall to many newcomers. Observation: unfamiliarity itself can be sold as identity, but that path targets the minority who read unfamiliarity as challenge rather than the majority.
- Dark Souls: the tutorial's first boss demon is a fight designed to encourage an under-equipped player to escape rather than confront it. “YOU DIED” and the bonfire checkpoint combine into the signal “try again after death.” Observation: the same death screen reads as an invitation to gamers and a verdict to ordinary people; a single failure presentation is translated in opposite directions according to origin.
- Angry Birds / Candy Crush: the first stage yields to a finger, and difficulty rises only after people decide to stay. Candy Crush is known to continue refining hard stages during live operation. Observation: the first stage's difficulty is an entrance guide rather than an exam; bind the difficulty curve so it does not outrun the skill curve.

**Animated Film**
- Disney and Pixar's new worlds atop familiar three-act structures: the story's skeleton remains in a familiar form, and novelty is concentrated in the world. Observation: familiarity of structure earns the budget needed to bear an unfamiliar world.

**Live-Action Film**
- Scream: the 1996 film observes nearly all the familiar grammar of slashers—a masked killer, a group of teenagers, an isolated house—while adding only one new layer: characters who know the rules of horror and speak them aloud. Observation: how “keep three and twist only one” works in a genre film; novelty concentrated into one twist.
- Star Wars: the 1977 film built an entirely unfamiliar world in space while borrowing the story skeleton from the most familiar hero narratives and Westerns. Lucas is often said to have acknowledged influence from Kurosawa's The Hidden Fortress. Observation: when the whole world is new, familiar structure and character types let audiences enter; the novelty budget is spent in the single cell of world.

**Literature**
- Regression, reincarnation, transmigration, and isekai fiction: these works borrow the entire familiar fantasy framework of medieval swords and magic, adventure and growth, even game-like levels and statistics, then add only one new device: beginning again in another world after death. Observation: lowering the entry threshold by concentrating novelty at one point atop a familiar frame; genre convention is a shared inventory of familiarity.
- Finnegans Wake: it simultaneously pushes a style mixing many languages, a circular structure whose final sentence returns to the first, and a narrative following dream logic, making even a plot summary difficult. Observation: when novelty is not concentrated but spread across the entire work, it becomes difficulty rather than attraction.

**Board Games**
- Catan / Ticket to Ride: they place one new core rule atop familiar actions such as rolling dice, using cards, and connecting routes, allowing newcomers to finish the first game. Observation: entry classics share the rule “make actions familiar and only one rule new.”
- Beginner-friendly “easy first game” variants: the first game is arranged to make getting stuck nearly impossible, bringing people inside before raising the full difficulty. Observation: difficulty before flow begins and difficulty after it begins are designed separately.

**Live-Action TV / Drama**
- One variation on a familiar format: crime and medical dramas borrow three familiar parts of the frame and make only one setting new, bringing viewers in quickly. Observation: serialized television effectively must pass a “four-principle score” every week; check whether the number of variations remains one.

**Music**
- The I–V–vi–IV four-chord progression: songs as different as U2's “With or Without You,” Bob Marley's “No Woman, No Cry,” and Adele's “Someone Like You” share the same four-chord skeleton, adding one new melody over familiar harmony. Comedy groups' “four-chord” medleys that string together many hits over one progression are often cited. Observation: placing one new tune atop a familiar foundation is a basic technique of popular music; even with the same foundation, a different new cell creates a different song.

**Devices / Consumer-Electronics UX**
- Tesla's consolidation into a large touchscreen: it moved almost every physical button other than climate and gear controls onto one screen, replacing familiarity at the fingertips with novelty all at once. Criticism often notes drivers leaving their lane while reaching for the screen, and European safety-assessment criteria are reportedly moving toward requiring physical controls for essential functions. Observation: what is charged when familiarity is withdrawn all at once; the cost of a screen with two or three overlapping “new” cells can also be calculated in units of safety.
- The iPhone's first multitouch: the first iPhone in 2007 kept screen and icon layout familiar and made only one thing new, directly swiping and pinching with fingers. Even that new action borrowed familiarity from the habits of hands touching physical objects. Observation: a double borrowing that makes even the single novelty depend on an existing bodily habit.
- The Nintendo Wii Remote: the 2006 Wii chose the shape of a TV remote instead of a gamepad. Developers said in interviews, in substance, that no one would hesitate to pick up a remote everyone already held at home. The familiar object lowered the entry barrier, and novelty was placed in the result beyond the hand—the screen responding to a swing. Observation: hardware can also place novelty beyond the hand, in response, rather than beneath it, in the input device.
- Self-service ordering kiosks: they seem intuitive to their makers, but for a first-time user, the layout, terminology, and payment procedure are all new at once. Add the social pressure of a line behind them, and studies report that orders become rushed and smaller. Observation: familiarity is judged by the user's hand, not the designer's mind; the number of “new” cells on the same screen is counted differently by each user.

**App UX**
- Duolingo: rather than forcing sign-up and login first, it lets users begin the first lesson immediately and recommends creating an account only after progress accumulates and becomes worth saving. Observation: a tangible example of lowering the barrier to the first action; the cost of sign-up is deferred until after value.
- TikTok's one-finger feed: it combines discovery, selection, and playback into a single upward swipe, reducing controls to one action everyone knows while placing novelty in the feed, where no one knows what comes next. Observation: borrowing the input and placing surprise in the result reduces control learning almost to zero seconds.
- Toss's simple money transfer: it reportedly gained ground by reducing transfers that once required certificates and security devices across many steps to a few taps resembling a message sent through a messenger app. The hand performs familiar taps; novelty lies in the result that the procedure disappeared. Observation: which steps fixed as “just how the process works” can be folded into familiar gestures; identify the equivalent of the old certificate in our own field.

## Chapter 15. Feedback: The World Responds Behind the Button

The examples collected here did not fit in the main text. They are grouped under headings by medium, from video games to music, theater, and everyday machines. Each entry ends with a "What to Observe" note explaining what a designer should examine. Keep the framework established in the main text in mind: the three layers of the physical answer that the hand made contact, the answer that the input was reflected in the rules, and the answer that meaning was created; and the distinction between the silence that omits the first answer and stops a person, and the silence that omits meaning and drives a person away. You will then see which layer of an answer arrives in what order in each case and where silence arises. Read with an eye for separating examples that make answers bigger from those that make them clearer.

**Video Games**
- Screen shake in Nuclear Throne: Every hit shakes the screen and sends enemies flying, amplifying the feel of impact. Developer Vlambeer is known to have organized and shared in a talk its techniques for building game feel by layering exaggerations such as shake, brief pauses, and knockback. What to Observe: Consider this alongside the main text's caveat that exaggeration is not automatically a good answer. The same shake is pleasure to a player who knows the agreement but noise to a newcomer. Ask what intensity is appropriate for whom.
- Quick-time events (QTEs) that suddenly appear during cutscenes: A person thinks they are passively watching a video when a prompt such as "Press X" suddenly appears. If the screen does not respond immediately to the press, they doubt whether the input registered and press the same button repeatedly. The silence left by a missing first-layer answer leads directly to failure. What to Observe: Watch whether people respond to the very brief silence after an input by button mashing. Repeated presses are the most honest signal that the first-layer answer is missing.
- Hit stop in fighting games: At the instant an attack lands, both characters freeze very briefly before moving again, letting a person physically feel that the hit connected. Stronger attacks stop for longer, conveying the weight of the answer as well. This makes an answer clear with a beat of stillness rather than enlarging it with spectacle. What to Observe: Notice that answers have weight, not just speed. The scale of the answer is taught through the duration of the pause without adding anything to the screen, clearly revealing the difference between making something bigger and making it clearer.
- Character-by-character speech sounds in the Animal Crossing series: A short vocal sound plays as each character appears in a dialogue box, creating the sense that the character is actually speaking. It is said to have become so central to the series' identity that fans call it "Animalese." What to Observe: Follow the path by which a low-level answer that sonifies progress itself also supports the third-layer meaning of a character's vitality. Even a modest answer becomes communion when it does not break.
- The puppy-petting response in Nintendogs (2005): Touch a puppy with the stylus on Nintendo DS, and it immediately reacts toward that spot and makes a pleased sound. The answer to contact and the meaningful answer that "my puppy liked it" arrive together, creating a model of the third layer in which a person feels genuine communion with life inside a screen. What to Observe: See how the answer to contact and the answer of meaning follow one another within a single action. Because the response begins exactly where the touch landed, the causality stays clear.
- Tetris Effect and Lumines: Every action of placing blocks and clearing lines interlocks with music, vibration, and visuals, making the entire world respond together. Each action returns as a musical answer, expanding the third layer until a person feels their hand and the world breathing on the same beat. What to Observe: Look beyond the answer to one action and see how far the third layer can expand when the whole world breathes to the user's rhythm. The many answers do not become noise because every one is aligned to a single beat.
- Hit markers and elimination sounds in FPS games: When a distant enemy is hit, a brief cross appears over the reticle and a tick sounds. Eliminating the enemy produces a second, different sound. At distances where the target is too small to tell from the image alone whether a shot landed, dedicated signals separately return the fact of a hit and the fact of a kill. What to Observe: Treat this as an example of separating the second-layer answer—reflection in the rules—into an independent signal. Examine which signal takes over when the on-screen performance cannot convey that answer.
- Spreading cracks while breaking a block in Minecraft: As a block is mined, cracks spread gradually across its surface, maintaining an uninterrupted answer that the long action is still in progress. What to Observe: Notice that an answer must continue even for an action whose result has not yet arrived. The longer the gap between the first answer and the final result, the more an intermediate answer must fill the silence.

**Live-Action Film**
- Sound feedback in ASMR and mukbang: Tapping, rustling, and chewing sounds themselves return a sensory answer that something was touched. A clear answer holds attention even without spectacle. What to Observe: This shows the main text's proposition that clarity alone can constitute an answer, in its purest form. Use it to practice separating the intensity of an answer from its clarity.

**Music**
- Call and response: One side sings and the other immediately answers, demonstrating the causality of an immediate reply to an input through sound. What to Observe: Because the interval before the answer is promised in units of beats, the body can feel what timing allows a person to perceive causality.
- Buildup and drop in dance music: Tension accumulates, one beat is left empty, and then the bass drops and releases it. Song structure itself shows that an answer must arrive in the order of building, emptying, and dropping rather than erupting all at once for people to feel causality and pleasure. A weak buildup also weakens the drop's answer. What to Observe: Pair this with the section arguing that the moment when an answer arrives is itself a design target. The short emptiness before the drop does the same work as the interval that clarifies causality.

**Live-Action Television / Drama**
- Correct- and incorrect-answer sounds on quiz shows: A sound answers immediately when a button is pressed, and the meaningful answer that it was correct follows. The order of the two answers is clear. What to Observe: Use this as the simplest model of an answer confirming the press followed by an answer confirming correctness. Imagine what disappears if the two answers are merged into one sound, and the value of their order becomes apparent.
- Reaction shots in sitcoms, such as looking at the camera in The Office: One beat after an absurd event, a character briefly glances at the camera. The answer "Can you believe this?" attaches to the event at the precise moment that releases laughter. What to Observe: See how editing timing separates an immediate answer from a meaningful answer one beat later. The same scene ceases to be funny if the reaction shot comes too early or too late, confirming that the timing of an answer matters as much as its content.

**Board Games**
- Immediate reactions in Jenga and Halli Galli: The moment the blocks collapse or the bell rings is the answer itself. Action and result are attached as one event. What to Observe: The original form of the answer that digital feedback imitates exists in the physical world. Use the feeling of action and result forming one event as a reference point, then measure how far apart they have become on your screen.

**Theater / Performance**
- Applause and laughter from the audience: The audience answers a performer's action immediately. If the answer is late or absent, the person onstage also loses their rhythm. What to Observe: Silence destabilizes not only the user but also the rhythm of the maker. A performer before a silent audience is a designer before a screen without metrics.

**General Apps**
- Transfer-complete animations in Toss and Kakao: A small animation clearly returns the fact that the money was sent, letting a person know at a glance that it is done. What to Observe: The greater the anxiety around an action, as with money, the clearer the meaningful answer must be. If the answer is vague, people reopen the transfer history to check it. That act of reconfirmation is itself an indicator that the third layer is weak.
- Smartphone haptics: A single vibration at the fingertip immediately returns the lowest-layer answer that the screen was pressed. What to Observe: When one lowest-layer answer underlies every screen, examine how much total silence disappears. This is a starting point for designing system-level and screen-level answers separately.
- KakaoTalk's unread marker "1": After a message is sent, a number beside the bubble says that the recipient has not yet read it. The number disappears when they do. The answer that it was sent and the answer that it was read arrive separately. What to Observe: Treat this as a structure separating the second and third layers. Making the state of "read but unanswered" visible created a new kind of silence, showing that a design which reveals answers can also create new emotions.
- Instagram's double-tap Like: Double-tap a photo and a large heart rises and disappears at the point of contact while the Like count increases. What to Observe: Notice that the answer begins exactly where the hand touched. The farther the answer appears from the point of contact, the weaker the causality becomes.

**Machines / Appliance UX**
- The pressed light and beep of elevator and microwave buttons: These immediately answer that the input arrived, keeping people from pressing the same button again. What to Observe: Repeatedly pressing the same button is the cheapest indicator of first-layer silence. The sight of people mashing a door-close button is a landscape of silence.
- The click of a car turn signal: Sound and light answer simultaneously that the signal is on, so the driver knows the state without looking at a screen. The sound originally came from a relay component. Even after cars moved to electronic components, many are said to deliberately replay it through a speaker. What to Observe: Look at the decision to preserve a sound specifically as an answer after its functional necessity disappeared. Here it becomes clear that an answer is not a by-product but a design target.
- Haptics for smartphone setting wheels and toggles: Turning a date or time wheel produces a tiny tap at the fingertip with each step; switching on a toggle produces a click-like vibration. A single vibration imitates the detent of a physical dial, returning through the fingertip the first-layer answer a screen alone would lack. What to Observe: See how a screen restores the first-layer answer once supplied by a physical dial. Fine-grained answers at each step fill the silence of a continuous action.
- Locking and unlocking melodies on digital door locks: Different melodies play when the door locks and unlocks, so the fact that it locked reaches even a person who has turned away and is walking off. What to Observe: Examine a channel that delivers an answer to someone who is looking at neither a screen nor a light. Assigning distinct sounds to two states reveals not only that an answer exists but also what state resulted.

## Chapter 16. Designing for Distraction: Buttons and LEDs Amid Noise and Clutter

The cases collected here show how this chapter's premise—that a user encounters a screen in the middle of distraction—is handled across different media and machines. They are grouped by medium, from video games to broadcasting, print, and real-world guidance systems. Each entry ends with "What to Observe." Keep in mind the distinction established in the main text between a signal a passing person receives from the corner of the eye and an explanation read by someone who has stopped, as well as the asymmetry through which design made for the worst conditions helps everyone else. You will then see what each case leaves as a signal and what it postpones as explanation. It is also useful to read while identifying which of the three responsibilities—what to press now, the current state, or the current problem—each device performs.

**Video Games**
- Tamagotchi's call sound and attention icon: This portable virtual-pet device from the mid-1990s plays a short call and lights one attention icon in the corner when the creature is hungry or needs care. Without explaining at length what is needed and why, one signal says, "Look at me now." Even a distracted person busy with something else returns because of that single signal. What to Observe: The signal follows the person into a pocket and only calls them back. Explanation waits until they take out the device and look at it. See the division of labor between signal and explanation implemented throughout a single device.
- Packed HUDs and red notification badges in MMORPGs: A screen where information to gamers accumulates all at once as noise a newcomer cannot read. What to Observe: See the asymmetry through which the same screen is information to someone who knows how to read it and noise to someone who does not. It is training material for looking at your own screen again through eyes that have not yet learned how to read it.
- Candy Crush's simple first screen: Only the one thing to press now shines large, so even after looking away and returning, the next action is immediately visible. What to Observe: Treat it as a model of a screen where "what to press now" shines. Its score is whether a returning person moves to the next action without hesitation.
- Journey's HUD-free screen (2012): With no health number, map, or score, the length and shimmer of the character's scarf show how much flight remains available. What to Observe: Treat it as an example of moving state display from a gauge at the screen's edge into an object within the world. It shares the character of the main text's LED story, in which the whole screen should emit one state like an indicator.

**Live-Action Film**
- Frantic rapid-cut editing in action movies: When too many things are shown at once, the audience misses the one shot that matters. What to Observe: See the paradox that increasing the amount of information decreases what is conveyed. Well-made action instead clears the screen immediately before the most important action to focus the eye in one place.

**Live-Action Television / Drama**
- Dense caption strips and breaking-news tickers: The more information fills the screen, the more the single important line becomes buried for someone watching from the corner of the eye. What to Observe: Notice the trap that every item is important from the channel's perspective. The sender's importance and the receiver's attention move in different units.
- Home-shopping screens covered with prices, captions, and phone numbers: Some instead show one large reason to buy for distracted viewers. What to Observe: Treat this as a field where two strategies—covering and clearing—divide over the same distracted viewer. Compare which one more accurately assumes the viewer's state.
- The score bug in sports broadcasts: Score and time or inning remain in one corner, allowing someone who joins midway to understand the situation with one sideways glance. This permanent on-screen score display is said to have become established in broadcasts during the 1990s. What to Observe: Treat it as an original device that states "the current state" without being asked. A design that lets someone catch up whenever they join does the same work as a screen that receives a user returning after looking away.

**Comics**
- Focus lines in comics: Lines converge toward one point in a panel, drawing the reader's eye to the one place that matters now. The panel does not render everything at equal weight; it is a spotlight on paper that gathers attention only at the most important point. What to Observe: See that the principle of suppressing the rest to make one thing shine is the same on paper. A panel where every element carries equal weight leaves no room for focus lines.

**Music**
- Background sound and announcements in stores and airports: Because people listen only halfway, truly important guidance is reduced to a short signal sound and one sentence. What to Observe: Examine the structure reduced to one signal and one sentence on the premise of a half-listening person. The key is that more important guidance becomes shorter, not longer.

**Theater / Performance**
- A stage spotlight: Instead of illuminating the entire stage, it gathers light only on the one performer the audience should watch now. What to Observe: Notice that illuminating and leaving dark form one operation. What a first screen decides to turn off is its lighting design.

**Physical Arcade Machines**
- Large buttons and a blinking "INSERT COIN" in an arcade: A design that makes the one action to take now shine large even in a loud, chaotic place. What to Observe: Examine the size, blinking, and position of signals refined in an environment where noise and competition are at their worst. Imagining a screen drawn in a quiet office placed in the middle of an arcade makes a useful test.

**Real-World Procedures**
- Airplane cabin exit lights and pictograms: For a panicked person, one pictorial signal points only to where to go now instead of using text. What to Observe: See the asymmetry through which design made for panic, the most severe distracted state, remains legible to everyone in ordinary conditions. The curb-cut principle has long been common sense in safety design.
- Colored route guidance in hospitals and airports: Instead of explaining an entire complicated route, one colored line on the floor can be followed without getting lost. What to Observe: See the method of providing one next step after another instead of making someone memorize the whole map. It does not merely reduce explanation; it removes it and leaves only the signal.
- From dot to dot on a subway map: It discards real geography, leaving straight and colored lines so that a person only needs to follow one color from the current dot to a destination dot. This approach is said to have originated with the diagrammatic London Underground map of the 1930s. What to Observe: See how deciding to discard accurate geographic information does not deprive people of information but gathers their attention. A signal becomes clear only to the extent that someone chose what to remove.

**General Apps**
- One next instruction in car navigation: Instead of displaying the entire route, it shows only "Turn right next" in large type, remaining legible while driving. What to Observe: See the restraint that distinguishes what the system knows from what it should show now. A system that possesses the full route tells the driver only the next move.
- Apps with one-handed mode and large bottom buttons: They place what must be pressed now where the thumb can reach in a shaking place, rather than turning an upper-corner button into no button at all. What to Observe: Confirm with your hands the main text's sentence that an unreachable button effectively does not exist. Hold the phone in one hand and press each button on your screen.
- Google's empty first screen: One search box sits in the center of a white background, so even a newcomer knows at a glance what to do. The empty space around it is not vacant but a device that gathers the eye on that one thing. What to Observe: See how emptiness itself is a function. It is a model for how far one can resist the urge to add things out of fear that a screen will look sparse.
- Error screens rewritten in human language: More screens now say, "We couldn't find that page. Return to the start?" instead of codes such as "Error 404." The maker's language requires another thought, but the user's language lands in one sideways glance. What to Observe: Reread the sentence through the main text's criterion separating signal and explanation. If its meaning lands in a glance, it is a signal. If it requires thought, it is an explanation.
- One-line state displays in delivery and taxi apps: They display only the current stage in large text—"Your order is being prepared" or "Your driver is on the way"—and fold the details away until pressed. What to Observe: Treat this as a state display built directly on the pattern of users glancing, checking, and returning to something else. The assumption that a user does not stand watch over the screen reaches all the way down to the sentence.
- Continue Watching in video apps: The content someone was watching appears in the first row and resumes from the scene where they stopped. What to Observe: Treat it as a device that prevents a returning person from asking, "What was I doing?" Whether a returner immediately resumes their task or wanders from the beginning is the indicator recommended in the main text.

**Machines / Appliance UX**
- Master caution and warning lights in an airplane cockpit: Whatever system develops a problem, a large light in the pilot's line of sight first says, "Look now." A two-light arrangement is generally said to assign immediate action to a red master warning and inspection to an amber master caution. Which component failed and why is checked on the next panel. The first signal consists only of color and blinking. What to Observe: See the two-stage division of labor in which attention is captured first and content is left to the next step. It also lets us infer in reverse what breaks when the first signal tries to carry the explanation too.
- The iPhone's silent switch: For many years, it was a physical switch on the side. Without turning on the screen, a person could feel its position with a fingertip to know whether silent mode was active and confirm it at a glance through the exposed orange color. Recent models have replaced it with a pressable button. What to Observe: See a design that makes one state legible through two channels: touch and a sideways glance. The sense that even turning on a screen is a fee is inscribed in the hardware.
- Distinct left and right vibrations in smartwatch navigation: Wrist-device navigation is said to use different vibration patterns for left and right turns, telling people which way to turn without looking at the screen. What to Observe: Treat it as a design that moves the signal into touch under the worst condition, when the eyes are fixed on the road. Signals can change senses, and the signal that avoids the busiest sense survives.
- Completion melodies on rice cookers and washing machines: When cooking or washing ends, a melody reaches someone doing something else in another room. What to Observe: Treat it as a design that fully accepts the premise that the user is not standing in front of the machine. It is the appliance version of the main text's claim that the person is not looking only at that screen.

## Chapter 17. Errors and Recovery: How Not to Take Away a Sense of Control

The cases collected here show how this chapter's argument—lay recovery down first instead of eliminating failure—appears inside and outside games. They are grouped by medium, and each entry ends with "What to Observe." Keep in mind the framework established in the main text: the philosophy of preserving failure without taking away control, the distinction in stakes through which tension belongs to this one attempt while accumulated progress and possessions remain protected, and language that does not pass the system's fault onto the user. You will then see what each failure makes someone lose and what it protects, and whether that failure reads as punishment or rhythm.

**Video Games**
- Super Meat Boy (2010): Death immediately restarts the section, reducing the interval between failure and retry almost to zero. After clearing the section, a replay shows every failed attempt running simultaneously on one screen, turning accumulated deaths into a record rather than shame. What to Observe: Alongside the principle that a faster restart turns failure from punishment into rhythm, watch the ending that returns traces of death as a spectacle. Handling failure has two layers: immediate speed and retrospective meaning.
- Prince of Persia: The Sands of Time (2003) and Braid (2008): Immediately after a mistake, time can be rewound to undo even the protagonist's death along the same trace. After falling to his death, the narrator prince says, "No, that is not how it happened," corrects the story, and returns to the previous moment. That one line renames death from "failure" to "a story told incorrectly," turning the same failure from punishment into rhythm. What to Observe: Alongside rewind as a mechanism, see how the language used to name failure changes the texture of the experience. The same function can be received differently depending on the sentence the screen offers.
- Console and PC autosave, and Resetti in Animal Crossing: In early Animal Crossing games, turning off the power without saving caused the mole Resetti to appear and deliver a long scolding. In New Horizons (2020), where autosave became the default and the system held onto progress automatically, even that scolding role disappeared. What to Observe: When the era of saving as the user's homework ended, the need for a scolding character disappeared with it. When the system takes responsibility, there are fewer reasons to reprimand the user.
- Hades (2020): Death is not the end of progress but a beat in the story. Every return home after death brings different dialogue from characters such as Hypnos, who points out what defeated the player this time. What to Observe: See a structure that attaches a new story to every failure, making death itself a reward. If immediately after failure is the moment when people are most likely to leave, this example decides in advance what to give them at precisely that moment.
- Cuphead's boss retry screen (2017): After death, a progress line shows which stage of the boss was reached, putting "You got this far" first, then immediately restarts the boss. What to Observe: Notice that the failure screen shows progress before loss. When progress is visible, the same failure becomes one a person wants to try again.
- Super Mario's Super Guide and Invincibility Leaf: Beginning with New Super Mario Bros. Wii (2009), dying several times in the same section produces a block that demonstrates play. Later games are known to quietly offer items such as a white Tanooki leaf that grants invincibility. They do not force a difficulty choice at the beginning. What to Observe: Notice the order in which help arrives as a suggestion after observing how someone is blocked, rather than as an advance choice. Observe before asking, and offer a hand only after observing.

**Literature**
- Save and undo in interactive fiction such as Zork: They allow readers to reverse a choice and test another path, preventing mistakes from becoming dead ends. What to Observe: See how the guarantee of reversibility leads a reader toward bolder choices. Recovery increases the amount of exploration.

**Board Games**
- Allowing takebacks in introductory cooperative games such as Pandemic: When a beginner makes an obvious mistake, allow one move to be taken back so the first game does not become punishment. What to Observe: See facilitation that protects the first game's experience above strict adherence to rules. A takeback does not ruin the game but invites another one. Among experts, the same takeback reduces tension, revealing that recovery mechanisms also have an audience and a time.

**Film**
- Edge of Tomorrow (2014) and Groundhog Day (1993): The protagonist returns to the beginning of the same day after every death, but memory and skill accumulate, turning death from an ending into practice for the next attempt. What to Observe: See the distinction in stakes—only that day is lost, while everything learned remains—translated into film grammar. It forms a useful bridge when explaining to audiences outside games how a gamer learns one run.

**Animation / Comics**
- Return by Death in Re:Zero − Starting Life in Another World: Death returns the protagonist to a fixed point to begin again, but the pain and memory of death return with him. What to Observe: Treat it as a variation that prices recovery. Everything can be undone, but not for free. It helps calibrate that degree, while reminding us of the emotional dimension of recovery: what the user experienced remains even after returning.

**Live-Action Television / Drama**
- Prerecording and broadcast delay for live television: A short interval before transmission allows an accident to be reversed so an unfixable mistake does not go out unchanged. What to Observe: See how deliberately inserting delay before an irreversible action creates room for recovery. Buying time can be another path instead of displaying a confirmation dialog.

**Music**
- Punching in during recording, rerecording only the mistaken bar: The performer does not sing the entire song again from the beginning, but restarts only at the blocked measure. What to Observe: See how different "restart immediately where blocked" feels from returning to the beginning. Preserving the parts that went well preserves the desire to retry.

**General Apps**
- Undo (Ctrl+Z), Trash, and restoration periods: Anything can be undone, and even deleted items receive a period in which they can be restored. What to Observe: See the main text's proposition that a reliable path back reduces the need to hide dangerous actions or obstruct them with confirmation dialogs. The Trash is why a Delete button does not feel frightening.
- Server error screens, such as GitHub's unicorn 500 page: They do not blame a system-caused failure on the user and lightly communicate that the user did nothing wrong. What to Observe: See blame-free language that turns attention toward a solution rather than scolding over what someone did or did not do. One sentence placing the source of the fault clearly on the system protects trust.
- Continuous autosave and version history in Google Docs and Figma: There is no separate Save button. Every change is preserved immediately, and if needed, a version from days earlier can be restored. What to Observe: Treat it as an example that makes "do not lose information" a default rather than a feature. Once saving ceases to be homework, people experiment without fearing loss.
- Forgiving input handling: An input field accepts hyphens or spaces in a phone number and spacing in a card number. If the meaning is clear despite a slightly different format, let it pass; do not make the user adapt something the machine can adapt. What to Observe: Treat it as a stage before handling an error well: a design that never turns it into an error in the first place. The principle of not burdening users with something they cannot fix descends to the level of an input field.
- Steam's refund policy: A purchase can be refunded without a major justification within two hours of play and fourteen days of purchase. It is said that once a recovery path appeared for the formerly hard-to-reverse action of buying, more people became willing to try unfamiliar games. What to Observe: See whether the main text's principle—that reversibility makes people bold before trying, not only after a mistake—also holds at the scale of commerce.
- Delayed bank transfer services: When enabled, transferred money arrives after a set period and can be canceled before then, creating time to reverse voice-phishing transfers or money sent by mistake. Such services have existed in South Korea since 2015. What to Observe: Treat it as a design that inserts one deliberate layer of delay into an irreversible action to create a recovery window. Sometimes one layer of delay is a stronger safeguard than one confirmation dialog.

**Machines / Appliance UX**
- Error-code displays on microwave ovens and washing machines: They clearly present the cause of a stop as a device state rather than the user's fault. What to Observe: Notice that placing the source of the cause on the machine prevents user self-blame. The code itself is still the maker's language, so also ask whether one line in the user's language can accompany it.
- Canceling an elevator floor by pressing its button twice: It lets someone undo a mistakenly selected floor immediately at the machine. Support is said to vary by model. What to Observe: A recovery method that exists but is unknown is equivalent to no method. Do not merely create the path back; make it visible.
- Start and gear confirmation procedures in cars: They add a physical step before an irreversible action so a momentary mistake cannot cause disaster. What to Observe: See the selectivity of adding steps only before irreversible actions. Adding a step to everything merely makes the system slow. Safety comes from adding one only where an action cannot be undone.
- A "Start Over" button on an unattended kiosk: It remains in the same place on every ordering screen, so a lost person is not trapped in a dead end. What to Observe: The fact that the recovery path is always visible in the same place protects a sense of control. More people benefit from knowing it exists and feeling reassured than from actually pressing it.

**Real-World Procedures**
- Checklists in aviation and medicine: They do not assign mistakes to individual responsibility, but recover from and prevent them through procedure. What to Observe: A culture that treats mistakes not as personal defects but as procedural matters is the institutional version of blame-free error language. It fixes the structure instead of trying to fix the person.
- Flight-simulator training: People learn through repeated failure in an environment where failure is not fatal. Failure is not a dead end but the starting line for another attempt. What to Observe: See the idea of creating a separate space where the cost of failure is artificially lowered. The first-experience section can be designed as a kind of simulator: a place where failure loses nothing.

## Chapter 18. Previewing the Experience and a Hundred First Impressions

These references, gathered from film, games, publishing, music, and everyday displays and grouped under media-specific headings, illuminate this chapter's argument that the first experience begins not on the first screen but in the preview that allowed a person to anticipate it. Each item ends with a “What to examine” note. Keep the chapter's framework in mind: a preview is a promise and must promise only what can be kept; it should show the big picture while saving the substance; and being chosen in one encounter differs from the durability to withstand repeated viewing. Then each case reveals what it promised, whether the promise was kept, and where any mismatch occurred.

**Live-Action Film**
- Movie trailers: The original form of the preview, showing the big picture while saving the ending and offering only a promise worth coming to see. What to examine: Observe the restraint that establishes tone, genre, and expectation within a few minutes while preserving the substance. A trailer's skill lies in what it ultimately refuses to show.
- Trailers that reveal all the feature's best scenes: By exposing the substance in advance, they become spoilers that leave nothing to receive from the work itself. What to examine: Treat this as an example of the boundary between big picture and substance collapsing. Fixating only on being chosen spends the experience of the feature before it begins.
- *Burn After Reading* (2008): This Coen brothers film is often cited as having been marketed as a lively comedy although the film itself was much darker and drier. What to examine: A promise of tone is as powerful as a promise of plot. People feel deceived faster when the texture is wrong than when factual content differs.
- *Drive* (2011): A viewer drawn in by a trailer cut like a fast chase movie reportedly sued for a refund over the feature's slower mood; the court found that the trailer was not inaccurate. What to examine: Even if a trailer is not factually false, it is read as a promise. Legal fault and user disappointment occur on different layers.
- Teaser trailers: They conceal the ending and show only one restrained piece of the big picture. What to examine: Find the range in which reducing information increases curiosity. Conceal too much and people cannot even tell what the work is; a teaser still needs a minimum amount of big picture.

**Video Games**
- Game trailers: Because a preview is a promise, backlash is strong when the experience promised by a spectacular video differs from the feel of actual play. What to examine: Evaluate production quality and accuracy of promise separately. A good preview is not the most impressive one but one that can be kept.
- *Cyberpunk 2077* (2020): Soon after launch, frequent crashes and severe performance problems made the game effectively unplayable on older consoles such as PS4 and Xbox One. It was eventually removed from the PlayStation Store for a time and refunds were offered. What to examine: The greater the expectation gathered by a preview, the more the gap at first launch becomes an event. The size of a promise is the leverage of disappointment.
- *Aliens: Colonial Marines* (2013): Differences in graphics and polish between demonstration footage and the released game are known to have led even to a false-advertising lawsuit. What to examine: Observe the endpoint where a preview's mismatch with reality becomes not merely disappointment but legal conflict. A promise is not just a marketing asset; it is an utterance that carries responsibility.
- The *Killzone 2* E3 2005 video: A pre-rendered “target video,” rather than real-time footage, was reportedly presented like actual gameplay and caused controversy. What to examine: Watch the gap between what is shown and what people receive accumulate into distrust. One exaggeration makes every later preview suspect.
- Trailers labeled “actual gameplay footage”: As gaps between staged footage and real screens repeatedly caused controversy, labels such as “actual game-play footage” became an industry convention for clarifying whether the current image is presentation or product. What to examine: This prevents mismatch in advance by specifying the unit of the promise. Separate what is promised from what merely creates atmosphere, and the same video no longer becomes a lie.
- The first screenshots and short video on a Steam store page: Most installation decisions are settled in the narrow passage formed by the first few images. What to examine: Determine whether those images list features or show “what I will get to do with this.” The narrowest passage should carry only one clear promise.
- Arcade attract mode: Before a coin is inserted, an idle machine plays its own demonstration, shows a high-score table, and flashes “INSERT COIN” to draw passersby. What to examine: Observe a structure in which the idle screen itself becomes a promise. A demo is the game's most honest self-made preview because what it shows is what the player will do.
- Game demos and trial versions: They put one piece of the big picture in the player's hands while saving the full work, helping them decide whether to buy. What to examine: Choosing which piece to give away is itself promise design. To avoid mismatch, it should represent the work rather than merely being its most spectacular fragment.

**Animated Film**
- Pixar shorts shown before features: A short work delivers tone and promise ahead of the feature. What to examine: A separate work can calibrate expectations for the main one. A preview need not be a fragment of the feature; a matching texture is enough to make a promise.

**Live-Action TV / Drama**
- Next-episode previews: They conceal the outcome and promise in one shot to make the viewer return next time. What to examine: This preview addresses someone who has already seen the main work. For users with accumulated trust, a promise can be formed with less information.

**Online Video**
- YouTube clickbait thumbnails and watch duration: An exaggerated thumbnail wins clicks, but when viewers immediately leave content that differs from the promise, watch duration falls. Recommendation is known to value watch duration, so exposure itself ultimately declines. What to examine: See how an unkeepable promise returns through measurement. The chapter's signal—strong ad performance paired with many immediate exits—has been institutionalized on video platforms.

**Literature**
- Book covers and back-cover copy: Without revealing everything, they use a brief first impression to decide whether someone buys. What to examine: A cover promises genre and tone. Look at the reception of books whose cover texture clashes with their prose to see the weight of that promise.
- E-book “Preview/Look Inside”: The opening 10–20 percent, usually the introduction, is offered free so readers can decide whether the style and beginning fit them. What to examine: The boundary between offering one piece of the big picture and saving the substance for the work itself is drawn in percentages. Deciding where to stop is preview design.

**Music**
- Pre-release album singles: One song creates expectation without revealing the whole album. What to examine: Ask whether the single represents the album's overall texture. If the most popular song is placed first but the album feels different, the expectation drawn in by that song diverges inside the album.
- Thirty-second previews: A short exposure helps someone decide whether to buy. What to examine: Observe which thirty seconds are chosen. Selecting the highlight is the same as selecting the promise the song makes.

**Comics / Comic Books**
- Magazine covers and next-issue preview panels: One image promises to make readers wait for the next installment. What to examine: This promise repeats every week. Because there is another week after each mismatch, trust is accumulated or eroded episode by episode in a long campaign.

**Webtoons**
- Work thumbnails and one-line descriptions: In a crowded list, a first impression alone decides whether someone clicks. What to examine: First impressions work through relative evaluation against competing works in the same list. They do not compete in a vacuum but on a display shelf.

**Web Fiction**
- Titles, covers, and descriptions: At the farthest point before episode one, they promise to filter readers before entry. What to examine: This market offers an extreme in which the title becomes the full text of the promise. Its speed of promise fulfillment is measured by whether the development named in the title appears immediately near the beginning.

**Advertising / Marketing**
- Ad fatigue and creative rotation: Repeatedly showing the same ad to the same person lowers clicks and response, so periodically rotating creative is an industry commonplace. What to examine: Treat it as evidence that the same first impression wears out under repetition and needs variation. The durability described in the chapter—preserving the major promise while changing only the edges—is routine work in advertising operations.

**Devices / Consumer-Electronics UX**
- Product-package photographs and the real object: Trust depends on whether the item revealed on opening matches the appearance promised by its packaging. What to examine: Unboxing is the moment a promise is tested. Whether the product is devalued by the expectation raised by the package or fulfills it determines repeat purchase.
- Driving scenes in car ads: A real test drive must return the experience that was promised. What to examine: This is a market in which a verification stage—the test drive—has been institutionalized. Placing an experience between promise and fulfillment catches mismatches before purchase.

**Offline / Everyday Life**
- “Serving suggestion” photos on instant-noodle packages: The package shows a bowl piled with garnishes, with the small words “serving suggestion” nearby. Everyone knows the gap between the bowl promised by the photograph and the actual cooked product. What to examine: A disclaimer cannot prevent disappointment. The promise is made not by the small print but by the largest image, and people build expectations from that image.
- Restaurant food models and photographic menus: A display-window model shows “what you will receive” more powerfully than a written menu. Japanese restaurant districts are famous for their culture of models made to resemble the real food. What to examine: Treat this as a preview that shows the result in a newcomer's language. It matches the chapter's prescription to show one scene of what they will receive instead of listing specifications.
- Supermarket sampling counters: They offer one bite while keeping the full product packaged. What to examine: This is the offline origin of demos and trials. Drawing the line between what to taste and what to save is preview design itself.
- Apartment model homes: A full-size sample home is furnished before units are sold, and people often say that furniture placement and mirrors make it look larger than it really is. What to examine: The farther fulfillment lies in the future, the stronger the temptation to inflate a promise and the later its verification. The longer the delay between promise and fulfillment, the more heavily the promise's honesty matters.

## Chapter 19. Colliding Experiences and Layers

These examples show how collisions between good elements are directed outside games: by appointing only one protagonist for one screen and one moment, then delaying the rest, merging them into one action, or removing them from the current first experience. They span not only video games and apps but also film, broadcasting, music, comics, theater, consumer devices, and offline spaces. Each item ends with a “What to examine” note. Read by identifying which of the five layers—world, system, sensory, social, and business—are in conflict, and whether the conflict was resolved through delaying, merging, or removing. Stories from other media then become tools for repairing our first screen.

**Video Games**
- *Half-Life 2* (Valve, 2004): Rather than taking control away in cutscenes, it leaves the screen in the player's hands and maintains a first-person view from beginning to end as the story unfolds. New tools such as the Gravity Gun are learned naturally by playing catch with the robot Dog rather than through a separate lecture. World explanation and control learning continue in one uninterrupted flow. What to examine: This resolves conflict between world and system through merging rather than delay. Find where one action does the work of two layers at once, then consider which guidance on our screen could merge into direction.
- *Portal* (Valve, 2007): Whenever a new mechanic appears, it provides one clean test chamber with every other element removed and locks the path forward until that concept is understood, handling only one concept in each room. Rather than unfolding everything on the first screen, it reveals things one by one when they become necessary. What to examine: The structure enforces one protagonist per screen at the spatial level. Ask whether our first experience contains a stage equivalent to “a room with everything removed except one concept.”
- The opening Great Plateau of *The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild* (Nintendo, 2017): Before entering the vast main game, the player stays on an isolated plateau and learns one ability in each shrine rather than seeing every menu. Only one layer becomes the protagonist at a time. What to examine: It compromises between freedom and guidance through “freedom within a narrowed area.” Read it as a middle solution that prevents overload without delaying everything.
- The first level of *Candy Crush Saga* (King, 2012): Instead of a dense menu, it first asks the player to perform only one action—matching three of the same color. Features such as boosters appear one by one in the levels where they become necessary. What to examine: This is a classic delay that spreads feature disclosure across the timeline of level progression. Count how many of our menus are truly needed in the first round.

**App UX**
- Duolingo's first-use flow: It does not place account registration first. The user tries a short lesson, and only when they want to save progress does it suggest signing up. Industry discussions often cite this delayed registration requirement as reducing drop-off. What to examine: This is an example of making the business and registration layer speak last. Compare whether sign-up, payment, or permission requests on our first screen arrive before trust.
- Google's first search screen: In the late 1990s, when portals packed their first screens with news, weather, stocks, and directories, Google began with only a logo and a search box. That emptiness became its identity, and the skeleton remains today. What to examine: This applies removal to the entire first screen. Read the composition as every other function withdrawing behind the screen for one protagonist: search.

**Live-Action Film**
- Film mise-en-scène: Even when a frame contains light, people, and background, composition, lighting, and focus gather the eye around one protagonist. What to examine: This proves hierarchy alone can appoint a protagonist without removing elements. Ask whether the size, color, and movement on our screen point to the protagonist of this moment.
- Over-expository dialogue that crowds information into one scene: Several good pieces of setting are spoon-fed simultaneously, collide, and leave none of them memorable. What to examine: This is the film version of the proposition that collisions occur not between bad elements but between good ones. Count how many things one piece of our guidance tries to say at once.

**Animated Film**
- The opening of *WALL-E* (Pixar, 2008): For roughly the first thirty minutes, it nearly empties the film of dialogue and shows only a devastated Earth and one cleaning robot. The world layer speaks alone before the other layers interfere. It creates immersion by appointing only one layer as protagonist at a time. What to examine: This is an extreme delay that gives one layer an uninterrupted block of time. Ask whether our first experience gives the world layer even a few seconds to speak alone.

**Live-Action TV / Drama**
- News lower thirds and breaking-news tickers: Scrolling tickers at the bottom of the screen have been criticized for pushing in too much information at once and creating overload. CNN removed its scrolling ticker in 2008 and replaced it with a static caption that changed one sentence at a time, then returned to a ticker in 2013; MSNBC also removed its lower ticker in 2018. What to examine: The same broadcasters moved between showing everything and showing only one thing. Traffic direction is not a single decision but a conflict that requires continued management.

**Music**
- Frequency masking and EQ cleanup in mixing: When instruments overlap in the same frequency range, good sounds obscure one another and become muddy. A subtractive EQ cuts overlapping bands, or the arrangement moves an instrument by an octave to create space in advance. The other sounds are cleared away so one can lead in a passage. What to examine: Good sounds obscuring one another is the same phenomenon as this chapter's definition of collision. Consider which element on our screen needs the equivalent of “subtractive EQ.”
- Arrangements that clear the accompaniment for a vocal: The other instruments briefly quiet down for the one layer that should be heard most. What to examine: The protagonist changes within a song second by second. Ask whether the other elements on our screen truly withdraw when its protagonist changes.

**Comics / Comic Books**
- Panel divisions and eye guidance on one page: Several events occupy one page, but one large panel determines the visual protagonist, while panel size and placement guide reading order. What to examine: This technique creates sequence while showing things simultaneously, suggesting that spatial hierarchy can imitate a time difference.
- Overcrowded panels in which sound effects, speech balloons, and backgrounds cover the art: Trying to show everything causes collisions that hide the important picture. What to examine: This is a collision in which the sensory layer obscures the world layer. Ask whether our effects and notifications cover what should actually be seen.

**Theater / Stage**
- Follow spots and blocking on stage: Even with several actors present, the follow spot tracks only the designated performer. When two actors cross, the lights change direction to illuminate the respective characters. Placing a performer at downstage center, close to the audience, gathers attention on the one person who should be watched now. What to examine: Lighting the protagonist and leaving the surroundings dark form one action. Emphasis is completed not by addition but by subtracting the surroundings.
- The stage convention that supporting actors step aside when the lead speaks: One person is the protagonist at a given moment, and the rest withdraw. What to examine: A protagonist exists only when those stepping aside follow a discipline. Ask whether the supporting elements on our screen have a defined “promise to withdraw.”

**Devices / Consumer-Electronics UX**
- Automotive infotainment and the return of physical buttons: As every function was placed on one large touchscreen, criticism grew that drivers had to stare at the screen too long for one action and became distracted. Beginning with its 2026 assessment criteria, Euro NCAP reduces scores when core functions such as turn signals, hazard lights, the horn, and windshield wipers lack physical controls, making the highest rating harder to receive. What to examine: Safety is evaluated partly by whether one urgent action is directly within reach. Check whether the “one urgent action” on our screen is buried several menu levels deep.
- Devices that sound every warning and notification at once: Sending every signal simultaneously makes the truly urgent one impossible to hear. What to examine: This counterexample shows that notifications also need a hierarchy of protagonist and supporting roles. Ask whether our notifications all sound at once at the same intensity.

**Offline / Everyday Life**
- IKEA's one-way store path: The whole store is arranged as one path that leads newcomers through showrooms in a fixed order, with occasional shortcut doors instead of forks. With no route to deliberate over, displays enter view in sequence. What to examine: The design tilts the conflict between freedom and guidance heavily toward guidance. Also observe the other side: the same route can frustrate regulars who already know it.
- The London Underground map: In 1933, Harry Beck discarded real geographic distances and curves and redrew the system as a diagram using straight lines, 45-degree angles, and even station spacing. When two kinds of good information—geographic fact and transfer information—competed, he removed one. What to examine: Removal does not mean damaging information. It means surrendering other good information to answer the current question: “Where do I transfer?”
- The “one slide, one message” principle in presentations: Widely recommended in presentation design, it teaches that if a slide contains several points, audiences cannot listen while reading. Leave one message on each slide and move the rest to the next. What to examine: Reading and listening compete for the same attention in a collision of the sensory layer. Apply it directly to our tutorials when screen and voice speak simultaneously.
- White-cube museum exhibitions: White walls are left empty and works are spaced apart so that only one work enters the field of view of a visitor standing before it. What to examine: Empty space is not decoration but a device for appointing the protagonist. Ask for which protagonist the empty space on our screen has been cleared.

## Chapter 20. The Starting and Ending Points of the FTUE

These examples from other media show how to declare in one sentence where a first experience begins and ends: pulling the starting point ahead of first launch, and placing the endpoint at the first moment of “Ah, so this is what makes it good.” They mix the first boss of a game with film openings, television pilots, the first three chapters of serialized manga, a restaurant's first bite, and product packaging. Each item ends with a “What to examine” note. Read each case against the chapter's two questions: How far forward should the starting point move, and at which achievement should the endpoint be placed?

**Video Games**
- The first Genichiro battle in *Sekiro* (FromSoftware, 2019): The enemy encountered at the end of the introduction is a fight designed to be lost rather than won. Through that defeat the protagonist loses an arm, receives a prosthetic, and the main story finally begins. Unlike the orthodoxy that entry requires defeating the first boss, the finish line here lies not in victory but in learning the feel of deflecting a blade. What to examine: It shows that an endpoint can be defined independently of winning or losing. Ask whether our game's finish line truly needs to be “winning.”

Identify our game's “one job” and where it is first completed.
- The first day in *Animal Crossing*: The endpoint is not defeating a boss but choosing where to pitch a tent on a deserted island, naming it, and gaining a place of one's own. The same first experience draws a different finish line. What to examine: The endpoint of settlement determines the entire path of the first day, showing that a different endpoint creates a different road before it.
- The first harvest in *Stardew Valley*: The first core pleasure lies not in combat but in the first handful of crops harvested, an achievement that takes several in-game days of waiting. What to examine: If harvest after a wait is the endpoint, small hooks are needed to sustain people in between. Examine both the distance to the endpoint and the stepping stones along the way.

**Live-Action Film**

What to examine: The introduction provides a complete reward in itself while becoming motivation for the feature. Ask whether our introductory segment stands as a self-contained first paragraph.
- The point where opening credits end and the feature begins: It separates how far the introduction extends from where the main work starts. What to examine: Audiences feel the boundary in their bodies and adjust their posture. Ask whether our first experience has a signal that says, “The main experience starts here.”

**Live-Action TV / Drama**
- The cold open of the *Breaking Bad* pilot (2008): An RV stopped in the desert and a protagonist in a gas mask and underwear imprint within episode one the tone and color that will span five seasons. A single pilot declares, “This work begins here.” What to examine: Declaring a starting point is declaring identity. Ask what tone our first screen declares.
- Pilot episodes of series: Episode one separates introducing the world from settling people into it afterward. What to examine: Just as a pilot and a main series have different standards of success, the scorecard for a first experience must differ from the scorecard for later settlement.
- A season's first point of immersion: The episode of which a viewer says, “This is where I got hooked,” becomes the endpoint. What to examine: If that episode differs by person, endpoints differ by group. Recall the chapter's principle of choosing one by the primary group we intend to welcome.

**Literature**
- The first three chapters of a *Weekly Shonen Jump* serialization: Reader-postcard survey rankings begin immediately after debut, and low rankings can cause early cancellation. Authors and editors are therefore known to design together what will capture readers within the first three chapters. What to examine: This is a harsh case in which the survival of the work depends on where its first-experience finish line lies. Measure how much of our work corresponds to “the first three chapters.”
- The last chapter of volume one—the chapter that calls for the next volume: It draws the line where the first experience for which one volume is responsible ends. What to examine: The finish line has a dual role, providing completion while also serving as a hook for what follows.

**Board Games**
- The clear condition of an introductory scenario: Instead of making players read the entire rulebook, it lets them finish a first game with reduced rules and places the endpoint at the point where “So this is what the game is like” arrives. What to examine: The finish line is completing one game, not learning all the rules. The endpoint lies in “tasting,” not “knowing everything.”

**Music**
- “Speak to Me,” the first track of Pink Floyd's *The Dark Side of the Moon* (1973): Beginning with a heartbeat, it lays out a collage of clocks, cash registers, and laughter that will return later, previewing the whole album's identity in an overture of barely a minute. What to examine: The first track itself declares the starting point. Ask what our first screen lets people hear in advance from the experience to come.

**Real-World Workflows**
- A new hire's first result—the first completed task: Settlement begins not with equipment setup or training, but at the point where they first complete real work. What to examine: Place the onboarding endpoint at a first result rather than training completion, then compare what changes with the difference between tutorial completion and first pleasure.

**App / Device UX**
- Duolingo's first question before sign-up: It delays account creation, asks for language and goals, then immediately presents the first translation question, letting people feel “the pleasure of learning” before the threshold of registration. What to examine: This arrangement pulls the endpoint of first pleasure ahead of the starting threshold. Ask whether our sign-up process truly has a reason to precede first pleasure.
- Early Twitter's “follow 30 people”: The early growth team reportedly traced frequent returners backward and found a signal that people who followed around thirty accounts rarely left. Josh Elman, who was on the team, has said the post-registration flow was then redesigned to first help people find accounts to follow. What to examine: Choosing one endpoint caused the whole onboarding to be relaid toward it, echoing the Facebook story in the chapter.
- Dropbox's first file: In early growth discussions, Dropbox's activation point is often described as the moment someone first placed one file in a folder. A person who finished installation without adding a file had never seen the product's value. What to examine: Installation completion and experiencing value are different points. Find the false endpoint in our game that merely looks like “installation complete.”

**Offline / Everyday Life**
- Apple's packaging design: *Inside Apple* (2012) recounts a dedicated room stacked with hundreds of packaging prototypes where designers spent months refining only the experience of opening a box. The first experience began at unboxing, before the product was even turned on. What to examine: This pulls the starting point far ahead of first launch. Ask whether our starting point should reach back to the store screen or installation-wait screen.
- Main Street, U.S.A. at Disneyland: After passing the front gate, visitors do not immediately encounter a ride but walk through a street from an idealized old America. Walt Disney is said to have designed entering the park like entering a movie theater. The castle visible at the street's end draws the eye and pulls visitors inward. What to examine: This starting-point design introduces a ritual for crossing into the world instead of immediately imposing the main experience. Draw the “street” laid before our first screen.
- The amuse-bouche in a course meal: This chef's bite arrives at the very beginning even though it was not ordered, giving an advance taste of the meal's character and awakening the palate. What to examine: A small but complete first reward calibrates expectations for the whole meal. A starting-point declaration and first reward can share one plate.

**Sports / Game Rules**
- A race's starting and finish lines: A record can exist only after deciding where timing begins and what counts as finished. What to examine: Without both lines, measurement itself is impossible. This is the most compressed image of the chapter's conclusion that declaring boundaries is a prerequisite for measurement.
- The definition of finishing a marathon: Where the endpoint is placed redesigns every training plan before it. What to examine: This is the athletic version of the proposition that the endpoint lays the road by designing the entire preparation process backward.

## Chapter 21. The First 30 Seconds, 3 Minutes, 30 Minutes, and Day

This collection shows how other media practice time design: dividing what to give and what to postpone according to the ladder of questions users climb at 30 seconds, 3 minutes, 30 minutes, and one day—What is this? What do I do? What makes it fun? Is it worth continuing? Will I come back? The examples mix games and apps with film, television, music, novels, webtoons, short-form content, and offline experiences. Each ends with a “What to examine” prompt. The length of the scale expands or contracts by medium, but the order of the questions remains the same. Read each example as a test of that claim.

**Video Games**
- Super Mario Bros. World 1-1 (Nintendo, 1985): The first screen leads Mario naturally toward a Goomba, letting the player collide with the rule “jump on it to defeat it” without words, then reveals rules one at a time through progress. What to examine: This is the standard for embedding the first 30 seconds of learning in action without explanation. Does the first lesson on our first screen arrive as text or action?
- Mega Man X's intro stage (Capcom, 1993): During play, it drops the player into a pit they cannot escape normally, leading them to discover wall jumping without a single line of explanation. What to examine: This is a carefully shaped path for learning by collision. Compare it with the chapter's two approaches: does a control discovered independently remain longer than one performed on command?
- Bayonetta's opening action (PlatinumGames, 2009): The first scene immediately hands players a spectacular battle that is difficult to die in, establishing identity and tactile pleasure first. The controls tutorial covering punches, kicks, and dodges comes afterward. What to examine: Its sequence follows the chapter's claim exactly—the first 30 seconds belong to identity, not learning.
- Portal's first test chamber: Environmental cues such as wall color and lighting guide the eye toward where to fire a portal, allowing players to discover the solution through experiment rather than written instruction. What to examine: When the environment replaces explanation, reading time becomes action time. Which of our instructions could be moved into environmental cues?
- Animal Crossing's daily loop: Newly buried fossils, replenished rock resources, and villagers to greet are tied to the real-time clock, creating a reason to launch again tomorrow even after stopping today. What to examine: This plants the “reason to return one day later” not in an individual piece of content but in the system's time structure. What bait for tomorrow does our first session leave behind?

**App UX**
- Duolingo's first entry: It postpones account registration and immediately gives the newcomer a short translation or multiple-choice exercise. Learning itself becomes the first experience instead of an explanation, and sign-up comes afterward. What to examine: The flow answers the first three minutes' question—“What do I do?”—with action. Are our first three minutes filled with reading or doing?
- Candy Crush Saga's early levels: The first few rounds are made so easy that they are almost impossible to lose, handing the player an early “I'm good at this” achievement before gradually increasing difficulty. What to examine: It guarantees the first achievement in the 3-to-10-minute interval through design rather than luck. Does our first achievement reach everyone?
- Wordle's one puzzle a day: This word puzzle, which spread at the end of 2021, gives everyone the same single problem each day. Once it is solved, there is nothing more until tomorrow. The restraint of offering no more becomes a reason to return, while a one-line colored-grid result drove word of mouth. What to examine: It fills the “one day” box with a restriction rather than added content. A reason to return does not have to be a reward.

**Device UX**
- Tamagotchi care alerts: When it grows hungry or its mood falls, a beep calls the owner back to care for it. This short-cycle summons creates a reason to return after stepping away. What to examine: The device itself manufactures and offers the excuse to return. Where is the line between a welcome call and an annoyance?

**Short Form / Streaming**
- The first one-to-three-second hook in short-form video: Among TikTok and Reels creators, the assumption that viewers immediately swipe away if the first second or two fails to capture attention functions almost like standard grammar. Platform production guides likewise recommend a hook in the opening seconds. What to examine: In this medium, the gateway is the first 3 seconds rather than 30. Even as the scale shrinks by channel, the order of questions—starting with “What is this?”—remains unchanged.
- Netflix's “Skip Intro” button: Since 2017, series openings have offered a skip button that removes the time spent repeatedly watching an introduction viewers already know. What to examine: The same intro establishes identity in episode one but becomes a toll by episode five. This shows that timing determines value as much as content.

**Live-Action Film**
- Film's three-act structure: The same information can be dull in the introduction and tense in the middle, so the structure designs when to give it. What to examine: The value of information comes from its position as much as its content. Imagine moving one of our explanations to a different interval.
- The opening hook and slow buildup: If the first few minutes fail to hold viewers, later masterpieces never get the chance to be seen. What to examine: This is the film version of the funnel—the good things later exist only if the beginning is passed.

**Live-Action TV / Drama**
- Breaking Bad's pilot cold open: It first grabs viewers with a man in his underwear driving an RV through the desert and aiming a gun at approaching sirens. Only afterward does it return to ordinary life and explain who he is and how he got there. What to examine: This reverses the order by placing the hook first and explanation later. Does our worldbuilding explanation come before our hook?
- The end-of-episode cliffhanger: It plants an excuse at the end of the first session that calls the audience onward and makes them start again. What to examine: Since the reason to return is planted at the end of a session, what does the last screen of our first session leave behind?

**Music**
- The buildup from intro to verse to chorus: The opening seconds catch the ear, and the first reward—the chorus—arrives on a precise beat. What to examine: The structure promises the arrival time of the first reward through rhythm. At what minute have we promised ours?
- Intros shortened around streaming's 30-second accounting threshold: Because a stream must play for a certain amount of time to count, long intros and late choruses became costly, reportedly shortening intros and pulling hooks forward. What to examine: Measurement structures can reshape the content's time design in reverse. How are our metrics carving our opening?

**Literature**
- The pacing of a novel's first chapter: It places a hook on the first page and postpones background explanation until the reader is absorbed. What to examine: The distribution of explanation is the craft of Chapter One. Recount the amount of explanation on our first screen.
- Chapter One of Gone Girl (Gillian Flynn, 2012): It drops the reader into the moment a wife disappears on the morning of her fifth wedding anniversary, laying down anxiety first, then revealing the couple's history later through alternating diaries and memories. What to examine: This is the restraint of holding a setting without explaining it immediately. Which pieces of our worldbuilding can wait until a later session?
- An information-heavy first chapter that dumps the setting in the opening: It loses the reader by spoon-feeding explanation before they can enter the story. What to examine: This is an advance charge on time in writing. Compare it with the volume of our tutorial's first screen.

**Newspapers / Articles**
- The inverted pyramid: This longstanding news-writing convention puts the most important fact in the first sentence, then adds progressively less important detail, so the reader retains the core no matter where they stop. What to examine: The structure assumes departure. Is our first experience designed so that anyone who leaves at any interval still carries away one clear identity?

**Webtoons**
- The opening of Tower of God: It first grabs readers with one question—“What do you desire?”—and the lure of the Tower, then gradually reveals the Tower's rules and world through tests. What to examine: One question carries the reader through 30 seconds while the setting travels inside action. What action carries our world explanation?
- The fast opening of Episode One: The first few panels of a vertical scroll make readers want to continue, postponing world explanation. What to examine: This convention pulls the scale forward to fit the medium's pace. Does our scale fit our channel's pace?

**Web Novels**
- A fast opening that prevents Episode One drop-off: The convention is well established that if the beginning of the first installment does not catch readers, the later story will never be shown. What to examine: A serialized medium stakes everything on the funnel's first interval. Compare its choice with our answer about what belongs there.

**Real-World Work Procedures**
- A new hire's first day, week, and month: The same information is poison if poured out on day one and medicine if given in the week when it becomes necessary. What to examine: The ladder of questions appears unchanged at work—Where am I? What do I do? Am I useful? Move the chapter's table for dividing what to give and postpone into onboarding.
- Job training that lets people try first and explains later: It allows someone to complete one small task before making them read the entire manual. What to examine: This is the workplace version of action before explanation. On which screen does the first action appear in our tutorial?

**Offline / Everyday Life**
- A gym's first consultation and session: Experienced trainers commonly advise against pushing a newcomer to their limit on day one, because several days of soreness may drive them away. Instead, end the first session lightly and finish with one small success. What to examine: This is the offline version of the “one day” box, where first-day intensity determines next-day return. How far does our first session push people?

## Chapter 22. Defining the Outcome Dashboard

This collection shows how other fields choose the numbers that illuminate a first experience: replacing distant, large revenue with small, close outcomes for which the first experience is directly responsible; enlarging one as the North Star; and distinguishing numbers treated as alarms from those read as trends. The examples range beyond game operations to aviation, medicine, broadcasting, film, music, and the interpretation of metrics and data. Read each against the chapter's conclusion: what we watch determines what we do.

**Real-World Work Procedures**
- Apple Watch Activity rings and “closing the rings”: The Move, Exercise, and Stand rings reveal a day's outcomes at a glance. Instead of listing every value, they compress them into one clear goal: “Did I close all three rings today?” What to examine: This is North Star setting that makes one thing to complete stand out among several outcomes. Which box should be largest on our dashboard?
- Strava's weekly summary: It gathers outcomes such as distance and time, but users do not push the numbers directly. The numbers follow as the sum of running and riding. What to examine: It shows exactly how an outcome number follows behavior like a shadow. Where is the line at which the displayed number becomes motivation rather than pressure?
- An operations meeting that watches only revenue and user counts: A counterexample in which the team stares at large, distant numbers and cannot distinguish the effect of a screen changed by hand. What to examine: The numbers placed on the meeting table become the list of what the organization believes it can repair. Write down the numbers that appear habitually in our meetings.
- Emergency-room triage: Incoming patients are divided by severity rather than arrival order, separating signals of immediate danger to life from signals that can wait. What to examine: This is the original form of the chapter's distinction between alarms and trends, since it refuses to sound every signal at the same volume. Does our dashboard separately identify a number that sends us running after even a one-day spike?

**Video Games**
- A tutorial funnel report in a game-analytics tool: A table shows the remaining users at each stage, drop-off versus the previous stage, and average time between stages, pinpointing where the most people leave. When the steepest interval appears—for example, “tutorial completion 30 percent, reached Stage 5: 5 percent”—the person who repaired the first screen can recognize their work in that exact cell and find what to repair next. What to examine: This is a model of numbers close enough for a first-screen designer to recognize the work of their own hand. Is our funnel divided stage by stage?
- A dashboard for tutorial completion, D1, and D7: It displays the small, close outcomes for which the first experience is directly responsible instead of distant, large revenue. What to examine: Even on the same board, completion rate becomes a false number if read alone. Have we decided which numbers must be read beside which others?
- Operations that boast only about concurrent users: The large number is highly visible, but it cannot distinguish whether the first screen succeeded. What to examine: Numbers used for external boasting may differ from numbers used for internal work. Are we placing press-release numbers on our dashboard?

**Mobile / Apps / Services**
- YouTube's shift from views to watch time: In October 2012, YouTube changed the core signal for recommendations and search from views to watch time. Views were easy to inflate with clickbait titles and thumbnails, so the platform chose to illuminate “Did they keep watching?” rather than “Did they click?” Views reportedly fell sharply immediately after the change, but YouTube kept it because it believed the result was better for viewers. What to examine: This industry-scale example shows that the number placed on a dashboard changes the behavior of creators too. It directly demonstrates that what we illuminate determines what we make people do.

**Aviation / Transportation**
- The aircraft six-pack and primary flight display (PFD): The airspeed indicator, attitude indicator, altimeter, turn coordinator, heading indicator, and vertical-speed indicator were defined as the six outcomes essential to flight and gathered in one place. The attitude indicator sits in the center, with speed on the left and altitude and vertical speed on the right. What to examine: This is the canonical layout for deciding what to watch first and enlarging the most important item in the center. What is our dashboard's “central attitude indicator”?
- The glass cockpit: It gathers scattered instruments on one screen, but only after deciding what to illuminate. What to examine: Gathering everything does not make everything visible; only what is selected becomes visible. Selection must precede dashboard consolidation.

**Live-Action TV / Broadcast**
- Score and statistics graphics in sports broadcasts: They select only the outcomes viewers need to see now from the whole game and place them at one edge of the screen. What to examine: Someone preselected these outcome numbers for the viewer seated at the console. Deciding what to omit matters as much as deciding what to include.

**Live-Action Film**
- The electrical-power scene before reentry in Apollo 13 (1995): The characters build tension by staring at one number alone, watching the black needle of an ampere gauge to keep it below the red 20-ampere mark. What to examine: This extreme image shows that narrowing attention to one thing accelerates judgment. Have we decided in advance which single number to watch in a crisis?
- Moneyball (2011): The general manager and analyst collect undervalued players by making on-base percentage, rather than a traditional metric such as batting average, their core number. What to examine: Changing the number they watched changed the entire judgment used to select players. How does replacing a North Star change organizational behavior?

**Music**
- Spotify for Artists dashboard: It foregrounds behavioral numbers such as streams by song, unique listeners, save rate, playlist additions, and listeners by city, while not showing income at all. Earnings are settled separately through distributors with a delay of two or three months, so the dashboard an artist watches places behaviors that directly reflect value in front of them. What to examine: This choice removes the distant output of money and leaves only close behavioral numbers, following the chapter's prescription to move revenue to the edge.

**Metrics / Data**
- Confirmed case counts and positivity rate on COVID dashboards: During outbreaks, confirmed case counts fluctuated with the amount of testing. Public-health statistics repeatedly warned that positivity, hospitalizations, and intensive-care admissions had to be read alongside case counts to understand the trend. What to examine: One number may be the shadow of another input—the amount of testing. Borrow that suspicion and ask whether our D1 is merely the shadow of advertising volume.
- Daily weight fluctuation and reading the trend: Body weight can move one or two kilograms within a day depending on hydration and meal timing. The common recommendation is not to celebrate or despair over a single day, but to read week-level trends. What to examine: This everyday example shows the cost of reading a trend metric as an alarm. Are meetings being shaken by a single day's fluctuation in D1 or D7?
- Leading and lagging economic indicators: Economic statistics distinguish indicators that move ahead of the economy, such as new orders and consumer expectations, from indicators such as unemployment that move only well after conditions change. What to examine: This follows the chapter's rule of measuring distance by causal-chain length rather than days. It lets us reread the claim that revenue is a lagging indicator of the first experience in the language of statistics.

## Chapter 23. Causality Between Levers and the Dashboard

This collection shows how other fields separate levers—the inputs we touch—from dashboards—the outputs that emerge as results; trace the chain from input through experience to output to find leaks; and read groups separately instead of relying on one average. It combines dashboards from automobiles and aviation with examples from health, manufacturing, film, and classic statistics. Read each against two statements from the chapter: keep the hand on the input and the eye on the output; pulling an outcome as a handle breaks the path to that outcome.

**Automotive / Mechanical Dashboards**
- Engine warning lights and limp mode: The warning light is only an output showing a symptom. The place to touch is the sensor or fuel system that made it illuminate. What to examine: The sequence is not to hide the output, but to find the input it points toward. Practice looking for a cause, not the number, when a low result appears.
- OBD diagnostic codes: A mechanic reads the code to locate the fault, then physically works on the causal component. What to examine: In a repair shop, the chapter's distinction between the pointing output and the touched input has become professional common sense.
- A thermostat and room temperature: We touch the setpoint control, not temperature itself. Temperature follows the setting. What to examine: This proves that everyday life already separates input from output. Why do we forget the same distinction only when facing metrics?

**Aviation Dashboards**
- A pilot's instrument cross-check: The pilot scans several instruments to read flight state while keeping hands on the controls and throttle. Instruments are watched; control surfaces are moved. What to examine: This profession trains people to separate where the eye rests from where the hand rests. “Hand on input, eye on output” has become a skill.
- Primary and supporting instruments: At each stage, the pilot focuses on one primary instrument and cross-checks its reading with adjacent instruments. What to examine: This is the original form of the chapter's rule to place a number beside another instead of trusting it alone.
- The “power + attitude = performance” principle: This is a textbook model of producing outcomes such as speed and altitude by manipulating the direct inputs of throttle and control. What to examine: The formula teaches adjustment of two inputs rather than the result itself. Choose two or three inputs corresponding to our “power and attitude.”

**Health / Self-Tracking Apps**
- Activity rings and step-count dashboards: The ring on the screen merely displays an outcome. Filling it requires actual walking; a finger cannot fill the ring. What to examine: This example exposes directly that an output cannot be pushed. Which number on our dashboard is someone trying to push with a finger?
- Scale weight versus diet and exercise: We watch weight as the output and touch food and movement as inputs. Staring at the number does not change it. What to examine: See how helplessness before an output becomes a list of actions the moment inputs are found.

**Real-World Work Procedures**
- Stage-by-stage conversion in a sales pipeline: Instead of looking only at total revenue, a team finds the stage where deals leak and changes activity there. What to examine: This is the sales version of dividing the chain into stages and finding its steepest cell.
- Separating performance by acquisition channel—paid versus organic: Different groups are mixed inside the same average. Only after separating them does the place to repair emerge. What to examine: This is the chapter's scene where 5 and 25 percent are crushed into a 10 percent average. Which two groups hide behind our average?
- Amazon's controllable input metrics: According to *Working Backwards*, written by former Amazon executives, Amazon chooses inputs it can move directly—selection, price, and delivery speed—rather than outputs such as share price or revenue, and reviews those first in weekly business meetings. What to examine: This embeds “hand on input, eye on output” into a company's meeting system. Count how many agenda items in our weekly meeting are outputs and how many are inputs.
- Wells Fargo's cross-selling target: After the number of accounts per customer was pressed down as a sales target, employees trying to meet quotas secretly created millions of fake accounts. The practice was exposed in 2016 and led to enormous fines and mass dismissals. What to examine: This is the costliest counterexample showing that when an output number is pulled as a handle, the number rises while reality collapses. Ask in advance what gaming our target might encourage.

**Metrics / Data**
- Simpson's paradox in kidney-stone treatment: In a comparison published in a British medical journal in 1986, the new procedure appeared to have a higher overall success rate. Divided by stone size, however, open surgery had a higher success rate for both small and large stones. More difficult large-stone cases had been concentrated in the surgery group, reversing the average. What to examine: This textbook case shows a conclusion reversing after segmentation. Place it beside the Berkeley admission statistics and build the habit of doubting a single average.
- Wald's bomber armor: During World War II, statistician Abraham Wald is said to have advised reinforcing not the areas with many bullet holes on returning bombers, but the engines, where holes were absent. The distribution recorded only aircraft that returned; planes hit in the engine never entered the data. What to examine: This precisely exposes the dashboard's limitation to users who remain. Where in our funnel do we read the blank left by those who departed?
- The cobra-bounty story: A story is often told that colonial authorities in India offered bounties for dead cobras, leading people to breed snakes for rewards. When the program ended, breeders released the snakes and increased the population. The historical record is not clearly verified, but the tale became the name “cobra effect” for the lesson that directly rewarding an outcome number distorts the path to it. What to examine: This is a fable about pulling an output as a handle. Imagine what kind of breeding our KPI reward might invite.

**Video Game Operations**
- Adjusting one Candy Crush Saga level: Stage-by-stage passage and drop-off data locate the cell where people leave in a rush, then A/B-test the input of that level's difficulty. What to examine: This exactly follows the chapter's method—find the steepest cell and touch its input. Where is that one cell in our funnel?
- The conversion-versus-drop-off tradeoff on one Candy Crush Saga level: Raising difficulty to pull the payment-conversion output directly is known to increase the short-term number while driving people away at that cell and reducing long-term retention. What to examine: The hand lifting one output cuts another, which is why we watch adjacent numbers whenever touching one.

**General Apps**
- Funnel and cohort screens in product-analytics tools: They split the outcome called conversion by stage and group, illuminating leaks hidden by a single average. What to examine: Since tools already provide group-based reading by default, average-only reporting is a habit, not a tooling limit.
- Credit scores and credit utilization: A score is an output that cannot be pushed directly. The inputs are lowering card utilization and paying on time. A score report even divides the weights of its factors to indicate where to act, mirroring the causality between lever and dashboard. What to examine: This is a model dashboard that attaches input specifications beside an output. Does our dashboard merely show numbers, or point to what to repair?

**Manufacturing / Field Operations**
- The andon cord in the Toyota Production System: When a worker finds a defect and pulls the cord, a signal light identifies the process with the problem. The light only points to the location; the actual repair happens in the causal process. What to examine: The signal points to a place and the hand goes to the cause. This is the chapter's attitude—receive a low number as a signal, not a verdict—in the language of the factory floor.

**Film / Nonfiction**
- Apollo 13: After the explosion, the crew watches the oxygen gauge needle fall but cannot stop it directly. Mission control works not on the needle but on the causal fuel cells and leak system. What to examine: This is the most dramatic image of watching a falling output while placing the hand on its causal input.
- Moneyball's turn to on-base percentage: Wins on the scoreboard are outputs that cannot be pushed directly. The team chooses on-base percentage rather than batting average as a manipulable input and uses it to produce runs. What to examine: Choosing a new input becomes strategy itself. Is the input we chose truly something we can grasp?

**Literature / Philosophy**
- Archery accuracy and form: Teachings of archery say that instead of trying to hit the target directly, one should focus on inputs of form—stance, draw, and breath—and accuracy follows as the result. What to examine: This meets the chapter's attitude of withdrawing the hand from the result and placing it on the input. Borrow its sequence and inspect form before pursuing a numerical target.

## Chapter 24. Business Goals and Quantitative Measures

The Reference Content section retained only the few cases directly tied to this chapter's argument; the rest are gathered here by medium. We begin with working procedures and metric frameworks, move through game operations, apps and commerce, film and sports, and offline settings such as kitchens and banks, and finish with cases in which the very choice of number on the dashboard became an event. Read them beside the chapter's two axes: input/output accounting, which writes what goes in and what comes out in separate columns, and the practice of reading a low number not as a mirror of talent but as a map pointing to what needs repair. The “point to examine” on each item will reveal which axis it touches.

**Real-World Work Procedures**
- The accounting basic of placing inputs and outputs in separate columns: Costs invested and results produced are not blurred into the same column. Putting in more does not guarantee more output; where you put it determines what comes out. Point to examine: Does our report mix effort and result in one column through a claim such as “we put in this much, so this much should come out”?
- Leading versus lagging indicators: A lagging result such as revenue cannot be pulled directly; it moves only when the preceding leading activities are touched. Sales teams have long distinguished between the number of contracts, which cannot be increased directly, and today's number of visits, which can. Point to examine: Mark every dashboard number as either a leading indicator you can pull or a lagging indicator composed of other people's decisions; goals without handles will reveal where they are hiding.
- Vanity metrics versus actionable metrics in Eric Ries's *The Lean Startup*: It distinguishes numbers that only rise and tell us nothing about what to do, such as cumulative downloads or total registrations (vanity), from figures that lead to action in the form “we changed X and Y moved” (actionable). Point to examine: Because the distinction forces a result slogan such as “increase revenue” to be rewritten as an activity we can change today, it points in the same direction as this chapter's conclusion that outputs must be translated into input language.
- Spotify Discover Weekly's pairing of qualitative and quantitative evidence: Spotify is known to have used interviews to learn what users expected from music discovery—the why—and form hypotheses, then verified them with quantitative evidence at scale. The pairing reportedly revealed that deep engagement with one song predicted satisfaction better than a week's cumulative plays. Point to examine: Qualitative evidence supplies why (the hypothesis), and quantitative evidence supplies where and how much (verification), precisely the pairing of two eyes described in the main text.
- Steve Krug's small-sample usability tests: In *Don't Make Me Think* and his later practical guide, he argued that makers know too much to see a screen anew, and that watching just three or four people actually use it reveals most serious problems. Point to examine: This helps estimate the cheapest qualitative tool for filling in the “why” beside the place where the numbers say “people leak here.”

**Business / Growth-Metric Frameworks**
- The AARRR (pirate metrics) funnel: Acquisition, activation, retention, referral, and revenue are separated into stages, decomposing the output called revenue into the inputs that precede it. Investor Dave McClure proposed the framework for startups, and it spread widely. Point to examine: The moment revenue is divided into five stages, the input within reach of your own hands becomes visible.
- Google's HEART framework: Proposed by a Google research team, it divides experience quality into happiness, engagement, adoption, retention, and task success. For each dimension, it requires teams to state a goal first, find signals of the goal, and only then choose metrics. Point to examine: Its sequence—moving from goals through signals to metrics instead of selecting metrics first—overlaps with this chapter's work of translating outputs into the language of inputs.

**Video-Game Operations**
- The difficulty-spike purchase structure of *Candy Crush Saga*: Analyses often note stages that are sharply harder than their neighbors every few levels, prompting stuck players to purchase extra moves or boosters. This can be read as a design of purchase moments spread gradually along the progression curve instead of concentrating acquisition-cost recovery on day one. Point to examine: Observe what kind of curve the chapter's checkpoint conclusion—defer the purchase output until after value is felt instead of squeezing it from the first screen—takes in actual operations.
- The rating prompt in the 2014 mobile *Dungeon Keeper*: Reports said that pressing the rating button sent a five-star response to the store, while responses from one to four stars were diverted to a feedback form for the developer. Point to examine: This is a contrary case that did something worse than using a mirror: instead of receiving a low score as a map pointing to repair, it tried to hide the output number.
- Backlash against the first progression design of *Star Wars Battlefront II* (2017): The opening design made players grind for dozens of hours or pay to unlock popular heroes such as Darth Vader. When the company explained that it sought to give players “a sense of achievement,” the explanation famously intensified the backlash. Point to examine: See how one first-experience decision can simultaneously reduce several outputs—revenue, reputation, and brand recall.

**Health / Self-Tracking Apps**
- Goal weight as an outcome versus behavioral goals: “Lose five kilograms” is an output you cannot pull directly, so it has to be translated into inputs such as diet and exercise before you can act. Point to examine: This everyday exercise in converting an output goal to input behavior provides a model for deciding how to translate “increase retention” in your game.

**General Apps / Commerce**
- The trap of targeting churn directly: Cancellation is the user's decision; what we can touch are the experiential inputs along the path to that decision. Point to examine: Is there a one-line input you can touch written beside the goal “reduce churn”?
- Duolingo's repeated A/B tests for retention: Rather than pulling first-day revenue directly, Duolingo breaks the output called retention into inputs and runs hundreds of experiments at once in a repeated cycle of changing and measuring. It lets users feel value through a free experience first, and when an output figure disappoints, moves to deciding which input to touch rather than collapsing. Point to examine: This is what the practice of reading numbers as maps looks like when fixed not merely as an individual attitude but as an organization's way of working.

**Film**
- The “not quite my tempo” scene in *Whiplash*: The teacher repeatedly judges the drummer's playing as fast or slow, although some analyses find the actual differences in tempo between the takes nearly impossible to distinguish by ear. Point to examine: See the danger of the mirror—how accepting a low evaluation as a verdict on talent freezes the hands—alongside the power held by the evaluator.
- On-base percentage (OBP) in *Moneyball*: The team looked past the conspicuous number of batting average to on-base percentage, an undervalued figure more closely connected to scoring runs. Point to examine: It shows the weight of metric selection: which number you place on the dashboard determines what you repair and even whom you recruit.

**Sports / Coaching**
- Basketball coach John Wooden's “do not watch the scoreboard; watch the ball”: He taught players to focus on controllable effort rather than uncontrollable wins and losses, and the score would follow. Point to examine: Hear how shifting attention from output (victory or defeat) to input (quality of practice) sounds in the language of coaching and compare it with this chapter's input/output distinction.

**Offline / Everyday Life**
- The chef who tried to return his Michelin stars: In 2017, French chef Sébastien Bras asked to be removed from the guide, saying that the weight of three stars oppressed the kitchen. Reports said the guide omitted the restaurant from its 2018 edition, then controversially restored it with two stars in 2019. Point to examine: The same thing happens in a kitchen outside games: an output evaluation becomes a mirror for the maker and freezes the hands, and even setting the evaluation aside carries a high cost.

**Metrics / Data**
- Netflix's retirement of star ratings and switch to thumbs (2017): Netflix replaced five-star ratings with thumbs up or down. It found that people gave high star ratings to worthy documentaries while actually watching light comedies—the score reflected the self they wanted to be more than their real preference—and said that participation rose sharply after the change. Point to examine: Depending on which signal is collected from the same user, a number can become a mirror reflecting ideals or a map pointing to behavior.
- YouTube's switch from view count to watch time (2012): YouTube announced that it was changing the recommendation criterion from clicks to watch time. When views were the standard, titles that made people click won; when watch time became the standard, videos that kept people to the end won. Point to examine: A single number placed on the dashboard changes the entire behavior of makers; this belongs to the same branch of decisions as on-base percentage in *Moneyball*.
- Wells Fargo's “eight accounts per customer” goal: When the American bank Wells Fargo tied aggressive cross-selling goals to employee evaluations, employees under pressure opened millions of accounts without customers' knowledge. The practice came to light in 2016 and led to enormous fines and a collapse of trust. Point to examine: It shows the scale of real corporate disaster produced by Goodhart's law: force an output number as a target, and the number is filled while its original meaning becomes empty.

## Chapter 25. Measurement and Iteration

The main Reference Content section retains only the few examples directly tied to this chapter's argument; the rest are collected here by medium. We begin with basic UX-research procedures, pass through experimental culture in apps, game operations and playtests, film production, and offline settings such as factories and stages, and end with examples of explaining redesigns. Read them beside the chapter's four arguments: the attitude that sees launch as the start of measurement; the measurement loop that pairs qualitative why with quantitative where and changes only one thing at a time; re-FTUE, in which existing users become beginners again after an update; and the boundary of measurement addiction, where repairing only what can be measured loses what cannot. The “point to examine” attached to each item will reveal which argument it touches.

**Real-World Work Procedures / UX Research**
- Paper-prototype usability testing: Have people point to hand-drawn screens before a line of code is written and filter out major misunderstandings first. Release rough work early and learn early. Point to examine: This is the cheapest first turn of the measurement loop; compare it with the stage when your team first shows work to a person.
- Wizard of Oz testing: Before building, have a person imitate responses behind the scenes so the system appears automated, cheaply validating the flow. A 1971 self-service airline-ticketing experiment and a 1983 “listening typewriter” speech-recognition experiment are cited as prototypes. Neither had automation; a person answered behind the scenes so only the seemingly real flow could be tested first. Point to examine: The order verifies the flow before building. The same method can cheaply test the spoken guidance screen in Chapter 27 before introducing it.

**General Apps / Experimental Culture**
- The traps of sample and duration in controlled experiments: Judge before enough people have gathered, and coincidence appears to win, making you choose the wrong side. Point to examine: Before deciding, first check that a baseline is recorded and sufficient observations have accumulated.
- Changing only one thing at a time: Change several things simultaneously and you cannot know what worked. Change only the steepest section and measure again. Point to examine: For a small team without infrastructure that separates experiments, this rule is almost its only safety device.
- The Google designer's “41 shades of blue” story: In a 2009 farewell post, departing Google designer Douglas Bowman complained that the team could not decide between two blues, tested 41 shades, and demanded data even on whether a border should be three or four pixels thick. Point to examine: This touches the warning about measurement addiction in the main text: when measurement begins to replace every decision, unmeasurable judgment leaves first.

**Video-Game Operations**
- Post-launch operation of live services: Launch is not the end but the start of measurement, when real people enter for the first time. After the original 2010 release of *Final Fantasy XIV* collapsed under poor reviews, the game was wholly rebuilt and relaunched in 2013 as *A Realm Reborn*. *No Man's Sky* is also known to have reversed its evaluation from negative to positive through repeated free updates after a sparse launch. Point to examine: These are evidence on the largest scale that a work can be repaired after launch, counterexamples to the habit that says launch means finished.
- Soft launch / limited regional prerelease: Open the game to only some people first, measure logs from a real environment, and repair before expanding. In early 2016, *Clash Royale* launched only in several countries including Canada, Australia, and New Zealand before its worldwide release, refined the game for nearly two months with real-user figures, then expanded globally. Point to examine: The sequence—release something rough to a subset, learn, and then expand—has the same shape as the ladder in the main text from paper through prototype to field measurement.
- Existing users readapting after a season or major update: When menus change, the people who stayed longest become the most lost. Do not teach everything; point out only what changed. Point to examine: This is the basic prescription of re-FTUE. Check whether your redesign notice contains a sentence pointing to “only what changed.”
- Retention by cohort diverging over time: Early and later arrivals have different textures, producing different figures on the same screen. Point to examine: This provides the eye that distinguishes whether a falling number comes from the screen or from a change in the texture of arrivals.
- Games refined by releasing rough work first in early access: *Baldur's Gate 3* opened Act 1 in 2020 and reflected feedback from millions of players by changing some ability checks and one companion's voice actor and story. *Hades* is also known to have taken shape through feedback received during early access. Point to examine: The principle of releasing rough work early and learning from real users instead of releasing a finished product all at once became a form of game distribution.
- *Halo 3* heat maps of death and kill locations: Logs of where people died and made kills on multiplayer maps were plotted as heat distributions on a map and reportedly used in large-scale playtests before and after launch. Point to examine: Unfold numbers into an image and “where the leak is” becomes visible at a glance—a model of quantitative visualization.

**Video Games / Playtest Observation**
- Valve's observation of fresh testers: Valve brings in someone who has never played, then watches beside them as they launch for the first time. In *Portal*, it is said that the originally cluttered environment became today's blank white walls and floors after testers could not tell what to solve first. Point to examine: Qualitative observation of where hands stop changed even the art direction, broadening the range of what observation can repair beyond screen layout.

**Film / Production Procedures**
- Films whose endings were reshot after test screenings: *Fatal Attraction* (1987) changed its ending in a three-week reshoot after test audiences disliked the original. *I Am Legend* (2007) is also known to have received a different ending after test audiences rejected the original. Point to examine: The film industry has institutionalized the procedure of showing a work to real audiences once before release; observe how qualitative measurement operates outside games.
- Pixar's unfinished screenings and Braintrust: Pixar is known to screen films internally in a rough state every few months, where directors and storytellers gather to exchange candid criticism. Ed Catmull's remark that “our films all start out terrible” is often retold with the practice. Point to examine: It institutionalizes the courage to show work before completion while leaving the director the authority to decide whether to apply criticism, a boundary that protects the work's identity.

**Offline / Everyday Life**
- The andon cord in the Toyota Production System: Any worker who finds an abnormality can pull the cord and stop the line, making the problem visible on the spot and creating a culture of finding its cause. Point to examine: It is a prototype that places measurement and repair in the middle of the process rather than at its end, contrasting with the habit of beginning repairs only after launch.
- A stand-up comedian refining material in clubs: Before a large stage or special recording, comedians widely tour small clubs, test the same joke dozens of times, and discard or repair the parts that do not draw laughter. Point to examine: Using audience laughter as a log, this is the measurement loop closest to the body, repeatedly refining a rough draft.

**Real-World Work Procedures / Education**
- The “we moved it” sign in a renovated store: When regulars cannot find a product where it always was, the sign does not teach them again but points only to the changed location. Point to examine: This is the smallest specimen of the re-FTUE prescription to point out only what changed instead of teaching everything.
- A redesign that moved every familiar place and left regulars lost: When Windows 8 removed the Start menu, longtime users faced fierce frustration because they could not find the button they had always pressed. Windows 10 eventually restored it. Point to examine: The warning that those who stayed longest become the most confused played out at the scale of an operating system.
- Microsoft Office 2007's Ribbon redesign: Replacing menus and toolbars wholesale with the Ribbon led longtime users to complain for some time that they could not find familiar commands; tools even circulated to locate their former menu positions. Point to examine: Apart from whether the redesign itself was good or bad, ask whether there was a sufficient bridge pointing out changes to existing users who had lost embodied routes.
- What's New guidance in software version updates: Instead of retraining everything, it clearly announces only what is new. Point to examine: Understand why the format of telling existing users only the differences became an industry standard through the cost accounting of re-FTUE.

## Chapter 26. Can We Entrust the Expansion of Experience to Users?

The main Reference Content section retains only a few examples directly tied to the chapter's argument; the rest are collected here by medium. We begin with UGC platforms, mod communities, and creative tools where users become builders; continue through games that read people from their first choices, behavioral-economics experiments, apps and commerce, offline checkout counters, literature, and video. Read them beside the chapter's three arguments: reading user choice as a hypothesis rather than proof of taste; recognizing that choices are costs before freedom; and providing a creative FTUE and quality fence because builders also have a first thirty seconds. The “point to examine” on each item will show which argument it touches.

**UGC Platforms / Games and Toys**
- *LittleBigPlanet* (2008): An early case that made “Play, Create, Share” its slogan and put the expansion of experience in users' hands. It showed on consoles a structure where user-made stages become other users' activities. Point to examine: Observe how a game raised the transformation from spectator to builder into its identity slogan.
- *Dreams* (2020): Users make and share the games themselves, so builders become the content. Point to examine: As creative tools deepen, the exam paper facing a first-time user grows thicker; examine how creative FTUE was laid out.
- LEGO Ideas: When a fan-designed set gathers 10,000 supporters, it receives company review and may become an official product; selected designers receive part of the revenue. Point to examine: Creation is entrusted to users but the company retains a quality fence before it reaches others, placing it in the same branch as *Mario Maker*'s clear check.

**Mods / Maker Communities**
- *Minecraft* mods and custom maps: Users expand the world, enriching each person's experience beyond what designers laid out individually. Point to examine: This is the representative gain from entrusting expansion; see how a community crosses the limit of what designers alone can make.
- The *Skyrim* and Steam Workshop mod ecosystem: Inviting people to help make the road binds them more deeply than laying every path for them. Point to examine: Look for the source of a life that continues more than a decade after release in the motivations of people who build and share mods.

**Creative Tools / Education**
- Scratch's remix culture: The children's coding tool created at MIT encourages people to open and modify someone else's project rather than begin from a blank page, and publicly shows the lineage of remixes. Point to examine: It precisely follows the creative-FTUE prescription of replacing the heaviest exam—a blank canvas—with a half-built sample.

**Creator Platforms**
- The YouTube and Twitch creator economy: Someone who came to watch becomes a maker, and what they make becomes something others watch. Point to examine: The staircase from watcher to maker became the platform's business model itself.

**First Choices in Video Games**
- Character creation in *Animal Crossing*: Immediately after launch, players choose skin tone, hair, eyes, and nose. What they choose reflects preferences they cannot state. A mirror at home lets them change at any time, treating the first choice as a hypothesis rather than proof. Point to examine: See how the device that keeps a first choice hypothetical—the door for changing one's mind—is placed inside the game.
- Creating a Wii Mii: “Choose a look-alike” lets people begin from a prebuilt face and edit it, reducing the burden of constructing from a blank page. Point to examine: See what the prescription of reducing blank-page burden with a sample looks like in character creation.
- Paragon/Renegade in *Mass Effect*: Dialogue choices accumulate toward noble or rough directions, forming the protagonist's personality and reflecting the player's inclination. Point to examine: It matches the main text's idea of reading texture accumulated in the same direction, rather than a single choice, as signal.

**Real Life / Behavioral Economics**
- Organ-donation opt-in and opt-out defaults: Registration rates reportedly differ sharply depending only on whether the default is “not a donor” or “a donor.” Point to examine: Much of a first choice is in fact produced by the designer's default, so its contribution must be subtracted when reading choice as taste.
- Stated preferences versus preferences revealed by behavior: People often write ideal answers in surveys but behave differently when paying a real cost. Point to examine: Confirm that this chapter's principle—observe choices instead of asking—is longstanding common sense in behavioral economics.
- Filter bubbles: Recommending only more of the texture shown once may deprive users of a chance to encounter textures they did not know. Point to examine: This supports the need for a fence that infers from choice without blocking the route beyond the inference.
- The IKEA effect: Experiments report a tendency to value a self-assembled box more highly than the same completed object (Norton et al., 2012). Point to examine: Entrusting a small effort creates attachment, but when effort crosses the line of delight, it becomes an exam on the first screen.
- The cake-mix egg legend: A marketing legend claims that mixes requiring only water failed to sell until people were asked to add one egg. But the decision to require an egg preceded that psychological advice and was made for flavor and quality, so verification describing the story as closer to myth also circulates. Point to examine: The intuition of leaving one handprint has independent support from the IKEA effect, but the popular anecdote itself remains an object of verification.

**General Apps / Commerce**
- Fatigue from comparing dozens of similar products: More things to choose slow decisions until people choose nothing and close the page. Point to examine: It presents in a bodily familiar form the claim that choices are costs before freedom on the first screen.

**Offline / Everyday Life**
- The chain of questions at a sandwich shop: New customers commonly freeze at a counter that asks them in succession to choose bread, length, cheese, vegetables, and sauce. The questions that let regulars enjoy building it their way become an exam without right answers for newcomers. Point to examine: At the checkout counter too, the same choices are freedom for the familiar and cost for the unskilled.

**Apps / Video and Music**
- Choosing artists just after Spotify registration: New blank-page users choose a few favorite artists, and those choices become the first seeds of recommendations. As listening behavior accumulates, real behavioral signals gradually replace the seeds. Point to examine: This is the standard implementation of reading a first choice as a seed and replacing it once behavior accumulates.
- “Choose titles you like” after Netflix registration: A new profile chooses several favorite titles to seed a taste profile, but later behavior—watching, stopping, and rewatching—overwrites those choices. Point to examine: Letting later behavior overwrite the first choice avoids preserving a person incorrectly from a single decision.
- TikTok's recommendation feed: Without a survey asking what people like, it reads taste solely from behavior—videos watched to the end, immediately skipped, and time spent hesitating—to choose what comes next. Point to examine: This is the extreme of using hands rather than words as signals, showing both the power to read texture without asking and the danger that the reading may confine a person.

**Literature**
- Gamebooks such as the *Choose Your Own Adventure* series: Written in the second-person “you,” they make the reader the protagonist and branch endings through direct choices at each fork. Point to examine: This is one of the oldest devices that turns a reader into a builder of the story.

**Film / Drama**
- *Black Mirror: Bandersnatch* (2018): Viewers choose within ten seconds and send the story down branches; if they do not choose, a default carries it onward. Point to examine: It turns a watcher into a chooser while using defaults to reduce the burden on someone who hesitates, a device for regulating choice weight.

## Chapter 27. FTUE in the Age of AI

The main reference section retains only a few examples directly tied to the chapter’s argument; the rest are organized here by medium. They begin with the principles of recommendation systems and proceed through common patterns in personalized products, conversational guidance, trust and transparency, the order of tools and goals, offline settings such as roads and hotels, and finally images from film and games. Product features change quickly, so the selections emphasize principles and failure patterns that will not soon become obsolete. Read them alongside the chapter’s three arguments: the cold-start paradox, in which the first encounter that appears to need personalization most is precisely where a lack of data makes personalization least accurate; the distinct failure modes of personalization, conversational guidance, and on-the-spot generation; and the trust fence that explains why something is shown, lets the user turn it off, and lets them correct it when it is wrong. The “point to examine” attached to each item will then show what it connects to.

**Principles of recommendation systems**
- Initial exploration in TikTok’s recommendation feed: the system knows little about a new account, so its first session is almost entirely exploratory and the feed begins with little personalization. By quickly reading time spent on each short video and where the user’s finger pauses, it accelerates the writing of the first line on the blank page. Point to examine: the two sides of solving cold starts through behavioral observation rather than questions—the speed, and the possibility that this speed may also confine a person sooner.
- Non-personalized fallback: when nothing is known, first show broadly popular items; an ordinary first screen is better than a bad fit. Point to examine: whether our first screen has a criterion for refusing to dress an insufficient guess in the clothes of certainty.
- Asking about tastes immediately after sign-up: a common method of writing the first line on a blank page with a few initial choices, addressing the same difficulty as the first choice in Chapter 26 by another means. Point to examine: at what number and weight of questions does a tool for resolving the blank page become another exam paper?
- Choosing artists immediately after joining Spotify: on first launch, users choose several artists they like, writing a first line on an otherwise data-free blank page. Those few choices immediately shape the home screen and initial recommendations around that person’s disposition. Point to examine: a standard implementation of a cold-start remedy, in which the first few choices directly determine the texture of the first screen.
- Collaborative filtering’s “similar people” inference: it guesses from the behavior of similar people, but when that inference misses, the very first impression is wrong. Point to examine: personalization failures begin not with a technical defect but with the nature of inference, which is why a correction handle must always accompany them.
- Recommendations that fossilize one-off behavior into taste: failures such as recommending strollers for months after someone bought one as a gift occur across commerce. Behavior is an honest signal, but treating a single behavior as identity turns that signal into a cage. Point to examine: the Chapter 26 principle of treating a choice as a hypothesis rather than a conclusion also applies to machines, and the user needs a handle that says, “This is not my taste.”

**Common patterns in personalized products**
- Netflix’s personalized title artwork: the same work receives different representative images for different viewers. Someone who has watched a film featuring Uma Thurman, for example, may see *Pulp Fiction* represented by a shot featuring Thurman. The first screen itself is rebuilt to match that person. Point to examine: because this most clearly implements the chapter’s premise that the same service can give each person a different first moment, examine the amplitude both when it fits and when it misses.
- Microsoft Office’s Clippy (1997): if a document looked like a letter, the assistant interrupted with “It looks like you’re writing a letter.” It often misread the context, repeated the same interference, and offered no obvious way to turn it off, so it became widely disliked and was removed from later versions. Point to examine: pre-generative technology already showed that kindness becomes interference when the three pillars—why it is shown, whether it can be turned off, and whether mistakes can be corrected—are absent.
- The Target pregnancy-prediction coupon anecdote: a widely circulated 2012 article said the American retailer inferred pregnancy from purchasing patterns and sent related coupons, leading a father to learn of his daughter’s pregnancy after the store did. Critics have noted that the anecdote’s details are difficult to verify. Point to examine: even accurate personalization feels like surveillance rather than kindness when the reason it is shown remains hidden, supporting the transparency pillar.

**Principles of conversational guidance**
- An airline chatbot’s confident error and compensation ruling: an airline chatbot in Canada wrongly said that a bereavement fare could be requested after travel. In 2024, a local dispute-resolution body ruled that the chatbot was part of the website, held the company responsible for its words, and ordered it to pay the difference. Point to examine: the company ultimately pays the cost of a polished wrong answer; introducing a conversational screen requires designing responsibility for its errors at the same time.

**Principles of trust and transparency**
- Reveal why this is being shown: users need at least a glimpse of where a received screen came from for it to feel like kindness rather than interference. Point to examine: this is the general rule behind the fence’s first pillar; check whether a line naming the origin appears with recommendations on our first screen.
- Fairness skew in personalization: systems may fit frequently observed groups well and rare groups badly, treating people differently from their first encounter. Point to examine: whether we measure which groups are especially poorly served and whether the quality of the first moment differs by group.

**The order of tools and goals**
- Do not let the tool determine the goal: even when a new technology arrives, first determine what to accomplish and only then find where AI is better. Point to examine: whether the first question is not “What can we do with AI?” but “Is AI truly better at implementing this principle?”

**Offline / everyday settings**
- Blind faith in navigation systems and automation bias: incidents in many countries have involved people following directions without question into blocked roads or bodies of water. Researchers call the tendency for human doubt to weaken before confident machine guidance “automation bias.” Point to examine: why a polished machine error is more dangerous than hesitant human guidance, and how the chapter’s warning—that newcomers lack the knowledge to question it—plays out on the road too.
- A hotel concierge greeting a first-time guest: an experienced concierge does not define the newcomer at once. They form a hypothesis with two or three light questions, narrow the recommendations while observing the response, admit when they do not know, and offer to find out. Point to examine: this human model for handling a cold start gives machine guidance a baseline—begin with questions rather than certainty and correct the course immediately when it misses.

**Images from film, television, and animation**
- The Gap store scene in *Minority Report* (2002): a store scanner reads the protagonist’s eyes and greets him as “Mr. Yakamoto,” asking about a previous purchase. But the eyes were transplanted from someone else, so the system confidently treats him as the wrong person. Point to examine: confidence in having read someone and actually reading them correctly are different things; observe what kind of treatment confident misidentification produces.
- *Black Mirror*, “Joan Is Awful” (2023): a streaming company automatically generates a series starring each individual subscriber. It shows an extreme future in which a machine gives every person a different first moment, while also exposing the betrayal felt when the generated “me” diverges from the real me. Point to examine: what on-the-spot generation becomes when taken to its limit, and the cost in trust exacted by generation that misses.
- The Sibyl System in the anime *Psycho-Pass* (2012): the system scans every citizen’s latent criminality, sorts people by number, and treats them accordingly. Yet some people are misclassified, remaining numerically low even as they commit crimes. Point to examine: the more precise the classification, the greater the mismatch when it fails, expanded into a world where classification determines how a person is treated.

**Examples from games**
- The AI Director in *Left 4 Dead* (2008): instead of placing enemies in fixed locations, it reads the players’ health, ammunition, progress, and tension in that run and lays out enemies and supplies differently. It infers from the current situation to build a different experience for each group on the same map. Point to examine: games were building different experiences for different people before generative AI, though the target being read was the player’s momentary state rather than their taste.
- Drivatars in the *Forza* series: the system learns how a real player brakes and takes corners, then creates an AI double that drives like that person and appears in other people’s games. Point to examine: when personalization goes as far as imitating a person, the imitated self appears in someone else’s first experience, creating a new responsibility.
