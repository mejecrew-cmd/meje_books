# Chapter 6. Four Paths to Material — Library Headings and the Density of Daily Life

A common block occurs when character, world, and will all exist, yet the writer cannot decide what happens today. Material is not absent. There is either too much to choose from or no method for finding it.

This chapter introduces four discovery paths.

## Before Discovery: The Nature of an Everyday Cross-Section

An everyday cross-section is “one scene from daily life, with a beginning and landing, experienced by one character in one setting.” A huge contract dispute is climax and drama, not an everyday cross-section. Cross-sections occupy the days between.

Their core is the density of “this is how people live in this world”: thirty minutes choosing a drink after Monday practice, a person looking at a phone while waiting at a signing, a silent nod to another team in a dorm corridor.

Park Wan-suh's *Mother's Stake*, Chekhov's stories, Hirokazu Kore-eda's *I Wish*, and the Korean film *House of Hummingbird* compress a period and a person into small units of ordinary time.

Smaller is not automatically better. Density matters more than size. A tiny event holding a life is strong; a huge event disconnected from interiority is empty.

## Path One: Explore Vault Headings

Begin with the six headings: background, material, people, events, objects, and places.

A background reveals recurring life: arriving alone early at the practice room, leaving last at night, or looking into a mirror under lights that never switch off. Cross unused material headings against the Coverage Matrix. From a character record, ask how a trait, habit, or past event appears in daily life. If Myeongsuk bites her left thumbnail under extreme tension, the moment when that habit appears is already a scene.

Look into gaps between major events: ten days between Performances A and B, the night before contract renewal, or two days before conflict surfaces. Finally, ask how an object or place meets this person. A concrete object or location becomes the **ignition point** that lights a vague idea into a scene.

## Path Two: Combine the Basic Questions

When exploration produces nothing, use a mechanical but effective combination: **who + where + what**.

The collision creates unexpected freshness and forces an abstraction such as “a tense story” into “Myeongsuk waiting in a hospital.” Choose “who” from characters, “where” from place headings, and “what” from material headings.

Myeongsuk + hospital waiting room + waiting immediately prompts questions: injury test, accompanying another member, or her own examination? Any direction can become a piece.

Awkwardness is a clue. Myeongsuk + parking lot + phone call feels odd; ask why she took the call in an empty lot and whether the news is good or bad.

If three elements stall, add **when**: 6 a.m., midnight, thirty minutes before a performance, or a debut anniversary. Time changes the material's temperature.

## Path Three: Explore Cross-Relationships

Material often comes from relationships rather than one person.

**Map relationships.** List teammates, other teams, staff, and fans. Every relationship contains material.

**Find tension points.** What remains unsaid? What discomfort lies beneath harmony? What long-held gratitude has not been voiced?

**Find turning points.** If the Vault records a relationship-changing event, use daily life immediately before or after it: the day before change, or the first meeting afterward.

**Reverse roles.** Put the usual strong person in the weak position, or the supported person in the supporting role. If leader Myeongsuk must ask the youngest member for help, that reversal becomes material.

## Path Four: Inherit from Other Manuscripts

Once pieces accumulate, completed manuscripts begin generating material.

**Inherit an open ending.** A completed landing may leave a question. Daily life after Myeongsuk's decision becomes another independent cross-section, not merely a sequel.

**Follow a cross-appearance.** If supporting character B reveals an intriguing side in A's piece, make that side the subject of a B-led piece.

**Inherit a world discovery.** New detail from one manuscript enters the Vault and generates material for another character. Writing enriches the world, which generates writing—the material side of the feedback loop.

**Follow unfinished threads.** A briefly seen person, unexplained custom, or mentioned but undeveloped event is a seed.

## Material Density Test

Ask five questions before promoting an idea into a manuscript:

1. Is there something the character can lose—stakes?
2. Can this happen only in K-pop K, or in any world?
3. How can the protagonist's body appear?
4. Which form fits best?
5. Does it reveal something not yet said about this character?

Question 5 matters most. Repeating a sufficiently explored face wastes material. Density is high when the idea reveals something otherwise inaccessible.

## Managing the Material List

Many ideas cannot be written immediately and will be forgotten unless recorded. Keep a simple list: character name + summary + expected form + discovery path.

Example: “Kang Myeongsuk / quietly waits with another team's member in a hospital one afternoon / sketch or dialogue / cross-relationship exploration.”

Integrating the list into the Vault puts material inside the SSOT. Once the list grows, writing plans emerge automatically: choose an item that fills an empty Matrix cell.

## Material Is Not Absent

Most blocks do not come from missing material. Writers may lack a discovery method, fear that the idea is too small, or doubt that it can become a manuscript.

The cross-section lives in small things. Five minutes in which a K-pop K member cooks ramen alone can work: the cooking method, music during those minutes, and room temperature produce the density of “this is how life works here.”

Material already exists in the Vault, relationships, and margins of prior manuscripts. Enter through the four paths and it appears.
