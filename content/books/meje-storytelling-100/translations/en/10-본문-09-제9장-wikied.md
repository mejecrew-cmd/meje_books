# Chapter 9. Ending Landings and Dramatizing Numbers — What Remains at the End

How a story ends determines what readers take away. If a manuscript has been good throughout but ends on a strangely wrong note, readers feel disappointed with the whole piece. Conversely, even if the middle felt a little dull, a final scene that lands precisely makes the manuscript memorable.

This chapter addresses Criterion E (ending landing) and Criterion F (dramatizing numbers) together. Both matter in the closing phase. They share a chapter because the most powerful landing occurs when the physical scale of the world is made sensory in that same moment. A manuscript reaches its greatest density when E and F meet in one scene.

## What Is a Landing?

“Landing” evokes an airplane settling onto a runway. A good landing brings the plane to the ground with barely any impact felt by the passengers. A manuscript works the same way. A well-landed piece lets readers emerge naturally from the story while carrying its resonance with them.

There are two opposites of landing.

First, **abrupt severance.** The story does not end; it suddenly stops. The final scene seems built to continue into another scene, but that next scene simply disappears. The reader is left in midair, asking, “Is that the end?”

Second, **over-explanation.** The story has ended, but the writer keeps talking: what the story means, what the character learned, and what will happen next. The writer draws the conclusion in the reader's place, leaving no room for the reader to enter.

A landing lies between them. The story is fully over, yet the reader can remain inside its aftereffect for a moment. The final line of Hemingway's *The Old Man and the Sea*, in which the old man sleeps and dreams of lions; the mother's calling voice at the end of Park Wan-suh's *Who Ate Up All the Shinga?*; and the ending of Chekhov's “The Lady with the Dog,” where he understands that the hardest and most complicated part is only beginning. None is a severance or an over-explanation. Each leaves one final beat in which the reader stays briefly where the story ended.

## Three Types of Landing

Three kinds appear frequently in slice-of-life scenarios.

**Still landing.** The story ends with a character stopped in a particular state or posture. Kang Myeongsuk stands before the practice-room window and looks outside. The narration does not state what she thinks or what she has decided. The still image is the ending.

A still landing gives readers the most space. They fill in the character's interior while looking at the scene. Like the “gutter”—the empty space between panels that the reader's imagination fills to complete the story—in Scott McCloud's *Understanding Comics*, the reader's imagination completes the work inside the still landing's open space.

**Action landing.** The character does something, and the action summarizes the entire story in one line. Kang Myeongsuk smooths the crumpled waiting ticket and sets it straight again. Without words, the action conveys her attitude toward today's wait and her preparation for what comes next.

An action landing overlaps with the body anchor. When the final body anchor becomes the landing, Criteria B and E are satisfied at once. This is one of the most effective ways to land.

**Dialogue landing.** The story ends with a brief exchange between two characters. This is especially natural in comic-dialogue form. The final line reverberates through the entire story; when readers revisit it after finishing the manuscript, it sounds different.

The principle for dialogue landings is that the final line must not explain. A didactic line such as “In the end, this is what really matters” is a summary, not a landing. The final line must create the silence that follows.

## Failed Landings and Their Correction

Knowing the failure patterns makes revision easier.

**Pattern 1: the summary ending.** Just before the manuscript ends, it summarizes the whole story. “Kang Myeongsuk learned something from all these experiences. She would be different from today onward.” The writer is concluding in the reader's place.

Correction: delete the summary. The story should already have said it without that sentence. If the story feels incomplete once the summary is gone, the body of the piece has not accumulated enough.

**Pattern 2: the new-introduction ending.** A new character appears or a new situation begins just as the manuscript is almost over. “Then the door opened. The person standing there was someone she had never expected.” This introduces the next story; it does not land this one.

Correction: delete the new introduction. End with what the story already contains. Begin the new material in another manuscript.

**Pattern 3: landing too early.** The story lands before it has built enough. When readers ask, “Is that all?”, the problem is not necessarily the landing itself. The story has not developed sufficiently before reaching it.

Correction: reinforce the scene immediately before the landing. A character's state must change just beforehand. That change gives the landing meaning.

**Pattern 4: misuse of the open ending.** An “open ending” is confused with “not ending.” An open ending lands in a way readers can interpret in several directions. Simply not ending is merely stopping. Even an open ending must land.

Correction: make the character's state clear in the final scene or sentence. A clear state lets readers imagine what follows in multiple directions. If the state is unclear, they cannot imagine anything.

## Dramatizing Numbers: Turning Data into Sensation

Criterion F is the most specific to the MEJE method among the six quality criteria. A Vault created through Librarying contains numerical data, and Criterion F governs how those figures enter a manuscript. Its essence is: **do not convey data merely as numbers.** A number is information, and information is not felt. Only a dramatized number becomes sensory.

When a number is simply written into a manuscript, readers process it as information rather than sensation. “K-pop K has a fandom of twelve million” is information. Dramatizing numbers makes those twelve million perceptible.

The principle is simple: **turn a large number into a small body**, or **turn an abstract figure into a concrete scene**.

## Techniques for Dramatizing Numbers

**Scale down.** Reduce a large number to a size a character can experience directly.

There are “twelve million fans.” Kang Myeongsuk sits at a fan signing. Three hundred people pass before her—three hundred people whose eyes met hers today. Yet three hundred out of twelve million is 0.0025 percent. That number rests on her hands.

“Twelve million” cannot be felt. “The three hundred who held my hand today” can. Scaling down makes the transition.

**Convert time.** Turn a duration or repetition count into a physical trace of time.

There is a “seven-year career.” Calculating that the character has spent over thirty-five thousand hours in practice rooms is not dramatization. A slightly sunken floorboard where she has stood for years before the same mirror is. Seven years are inscribed in the hollow in the floor.

**Density of objects.** Express a large count through the density of a single scene.

There is a “one-hundred-person team.” A scene with all one hundred people in one space is chaotic. Instead, shoes pack the dormitory's entryway cabinets, with each person's name written on each pair. What does one hundred pairs of shoes in one home look like? That sight makes the number one hundred sensory.

**Contrast comparison.** Compare two periods or conditions to make the magnitude of change perceptible.

There is “change before and after debut.” Compare the bag brought to this practice room seven years ago with the bag used there now. Two bags contain seven years of change.

## Criterion F When There Are No Numbers

Not every manuscript includes numerical data. Depending on the material, figures from the Vault may not connect directly.

In that case, apply Criterion F by making the world's scale and density sensory. Does the manuscript contain details through which readers can physically feel the scale of K-pop K's world?

The density of the schedule posted in the practice-room corridor; laundry always waiting for the dormitory's communal washing machine; a fan-signing queue whose end cannot be seen. These descriptions mention no figures, yet they make the scale of K-pop K's world perceptible.

## Connecting Criteria E and F

There is a reason ending landings and dramatized numbers share a chapter. The strongest manuscripts emerge when the world's numerical scale becomes sensory in the landing.

If the scene that lands a Kang Myeongsuk story also contains K-pop K's scale, two things occur at once. Readers feel the resonance of the story ending while realizing how large its world is.

For example, Kang Myeongsuk sits alone in the waiting room after a fan signing. Notes left by fans today are stacked on the desk. She looks at the thickness of the pile and lightly presses its edge with a finger. The manuscript ends.

This landing satisfies Criterion E. It is a still landing in which Myeongsuk feels something within that thickness. It also satisfies Criterion F: the thickness of the note pile makes the scale of today's signing sensory. Without a number, thickness takes the number's place.

## The Weight of the Final Sentence

The final sentence occupies a special position. It is the line on which the reader's eyes remain longest after finishing a manuscript, and it determines the resonance of the whole story.

Several principles guide it.

**Shorter is stronger.** A short final sentence leaves a longer resonance than a long one. Too much explanation occupies the reader with processing and leaves no time to feel the aftereffect.

**End with action or sensation.** End on concrete action or sensation rather than abstraction. “She removed her earphones” is stronger than “She realized something today.”

**Echo the first sentence.** When possible, let the final sentence echo an image or word from the first. Connecting first and last completes the manuscript as a circle, and the reader feels that circle.

**Create silence.** The final sentence should make readers pause. That pause is the manuscript's true final part.

## What Chapter 9 Conveys

A manuscript is remembered by its final sentence. This is why Criterion E is MUST PASS among the six quality criteria. Without a landing, there is effectively no manuscript.

Criterion F, dramatizing numbers, increases a landing's density. It turns quantitative data into sensation and places the weight of the world inside the manuscript. That weight makes the landing firmer.
