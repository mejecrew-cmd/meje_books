# Chapter 11. The Reference-Writer System — Integrating Technical and Professional Routines with Interior Sensation

Writing inevitably brings points of blockage: how to write this scene, why this sentence feels dead, or how a character's interior should reach the reader. The reference-writer system is one way through them.

It consciously studies a specific technique from a particular writer and applies it to one's own manuscript. This is not imitation. It means learning how the writer handled a situation, then transforming that technique to suit one's own world and characters.

## Principles of the Reference-Writer System

Every writer learns from other writers; there is no reason to hide it. In his 1920 essay “Philip Massinger,” T. S. Eliot wrote that immature poets imitate while mature poets steal. Here, “steal” means understanding the principle of a technique, digesting it, and making it one's own.

Our system follows two principles.

First, **the pairing principle.** Pair a foreign writer with a Korean writer. The foreign writer supplies technical structure; the Korean writer supplies linguistic sensibility and emotion. Referring only to a foreign writer when writing Korean can make the language feel translated. Paired with a Korean writer, the technique works naturally within Korean.

Second, **the material-matching principle.** Choose the reference writer according to the material. Do not use the same writer for everything. Identify the technique the material requires, then select a writer who excels at it.

## Proven Pairings

The four principal pairings correspond to four techniques repeatedly needed in K-pop K material: precise portrayal of professional routine, minute interior change, irony in everyday relationships, and language for music and stage experience.

**Cormac McCarthy + Kim Hoon: technical and professional routines, precise description**

McCarthy is exceptionally specific when portraying work and technique. In *The Road* and *No Country for Old Men*, he precisely describes which tools characters handle, how they handle them, and in what sequence they work. That precision roots the character inside the occupation.

Kim Hoon treats the techniques of war and survival with similar precision in *Song of the Sword* and *The Fortress*. Yet his Korean rhythm creates a different sensation. McCarthy's precision is cold; Kim Hoon's is painfully suffocating.

In K-pop K, use this pairing for a dancer repeatedly practicing a particular move, a sound engineer checking stage equipment, or a manager handling daily logistics.

**Ted Chiang + Oh Jung-hee: interior sensation and minute psychological change**

Ted Chiang excels at conveying complex ideas through a character's interior experience. In “Story of Your Life,” the protagonist's altered perception of time while learning an alien language emerges through fine psychological changes. A vast concept becomes the language of body and interior.

Oh Jung-hee is among the Korean writers most precise in portraying women's inner lives. In *The Bird* and *The Garden of Childhood*, sensation, memory, and emotion flow without separation. Her characters exist where body, feeling, and perception intermingle.

Use this pairing for a member internalizing the rules after first entering the idol world, a character gradually recognizing change in a longstanding fandom relationship, or an altered state of consciousness onstage.

**Raymond Carver + Park Wan-suh: everyday relationships, routine, and small ironies**

Carver reveals enormous things through small details of daily life. Two people eating, talking, or doing nothing communicate what cannot be said between them. His technique is omission: speaking by not saying.

Park Wan-suh captures everyday irony with exceptional sharpness. In *The Naked Tree* and her short fiction, an ordinary scene suddenly reveals a deep wound or contradiction without becoming heavy. A light touch resonates within the reader for a long time.

Use this pairing for an old team conflict dissolved into daily life without resolution, invisible tension in an ordinary moment between senior and junior, or a small irony in the relationship with fandom.

**Haruki Murakami + Yi Sang: music and performance, interior thought**

Murakami is especially skilled at connecting music to a character's inner state. A particular piece links with memory, emotion, and thought. Music becomes not background but psychological coordinates.

In Yi Sang's “Wings” (1936), stream of consciousness was one of the earliest experiments in modern Korean fiction. Fragmentary questions—why am I so tired, what is my wife thinking—flow in succession, without separating observation of reality from interior monologue. The story moves freely between outer reality and inner life. Combined with Korean rhythm, that freedom creates a singular sensation.

Use this pairing for moments just before or after a performance, when particular music recalls a memory, or when the selves above and below the stage collide.

## How to Use Reference Writers in Practice

The system operates concretely in actual drafting.

**Step 1. Identify the blockage.** Determine which part is dead and what technique is needed. If you cannot portray a professional routine, choose McCarthy and Kim Hoon. If you cannot capture interior change, choose Ted Chiang and Oh Jung-hee.

**Step 2. Read a reference passage.** Find a similar scene in the selected writer's work and read one or two relevant passages. There is no need to reread the entire work.

**Step 3. Analyze the technique.** Examine sentence length, sensory order, information placement, and the location of omissions. Put the technique into words: “When McCarthy describes an action, he pairs the tool's name with the body part it touches.”

**Step 4. Transform it into your own language.** Adapt the analyzed technique to K-pop K and the character at hand. Apply McCarthy's method of describing firearm maintenance to Kang Myeongsuk's microphone grip.

These four steps take five to ten minutes while drafting. Doing this when blocked is far more productive than remaining blocked.

## The Special Nature of Music, Performance, and Stage Routine

K-pop K is a world where music and performance are everyday life. Its material needs approaches that differ from ordinary fiction.

Music is abstract and difficult to describe in text. Murakami's method is to portray not the music itself but what it does to a character: shoulders lowering slightly at the first phrase of a song, or a character entering another time the moment music begins.

The stage is a space of duality. The person onstage and offstage is the same yet functions like a different being. Yi Sang's stream of consciousness helps portray this duality: a character performs a movement while thinking of something else, and the two layers overlap.

Professional routine is repetition, and it is difficult to describe repetition freshly every time. McCarthy and Kim Hoon add a condition unique to today's repetition rather than presenting repetition as repetition. Today the wrist feels slightly heavy. Today the practice room is quieter than usual. A small difference makes repetition a new experience.

## Reference Is Not the Standard

A reference writer's technique must not become the standard for a K-pop K manuscript.

The goal is not to write in McCarthy's manner or to write K-pop K's Kang Myeongsuk as though she were McCarthy's character. The goal is to borrow a technique so K-pop K's slice of life is conveyed more effectively.

Technique must not precede character. If a character moves to accommodate the reference technique, the manuscript has reversed its priorities. Character is central; technique is a tool.

Nor must the system be used every time. When writing flows and technique emerges naturally, no reference is needed. Take it out when blocked or when facing an especially difficult kind of scene.

## Expanding the References

The four pairings are a starting point. New combinations will emerge across one hundred pieces.

K-pop K contains diverse material, and another pairing may suit some material better. Record each discovery.

For example: “For this material—a changing relationship with fandom—Choi Eun-young's approach worked best.” Choi, author of *Shoko's Smile* and *Someone Harmless to Me*, excels at calmly capturing minute shifts in relational temperature. As such notes accumulate, our own reference-writer system takes shape. It begins with four pairings and ends the one-hundred-piece project broader and more precise.

## What Chapter 11 Conveys

The reference-writer system is not imitation practice for aspiring writers. It uses a storehouse of technique to maintain stable quality across a project of one hundred pieces.

K-pop K's material is distinctive. When proven techniques join that material, a manuscript becomes literature carrying a world. McCarthy's precision and Kim Hoon's pain meet in K-pop K's practice room; Ted Chiang's interior language and Oh Jung-hee's sensation carry a member's stage experience.
