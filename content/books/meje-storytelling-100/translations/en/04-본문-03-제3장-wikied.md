# Chapter 3. Stakes, Viewpoint, Form, and Length — Four Decisions before Writing

Before opening the manuscript folder, decide how large the story is, whose eyes see it, which form carries it, and how long it will be. Without these choices, a draft loses direction and may end as neither short story nor sketch.

The four decisions—stakes, viewpoint, form, and length—are intertwined. Higher stakes require more space; viewpoint limits form; form changes where body anchors and world signals appear.

## What Are Stakes?

In *Poetics*, Aristotle names fear and pity as conditions of tragedy. They arise only when a character can lose something. Stakes measure that possibility of loss, even in an everyday cross-section.

Kang Myeongsuk passing today's audition, Contractor B missing one clue, or an idol hiding an injured finger through practice are small but real stakes. A world-ending crisis feels irrelevant if it does not touch this person's life; a tiny decision creates tension when it truly matters to them.

We design along two axes. **External stakes** are visible outcomes—pass/fail, success/failure, discovery/concealment. **Internal stakes** involve pride, relationship, identity, or self-belief. The strongest pieces carry both.

Stake size correlates with length. Low stakes may fit a sketch; high internal and external stakes need a short story. Decide stakes first and the length range narrows.

**Questions:** What can the character lose? Are both external and internal stakes present? Are they connected deeply enough to make readers tense?

## Choosing Viewpoint

Viewpoint asks whose eyes see the story, but **psychic distance** matters more here than textbook categories such as first person or omniscient third.

John Gardner describes five distances. At the farthest, “It was winter” states the world objectively. In the middle, “He watched snow outside and thought about work” filters observation through a narrator. At the closest, “Damn it, this time again” exposes consciousness directly.

Our default is **close without being overwhelmed**: near sensation and emotion, yet with enough objectivity for readers to identify without collapsing into the character. It usually appears as intimate third-person limited or calm first person.

This balance is important for idol IP. Too distant feels cold; too close becomes indistinguishable from fan fiction. These texts belong to the official world, and psychic distance helps maintain that difference.

Form can restrict viewpoint. A dialogue routine transmits most information through speech and minimal stage directions, automatically increasing distance; voice and rhythm replace interior narration. A four-panel script almost eliminates conventional viewpoint, replacing it with scene selection.

**Questions:** Whose eyes and what distance? Does that choice fit character and stakes? Does it remain consistent?

## Choosing a Form — Four Options

Form is the vessel. The same material becomes a different manuscript as a short story, sketch, dialogue, or comic because form controls content, beat, and tempo. Syd Field's *Screenplay* treats a beat as the smallest action change within a scene; Scott McCloud shows how panel count and arrangement shape a reader's experience of time.

**Short story — default**

Length: 6,000–10,000 Korean characters. A complete opening, development, and landing freely combines narration, description, dialogue, and interior monologue. Use it for medium-to-high stakes, important interiority, and material needing room for world detail—for example, Myeongsuk's week preparing her first solo stage in year seven.

Structure: title / body (opening → development → landing) / closing mark.

**Comic sketch — short comedy narrative**

Length: 1,500–3,500 characters. One small complete episode built around irony or reversal, usually dialogue-heavy and requiring a sharp landing. It suits low stakes with strong paradox, everyday comedy, and world irony—such as the youngest K-pop K member wondering how to address an older fan at a signing.

Because space is short, Criterion F must compress scale into one sentence or word.

**Dialogue routine — stage direction + dialogue**

Length: 2,500–6,000 characters. Two or more people lead through speech with minimal directions; buddy dynamics activate naturally. Use it for relationships, contrasting personalities, or conveying rules through natural conversation—an old and new member speaking, or Contractor B negotiating with a client.

Criterion A enters through speech habits and terminology within the first three exchanges.

Structure: title / [A] dialogue + optional direction / [B] dialogue + optional direction / closing.

**Four-panel comic scenario — panel script**

Length: 800–2,000 characters. Specify scene, characters, dialogue, and effects for four panels. It is shortest, but each panel needs a clear role: panel 1 setup, panel 2 development, panel 3 crisis or pre-reversal, panel 4 ending. It suits a simple strong reversal, visual contrast, expression, or pose.

Criterion B is satisfied through precise physical action or expression in panel descriptions.

Structure: title / scene and dialogue for panels 1–4 / closing.

## Principles for Selecting Form

First, assess **material density**. Rich material and inner life call for short story; one ironic episode for sketch; relationship and speech for dialogue; simple visual reversal for four panels.

Second, assess the **Fun Engine**. Buddy dynamic favors dialogue; miracle of specificity favors short story; reversal favors sketch or four panels; time bomb favors the temporal room of a short story.

Third, balance the **Coverage Matrix**. If one character has only short stories, add a sketch to diversify the IP asset.

Fourth, consider **Vault data type**. Episodic data favors short story, interviews and dialogue records favor dialogue, photographs and appearance records favor four panels.

When principles conflict, priority is **material density > Fun Engine > Coverage balance > Vault data**. Material's demand comes first, diversity later.

One character may own several forms: one Myeongsuk short story, two sketches, and one dialogue piece. Multiple angles enrich both character and fan experience.

## Principles of Length

Length is a design input, not an accidental result. Managing one hundred manuscripts requires setting it in advance.

- Short story: 6,000–10,000 characters; medium-to-high stakes.
- Comic sketch: 1,500–3,500; low-to-medium stakes.
- Dialogue routine: 2,500–6,000; low through high stakes.
- Four-panel scenario: 800–2,000; low-to-medium stakes.

Within a range, write shorter if the story permits. Padding dilutes. Excess also breaks form: a 5,000-character sketch should become a short story, because extending the sketch destroys the intensity made possible by brevity.

## Decision Order and Recording the Design Sheet

Record all four on the design sheet before writing. The recommended order is:

1. **Confirm stakes** from the Vault—both external and internal loss.
2. **Choose form** from stake size and material density; this narrows length automatically.
3. **Choose viewpoint** and psychic distance inside the form—close for interiority, farther for dialogue.
4. **Set a numerical target**, such as “about 3,000 characters,” within the form's range.

The blank document now has a marked start and destination.

Choices may change during writing, but only for a reason. “I am stuck, so I will change form” is escape. “This material needs deeper interiority than expected, so I will convert it to a short story” is a design revision. When manuscript 50 raises the question of why manuscript 1 chose its form, the design sheet preserves the answer. Memory alone loses consistency after fifty pieces.

## What Chapter 3 Leaves

Stakes, viewpoint, form, and length are writing infrastructure. Unstable infrastructure produces an unstable story.

Choose a K-pop K member, open their Vault material, imagine one everyday scene, identify what they can lose, choose a form proportionate to it, set psychic distance, and write a numerical length target. Once the four decisions are on the design sheet, the blank document is ready.
