# Chapter 2. What Makes One Piece Alive — A Map of Six Quality Criteria

After finishing a manuscript, we pause. It has length, a protagonist, an event, and an ending, yet feels flat. If readers finish and ask, “Where did this person belong again?”, it has already failed.

Repeated failure is fatal in a hundred-piece project. The count rises but the world does not. If all one hundred members of K-pop K—our fictional hundred-member idol IP—receive flavorless stories, one hundred remains a number rather than an IP asset.

We therefore designed six minimum indicators that make each manuscript a living text: the **six quality criteria**.

## Why Criteria Are Necessary

“Write well” means almost nothing. People with instinct already know; those without it do not change because of the phrase. We need questions that can be checked against a finished manuscript: Does this paragraph signal the world? Is the protagonist's body captured concretely? Where does the reader land at the end?

Novelist and creative-writing professor John Gardner described good fiction as an “uninterrupted dream” of detail. Readers wake when concreteness disappears—when world, body, or ending floats. These six points preserve that dream.

## Criterion A: World Signal

Within the opening two paragraphs, readers must sense which world contains the story.

This is a signal, not an explanation. Do not summarize K-pop K's rules in two lines. Let a prop, institution, custom, speech habit, or occupational term appear naturally so readers think, “This is that kind of world.” If member Kang Myeongsuk greets a manager with fandom jargon used only inside K-pop K, that one phrase is a signal.

Timing varies by form: the first two paragraphs of a short story, first three sentences of a comic sketch, first three exchanges of a dialogue routine, or panel 1 of a four-panel comic. The shorter the form, the faster and denser the signal.

Screenwriting theorist Robert McKee calls setting the story's first character. Criterion A requires setting to enter as signal. Without it, the story's location remains unclear to the end.

**Checks:**

- Does the opening contain at least one expression, prop, institution, or custom possible only in this world?
- Is it embedded naturally in action, description, or dialogue rather than explained?
- Can an outsider feel that this world follows rules different from ordinary reality?

## Criterion B: Body Anchor (MUST PASS)

Criterion B is one of two MUST PASS criteria. A manuscript cannot pass without B and E, regardless of its other strengths. Without B, a character does not exist for readers; without E, the story never arrives. A, C, D, and F enrich density, pleasure, attribution, and scale, but B and E establish story itself.

Every thought and emotion reaches readers through a body. Yet many manuscripts stop at clichés such as shining eyes or a smile. Those are not body anchors.

T. S. Eliot's “objective correlative” conveys emotion through a concrete chain of objects, situations, and events rather than stating it. A body anchor uses the body as that correlative. Stephen King's “telling detail” similarly suggests that one precise movement, sensation, or reaction—not a full appearance description—makes a person memorable.

The idol world makes this especially important: knees touching a practice-room floor, toes moving minutely while counting through earphones, sweat gathering around a microphone grip, speaker resonance rising into the soles.

Three major types are:

1. **Contact with a tool or prop**: what is touched, how it is held, how much force is used—string friction, grip angle, fingers pulling a costume edge.
2. **Sensation from jewelry or clothing**: how an item touching the body reveals a position within the world.
3. **Pulse, vibration, resonance**: heartbeat, speakers, or reflected beat connecting inner emotion to physical stimulus.

In *On Beauty and Being Just*, Elaine Scarry explains that language cannot transmit sensation directly; precise description activates readers' own bodily memory. Remembering one's own sweaty palm lets a character's tension arrive.

**Checks:**

- Is the protagonist's body described concretely at least once without cliché?
- Does it connect to inner state or physical world?
- Can readers call up a sensory memory from their own bodies?

Without B, the story floats. Readers do not believe a bodyless character.

## Criterion C: Fun Engine

Fun is an outcome; a **Fun Engine** is the mechanism installed to produce it. Five types are available.

1. **Buddy dynamic**: energy from friendship, rivalry, hierarchy, intimacy, or hidden tension between two people. Kang Myeongsuk and the youngest member at a convenience store become interesting through their gap and closeness.
2. **Miracle of specificity**: precise refrigerator drink order or a dorm corridor's exact color temperature creates pleasure by making the world real.
3. **Aesthetics of paradox**: the greatest star worries about something trivial; emotionless Contractor B collapses before a cat; mundane life follows solemn drama. Contradiction gives dimension and rhythm.
4. **Time bomb**: readers know something will occur—a result next week, a message needing an answer today, a difficult conversation after practice.
5. **Betrayal or reversal**: not a trick, but a logical outcome different from expectation. In a scene where readers expect praise, Myeongsuk offers an apology instead, revealing depth.

Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi's *Flow* locates immersion where challenge and ability balance: too easy is boring, too hard produces anxiety. Stories likewise lose readers when perfectly predictable or incoherently surprising. Fun Engines tune that gap.

**Checks:**

- Does at least one of the five operate?
- Does it arise from character or world rules rather than an imposed plot device?
- Is there a point where readers predict, expect, or feel tension about the next scene?

## Criterion D: Narrator's Name

The protagonist or narrator's name must appear at least once. A name is the handle by which readers hold and remember a person.

In a hundred-piece project, it also determines asset ownership. A Kang Myeongsuk story, member #47 story, or Contractor B story must name its character so the Coverage Matrix can assign the correct cell. An unnamed text is ambiguous.

For dialogue routines, speaker labels satisfy D. For four-panel scripts, the scenario header may name the narrator even if the panels do not.

**Checks:**

- Does the name appear at least once?
- Does it appear naturally?
- Can the manuscript be assigned to that person's Coverage Matrix cell?

## Criterion E: Ending Landing (MUST PASS)

Where does the reader land when the story ends? If it stops in midair, readers carry nothing away. The state reached, what changed or remained, and the sensation left behind must create a landing.

Landing does not mean solving everything. A cross-section often needs no grand resolution. Myeongsuk remains alone in the practice room and sings one last note; its resonance is the landing. Contractor B looks back after a job and finds nothing there; that absence is the landing.

In *Understanding Comics*, Scott McCloud describes the gutter between panels as space completed by readers' imagination. Endings work similarly. Explain everything and no space remains; provide nothing and readers take nothing. Landing tunes the amount of space.

Three common failures are simply stopping, adding an explicit summary or moral, and introducing unrelated new information at the end.

**Checks:**

- Is there a sensation of arriving somewhere?
- Does the final scene or sentence resonate backward through the whole story?
- Does the landing follow logically from the development?

## Criterion F: Turning Numbers into Scenes

The Vault stores numerical world data. Copying a number produces information; turning it into a scene produces sensation. Though rooted in MEJE's process, F is the universal skill of converting abstract data into concrete experience.

Concert scale, practice hours, member count, fandom size, and album sales define the world. But “30,000 fans gathered” feels different from “even in the back row of the third tier, K-pop K's logo kept moving.” Numbers become the language of body, space, and time.

If debut occurred seven years ago, “seven years passed” fails F. Instead: “At the bottom of the shoe cabinet, the debut sneakers still wait. No one wears them, but no one discards them. About seven pairs.” Seven years becomes seven pairs.

Even a manuscript without explicit numbers can pass by sensorially showing the world's scale or density.

**Checks:**

- Has Vault numerical data become a scene?
- If there is no number, does one detail embody scale or density?
- Can readers feel the world's size through it?

## Map of the Six Criteria

A asks for a world-specific signal in the opening. B, MUST PASS, asks for a concrete body. C asks for an operating pleasure mechanism. D requires the protagonist's name. E, MUST PASS, asks where readers land. F asks whether numerical data becomes a sensory scene.

Both B and E must pass. Of A, C, D, and F, at least three must pass. The ideal manuscript passes all six; the practical acceptance line is **B + E + any three of the remaining four**.

However short the form—comic sketch or four-panel script—it still needs body and landing.
