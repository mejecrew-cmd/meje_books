# Chapter 5. One Hundred Protagonists — Coverage Matrix and Character Balance

K-pop K has one hundred members, a number that first feels exciting and later overwhelming. Must everyone have a manuscript? Who comes first? How do we prevent a few favorites from taking all the work? This chapter answers those questions.

## What the Number One Hundred Means

One hundred members is a declaration of world density, not merely a large count.

In *Convergence Culture* (2006), Henry Jenkins describes transmedia storytelling as spreading a world across platforms, with each piece holding an independent part. If every member owns at least one manuscript, one hundred perspectives illuminate K-pop K. Fans enter through a favorite and encounter hints of others.

Writing one hundred people creates one hundred doors through which any fan may enter.

The MCU pursued this in Phase Four: *Eternals* distributed backstories across its ten heroes, while nine *WandaVision* episodes showed everyday cross-sections of one character in different tones. *Haikyu!!* develops an entire team and scatters more than fifty daily moments; the Korean webtoon *Lookism* operates multiple viewpoints. They simply stop short of one hundred.

In practice, writers favor rich, easy, or personally interesting characters while postponing underdeveloped ones. The Coverage Matrix exposes this bias.

## Coverage Matrix

The Coverage Matrix is a one-hundred-character-by-manuscript table. Member names form the vertical axis; completed piece numbers form the horizontal. Fill a cell when that member appears.

At a glance it shows members with several cells or none, those with only sketches but no short story, and those always paired with the same people. Once bias is visible, it can be adjusted.

Track four distributions:

- **Protagonist distribution**: who leads each manuscript and whether counts concentrate.
- **Appearance distribution**: important supporting appearances, revealing perpetual supporting roles or complete absence.
- **Form distribution**: variety of short stories, sketches, dialogues, and four-panel scripts per person.
- **Material distribution**: whether one subject, such as pre-performance anxiety, repeats for a character.

Chapter 13 details operations; here the concept and principles are enough.

## Depth of Character Development

Not all one hundred can or need to have equal depth. We use three tiers.

**Tier 1: Core characters.** Central figures with complex arcs across multiple works, the thickest Vault records, and the widest forms and subjects. Kang Myeongsuk may have short stories, sketches, and dialogues.

**Tier 2: Middle characters.** Important in specific contexts but less central, with several manuscripts and presence through relationships to core figures. Their Vault is thinner but should have no critical gaps.

**Tier 3: Background characters.** Existing mainly in the background or leading one piece. They add density without requiring deep individual arcs.

Initial assignment uses three factors: **narrative density** in the Vault, **relationship complexity**, and **writing readiness**. Tiers need not remain fixed; discoveries can raise a character.

A healthy structure might contain twenty Tier 1, forty Tier 2, and forty Tier 3 characters. Each only needs manuscript and Vault depth appropriate to its tier.

## Who Should Be Written First?

Balance **what you want to write** with **what must be written**. Desire alone concentrates manuscripts; obligation alone drains energy.

The recommended ratio is **2:1**: two pieces for desired characters, one for a person underrepresented in the Matrix. It is an empirical guide, not a law. Early work may use 3:1; after fifty pieces, tighten to 1:1. This preserves both motivation and portfolio balance.

Respect desire. Attraction often signals discovered narrative potential, an audible voice, or several ready ideas—all of which affect quality.

But also respect need. A Tier 2 or higher character with no manuscript creates a hole that tells fans the member lacks presence.

## Supporting Roles and Cross-Appearances

A piece led by one member may feature another as support. This cross-appearance enriches the world: Myeongsuk's story briefly shows B, and B's story briefly shows Myeongsuk, letting fans experience their relationship from both sides.

Three cautions apply.

**Check the supporting character's Vault.** Speech, signature behavior, and current state must align with other work.

**Do not let support hijack the story.** A magnetic supporting character cannot become the real protagonist; their role supports the lead's arc.

**Keep both manuscripts consistent.** If A's story defines A and B as close but awkward, B's story must preserve that relationship.

## Selecting Material for a Character

Repeatedly writing Myeongsuk's pre-performance anxiety shows only one face. Vary the six library headings.

**Background variety** moves her beyond the practice room to a car, signing venue, dorm kitchen, or hospital waiting room.

**Material variety** adds boredom, unexpected kindness, small mistakes, and solitude. Ordinary states can reveal more than crisis.

**Relationship variety** places her with family, fans, strangers, or old acquaintances rather than only teammates.

Record material types in the Matrix: “Myeongsuk: pre-performance ×2, travel ×1, alone ×0, fan encounter ×0.” Empty cells suggest the next subject.

## Does Every One of the Hundred Need a Manuscript?

The purpose changes the answer. One hundred pieces need not mean one protagonist piece per member. They prove that the world is rich enough to sustain one hundred everyday cross-sections.

A member who never leads but appears vividly in several supporting roles is alive in the world. The goal is one hundred strong pieces, not mechanically assigning one to each person.

The Matrix still tracks presence. A Tier 1 member who never leads is an asymmetry and must be corrected.

## What Chapter 5 Leaves

The hundred protagonists have tiers, relationships, and positions. The Coverage Matrix maps how fully those positions are occupied.

Use it to plan the next lead, form, material, and supporting cast. Writing begins with instinct but is completed on a map.

The next chapter covers four paths to material: finding it in the Vault, using library headings, discovering it in daily life, and inheriting it from another manuscript.
