# Chapter 12. Standing Before the Passing Line — QA Reports and Self-Revision

After finishing a manuscript, two reactions intersect: “I think it is good” and “Something feels wrong.” It is hard to know which is right because a writer remains too close to a newly completed draft to see it objectively.

A QA report creates that distance. It evaluates a manuscript by criteria rather than intuition, checks its strengths and deficiencies item by item, and guides reinforcement based on the result.

This lecture explains how to write an actual QA report and how to conduct self-revision.

## Why QA Is Necessary

Writers have the greatest difficulty viewing their own manuscripts objectively. This is not a matter of skill. Because they already know what they intended, they read things that are absent as though they were present. If an intended physical anchor exists in the writer's mind, it can feel sufficiently written even when the page barely contains it.

A QA report closes this gap by distinguishing “what I intended” from “what is actually in the manuscript.” Pixar's Braintrust—where other directors and writers offer candid opinions on a work—and *The New Yorker*'s fact-checking department borrow outside eyes. A QA report lets a writer lend outside eyes to their own manuscript once.

QA has another importance in a one-hundred-piece project: the fiftieth manuscript must be judged by the same standard as the first. Standards blur with time. A rule applied strictly at the beginning relaxes into “this is probably good enough.” QA reports preserve consistency.

## Structure of a QA Report

A QA report evaluates each of the six quality criteria. Its format is simple.

```
## QA Report — [Manuscript Title]
Review date: YYYY-MM-DD

### A. World Signal
Location: [opening/none]
Signal:
Assessment: [pass/rework/caution]
Notes:

### B. Physical Anchor [MUST PASS]
Anchor location: [opening/turn/landing]
Anchor:
Cliché present: [no/yes]
Assessment: [pass/fail]
Notes:

### C. Fun Engine
Type:
Operation: [working/weak/absent]
Assessment: [pass/rework/caution]
Notes:

### D. Narrator's Name
Name stated: [yes/no]
Location:
Assessment: [pass/fail]

### E. Ending Landing [MUST PASS]
Landing type: [stillness/action/dialogue]
Landing sensation:
Summary ending present: [no/yes]
Assessment: [pass/fail]
Notes:

### F. Turning Numbers into Scenes
Relevant numerical data:
Converted into scene: [complete/absent]
Sense of world density: [present/weak]
Assessment: [pass/rework/caution]
Notes:

### Overall Decision
MUST PASS results: B [ ] / E [ ]
General criteria passed: [_ of A/C/D/F]
Decision: [pass/rework]
Items to rework:
```

Complete this report within thirty minutes of finishing the manuscript. If too much time passes, renewed familiarity reduces objectivity.

## How to Check Each Criterion

**Criterion A: World signal**

Copy the first two paragraphs—or the opening appropriate to the form—and examine them separately. Check for an expression, prop, institution, or custom possible only in K-pop K or the relevant world.

A common failure is an opening that could occur anywhere: “She woke in the morning. There was work again today.” It contains no world signal.

Revision: place one concrete element of K-pop K in the opening paragraph. It must appear within action or description, not explanation.

**Criterion B: Physical anchor**

List every bodily description in the manuscript. Separate true physical anchors from clichés. “Her eyes shone,” “he smiled,” and “she clenched her fist” are clichés.

If nothing remains after excluding clichés, Criterion B fails.

Revision: choose a physical-anchor type suited to the character from Chapter 7 and insert it at the manuscript's turning point.

**Criterion C: Fun Engine**

While reading, ask whether any moment makes you wonder about the next scene, anticipate something from the energy between two characters, or produce an odd smile.

If no such moment exists, the Fun Engine is absent or weak. Verify that the engine planned in the design document actually operates in the manuscript.

Revision: find its most natural position. Add a short exchange revealing buddy dynamics midway through the development, or insert one paradoxical action.

**Criterion D: Narrator's name**

Search the manuscript for the protagonist's name. One occurrence is enough to pass.

If absent, place the name somewhere natural: another character can use it in dialogue, or the narrator can think of their own name.

**Criterion E: Ending landing**

Read the final three paragraphs separately. Decide whether the ending lands, breaks off, or summarizes.

The simplest test is to append “And that was the end” to the last sentence. If it does not feel awkward, the ending has landed; the phrase should fit a story already complete.

Revision: for a summary ending, delete the last two or three sentences and end on the preceding scene. For an abrupt break, add a moment of character stillness or action to the final scene.

**Criterion F: Turning numbers into scenes**

Check the Vault for numerical data relevant to the manuscript, then check whether the numbers have become a sensory scene.

If there is no numerical data, look for detail that makes the scale of the world perceptible.

Revision: if relevant numbers exist but are absent from the draft, add one detail that turns them into a scene during the development. Use scale-down or time-conversion techniques.

## Principles of Rework

When QA calls for rework, first determine its scope.

**Local revision.** Only one criterion fails while the rest is sound. Add or correct the detail needed to satisfy that criterion.

**Section rework.** One section—opening, development, or landing—fails several criteria at once. Rewrite that section after consulting the design document again.

**Full rework.** Both MUST PASS criteria fail, or several criteria fail seriously at once. Reconsider the design document itself; the material or form may be unsuitable.

Do not fear rework. Among one hundred pieces, a manuscript that passes after revision is just as complete as one that passes immediately. Growth means needing fewer rounds of rework.

## A Practical Routine for Self-Revision

Use this routine when QA identifies work to do.

**Step 1: Cool down.** After completing the manuscript and QA report, do not look at the manuscript for at least several hours—preferably a day. Cooling creates distance.

**Step 2: Read aloud.** The ear catches what the eye misses: awkward sentences, broken rhythm, and passages that are too long or short.

**Step 3: Revise by criterion.** Work through the criteria marked for rework in the report. Begin with the MUST PASS criteria, B and E.

**Step 4: Recheck.** Write the QA report again. Confirm that reworked items now pass and that revision introduced no new problems.

## Reinforcement Prompts

When collaborating with AI, request reinforcement based on the QA result.

**Criterion A prompt (weak world signal):** “The opening of this manuscript lacks a sufficient K-pop K world signal. Within the first two paragraphs, naturally add one prop, custom, or form of address possible only in K-pop K. The form is [short story/sketch/comic dialogue/four-panel comic].”

**Criterion B prompt (no physical anchor):** “The protagonist's bodily description in this manuscript remains clichéd. Add a physical anchor unique to the character at [turning-point location]. This character's physical-expression traits are [physical record from the Vault].”

**Criterion C prompt (weak Fun Engine):** “The Fun Engine is not operating in this manuscript. At [mid-development location], insert one of [buddy dynamics/the aesthetics of paradox/betrayal and reversal]. It must arise naturally from this character and material rather than feel forced.”

**Criterion E prompt (incomplete landing):** “This manuscript ends with [a summary/an abrupt break]. Replace the final two sentences with [a stillness landing/an action landing]. Ideally, connect the final sentence to the physical anchor.”

**Criterion F prompt (numbers not converted into scenes):** “The numerical scale of the K-pop K world is not made sensory in this manuscript. At [mid-development location], insert one of the following: (1) scale-down—convert [relevant number] to a size the character directly experiences; (2) time conversion—express [duration/career] as a trace inscribed on the body; (3) numerical density—express [people/scale] as concrete density within one scene. It must enter scene description, not explanation.”

## QA and Growth

Accumulated QA reports reveal personal patterns: which criteria fail often, and which criteria weaken with which material.

“When I write a comic sketch, I always have to rework Criterion E.” Once this pattern is visible, plan the sketch's landing more carefully.

“Criterion A is often weak in my comic dialogues.” Then make the world-signal plan more specific in that form's design document.

This is how QA reports become measures of growth rather than mere checklists. A complete set of one hundred reports is also a record showing how one's writing technique developed.

## What Chapter 12 Conveys

The passing line is fixed. The six quality criteria do not change with each manuscript; what changes is the ability to meet them.

A QA report shows the present state of that ability: what passes, what does not yet pass, how it will be revised, and how it changes afterward.

Repeat this work across one hundred pieces, and by the final manuscript a writer develops an instinct for crossing the passing line even without a QA report. That is the state this lecture series seeks to create.
