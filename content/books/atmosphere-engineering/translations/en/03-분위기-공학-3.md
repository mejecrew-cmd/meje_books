# Atmosphere Engineering 3

### An Adjective Translation Dictionary and Presets

## How to Use the Dictionary

Part 1 organized the materials into eight layers, and Part 2 organized the assembly procedure as mixing and assignment. What remains is the point of departure. When a brief asks for an adjective such as ominous, cozy, or wistful—or when a creator chooses one as a target—a correspondence table is needed to translate it into a mix ratio and layer values. Part 3 is the first edition of that dictionary. It takes eight adjectives used most often in creation and critique and records each in four lines: definition, default mix, assignment, and common error, followed by commentary.

The numbers in an entry are starting values, not absolutes. Under the baseline principle from Part 1, atmosphere is a deviation from a work's defaults, so the same ominousness is implemented with different values in horror and romance. The dictionary's mixtures assume a neutral baseline as their initial setting. Entries are also connected by cross-references. An error in ominousness leads to fear; an error in the sublime also leads to fear; tension and excitement share an arousal bed. In this way, the dictionary records adjacency within the emotional coordinate system. The entries may be read in any order, but it is useful to read “Tension and Excitement” first because its two axes—arousal and valence—are common coordinates for the others.

## Adjective Translation Dictionary

**Ominous**  
Definition: an alarm operating before the grounds for danger have been presented  
Mix: everyday normality 0.80 / anticipation of threat 0.20  
Assignment: keep everyday normality unchanged in the bed. Assign anticipated threat as absence. Remove one layer from ambience—birdsong or wind—expand negative space, or break a repeated pattern once  
Common error: painting the entire bed as threatening with darkness and low strings

Vacuum silence, discussed in the sound section of Part 1, is the central material for this entry. Ominousness is made not by adding something but by removing what ought to be present. The moment the bed is painted with darkness and music, anticipation becomes present actuality and the emotion changes from ominousness to fear. The final entry in this dictionary returns to that boundary. A broken repeated pattern is powerful for precisely the reason described under the time layer in Part 1. If an evening bell has sounded three times and is absent on the fourth evening, the audience processes that fact as a larger alarm than ten shots of darkness.

The standard prose technique for describing absence is naming and removal. The sentence “there was no birdsong” names the sound that should exist and removes it at the same time. Readers are first given the expected inventory and then told that its place is empty. This is stronger than writing “it was dark” because the reader must perform the comparison and recognize the deficit.

**Uncanny**  
Definition: a state in which a minute deviation is detected within something familiar  
Mix: familiarity 0.85 / error 0.15  
Assignment: keep the bed at everyday values. Assign the error to a precise object: excessive symmetry, a smile with motionless eyes, a minute mismatch in repeated movement, or distorted proportion  
Common error: confirming the monster on screen

Freud's account of the *unheimlich*, the frighteningly unfamiliar within the familiar, is the classical basis for this response. Robotics' “uncanny valley” is a modern hypothesis organizing the same structure on the side of machines. Masahiro Mori's proposed curve predicts that affinity rises with human resemblance and then drops sharply in the region of very close resemblance. Later empirical studies disagree about the exact shape of that curve. Even so, several studies support the general direction relevant here: uncanniness is a function not of pure otherness but of resemblance. The majority term in its mixture must therefore be familiarity.

The assignment materials have already appeared in the previous two parts. Kubrick's excessive symmetry is judged abnormal; a smile in which only the lip corners contract while the eye area remains still becomes an object of Duchenne mismatch. The uncanny is produced not from a new material but from a precise misconfiguration of existing values. In webtoons and games, this entry is also a quality-control category. Unintended uncanniness—unequal eyes in a drawing style, or missing blinks in a 3D model—creates an error value of 0.15 that the creator never specified. The audience reports discomfort without being able to name the cause. Read the assignment sheet in reverse and it becomes a checklist for such accidents.

**Lonely**  
Definition: the feeling of confirming a space from which people and human sound have disappeared  
Mix: composure 0.70 / loss 0.30  
Assignment: maximize distance in the viewpoint layer and reduce the character to a dot in the frame. Lower saturation. Remove only human sounds while retaining natural ones. Use one object in the material layer made for two people  
Common error: mobilizing every layer at once—a rainy window, dark lighting, and slow music

Loneliness is a low-intensity emotion, so piling up materials causes it to be judged melodramatic. One dominant and one object are enough. Natural sound remains because the structure of the feeling is a contrast: the world operates normally, but people alone are gone. Its silence therefore belongs to the full rather than vacuum kind. An object made for two is especially efficient in prose when implemented as action. One sentence—“she took out two teacups, then put one back”—assigns loss 0.30 through two materials at once, prop and movement.

**Cozy**  
Definition: confirmation that an external threat has been blocked  
Mix: safety 0.85 / presence of the outside 0.15  
Assignment: color temperature below 3200K; a narrow space; increased material density through textile and wood textures; full silence composed of firewood or rain; one object shot of snow or rain beyond the window  
Common error: removing the outside from the frame

The second term of the mixture is often omitted from this entry. A barrier can be confirmed only when the blocked exterior is visible. The cold beyond the window is not decoration but a necessary condition. An interior from which the exterior has been erased is not cozy; it is merely indoors. The 0.15 is therefore not disposable margin but part of the condition under which the emotion exists.

Game design uses this structure at save points. The bonfire in *Dark Souls* is a representative example. It feels cozy not simply because of lighting or music, but because the world outside the bonfire is consistently dangerous. The mixing formula in which the scale of coziness is proportional to the scale of external threat is implemented directly in level design.

**Tension and Excitement**  
Definition: both are high-arousal states. What separates them is not arousal but the target's valence and the direction of information  
Mix: the shared bed is arousal 0.80; tension adds loss of control 0.20, while excitement adds anticipation 0.20  
Assignment: the arousal bed is identical—short shots and sentences, a narrow frame, accelerated rhythm. Objects separate the two. Tension blocks information: what lies beyond an unseen door, an unidentified caller. Excitement previews information: a sign of an approaching promise, the confirmed name of the other person  
Common error: removing arousal materials from an exciting scene and using only soft values

This entry is grounded in a classic psychology experiment. In 1974, Dutton and Aron administered the same survey on a high, swaying bridge and on a low, stable one. Men who met a female interviewer on the swaying bridge contacted her afterward at a much higher rate. The standard explanation is that they interpreted the bridge-induced arousal as attraction to the interviewer. The experiment therefore suggests the formula in this entry: arousal is a bed prior to interpretation, while the assignment of information determines whether it becomes tension or excitement.

This shared bed also helps explain why romantic thrillers can exist as a genre. Because romance and thriller share arousal, a scene can move between them by replacing objects. Conversely, remove arousal materials from an exciting scene and use only soft lighting and a slow rhythm, and the output is not excitement but calm. Half the critique that “the romance feels flat” identifies not a failure of emotion but a missing arousal bed. In prose, the time layer carries that bed. Sentences shrinking to action-scene length in the paragraph before a confession are not abnormal; they are the correct assignment.

**Sublime**  
Definition: the response when the scale of an object exceeds the observer's processing capacity  
Mix: awe 0.70 / fear 0.30  
Assignment: contrast of scale—an extreme long shot with the person reduced to a dot, low angle, backlit silhouette, sustained low-frequency sound, and long duration in the time layer. Control fear 0.30 through safe distance, with one boundary material such as a cliff edge or window between observer and object  
Common error: removing the material that guarantees safe distance

Edmund Burke argued that terror is a source of the sublime, but that the response arises only at a distance from immediate danger. In assignment-sheet terms, fear 0.30 is built through scale and darkness, while one boundary object must guarantee the observer's safety. Remove the boundary and the mixture crosses into fear. A storm viewed from behind a window is sublime; encountered in an open field, it is frightening.

The convention of filming a giant monster over a character's shoulder or through a vehicle window is a commercial use of this boundary material. Include the observer's position in the frame and the audience receives proxy safety, keeping fear 0.30 beneath its upper limit. Fill the frame with the monster alone and the image is read not as sublime but as a shot of threat. In prose, naming one thing that the viewpoint character grasps or leans against serves the same function. One line describing a hand gripping a railing is the minimum unit of boundary material.

**Longing**  
Definition: a state in which the present refers to an object from the past  
Mix: present composure 0.60 / past warmth 0.40  
Assignment: the difference between the two time periods is central. Raise saturation and use a color temperature around 3200K in recollection; lower saturation and raise color temperature in the present. The object is the return of a repeated motif: an item shown three times in the past returns once in the present, now altered  
Common error: treating the entire recollection in soft focus

The scale of longing is produced by the difference between past and present values, so neither period can establish it alone. The opening of *Up*, discussed in Part 1, raises saturation during the remembered period in order to create a gap from the low-saturation present. Treat a recollection only with conventional haze and the result is not longing; it is merely a sign announcing that the current passage is a flashback.

The repeated-motif object extends the repetition rule from Part 1's time layer across a long-form work. Introduce an object three times in the past, then bring it back once in the present—worn, empty, or under a new owner—and the difference between two periods is compressed into one thing. In serialized work, appearances may be separated by entire episodes, so it is safer to provide just enough context at the return to support the reader's memory.

**Anxiety and Fear**  
Definition: fear is arousal with a specified object; anxiety is arousal whose object remains unspecified  
Mix: both are high-arousal states at 0.70 or above. The difference lies not in the mix but in the state of the object slot  
Assignment: in fear, the threat object is present in the frame. In anxiety, its slot is empty. Expand negative space, place sound beyond the frame, or change a value without immediately presenting its cause  
Common error: confirming the cause too early in an anxious scene

The moment a cause is confirmed on screen, anxiety changes into fear, and the director's object of control changes from intensity to the threat itself. To sustain anxiety, the governing technique is therefore not the creation of a threat but the delay of confirmation. If there is a door, keep what lies beyond it outside the frame; if there is a sound, keep its source outside. This accounts for most of the entry's assignments.

The distinction from ominousness can be plotted on the same coordinates. Ominousness is low-arousal anticipation; anxiety is sustained high arousal; fear is high arousal with a specified object. Rather than three wholly different emotions, they are three positions produced by different arousal values and states of the object slot.

Their relation to synchronization, the fourth principle from Part 1, also needs clarification. That principle said ungrounded staging is judged manipulative. Anxious staging does not lack grounds; it defers their presentation. The deferred cause must later be retrieved. A value change that remains unexplained to the end is retrospectively judged manipulation at that point.

## Scales That Already Exist

Apart from the adjective dictionary, several layers already have numerical scales created by their industries. If the dictionary is a correspondence table for mixtures, these scales are unit systems for individual layer values. Put the two resources together and much of a specification can be written numerically.

The time layer has average shot length. A basic unit of the statistical style analysis pioneered by film scholar Barry Salt, it divides total running time by the number of shots. Cinemetrics, a public database, contains measurements for thousands of films. This is the source of reference values such as nine to ten seconds for classical Hollywood films and two to four seconds for modern action films. James Cutting of Cornell University analyzed films from 1935 to 2010 and numerically identified a historical tendency toward darker images, shorter shots, and more movement within the frame. In practice, find the measurement of a reference work in the database and use it as a target.

Transferred to prose, average sentence length becomes the corresponding metric. Divide a manuscript into sentences and measure its mean and distribution to identify the baseline of one's rhythm. For diagnosis, comparison by scene type is more useful. If action and everyday scenes have the same average sentence length, the time layer has probably been left unattended. A spreadsheet or an AI can perform this measurement in minutes.

The light layer has lighting ratio as a scale for contrast: the brightness ratio between key and fill. The conventional references are roughly 2:1 for the flatter value of a sitcom, 4:1 for standard drama, and 8:1 or higher for noir. This is the contrast scale promised in Part 1. Used with the color-temperature references of 3200K and 5600K, it allows most of the light layer to be specified numerically.

The color layer has Shigenobu Kobayashi's Color Image Scale. This practical tool places color combinations on a plane whose axes run from warm to cool and soft to hard, then associates adjectives with the coordinates. The work of this series—translating adjectives into parameters—already exists as an industry standard in the field of color.

Scales for the body layer appeared in the previous two parts. Facial expression can be written as a list of FACS units with intensities A through E. Interpersonal distance can be written using Edward Hall's four zones: intimate, personal, social, and public. Directions for expression and staging can therefore be communicated through codes and zone names rather than adjectives. Some layers, such as air and material, still have sparse numerical scales. The convention there is to let one photographic reference serve as the scale.

## Director Presets

When a creator's habits of mixing and assignment stabilize, they become a preset. To compare presets, define one metric: the ratio of props with a narrative function to all props visible in the frame. Call it functional density. It converts Chekhov's principle—that a gun hung onstage must be fired—into a proportional indicator and also measures the density of prior object exposure discussed in Part 2.

Wes Anderson's preset has high material density and extreme control over selection. Every prop filling the frame has been selected, but many establish tone and world texture rather than performing narrative functions. Its functional density is therefore not as high as appearances suggest. Maximum symmetry, frontal angles, and a narrow, highly saturated pastel palette combine to make the entire world feel controlled. Against that controlled baseline, the characters' emotional events stand out as deviations.

Robert Bresson occupies the opposite end. He reduces material density to a minimum while functional density approaches one hundred: everything left in the frame has meaning. He called actors “models” and removed expressive acting, fixing facial values in the body layer at neutral. His example shows that a preset is not only a list of values a creator uses, but also a list of those they refuse to use.

Andrei Tarkovsky raises the density of textures such as water, dust, and moss to a maximum while limiting symbolic objects to a few. He pushes duration in the time layer to an extreme and keeps fog and rain active in the air layer. Bong Joon Ho's preset is distinguished by the vertical axis of viewpoint and sudden transitions under the modulation principle. Stairs, semi-basements, and hills repeat compositions of ascent and descent; values can change from humor to catastrophe within one cut; and props with high functional density, such as the scholar's rock in *Parasite*, operate as objects.

Creating one's own preset combines three documents. First, write the tone and manner of one's work as a table of defaults for the eight layers—an application of the baseline principle in Part 1. Second, apply the reverse-engineering exercise from Part 2 to one's own manuscript and list recurring assignment habits. Third, as in Bresson's case, add a prohibited list of values one has chosen not to use. Once these documents exist, collaborators and AI can receive a style as numbers and lists. The vague request to “preserve my style” becomes a reviewable direction.

A preset need not exceed one page. Eight lines of defaults, roughly five lines of assignment habits, and about three lines of prohibitions are enough. That single page can be reused to preserve tone between seasons of serialized work, unify tone across coauthors, and review contracted art or AI generation. As a work grows longer, relying on one person's intuition to remember its style becomes more costly. A preset documents individuality and also serves as a maintenance tool for long-running work.

## Rain Ritual: Final Rendering

Apply prose's selective-rendering principle to the monochrome specification from Part 1 and the mixing specification from Part 2, and the series' running example is complete. The rendered layers are air, sound, and time. Doubt 0.07 is assigned to one gaze object in the final position of the paragraph.

The air was dry and acrid. Dust hung knee-high above the cracked paddy below the altar, while heat haze erased every form on the horizon. The king knelt on the dry earth. As the ritual text was read, its voice was the only sound; the windless, insectless midday continued unchanged even after the words had ended. The crowd remained prostrate and still. Only when one local official in the back row raised his head did his gaze reveal its destination—not the sky, but the sluice gate at the upstream weir.

This paragraph contains no adjective that instructs an emotion, yet the values in the specification perform the judgment instead. The written values belong to air, sound, and time. Values for light, color, and material remain outside the sentences, maintaining the consistency of detail. Of the two doubt objects in the assignment sheet, the prop has been omitted from narration; the gaze in the final sentence is the whole of the doubt rendered in prose. If a reader judges the paragraph solemnly resolute and then feels that something is suspect in the final line, assignment rather than an adjective has produced the result.

The same specification renders into other media. A webtoon can place one narrow panel containing only the official's gaze after wide overhead panels of the dry paddies. Film can place one insert of the official at the end of an overhead long take of the prostrate crowd. The units differ—sentence, panel, shot—but the assignment structure remains three beds plus one object. One specification document therefore works as a production brief for all three media.

## Combining the Three Parts

Combined into a workflow, the three parts operate as follows. When an adjective is supplied as a target, consult its default mix and assignment in the Part 3 dictionary. Adjust them to the work's defaults using the baseline principle from Part 1. Apply the three criteria from Part 2—narrative function, viewpoint, and position in the scene chain—to determine the mix ratio. Assign values to beds and objects, render them for the chosen medium, and finally reverse-engineer the resulting ratio and compare it with the design value.

Receiving an adjective through reviewing the result forms one procedural unit. As repetitions accumulate, a creator begins to write assignments without consulting the dictionary; that state is a completed preset. Suppose a brief asks for a lonely reunion. Consult both “Lonely” and “Longing,” add loss and temporal difference to a bed of composure, introduce a small amount of anticipation for the narrative function of reunion, and assign the object slot to the two-person object shared by both entries. Combining entries is scene design. The dictionary supplies coordinates; the procedure determines their values.

## Adjectives on Coordinates

Return to the distinction from Part 1 and the dictionary's purpose becomes clear. Adjectives are discrete words, while parameters are continuous values. The entries are examples of coordinates, not correct answers, and interpolation between them is possible. Combine half the values of “Ominous” with half of “Cozy” and the result is an unnamed state: an unexplained deviation sensed inside a safe room. Add the temporal-difference assignment from “Longing” to the arousal bed from “Tension” and another state can be made: sensing in advance a loss that has not yet arrived.

This dictionary does not end with eight entries. Each creator expands it. Add the adjectives most often requested in one's genre using the same four-line format, and the thickness of the dictionary becomes the range that creator can translate. When an adjective becomes a point of departure rather than a destination, a creator can make atmospheres beyond the vocabulary list. That is why this series uses the word *engineering*.
