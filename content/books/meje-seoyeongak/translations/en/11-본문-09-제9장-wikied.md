# Chapter 9. The World of a Work — Period Encyclopedia and Motif Materials

Last time we saw the thirteen skeletal materials made for any work. This time we examine the materials that put the color of *Pride and Prejudice* onto that skeleton: one Regency-period encyclopedia and fifteen motif materials, sixteen in all.

These sixteen are the chief reason the material folders of *Pride and Prejudice* and of other works—say, *Moby-Dick* or *City of God*—take different forms. They are produced by a work’s period and motifs.

## Period Encyclopedia — Regency Life

### 14. Regency-Period Encyclopedia

This one volume has twelve sections. It holds information not directly described in the work but necessary to understand the motives behind characters’ actions.

**The structure of a day.** Meals have five stages: breakfast (9–10 a.m.), no lunch, dinner (around 4 p.m.), tea (6–8 p.m.), and supper (9–10 p.m.). Dinner is the day’s main meal; supper is a light cold meal after a ball. The novel never directly says “dinner is at four.” But when Elizabeth intends to leave Netherfield at three in the afternoon, knowing that it is just before dinner is essential to translating the tension of the scene.

**Travel and communication.** Walking is three miles per hour; an ordinary carriage six to eight; a stagecoach eight to ten; an express stagecoach ten to twelve. Longbourn to Meryton is one mile, or twenty minutes on foot; Longbourn to Netherfield is three miles, or an hour. Post takes one or two days between towns; an express letter can leave at midnight and arrive at dawn. Only with this sense of time can one convey the weight of “Darcy delivered the letter himself the next morning.”

**The legal process of marriage.** There are two routes: the usual church banns and a special licence from the Archbishop of Canterbury’s office. The latter is very expensive and has no residence requirement, making it a symbol of wealth. This is why Mrs. Bennet, dazzled by Darcy’s wealth, demanding this form of marriage for her daughters has meaning.

**Fine distinctions of class.** Most major figures belong to the landed gentry, but fine hierarchies exist within it: the high aristocracy (Lady Catherine, and Darcy’s mother’s side); landholding lesser gentry (Darcy himself); newly rich people leasing estates (Bingley); country gentlemen with small estates (the Bennets); new gentry possessing only a title (Sir William Lucas); and the mercantile class (the Gardiners). It is rare for all these levels to appear together in one work; *Pride and Prejudice* is one such rare work.

**The reality of money.** What is Darcy’s £10,000 a year in contemporary currency? A simple conversion is meaningless. A farm worker then earned about £20 a year; a governess about £30. Darcy’s income equals the combined annual income of five hundred farm-worker households. Bingley’s £5,000 is likewise enormous. That weight underlies every marriage decision in the work.

**Changes of dress.** People change clothes at least two or three times a day: morning dress, walking dress (with bonnet and pelisse added), evening dress before dinner, and a ball gown (with shoe ornaments and gloves added) for a ball. Clothes themselves are the timetable of a day.

**The social-season calendar.** The London social season follows the parliamentary session, from late autumn to early spring. At country estates, Michaelmas (29 September), Christmas, and Easter mark time. Bingley comes to Netherfield at Michaelmas; Collins takes up his place at Hunsford at Easter. Such time markers compose the rhythm of the entire work.

Besides these, the volume includes twelve sections on religious life (Anglican liturgy), medicine (sea bathing and bloodletting), architectural styles, the physical form of letters, and more.

## Fifteen Kinds in the Motif Pack

### 15. Dictionary of Objects as Class Symbols

This material explains how carriages, card games, food, and instruments function as signals of class. Seven kinds of carriage are arranged by rank, from a chaise and four to a hackney carriage (taxi). Bingley arrives at Netherfield in a chaise and four. The townspeople’s excitement is not simple display of wealth but recognition of a class code. The nine card games work similarly. *Vingt-un*, loved by Bingley and Jane, is simple and intuitive, hinting at their temperamental similarity. Collins intruding despite not knowing whist and losing every hand exposes his incompetence.

### 16. Economics of Marriage (Map of Institutions and Economic Terms)

This material organizes the flow of money that makes the marriage plot work: dowry, the assets a bride brings to marriage; settlement, the widow’s provision guaranteed by the groom’s side; and pin money, the wife’s personal allowance after marriage. It also covers entail, the institution deciding the Bennets’ fate. Having no son, the Bennets are destined to lose their estate to Collins, so the marriages of their five daughters become a question of the family’s survival. Only when the system’s operation is clear can Mrs. Bennet’s fixation on marriage be translated not as satire but as tragedy.

### 17. Servant-System Chart

This organizes a household’s servant hierarchy: butler (supervising male servants), housekeeper (supervising female servants), housemaid, cook, coachman, valet (personal attendant to a man), and lady’s maid (personal attendant to a woman). The calling system runs on bell pulls: a pull in each room rings that room’s bell in the servants’ quarters. Only a few servants are named in *Pride and Prejudice*—Hill at Longbourn, Mrs. Reynolds at Pemberley, and Nicholls at Netherfield—but innumerable unnamed servants are present in the work, the invisible hands behind its actions.

### 18. Menus and Food Culture

This organizes the Regency table. Dinner generally has two or three courses: soup, a main dish (venison or chicken), vegetables, and dessert. Venison and hothouse fruit—grapes, peaches, pineapples—signal wealth, as both require an estate. This gives weight to the scene where the Bennets serve a leg of venison to Darcy and Bingley.

### 19. Costume Inventory

An inventory of Regency women’s clothing: distinctions between morning dress, walking dress, evening dress, and ball gown; bonnet, pelisse (winter outer coat), shawl, petticoat, and shoe-roses (ball-shoe ornaments). The color, cloth, and ornament of clothing are a character’s social position. This is why Caroline Bingley’s splendid dress contrasts with Elizabeth’s restrained dress.

### 20. Music and Sound Environment

Pianoforte, harp, and singing are three measures of feminine accomplishment. Piano is most common; harp signals greater splendor. Singing—Italian song being highest—is separate. In *Pride and Prejudice*, the scene in which Elizabeth is assessed as playing piano “well enough” is delicate: an honest assessment by a person neither excellent nor poor, who knows and acknowledges her limits. Ball music—reel, quadrille, minuet, country dance—is also separately organized.

### 21. Body Language and Etiquette

Ball etiquette, drawing-room etiquette, walking etiquette: prescribed gestures for when people meet. It records the procedure for a man asking a woman to dance, the conditions under which she may refuse, and the limit on how often the same pair may dance in an evening; the hierarchy expressed by seats in a drawing room; and which side men and women occupy when paired on a walk. Darcy first refuses to ask Elizabeth to dance and later asks her himself. The difference in weight between those scenes can be conveyed only with etiquette material.

### 22. Korean–English Correspondence Table for Emotional Vocabulary

This material records fine distinctions when rendering around thirty emotional terms central to *Pride and Prejudice* into Korean. *Esteem, regard, gratitude, admiration, attachment, affection,* and *love* carry different weights in English, yet can all too easily become “love” or “affection” in Korean. At that moment, an apple becomes an apple again. The table is an agreement about the Korean rendering of each word and the distinctions to preserve.

### 23. Foreign-Language: French Dictionary

French was the cultivated language of the Regency British upper class. Expressions such as *tête-à-tête* (a conversation between two), *ensemble*, *coup de grâce* (the decisive blow), and *savoir-faire* (expert ease) naturally enter the work. French itself signals class: Mrs. Gardiner’s speech contains almost none, while Caroline Bingley’s frequently does, marking their social positions. This material sets the policy for rendering French vocabulary in the work into Korean.

Had the work been *Frankenstein*, this place would have held a dictionary of mixed German and French; for *City of God*, a Latin and Greek dictionary; for *Moby-Dick*, a maritime-vocabulary dictionary mixing Latin, Hebrew, and Greek etymologies. The name “foreign-language dictionary” remains the same, but its language varies with the work.

### 24. Estimated Building Plans

This material reconstructs the plans of key estates such as Pemberley, Rosings, and Netherfield by combining period standards with fragments from the work: the locations of drawing room, guest rooms, dining room, study, and music room, and the route from front entrance to dining room. It makes it possible to visualize which room a scene occurs in and how a character moves through it.

### 25. Scene-Set Layouts

For about fifteen key scenes, this material organizes spatial arrangement, character movement, time of day, lighting, sound, and key visual images: the ball, first proposal, visit to Pemberley, second proposal, and more. Each scene is visualized on one page so a translator can picture it while translating.

### 26. Garden and Landscape Estimates

This organizes types of Regency gardens: naturalistic landscape gardens, artificial wildernesses, hermitages, and walks. In *Pride and Prejudice*, gardens mirror personality. Pemberley’s garden that preserves nature and Rosings’ artificial garden precisely correspond to the contrast between Darcy and Lady Catherine.

### 27. Estimate of Meryton’s Shopping Street

This material reconstructs Meryton, the fictitious country town that often appears in the work, from period standards and textual fragments: Clarke’s library, a social library frequented by officers; the milliner’s shop often visited by the Bennet sisters; the Phillipses’ office, inn, town hall, and market square. It also contains a brief defense of the legitimacy of such materials: we have inferred and creatively enlivened places only briefly described in the work.

### 28. Estimate of Religious Life

It organizes the procedure by which Collins receives his living, a week of Anglican liturgy, and the social function of Sunday worship. How Collins receives the Hunsford parish, where people sit during Sunday worship, and how a clergyman officiates a wedding: religious routines mentioned briefly or flowing only in the background.

### 29. Estimate of Character Daily Schedules

A supplement to the period encyclopedia. It organizes, by time of day, a weekday at the Bennets’, Darcy’s Pemberley routine, and Collins’s Hunsford routine. It lets one check whether the time when two characters meet in the work is possible in their schedules. When Darcy “happens” to encounter Elizabeth on her walks in *Pride and Prejudice*, the schedules reveal that the encounter is not accidental.

## What the Sixteen Create Together

We have now seen sixteen kinds. In compressed form:

> No. 14 puts the work’s **entire period** into one volume.
> Nos. 15–16 address its core motifs of **class and marriage**.
> Nos. 17–22 cover the characters’ **daily domains**: servants, food culture, costume, music, etiquette, emotional vocabulary.
> No. 23 covers its **foreign language**.
> Nos. 24–26 cover its **spaces**: buildings, scenes, gardens.
> Nos. 27–29 supplement its **places, religion, and daily routines**.

These sixteen put flesh on the skeleton of thirteen kinds from the previous installment, fixing *Pride and Prejudice* into a material folder unlike that of any other work. Had they instead been replaced with the motif materials of another work—for example, *Moby-Dick*’s maritime terms, ship diagrams, and whaling-office dictionary—the folder would have looked entirely different.

The next installment is the final bundle of materials: six additions unique to *Pride and Prejudice*, not found in the standard classification but decisive for this work.
