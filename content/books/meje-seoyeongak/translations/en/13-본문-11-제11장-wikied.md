# Chapter 11. The Protagonist’s Note — Elizabeth Bennet

> **Opening Part 4.** Through Part 3 we saw all thirty-five kinds of material. Part 4 enters character notes, the folder’s other axis. The *Pride and Prejudice* folder holds notes for thirty-four people; this part opens three figures at different ranks. This installment is protagonist Elizabeth Bennet; the next, supporting character Charlotte Lucas; the one after, minor character Mrs. Reynolds, together with the folder’s auxiliary files and division-of-labor technique. We begin with the work’s fullest character to see how one person’s note takes shape.

---

## Why Start with Elizabeth?

The work is titled *Pride and Prejudice*. Each word is embodied by one of its two protagonists: pride by Darcy, prejudice by Elizabeth. There is a reason to look at Elizabeth first.

Elizabeth traces the largest arc of change in the whole work. Few characters are drawn with such precision as they rebuild their very self-understanding from the root, from first appearance to last. That precision fills every field of a character note most richly. Elizabeth’s note therefore became the thickest volume in the *Pride and Prejudice* folder and the model showing how a character note establishes its household.

Let us open the note from its first page onward.

## The First Page — Basic Information

The opening of a note holds basic information about the character, about half a page. Elizabeth’s first page begins like this:

> **Original forms**: Elizabeth Bennet, Lizzy, Eliza, Miss Eliza Bennet
> **Korean rendering**: Elizabeth Bennet / Lizzy
> **Age**: 20 (established by “not one-and-twenty” in Chapter 29)
> **Family**: Second daughter of the Bennets (second of five sisters)
> **Residence**: Longbourn → Pemberley
> **First appearance**: Chunk 1, Chapter 1

They look like small facts, but all carry meaning. Four forms appear in the original-forms line because the same person is called differently depending on who calls her: Mrs. Bennet calls her “Lizzy”; her father, “Lizzy” or “Eliza”; Darcy first calls her “Miss Elizabeth Bennet,” then “Elizabeth” after they grow intimate; Caroline Bingley calls her “Miss Eliza.” The changing form of address is the changing relationship, and Korean must preserve it.

Her age of twenty is likewise fixed by one line: Lady Catherine asks Elizabeth’s age at Hunsford and she answers “not one-and-twenty,” meaning she has not reached twenty-one. That line permits calculation of her birth year and estimation of her sisters’ ages in relation to it.

Two residences are listed because they condense her narrative fate in advance. On marriage Elizabeth moves from Longbourn to Pemberley. A person’s note contains even the location at the work’s end because it is written as the result of having lived through the work’s entire time.

## Character Introduction — A Portrait in Two or Three Paragraphs

After basic information comes a two- or three-paragraph introduction. It moves beyond objective facts to say, “What kind of person is this?”

Elizabeth’s introduction begins:

> The second of the Bennets’ five daughters. An intelligent, witty woman favored by her father. With sharp powers of observation and a lively, playful disposition, she enjoys the ridiculous. Yet that very observation also leads her into the trap of prejudice. The figure who embodies *Prejudice* in the title *Pride and Prejudice*.

The final line is the heaviest: “The figure who embodies Prejudice.” It decides the weight of all her actions and speech. When she first sees Darcy and grows angry at his “tolerable” assessment, Korean tone is accurate only if one knows that her anger is not merely wounded pride but the first seed of prejudice.

The introduction is compact, placing personality, function, and role in the work within two or three paragraphs. It is not deep analysis; that enters the next section, change by event.

## Change by Event — The Heart of the Note

This is the longest and most important section of the character note. It follows how a person changes through the whole work, alongside event numbers from the event master.

Elizabeth’s change has five acts:

> **Act 1: Formation of Prejudice** (Chapters 1–17)
> **Act 2: Confrontation and Rupture** (Chapters 18–27)
> **Act 3: Turning Point** (Chapters 28–40)
> **Act 4: Reassessment and Love** (Chapters 41–54)
> **Act 5: Fulfillment** (Chapters 55–61)

For every act, key events are given in a line with their numbers. A portion of Act 1 reads:

> Event 3: At the Meryton ball, Darcy insults her with “tolerable; but not handsome enough to tempt me.” Yet she tells the story cheerfully to friends, making it material for wit.
> Event 5: At Lucas Lodge, she refuses Darcy’s request to dance, establishing their paradoxical pattern: the more she refuses, the more Darcy is drawn to her.
> Event 7: She walks three miles to nurse Jane at Netherfield, beginning intellectual exchange with Darcy.
> Event 14: She fully believes Wickham’s false testimony and becomes convinced Darcy is “dishonest and inhuman.” **The decisive fixing point of prejudice.**

The core of Act 3 reads:

> Events 29–30: Reading Darcy’s letter, she learns Wickham’s true nature. **“Till this moment, I never knew myself.”** It is recognition of prejudice and a crisis of identity: “blind, partial, prejudiced, absurd.”

And the core of Act 5:

> Event 46: She accepts Darcy’s second proposal, saying that “her feelings have greatly changed”—a complete reversal from “the last man in the world.”
> Event 48: She marries Darcy and becomes mistress of Pemberley.

With this development tracked by event number across five acts, one can see at once where Elizabeth stands in the work and where she changes. When a translator opens the note for a scene—say, Event 28, the rejection of the first proposal—they can determine Korean tone while seeing both what prejudices Elizabeth has accumulated to that point and how they will subsequently break.

## Core Relations — Six Arcs

After change by event come core relations: a compression of whom the character relates to and how. Elizabeth’s core relations are organized as six arcs:

> **Darcy**: Antipathy → prejudice → recognition → esteem → love → marriage
> **Jane**: Closest sister and confidante (unchanging trust)
> **Wickham**: Attraction → trust → disillusionment → contempt
> **Charlotte**: Close friendship → clash over marriage → rupture → partial recovery
> **Father (Mr. Bennet)**: Favored daughter, the only family member with whom he can exchange ideas
> **Mother (Mrs. Bennet)**: Source of embarrassment and an uncontrollable presence

Each relation’s arrows show its arc of change. One page condenses how a person’s change appears in relation to others. While Elizabeth’s relation with Darcy changes five times, her relation with Jane never changes; that gives us another fact about her character—she is consistent with those closest to her.

## Translation Notes — Gradations of Tone

The note’s final section concerns fine points to consider when rendering the character’s speech and description into Korean.

Elizabeth’s note records three.

First, gradation of tone. The early “lively, playful disposition” subtly matures into late “ease and liveliness.” Both are liveliness, but the former is youthful wit and the latter is quiet liveliness after self-knowledge. Korean must distinguish these moments with different words to preserve her growth.

Second, although the word *prejudice* itself is rarely used directly in the work, it pervades every judgment. We cannot use the Korean word for “prejudice” every time, but must translate knowing that all of Elizabeth’s categorical utterances are expressions of it.

Third, her expressions about Darcy change gradually: “disagreeable” → “improved on acquaintance” → “gentle sensation” → “gratitude” → “love.” These are five grades. Korean renderings must distinguish them finely; blur even one and the apple becomes an apple again.

## How a Note Is Used — Translating One Scene

Finally, let us use one scene to show how a note works.

Suppose we are translating the Hunsford proposal scene in Chapter 34, where Darcy proposes to Elizabeth and she refuses: a climactic scene.

The translator keeps Elizabeth’s note beside the text. Which lines are opened, and what decisions follow?

First, they look at the Event 28 entry in change by event: “Elizabeth fiercely refuses Darcy’s first proposal. ‘The last man in the world whom I could ever be prevailed on to marry.’ Three reasons: his insulting manner of proposal, separating Bingley and Jane, and mistreatment of Wickham.” This line establishes that her refusal is not simple rejection but the explosion of three accumulated angers.

Next, they see the Darcy entry under core relations: “Antipathy → prejudice → recognition → …” It locates the proposal at the peak of the “prejudice” stage.

Finally, they read the second translation note: “All of Elizabeth’s categorical utterances must be rendered knowing they express prejudice.” They must not forget that whatever she says at this point is a product of prejudice.

After confirming these three lines, the translator renders her refusal line by line into Korean, remembering that “the last man in the world” is not merely a phrase of refusal but a categorical explosion from the peak of prejudice—the last cry of a prejudice soon to break. The same sentence then receives another tone.

This is how a character note moves alive in translation. With the note at hand, a line in one scene is rendered within its position in the whole work.

Next we examine a figure at a different rank: supporting character Charlotte Lucas. Her note is shorter and more compressed than a protagonist’s, but it shows how one person’s decision can create ripples through the entire work and how that tracing is condensed into one volume.
