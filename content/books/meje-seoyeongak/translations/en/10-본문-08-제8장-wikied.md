# Chapter 8. The Skeleton of Every Work — Common Materials and the Novel Pack

> **Opening Part 3.** In Part 1 we established the place and framework of <Seoyeongak>; in Part 2 we looked inside the *Pride and Prejudice* work. Part 3 opens the thirty-five kinds of materials made there, one kind at a time. This installment considers thirteen: the eight common materials made for every work and five for novels. The next considers the period encyclopedia and fifteen motif materials; the one after that, six supplements unique to *Pride and Prejudice*. We will see the intention and living form of each material.

---

These thirteen are the most general materials in the *Pride and Prejudice* folder. They are made in almost the same form for other works as well. We will look at them before moving to materials colored by the Regency period of *Pride and Prejudice*.

Each is introduced briefly with its intention, structure, and one or two examples from the novel.

## Eight Kinds of Common Core

### 1. Character Relationship Map

A composite chart that lays out the relationships of the entire work on one page. In *Pride and Prejudice*, it divides figures into seven groups: the Bennets, Darcys, Bingleys, Collins–Lucases, Gardiners, militia, and others. Family trees appear as trees, overlaid with six arcs of key relationships—double marriage plot, hostile relations, mentor relations, and more. A final Mermaid diagram visualizes all relations at a glance. If notes give the depth of one person, the relationship map is the plane of all people: a map of who stands beside whom.

### 2. Chronology

Material about time inside the work. It has four parts: the basis for setting the reference year (the line “Monday, November 18th,” establishing 1811); a chronology of forty-one background events before the work begins; a timeline of forty-nine main events by month, week, and day; and a character-age comparison table. In *Pride and Prejudice*, roughly two years—from autumn 1811 to autumn 1813—are organized with near minute-level precision. A translator can check at once which month and weekday a scene occurs and how old the characters are.

### 3. Complete Character List

An index of every named character. *Pride and Prejudice* has sixty-five: thirty-five named people plus thirty unnamed roles. It is divided into four sections: major figures (protagonists and core families), supporting figures (involved in many events), minor figures (in some events), and Section D—people who exist only as roles without names. The maid at Longbourn, the clockmaker of Meryton, and the gardener at Hunsford appear in Section D. The principle is not to omit even a person who delivers one line but has no name.

### 4. Complete Place List

An index of every place appearing in the work. *Pride and Prejudice* has fifty-three. It distinguishes directly shown places—Longbourn, Pemberley, Netherfield, Rosings, Hunsford—from places only mentioned—Brighton, Newcastle, Lambton, Gretna Green. Each place has a brief account of events that occur there and people who reside there.

### 5. Complete Object List

In *Pride and Prejudice*, eighty-nine objects are indexed by category: transport (seven kinds of carriage), pastimes (nine card games), instruments (piano, harp, singing), clothing (bonnet, dress, pelisse), food (venison, hothouse fruit, tea), letters, furniture (pianoforte, chess table), and others. Every named object in the work appears here once.

### 6. Chapter-by-Chapter Translation Guide

The material a translator opens most often each day. Every chapter receives one page with eight things: a one-line **summary**; **place**; **characters**; **events** by number; **key lines** with original quotations and a line of nuance; **mistranslation risks** with dangerous words and proper renderings; and **references** showing where to find other materials needed for the chapter. All sixty-one chapters of *Pride and Prejudice* have one.

### 7. Dictionary for Avoiding Mistranslation of Period Language

This material organizes words whose meanings change by period into three grades: **[Risk]**, whose modern meaning is opposite or completely different; **[Caution]**, whose key nuance differs; and **[Reference]**, whose usage has disappeared in modern times.

One word shows why this is decisive. At the Meryton ball in Chapter 3, Darcy says of Elizabeth:

> *"She is tolerable; but not handsome enough to tempt me."*

An English learner might render *tolerable* as “bearable,” producing: “She is bearable, but not beautiful enough to tempt me.” That line ruins the work. “Bearable” in Korean implies “only barely worth looking at,” making Darcy seem to judge Elizabeth nearly ugly. Elizabeth’s anger then seems natural, but Darcy’s later attraction becomes awkward across the whole work.

For an English gentleman of 1813, however, *tolerable* means something different: “reasonably all right,” or mere ordinariness. Darcy is closer to saying, “She is all right, but not enough to catch my eye.” It is insulting, but not violently so: a lightly arrogant assessment by a gentleman preserving his status. Elizabeth is angered by the gentleman’s arrogance, not by being deemed ugly.

One word can yield two Korean renderings, giving the whole work different weight. The period-language dictionary marks such sites in advance. In *Pride and Prejudice*, about thirty words are [Risk] and about fifty [Caution]. Besides *tolerable*, traps include *civility* (modern “politeness,” but a deeper sense of social obligation in 1813), *amiable* (modern “kind,” but period “socially agreeable”), and *indifferent* (modern “unconcerned,” but period “ordinary”).

Translators are usually blocked not because a word is difficult, but because it is familiar: it seems safe to use its dictionary sense even though two hundred years ago it carried a different weight. This dictionary is a map of that trap.

### 8. Honorific and Tone Translation Guide

This has five parts. Part 1, the honorific system, covers period hierarchies of Mr., Mrs., Miss, Sir, and Lady, including the rule by which “Miss + surname” indicates the eldest daughter. Part 2 tracks changes of address by character. Part 3 records speech types: formal, witty, exaggerated, cynical, long-winded, commanding. Part 4 lists sarcasm, irony, and figurative language by character. Part 5 is a consistency checklist, checking whether standardized renderings, address rules, and turning points of tone remain consistent across all materials.

These are the eight kinds made in the same way for every work. The next five are for novels.

## Five Kinds in the Novel Pack

### 9. Narrator Guide

One of the most difficult materials. *Pride and Prejudice* uses third-person narration but frequently uses free indirect discourse (FID): the narrator slips into a character’s interior and narrates in that character’s tone. Narrator and character voices mingle within one sentence, making their source difficult to identify. The guide gives a complete list of every FID occurrence and, for each chapter, a perspective map of whose viewpoint dominates. Without it, the same sentence can become a narrator’s assessment or a character’s self-justification—opposite meanings in translation.

### 10. Nuance List

A tracking document for sentences whose surface and actual meanings differ. It has five types: narrative irony, free indirect discourse, subtext in dialogue, dramatic irony, and double meaning. *Pride and Prejudice* averages two or three entries per chunk, for about fifty nuance cases across eighteen chunks. Each case contains an original quotation, surface meaning, actual meaning, and translation guidance.

### 11. Recurring-Motif Tracker

Tracks repeated words and images whose meanings change through a work. In *Pride and Prejudice*, words such as “letter,” “walk,” “window,” and “civility” acquire different weights each time they recur. This material lists every occurrence of each key word and follows changes in meaning by time. It also contains a standardized-translation correspondence table, so that the promised Korean rendering for a word is applied consistently across all materials.

### 12. Epistolary Collection

An index of every letter in the work. *Pride and Prejudice* directly contains more than twenty-one letters. For each it records sender, recipient, date sent, length, stylistic type, and narrative function. Darcy’s letter to Elizabeth the morning after his proposal is refused (Chapter 35) receives a detailed independent analysis. It is the work’s axis of rotation: its structure, the difference in tone between its two explanations, and delicate expressions to watch in translation.

### 13. Character-Psychology Change Curve

Material that visualizes the trajectories of recognition for two or three protagonists. In *Pride and Prejudice*, Elizabeth’s and Darcy’s curves are drawn. Elizabeth: attraction → prejudice → recognition → love. Darcy: attraction → restraint → proposal/refusal → change → second proposal/marriage. The two curves cross the work and show on one page where they meet. Only with this curve can a translator identify a scene’s tone precisely: at a first meeting, peak conflict, or just after reconciliation.

## What the Thirteen Create Together

We have now seen thirteen kinds. Compressed briefly:

> Nos. 1–5 are the work’s **indexes**: characters, time, places, objects, groups.
> No. 6 is a **practical dictionary** that unfolds the work chapter by chapter.
> Nos. 7–8 are dictionaries examining the work’s **language**: period expressions and forms of address.
> Nos. 9–13 are dictionaries tracking the work’s **narrative techniques**: narrator, nuance, motifs, letters, psychology.

These thirteen are the work’s skeleton. If one is absent, the others weaken. Without a relationship map, the relations between character notes cannot be seen; without the period-language dictionary, mistranslation-risk items in the chapter guide cannot be filled. The thirteen are built to support one another.

Next come the materials that give *Pride and Prejudice* its color: the Regency-period encyclopedia and fifteen motif materials. We will see flesh being added to the skeleton of thirteen.
