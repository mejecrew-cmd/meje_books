# Chapter 12. The Supporting Character’s Note — Charlotte Lucas

Last time we saw the richest form of a protagonist’s note: five-act change by event, six core relationship arcs, and tonal gradation. This time we look at a figure of another rank, supporting character Charlotte Lucas. Her note is shorter and more compressed than a protagonist’s, but that brevity is not simple reduction; it is a different kind of depth.

## Why Charlotte Lucas?

Charlotte is Elizabeth’s closest friend. Her decision to accept Collins’s proposal—the key event of Chunk 6—shocks Elizabeth, fractures their friendship, and shifts one center of gravity in the entire work. Few examples in *Pride and Prejudice* show one person’s one decision sending such ripples through the whole work.

Charlotte is a supporting character, but not an ordinary one. Her decision offers another answer to a core question: what marriage is and what happiness is. If Elizabeth says that marriage without love is impossible, Charlotte answers that happiness is chance and marriage is separate from happiness. The two positions live in parallel within the work without ever fully colliding.

Let us see how this character’s note is built.

## The First Page — Basic Information

> **Original forms**: Charlotte Lucas → Mrs. Collins
> **Korean rendering**: Charlotte Lucas → Mrs. Collins
> **Age**: 27 (established by “about twenty-seven” in Chapter 5)
> **Family**: Lucas Lodge; father is new gentry with only a title
> **Residence**: Lucas Lodge → Hunsford Parsonage
> **First appearance**: Chunk 1, Chapter 5

It resembles Elizabeth’s note but differs in two ways.

First, the original-forms line has an arrow: “Charlotte Lucas → Mrs. Collins.” For a character whose form of address changes through marriage, both names are entered on the first page. When she begins to be called “Mrs. Collins” late in the work, one resource immediately confirms that it is the same person under another name.

Second, she is twenty-seven. This one line carries the weight of every decision she makes. An unmarried woman of twenty-seven in the Regency period stood near the end of the marriage market. The period encyclopedia’s marriage section shows that a first season generally came at eighteen to twenty-two and marriage by twenty-five was common. Unmarried at twenty-seven, she was nearly in the category of an old maid. Only with this fact can her acceptance of Collins be rendered not as mere realism, but as grasping a final opportunity.

Her movement from Lucas Lodge to Hunsford Parsonage likewise condenses her fate in advance. On marriage she departs for Kent.

## Character Introduction — A Portrait in One Paragraph

A supporting character’s introduction is shorter than a protagonist’s, about one paragraph. Charlotte’s begins:

> Intelligent and sensible—but a thorough realist about marriage. Her statement, “Happiness in marriage is entirely a matter of chance,” becomes one axis of the whole work. She precisely opposes Elizabeth’s romanticism—her refusal of a marriage without love. Her acceptance of Collins’s proposal becomes *Pride and Prejudice*’s first great shock, fracturing both Elizabeth’s view of friendship and her view of marriage.

The paragraph is short but densely compressed. Personality (intelligence, sense), function in the work (Elizabeth’s opposite), key line (happiness is chance), and role in the work (medium of fracture) all enter it.

The introduction is brief because the character’s thickness can be compressed into one paragraph. Where a protagonist has the thickness of five acts, a supporting character has the thickness of one decision or one position. The length of the note is proportional to the character’s scope.

## Change by Event — Centered on One Decision

Change by event for a supporting character is compressed from five acts to about three. Charlotte’s is organized as follows:

> **Act 1: Elizabeth’s Friend** (Chunks 1–5) — Their intimate friendship early in the work. In scenes where Charlotte disputes Elizabeth’s assessment of Darcy, the difference in their views of marriage begins to show.
> **Act 2: Accepting Collins’s Proposal** (Chunk 6, Event 20) — A turning point of the work. It shocks Elizabeth and opens a fracture between them; from Charlotte’s position, it is a final opportunity seized by an unmarried woman of twenty-seven.
> **Act 3: Lady of Hunsford** (after Chapter 28) — She survives in her own sphere, manages Collins efficiently, and builds her own routine. When Elizabeth visits Hunsford and sees her household, the friendship partly recovers.

Though there are three acts, their center is one event: Event 20, accepting Collins’s proposal. Act 1 is organized as before it and Act 3 as after it. The note is structured like a satellite around one event.

## Core Relations — Three Arcs

Supporting characters generally have around three core relations. Charlotte’s are:

> **Elizabeth Bennet**: Intimate friendship → clash over marriage → fracture → partial recovery at the Hunsford reunion
> **Mr. Collins**: Before marriage (an available candidate) → after marriage (a husband managed efficiently)
> **Lady Catherine de Bourgh**: Appropriate distance from a patron; a precise balance that maintains the relationship without submitting as Collins does

Charlotte is active in all three. She makes and partly repairs the fracture with Elizabeth, manages Collins, and regulates her own distance from Lady Catherine. The core-relations section naturally reveals her agency.

## Translation Notes — Two Modes of Speech

Translation notes for a supporting character usually compress to one or two points. Charlotte’s note stresses one: **distinguishing two modes of speech**.

Charlotte speaks in different tones according to the situation. Alone with Elizabeth, she is intimate, frank, sometimes provocative. “Happiness in marriage is entirely a matter of chance” is a provocative line thrown directly at Elizabeth. Before Collins or Lady Catherine, by contrast, she is restrained and formal—the appropriate speech for maintaining patronage.

Translation must keep these two modes alive. Charlotte before Elizabeth and Charlotte before Lady Catherine need different Korean tones for her intelligence to come through. One flat tone reduces her either to a merely conservative or a merely clever figure.

## How the Note Saves a Scene — Chapter 22, Accepting the Proposal

The scene in which Charlotte’s note is most decisively used is her acceptance of Collins’s proposal in Chapter 22. The novel’s description is restrained:

> She accepted him with as much readiness as he could expect.

One English line. Literally: “She accepted him with as little hesitation as he could expect.” What is visible in this short line?

Without Charlotte’s note, it looks merely like formal acceptance of marriage. But read with the character introduction—“twenty-seven, realist, happiness is chance”—and change by event—Event 20, seizing a last opportunity—the line has another weight. “As much readiness as he could expect” contains a small distance that becomes translatable only through the note. She accepts not from love but with the exact quantity of formal favor.

In Korean it must not become “She gladly accepted him,” because she is not glad. She calculates and gives precisely the degree of consent that will satisfy the expecting Collins. The Korean expression that conveys this fine distance is determined by the note’s information.

## How the Note Saves a Scene — Chapter 28, Hunsford Reunion

Another decisive scene is the Hunsford reunion in Chapter 28, when Elizabeth visits and sees Charlotte’s household.

The distance between the two changes subtly. Elizabeth initially still cannot understand Charlotte’s decision, but partly recognizes it as she sees Charlotte surviving in her own sphere: managing Collins adeptly, spending time in the garden, and maintaining proper distance from her patron. A recognition that “Charlotte is living in Charlotte’s way.”

This change is not rendered as a great event. It flows through one or two lines of dialogue, a glance, a short paragraph of internal monologue. Only with the core-relations note—“fracture → partial recovery at the Hunsford reunion”—beside it can its subtle change be captured in Korean tone.

## The Nature of a Supporting Character Note

Charlotte’s note is shorter than Elizabeth’s. But shortness is not lightness. It is the result of compression aimed at precisely tracking one person’s one decision and the ripples of that decision.

This compression is the essence of a supporting-character note. Where a protagonist’s note treats a person’s evolution over five acts, a supporting note treats the ripple cast across the whole work by one decision or position. It is another kind of depth.

When a translator renders all of Charlotte’s appearances—about twelve to fifteen scenes throughout the work—with this short note beside them, Charlotte is rendered consistently alive in every scene. Whether a figure lives consistently within a work is ultimately where precision in translation arrives, and the tool that guarantees that consistency is the character note.

The next installment treats the note for minor character Mrs. Reynolds. She appears in only one scene in the whole work, yet the weight of that scene makes her a case for a note built almost as a standard sheet. The latter half also opens the auxiliary files not yet covered and answers the question likely raised throughout the lecture: “So how did you make thirty-four character notes in that time?”—through the technique of division of labor.
