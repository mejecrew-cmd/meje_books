# Chapter 13. Minor Characters, Supporting Files, and the Technique of Division of Labor

This installment sees three things together: the note for one minor character, the auxiliary files in the folder that the lecture has not yet covered, and the answer to a question that has naturally arisen throughout it.

The question is this: “If the notes for the two protagonists alone run beyond seven thousand Korean characters each, how did you make all thirty-four within that time?” This installment answers it.

We begin with a minor character’s note.

## A Minor Character’s Note, Mrs. Reynolds

Mrs. Reynolds appears in only one scene in all of *Pride and Prejudice*: Chapter 43, when Elizabeth visits Pemberley with the Gardiners. She is the housekeeper who shows them through Pemberley; that is her place in the work.

She speaks for about five minutes. She says she has known Mr. Darcy since he was four, testifies that he has been the most generous person both as a child and now, and insists that though people call him proud, she has never seen it. That is all.

Yet those five minutes overturn an entire recognition. Until then, Elizabeth has known Darcy through Wickham’s false testimony and her own prejudice. The housekeeper’s testimony, heard by chance on her Pemberley visit, denies that constructed character. It is the first moment an external, objective voice enters. This single event, Event 35 in the event master, makes all later change possible.

Though she is a minor character, the weight of one scene is this great. Mrs. Reynolds’s note was therefore not made in the minimum form for a minor role, but at a length close to a standard sheet.

### The First Page of the Note

> **Original form**: Mrs. Reynolds
> **Korean rendering**: Mrs. Reynolds
> **Position**: Pemberley housekeeper
> **Relation to Darcy**: Has known the young master since age four; effectively a member of the family
> **First appearance**: Chunk 12, Chapter 43

The basic information is short, but its third line matters most. Knowing Darcy from age four grants authority to her testimony. An evaluation by someone who has watched him in the same house for twenty-four years carries a different weight from an outsider’s. Every other piece of information in the note points toward that line.

### Character Introduction

> Housekeeper of the Pemberley estate. An elderly woman who has served the Darcy family all her life. An observer and witness from the young master’s childhood. Though she appears briefly, one statement becomes external evidence decisively transforming Elizabeth’s understanding of Darcy. The pride of a servant in an upper-class household, maternal affection for the young master she has watched grow up, and curiosity toward a possible new mistress—Elizabeth—are all held in one voice.

Compressed into one paragraph are the character’s position, function, and complex tone.

### Change by Event

For a minor character, change is simple because there is only one event:

> **Event 35:** The Pemberley visit. Mrs. Reynolds testifies that she has known Darcy “from the age of four” and has never heard a harsh word from him in all her life. This testimony becomes external evidence for Elizabeth’s change of view. The event master lists it among the forty-nine main events.

One event, but a deep one. A short change section does not make the note less valuable.

### Translation Notes

Mrs. Reynolds’s speech is short and plain, but its tone is difficult to decide because three things enter it at once: the pride of a servant to an upper-class household, maternal affection for the young master known since childhood, and curiosity toward a possible new mistress. These cannot be separated in translation; all three must live at once in a Korean sentence.

When translating the scene, the one paragraph of character introduction becomes the translator’s criterion. Even a minor role does not lose a scene’s tone when it has a note.

## The Technique of Division of Labor

At this point a natural question arises. The protagonists’ notes alone exceeded seven thousand Korean characters each; supporting notes ran to about four thousand; Mrs. Reynolds’s minor-character note was about fifteen hundred. For thirty-four people, this is substantial volume. How was it all made in that time?

The answer is to separate work points exactly without removing human hands. There is a process worthy of the name “the technique of division of labor.” It has three stages.

### First, Make the Two Protagonists First

The first task is the notes for the two protagonists: Elizabeth and Darcy in *Pride and Prejudice*. These must be deep and faithful, with five-act composition, six arcs of core relations, and translation notes.

The two notes are not merely character materials. They become the form standard for all later character notes: the fields to include, the depth at which each field is filled, and the way event numbers are quoted. Only when these two are precise does subsequent work remain consistent.

This stage takes the most time. The two protagonists alone take one or two hours. Yet that time saves all later time.

### Second, Work on Many Characters Simultaneously by the Form

Once the protagonists’ notes are complete, their form becomes the standard. From then on, one person can take one character and work in the same form simultaneously with others.

In *Pride and Prejudice*, about ten major figures and twenty-two supporting or minor figures were made this way. Because the form is shared, consistency among notes is naturally kept. If one person wrote every note sequentially, the form could drift subtly; with the formal standard fixed, simultaneous work still produces results coherent as if they came from one hand.

This stage saves the most time. Writing thirty-two notes sequentially would take days; division of labor finishes them in two or three hours. The time spent making the protagonist notes precisely is recovered here.

### Third, Run One Final Omission Audit

When all notes are complete, make one final check. Compare every character name in the chunk-by-chunk tables (the Stage 2 output) against the files in the note folder. Is there a name in the tables without a note? A note for someone never mentioned in the work?

In *Pride and Prejudice*, this audit added three minor figures at a late stage: Lady Lucas, Maria Lucas, and, interestingly, Mrs. Reynolds herself. Mrs. Reynolds was initially missed and added by the omission audit. The fact that even a decisive one-scene figure can be missed shows why this audit must be a final stage.

### Fourth, Check Delegated Work for Integrity in Five Ways

For division of labor to truly work, delegated results cannot simply be accepted. If one person writes every note, form and tone naturally cohere; results that pass through several hands inevitably admit slight misalignments. So after delegation, the notes undergo five further checks.

**First, consistency of form.** Does every output note use the same sections, headers, and field order as the protagonist notes? If “Change by Event” in one note is called “Major Events” in another, the form has drifted. One hundred percent formal agreement is required.

**Second, integrity of event numbers.** Does every event number cited by a note actually exist in the event master? If a note cites Event 28 but it is absent from the master, traceability breaks. This check is handled by automated comparison.

**Third, conformity to length standards.** About 7,000 Korean characters for a protagonist, 4,000 for a supporting character, and 1,500 for a minor role are the standards. If a note departs greatly, the character’s rank and the note’s weight are misaligned. It must fall within 30 percent of the standard.

**Fourth, consistency of tone.** Does the core framing fixed in the protagonist note operate in the same posture in other character notes? In *Pride and Prejudice*, the core framing is “Elizabeth embodies Prejudice.” If one note describes her merely as “a lively woman,” that framing has broken.

**Fifth, fit with period context.** Has language contrary to the period and customs fixed by the work profile—Regency England—entered the text? No one will put “smartphone” in an 1813 character’s speech, but subtler intrusions such as “stress” are entirely possible. A period-vocabulary check is the last check.

If a note fails even one of these five, it is requested again. These checks support the reliability of division of labor. Delegation works not because it ends at delegation, but because the five checks run once more afterward.

### Why Division of Labor Is Possible

It is possible because the preceding stages are complete. It needs a form standard, event information for every character in the event master, and character-appearance information in the chunk-by-chunk tables. If the event master is incomplete, delegated notes will cite mismatched events; if the tables are incomplete, omission audits cannot work.

This is why the six stages must remain in order. Only when Stage 1 profiling, Stage 2 chunk tables, and Stage 3 event master are precise can Stage 4 character notes be made quickly through division of labor. Stages 5 and 6 work similarly: without earlier support, later stages go wrong.

## The Folder’s Supporting Files

Finally, we look at supporting files in the folder that we have not yet covered. Chapter 4 briefly passed through the folder’s five layers, but did not look deeply at the first layer’s guide, work profile, and materials list, or the final layer’s verification report. Let us open each now.

### README, the Folder’s First Greeting

A one-page guide for the first person entering the folder. It gathers the number of materials, a map of where to begin reading, and verification status. The first line of the *Pride and Prejudice* folder’s guide begins:

> A systematically organized package of every reference material needed to translate or adapt the original text of *Pride and Prejudice* (Jane Austen, 1813).

The guide is updated throughout the work: materials are added as they are made, and verification results recorded when complete. It is the first page a new colleague opens, and so makes the folder’s reliability into a first impression.

### Work Profile, the Result of Stage 1

The two-page document made in Stage 1 sits on the folder’s first layer. It is our diagnosis of what kind of work this is: period (Regency), genre (novel/social novel), twelve motifs, translation difficulty. It remains so the folder itself shows that this one sheet became the criterion for every material decision.

The profile remains after work ends. If the folder is opened again at some future point, it shows exactly how we first read the work.

### Materials List, a Map of Where the Thirty-Five Are

This one-page material organizes the folder’s thirty-five kinds by classification and location: eight common core, five novel-pack, one Regency encyclopedia, fifteen motif-pack, six work-specific additions. Each line gives a material’s filename, classification code, and production status.

It lets a translator know within thirty seconds where to look when a word or scene causes trouble. Even if thirty-five kinds are organized, they fail as materials if locating one takes thirty minutes. The list is not merely material; it is the tool for finding material.

### Handover Notes, Bridges Between Tables

This is the supporting file mentioned briefly in Chapter 5: a paragraph summarizing the work’s state at the point where one chunk’s table ends. All eighteen chunks have one.

Small though it is, the note guarantees continuity. A person entering the next chunk—or the same worker returning days later—recovers context through it. Without it, they must reread the preceding table in full every time. One short paragraph saves tens of minutes.

### Verification Report, the Product of Stage 6

The final layer holds the product of Stage 6 cross-verification. It records, by grade, which items were checked, which errors were found, and how they were handled. The *Pride and Prejudice* report contains about ninety check items; it records that five were corrected immediately and the rest deferred to the next cycle.

We will see the actual verification stage in the next installment.

### What “Supporting” Means

“Supporting” means attached to a main body. But it also means what the main body must have in order to work as a main body.

Without the guide, the folder loses its way; without handover notes, work breaks; without a verification report, material integrity is not guaranteed. “Supporting” does not mean lightness but precision of assistance.

The thirty-five kinds of materials and thirty-four character notes are the folder’s body. The guide, work profile, materials list, handover notes, and verification report are the supports that make that body live and move. Together, body and supports make the folder one reference book.

The next installment is the last of the fourteen. It will show how verification, the sixth stage, actually happens; settle the entire series; and close the time spent with *Pride and Prejudice*.
