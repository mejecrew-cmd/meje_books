## 4. DOM: How Viewers Become Residents

One-line summary: DOM is not someone who watches content, but someone who lives inside it.

---

## The Rice of the Midnight Screening

It is a theater at midnight on a Saturday. The moment a wedding scene appears on screen, rice flies up from the seats. During the rain scene, hundreds of people unfold newspapers over their heads as if on cue, and somewhere a jet of water from a squirt gun cuts through the air. On the stage in front of the screen, people made up to look exactly like the film's characters stand and shadow the actors' movements, while the audience answers every line with a prepared chorus of comebacks. The word "toast" on screen sends slices of white bread flying, and when the song begins in the darkness, hundreds of lighters and phone screens switch on at once. The troupe of costumed performers has a name — shadow cast — and the audience's talkback has a name of its own — callbacks. First-time attendees are called up to the stage before the screening to go through an initiation. Being new to this theater carries its own title: virgin.

The film is The Rocky Horror Picture Show, released in 1975. It was a box-office failure on release, and became something else the following year, when audiences at a midnight screening in a New York theater began talking back to the screen. None of it — the rice, the newspapers, the costumes, the chorus — was ordered by the studio. The callback script was never an official document but an oral one, passed down by word of mouth, so that New York, Los Angeles, and London each developed slightly different responses — dialects, after half a century of circulating. The audience built this entire procedure, none of which appears in any distribution manual, and senior fans have been passing it down to newcomers for fifty years now, which is how this film became the longest-running theatrical release in history. This is the scene chapter 1 mentioned only in passing — the audience that comes in costume to a midnight screening and chants the dialogue in chorus.

There is an arithmetic that does not add up here: that audience is packed with people who have seen this film more than fifty times. Someone who has the plot memorized has no reason to come see the movie. And yet they come. If they are not coming to see the movie, what are they coming to do?

## The Nation of Fans: DOM

To find the answer, let's first take a word apart. Fandom is fan plus the suffix -dom. This is the same -dom in kingdom and freedom, an Old English suffix that meant territory, dominion, and state of being. Just as a kingdom is the king's country, fandom is, by etymology, the fan's country. We have used this word for decades without ever really reading the territorial metaphor built into it. To be strict about etymology, we should not say people "join" a fandom — we should say they immigrate into one.

The etymology we dug up in chapter 1 resurfaces here. Colere, the root of cult, carried three meanings at once: to cultivate, to dwell, to honor. The second of these belongs to this chapter. Dwelling was already inside the etymology of cult; a country was already inside the etymology of fandom. The two words point to the same place.

So this book calls the residents of that country DOM. The existing vocabulary is not enough. A viewer is someone standing outside the screen. A subscriber is the name of a payment relationship. A fan only names the intensity of affection. No word on the market pointed to the state of living inside a world. Asking how much someone likes something and asking where they live are different questions, the way a thermometer for fan feeling and a residency registration proving where someone dwells are different instruments. As a bonus, to web developers DOM is already shorthand for the model that reads a document as a structural tree — a coincidence that fits suspiciously well. Just as a structural tree of the document sits beneath the visible screen of a webpage, the DOM of this book reads content not as individual works but as a single structure, and is the kind of being that lives inside that structure. DOM is not someone who watches content, but someone who lives inside it.

## Sociology Got There First

This distinction is not sentimental rhetoric — it is a finding scholarship had already made. In 1985, the sociologists of religion Stark and Bainbridge divided cults into three tiers by organizational intensity. The first is the audience cult: people who attend lectures and buy magazines with no organization and no membership at all, typified by the readership of an astrology column. The second is the client cult: a service relationship in which consultations and sessions are bought and sold. If someone who reads a horoscope for fun is the audience, someone who gets regular fortune-telling readings while belonging to no sect at all is the client. The third is the cult movement: a complete organization that gives its members an identity.

Translate this forty-year-old ladder into the language of the content industry, one rung at a time, and it fits with a precision that raises the hair on your arms. The audience cult is the viewer: the world of view counts, a tier that consumes whatever the algorithm sends past and drifts on. The client cult is the subscriber: the world of payments and memberships, a tier that stays while the service is good and leaves without regret when it isn't. And the cult movement is DOM: the world of identity, a tier that introduces itself by that name. Sociology of religion had already sketched the content industry's funnel half a century in advance.

Let me draw a line so this isn't confused with the ladder of participation from chapter 1. That was the ladder for the builders — a supplier's ladder climbing from unaffiliated through tenant and evangelist to architect. The ladder introduced here is the receiver's ladder, one that deepens from viewer through subscriber to DOM. Community forms at the point where these two staircases interlock. The higher you climb, the fewer people remain on the ladder, but what's lost in numbers is gained in solidity. View counts, subscriptions, and residency are not a difference of degree. They are a difference of kind.

## The Sterility Theorem

The receiver's ladder has one threshold that everyone runs into: people climb as far as subscriber, and no DOM forms above it. Set two channels with similar subscriber counts side by side and the location of that threshold becomes visible. One is a how-to channel offering practical tips; the other has a distinct world of its own. View counts usually favor the former, because search carries the traffic in. And yet community forms only in the latter.

Information and atmosphere have different material properties. Information is consumed the instant it's delivered. Once you have the answer, your business is finished, because the value of information exists only for as long as you didn't yet know it. So visitors to informational content are structurally passengers at a station — they come when a problem arises and leave when it's solved. Even if the comment section of a spreadsheet-function channel is full of thank-yous, that gratitude is no promise of a return visit, and no matter how much foot traffic passes through a transfer station, nobody lives at the station. Atmosphere has the opposite material property. It cannot be summarized, cannot be transmitted — it can only be experienced by stepping inside it. That is the distance between reading about a Rocky Horror midnight screening and sitting in the seats and getting hit by the rice. The gap between the person who read a summary and the person who was there is not a gap in information. It is a gap in dwell time. Whatever can only be conveyed through experience carries revisiting with it, structurally.

So a law holds, and this book will call it the sterility theorem: content with no atmosphere built into it cannot produce community. What a how-to channel with millions of views has is not residency but traffic, and while traffic can be bought with ad spend, residency only comes from building.

## Cross-Examining the Counterexample

Here a counterexample raises its hand. What about developer forums? What about wikis? Aren't there perfectly healthy communities built on information alone? Let's cross-examine the counterexample head-on.

Open up the inside of a developer space that actually deserves the name community, and you find more than information. There is a humor all its own — the old jab to read the manual before asking, the taunt of reinventing the wheel, the law of the bike shed, which holds that the more trivial the decision, the longer the argument runs. There is a hierarchy of values — elegant code ranks above merely working code, and a running demo ranks above a flawless plan. There are legends of programmers, age-old holy wars over editor choice, rites of passage that newcomers go through, and landmark posts from a decade or more ago that get summoned by link like scripture. None of the items on this list are information. They are the thick atmosphere called hacker culture.

The control group is just as clear. Spaces with information and no atmosphere — official documentation sites, search results pages — sit at the very top of the traffic hierarchy and have no community at all. No user introduces themselves as belonging there, and the accurate name for such a space is not community but utility. Even Wikipedia proves the point. Inside its circle of editors there is an atmosphere made of debate norms, seniority hierarchies, and war stories of repelling vandalism, but mirror sites that duplicate the exact same information wholesale never grow an editor community. The information was copied. The atmosphere was not. So the verdict of the cross-examination reverses itself: information-based communities are not counterexamples to the sterility theorem but evidence for it. Wherever community has taken root, atmosphere was, without exception, already laid down first.

This theorem carries more weight in the fifth wave. Once generative AI begins pouring out endless how-to content, information is consumed even faster. In an age where the usage of any function is answered the instant you ask, even the station's standing — a standing built on information delivery alone — starts to shake. Atmosphere, by contrast, was never summarizable to begin with. The better machines get at summarizing, the more valuable the unsummarizable becomes. That is why, in the fifth wave, residency rate is not one metric among many. It is the front line.

## The Evidence of Dwelling

Dwelling is a state of mind, but fortunately it surfaces in behavior that can be observed from the outside. There are three kinds of evidence.

The first is connoisseur utterance: the sentence "this isn't very X." To be able to say what counts as X, the speaker has to have that world's baseline standard already built inside them. The fact that every new release sparks an argument over whether it's really Gundam is not a franchise in crisis — it is evidence of dwelling. Someone with no baseline cannot even enter that fight in the first place. The same utterance is observed around brands too: the customer who sets down a new product saying it isn't very [brand] is exactly the brand's resident. The second is internal language: the habit of deliberately using vocabulary that doesn't work outside the group. This is not a signal of communicative efficiency — it's a signal of identification. Why inefficiency becomes a signal is a separate matter, taken up in chapter 13. The third is no-audience revisit: footsteps returning on a day with no new release, no event, and nobody watching. It's the phenomenon of a check-in comment still landing today on a board that's posted a hiatus notice. Why consumption under conditions where showing off is impossible is the strongest evidence of all becomes the central material of the next chapter.

Once these three kinds of evidence are confirmed, it's time to swap out the dashboard. View count is an audience metric — a correct report card for the audience-cult stage, but useless as an instrument for measuring dwelling. The replacement metric this book proposes is called residency rate: not how many people passed through, but how many come back, how many speak by that world's standards. The revenue structure follows this same metric. Ad rates are set by view count, but donations, merchandise, and concert tickets are bought by residents, not viewers. The measure of successful content is not how many watched. It's how many live there.

## Exercise: Diagnosing Your Residency Rate

This chapter's tool is a diagnostic sheet that converts the three kinds of evidence just described into numbers.

1. The connoisseur index. Count occurrences of "this is/isn't very X" phrasing in the past three months of comments and posts. Count negation-form arguments too — "this isn't it" — because negation is also an utterance only someone with an established baseline can make.
2. Internal-language penetration. Compile a list of vocabulary that only works inside your world, and time how long it takes new arrivals to use it in their first comment. If that interval is shrinking, onboarding — the process of settling newcomers in — is working.
3. The purposeless-revisit rate. The share of visits that occur on days with no new content. The number of people who still wander in during a week with no uploads is the floor of your resident population.

If you need a sense of baseline values, the fastest route is to borrow metrics from online gaming, the field that has measured this most deeply. The games industry has long managed, as standard metrics, how many players return after a day, a week, and a month; carried over to community, it reads like this. The rhythm of return visits varies with a content's release cadence, but online, daily to seven days is the baseline, and a resident who goes dark for a month is treated as a sign of churn. The games industry also confirmed first that the rhythm of mobile, which is always in hand, and PC, which requires sitting down, differ from each other. The observation window needs to run at least 100 days to tell a passing trend apart from genuine dwelling. To give this a sense of scale, here is one real measurement: at the fandom activity service I ran at MEJE WORKS, 48 percent of unique visitors converted into people who came every day, and they logged in an average of 23 times a day for a combined one hour and two minutes. This is what the numbers look like in a world where dwelling has taken hold.

For all three, the trend matters more than the absolute value, and a quarterly measurement cycle is enough. Simply placing these three lines next to the view-count graph changes the question asked in the meeting room — from how many came this month to how many stayed this month. If all three metrics sit at zero while view count alone is large, what you are running is not yet a country. It's a broadcasting station.

Let me close by knocking on the door of the next chapter. Look closely at people whose residency rate has risen, and something strange is happening. As a person moves into a world, something from that world moves into the person as well. Fans start speaking in the speech patterns of the character they love; a brand's customers start choosing products in the brand's own personality. A person goes in to look around the world and comes out having become the person the world demands — dwelling, it turns out, was never a one-way street. When a person moves into a world, a character from that world takes office inside the person. The identity of that office-holder is the subject of the next chapter.

### Further reading

- The Rocky Horror Picture Show (1975). Shadow cast and callback culture can be traced through the official records and documentaries of fan troupes in different cities.
- Stark and Bainbridge's three-tier classification of audience cult, client cult, and cult movement. Found in The Future of Religion.
- Eric S. Raymond, "The Cathedral and the Bazaar," and the Jargon File. Insider documents recording the internal language and norms of hacker culture.
- The SCP Foundation. A dwelling-type community built on a collaborative-creation wiki, a case where atmosphere, not information, is what holds people.
- Star Trek fandom. The documentary Trekkies (1997) recorded portraits of its early residents.
- Hololive Production. A dwelling sample from just before the fifth wave, complete with a VTuber fandom's dwelling culture and even a farewell ritual called graduation (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hololive_Production).
- PLAVE. A Korean virtual idol group that climbed to a No. 1 spot on a Korean music chart show — a record proving that dwelling around an on-screen presence has been validated in the domestic mainstream market.
