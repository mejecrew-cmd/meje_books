## 7. Archetype: Myth Is Not Discarded, It Is Acquired

One-line summary: Propositions are forgotten, but stories remain.

---

## Egypt Across the River

In the cotton fields of the nineteenth-century American South, as the sun went down, a song would often start up: When Israel was in Egypt's land... Let my people go. To the ear of the white overseer, this song, "Go Down Moses," was a tale of someone else's people from three thousand years ago — a harmless hymn. To the people singing it, something entirely different could be heard. Egypt was the plantation in front of them, Pharaoh was the plantation owner, and the Jordan River was the Ohio River flowing north. That Harriet Tubman, who led the enslaved north, was called Moses among her companions was not a figure of speech but an accurate title, and some of these songs were in fact used as signals and route guidance for escape. When "Swing Low, Sweet Chariot" sang of a chariot coming to carry them home, only the singers knew whether that chariot belonged to heaven or to the far side of the border.

This scene, where the same lyrics are heard two different ways, forms an exact parallel to chapter 3's Korean barbecue restaurant. Just as that table read the company through the vocabulary of games, the military, and martial-arts fiction, the people of the cotton field were reading their own reality through a three-thousand-year-old lens called the Exodus. The difference is the stakes. Where the barbecue table's projection produced character assessments, the cotton field's projection produced identity, hope, and an escape plan. The narrative supplied, whole, who they were, what scene they were in, and where the ending was headed. The masters permitted this song because its lyrics were scripture, and that fact alone was this lens's armor. An interpretation built on a text that cannot be banned cannot itself be banned.

In the last chapter, we established Code as the first sentence of the worldview prompt. But propositions are not what gets memorized; what people memorize is story. Story — the vehicle that carries propositions and preserves them across generations — is this chapter's subject, and what we take up here are four things: origin, adversary, the chosen, and promise.

## The Function of the Origin Myth

The first is the answer to where do we come from: the origin myth. Let's be clear about its function first. The origin myth's job is not historical record but the supply of justification. In chapter 3 we defined the worldview as a synchronization engine that grounds every value, and the origin narrative is that engine's heart. The creation account in Genesis supplies the justification for the Sabbath, a norm about which day is which; the Exodus account supplies the justification for Passover, a norm about the table. Every question of why we do it this way converges, in the end, on the answer: because that is how we began. The myth grows more powerful the further down the questions go, and even at the point where a child's chain of whys exhausts an adult's knowledge, the origin narrative keeps supplying justification that never runs dry.

The contemporary specimen is the garage. A garage in Palo Alto bears a plaque reading Birthplace of Silicon Valley, and the space where Hewlett and Packard started with roughly five hundred dollars has become an official state historical landmark. Apple's garage founding story extended that lineage and became the industry's Genesis. What's interesting is that co-founder Steve Wozniak later admitted that almost no actual development happened in that garage, and that the garage was closer to myth. Yet that admission did not kill the myth in the slightest. Had the origin myth's job been factual reporting, this would have been a fatal wound, but its job is the supply of justification. From an origin of starting with bare hands, today's rebellious product philosophy and underdog narrative keep drawing their justification. A myth's durability is proportional not to its factuality but to the demand for justification.

To sum up: the origin myth is not a record of the past, but the heart of the engine that grounds every value in the present.

## Adversary and the Chosen

The second and third are a pair — the design of the enemy and the design of us — and the order matters. The enemy's coordinates have to be fixed first for our own coordinates to come into focus. Strip Pharaoh out of the Exodus narrative, and Israel's identity blurs with him.

The market-side textbook case is Apple's ad aired during the 1984 Super Bowl. In a gray world where a crowd in gray uniforms stares at Big Brother's speech on a giant screen, a runner carrying a sledgehammer bursts in and smashes the screen. The director was Ridley Scott of Blade Runner, and the ad completed its mission with a single national airing. The competitor's name never once appears, yet everyone knew who the giant was, and Apple secured its own coordinates with that single ad. The coordinates were the opposite of the giant, the opposite of conformity, and the Think Different campaign, years later, completed the other half of this pair. The tribute to "the crazy ones, the misfits, the rebels" was Code of the chosen — Code that commissioned customers as a select few. The grammar of Code of the chosen is not the conferral of qualification, but the narrative of recognition. The word order runs: not that you were picked because you are special, but that we recognized the special person you already were.

Here too there is a dial: the intensity of the binary. It runs on a continuum from the highest intensity, which designates the enemy as a demon, through a middle intensity that keeps it a rival, down to a low intensity that sets up an ideal with no adversary at all. The higher the intensity, the greater the mobilizing power, but the cost of managing the boundary and the side effects grow just as large. Coordinates can be fixed without an adversary at all: one outdoor brand, instead of a competitor, set up waste as its abstract pharaoh and ran an ad that said don't buy this jacket. The itemized bill for that ad we'll take up in chapter 13; here, the essential point is this: to fix the enemy's coordinates is to fix our own.

## Promise and the End-Time Narrative

The fourth element deals with the opposite direction in time. If origin draws its justification from the past, promise draws its motive force from the future. It is the answer to where are we going.

Promise carries a tense dial: the distant-form, which will be fulfilled someday, and the imminent-form, which will be fulfilled soon. The results religious history left behind as its experiment are clear: the imminent-form has proven to be the single most powerful mobilizing device humanity has ever invented. Faced with a narrative that judgment was imminent, people quit their jobs, liquidated their property, and tried to persuade their families. The Millerite movement anticipating the Second Coming, mentioned only in passing in chapter 1, is the specimen here. Once Miller, a farmer, calculated the numbers in the Book of Daniel and fixed a date, dedicated newspapers and traveling tent meetings distributed that date across the continent, and hundreds of thousands of people aligned themselves toward a single date. Imminence is priority's re-editor. Everyone has had the experience that the instant a deadline is set, every item on the schedule lines up again around that date. Translated into today's language, this is the state where a single release date aligns every customer's schedule, wallet, and topic of conversation, and teasers, countdowns, and pre-orders are all commercial versions of this device.

To say the motive force is the strongest is to say the debt is the largest too. The date arrives, and an arrived date puts the promise to the test. So seasoned organizations invented an imminence that names no date — a standing-imminence phrasing along the lines of that day will come like a thief in the night — and we'll open the profit-and-loss statement for both types in chapter 14. What happened on that hill on the morning of October 1844, and why, remarkably, that morning was not the end, I'll save for chapter 14's opening scene. Here, let me note only this: the imminent-form narrative is both the strongest mobilizing device and the largest debt.

## Recycling the Mytheme

Here I want to lay out, in full, the proposition that was this book's starting point. What myth, legend, folktale, and moral tale have been doing for thousands of years was not entertainment. It was distributing mentality to a community. A frame for interpreting the world and norms for behavior — what counts as courage, what counts as betrayal, how to treat a stranger, how to get through death — were packaged in the form of story and installed across generations. The tale of Kongjwi, a Korean folk heroine often likened to Cinderella, distributed the value of patience; The Loyal Brothers distributed the value of reciprocity; The Magpie That Repaid a Kindness distributed the value of good faith. In an age without writing or schools, this was the only distribution network for an operating system. Schools and media have since taken over part of that job, but the core technology of packaging norms as story and loading them onto emotion remains narrative's monopoly.

That job has never disappeared. Only the distribution network has changed, from the fireside to the theater and the screen. That is why old stories are not assets to be discarded, but assets awaiting acquisition. A legacy of narrative structure and symbol, field-tested for thousands of years, sits stacked in the storehouse with no copyright attached. The fact that Disney's earliest asset list consisted of the Brothers Grimm and Andersen proved, early on, the scale of this acquisition market. There is, however, one caveat attached to any acquisition. The Hero's Journey that Campbell mapped out was a useful guide, but after it was formalized into Hollywood's screenwriting manuals, it became a cliché the audience could recite scene by scene in advance. The hero with a thousand faces became a thousand movies, and in the process ended up with a single face. Chapter 2's principle holds here too: the mytheme is material to be taken apart, not a formula to be copied, and copying the formula regresses to the mean.

Myth is humanity's first mentality-distribution format, and content is that format's current version.

Let me leave one line, too, on this format's next version. The capacity named hyperinference in chapter 1 — the capacity to stitch scattered facts into a smooth narrative of cause and purpose — is now built into machines. The core technology of mentality distribution, which for thousands of years lived at the storyteller's fingertips, has acquired automated equipment. What narratives that equipment has begun to build, we'll see in chapter 10.

## In Today's Market

Let's confirm how the four elements are being used in the current market. The worldview narratives of K-pop are textbook in structure. The wilderness of obscurity and trainee years handles origin; an anonymous system or vast fate within the worldview handles the adversary; the fan who recognizes this narrative gains the position of the chosen; and the narrative destination of the completed lineup handles the promise. I'll withhold any specific group's name. The structure is common property, and a reader who knows this structure will be able to identify all four elements instantly in any group's worldview video. Fans take part in this world through the annotator's labor of decoding the video's symbols and predicting the next narrative beat. The fork between a worldview staying a settings document and becoming an engine was drawn in chapter 3, so here we only identify the structure.

The game is a medium that distributes the origin myth spatially. The narrative of the world's creation hides, broken apart, not in the first page of a manual but in a single line of item description, in place names, in the arrangement of ruins, and the player walks the ruins of Genesis instead of reading Genesis. It is a medium in which a single collapsed temple says more than ten pages of a settings document. This design of concealment connects directly to the subject of chapter 11. Liquid Death, which we saw in chapter 5, is a parody use of myth's grammar. It is a joke myth that sets water's origin as hell's own humor, and even a Genesis made for laughs still performs Genesis's job. It supplies a can with the justification for rebellion. The shell of a joke is, if anything, a shield: it does the same work while being exempted from the scrutiny a serious myth would face. In the age of memes, a funny Genesis spreads farther than a solemn one.

In short, the four narrative elements work across every industry without exception, and the only thing that doesn't work is not the elements but any plan to get by without them.

## Exercise: The Narrative Sheet

This chapter's tool is a four-box sheet.

1. Origin. Write, in one paragraph, where we were born and what we were born against. Write it not as a chronology but in the grammar of myth — in a form capable of supplying justification for every decision made today. If there's no garage, don't invent one; find instead what it was that we started out against.
2. Adversary. Designate the coordinates opposite ours, and specify a value for the intensity of the binary. It need not be a specific company. An abstract pharaoh — conformity, waste, rudeness — actually lasts longer, because companies reconcile or vanish, while an abstraction never retires.
3. The chosen. Write what select few our customers are commissioned as. A customer with no letter of commission is only a buyer, not a people. Write the sentence of commissioning closer to an admission notice than to ad copy. The door of the chosen is one that anyone can walk through, but that not just anyone can recognize.
4. Promise. Decide which future you're staking, and in which tense. If you choose the imminent-form, note in advance that the failure-preparedness sheet covered in chapter 14 is a required attachment. The draft for these four boxes needn't be long. Just as the whole of the Exodus compresses into a single sentence, one line is enough for the core of each box, and everything else is that one line's rendering.

Fill in the four boxes, and you now have a story. But a story that is only read is not, by that alone, stored in the body. The Exodus narrative lived for three thousand years because there was a Passover meal, reenacted on the table every spring. Narrative needs a storage device. That device, the one that inscribes itself onto the body — ritual — is the subject of the next chapter.

### Further reading

- African American spirituals and the Underground Railroad, Harriet Tubman. Historical records of how songs became signals.
- The HP Garage (Palo Alto). California Historical Landmark No. 976; the physical plaque reading Birthplace of Silicon Valley still stands.
- Joseph Campbell, The Hero with a Thousand Faces. The original map of the Hero's Journey, and the starting point of the cliché-ification this chapter warns against.
- Apple's 1984 ad. Directed by Ridley Scott; the record of its single Super Bowl airing survives.
- Patagonia's 2011 ad "Don't Buy This Jacket." The ad this chapter cites as an example of the abstract pharaoh; the original text is publicly available.
- Greenpeace's founding voyage of 1971 and the Rainbow Warrior. A record of an environmental movement taking an old prophetic legend as its origin myth.
- Just Stop Oil (2022–). An environmental direct-action group that designed controversy itself through protests such as throwing soup at famous paintings — a 2020s case of adversary coordinates (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Just_Stop_Oil).
- Liver King. An influencer who grew explosively on a primal-masculinity narrative built from nine ancestral tenets urging people to live like their ancestors, then collapsed after a 2022 exposé — a single case embodying both the promise and the peril of commodifying an origin narrative (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver_King).
- SpaceX's narrative of Mars colonization. A present-tense case of a company's technology roadmap operating in the grammar of promise narrative.
