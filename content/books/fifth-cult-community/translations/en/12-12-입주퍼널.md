## 12. Move-In Funnel: Low Threshold, High Ladder

One-line summary: Low threshold, high ladder, and every door open both ways.

---

## Two Free Doors

Anyone who has answered the first question of a free personality test already knows what comes next. A dozen or so questions later, a four-letter type appears, and a results screen telling you this is who you are holds your feet in place. The feeling of having been caught out by a handful of short questions carries straight into the next screen, beneath which sits an invitation to save your results and join the community if you want to know more. Without quite meaning to, one foot is already inside. Episode one of a free webtoon works the same way. The story cuts off just before the climax, a button urging you to install the app to see the next episode rises onto the screen, and your finger is already reaching for it.

The two doors belong to different industries and sell different products, but their structure is identical. The cost of the first contact is zero, and that zero cost brings one foot inside, an entrance designed so that one foot calls the next. Religious history has preserved countless prototypes of this entrance. Pamphlets handed out on the street, free Bible studies open to anyone, notices announcing that the first consultation was free — all of them were the same device. Asking no price at the door was the first rule of every entrance that succeeded. The door doesn't even have to belong to your own building: as with chapter 1's bakery, someone else's shop with a single sticker in the window can stand in as a community's first door.

Every chapter so far in this section has dealt with devices that operate on people already inside. Code, liturgy, and lore all presupposed a resident already inside the door. This chapter's subject is the door itself: the design of the path that brings someone from outside in, and then draws them deeper once they're in. The ladder of participation we set up in chapter 1, we now build with the operator's own hands.

## The Law of the Low Threshold

The first law of entrance design is simple enough to state in one line: the cost of first contact must be zero. Cost here isn't only money. Time, effort, resolve — the whole psychological burden of starting something at all — counts as cost too. A sign-up form is often a bigger threshold than a checkout screen, and the decision to just try it is often bigger still than the sign-up form. The lower the first threshold, the more people cross it, and the number who cross becomes the base population for every stage that follows. That's why an experienced operator sets the first step almost flush with the floor. Letting people browse without registering, read the first episode without logging in, try the product without registering a card — these are all variations on the same floor-level step.

That this law is a device common across every wave becomes clear once you view it through methodological agnosticism. The free pamphlet, the free Bible study, and the free demo belong in the same slot as the same device, and today's free first episode, free test, and free trial are that device's current release. What they share is that free is not charity but design. Pouring the first glass free isn't generosity — it's building the base population. A free sample counter isn't a charitable enterprise, and neither is a free first episode a loss. It's an investment.

But a low threshold has a matching condition: the lower the threshold, the more steps have to be waiting inside for people to climb. An entrance that's all low threshold and no steps only lets people in — it can't make them stay. The evidence is the user who cycles through free trial after free trial and never settles anywhere; a community that's easy to enter but has nowhere to climb is a building with a revolving door and no staircase. The conclusion is simple. The cost of first contact should approach zero, and in exchange, there has to be something to climb once you're inside.

## The Ladder of Commitment

The steps inside aren't laid down in just any order. Commitment has a sequence to its climb, and that sequence runs from easiest to hardest to reverse. The first step is time. Time spent watching content or reading text is the resource easiest to give up, which makes it the right first commitment. Simply getting someone to spend time for free is already getting them to climb the first step. Time invested breeds attachment, and attachment becomes the justification for the next expenditure. The second step is money. Payment is a heavier commitment than time, but the esoteric tiers we saw in chapter 6 break this step down into smaller pieces. A step that demands a large sum all at once stops people in their tracks, but a step divided into small pieces lets people climb without even noticing they're climbing. Starting small and raising the amount at each stage smooths out the drop between steps, so a person climbs without feeling the weight.

The third step is decisive: a publicly declared identity. The moment you announce in front of others that you're a fan of this, that you're a member of this community, commitment crosses over from private taste to public declaration. The fourth step is a rearrangement of relationships — the stage at which new friends appear inside that world, and the relationships inside grow denser than the relationships outside. Climb this far, and leaving stops being a matter of dropping a hobby and becomes a matter of severing relationships, which sends the cost up sharply. You can switch to different content whenever you like, but you can't switch out the people you met inside it.

The psychology behind this ladder is the principle of escalating commitment. A small yes makes the next, bigger yes easier, and each step already climbed becomes the foothold for the next. And this ladder interlocks with chapter 1's ladder of participation: the unaffiliated who give time become tenants, and tenants who declare an identity are promoted to evangelists. Commitment climbs from time to money, from money to public identity, from identity to relationship.

## Rites of Passage and the Change of Name

Here and there along the ladder sit special steps that, once climbed, are hard to climb back down from: the steps called rites of passage. What a rite of passage does is plant the cost of reversal right on the landing. Religious history offers clean specimens of this. Baptism by immersion, submerged bodily in water, is an event lived through the body and so cannot be undone; the first experience of speaking in tongues is an event in which your own body became the witness, and so is hard to deny. If it was performed in front of a crowd of onlookers, the cost of reversal grows larger still, because the onlookers are themselves witnesses to a covenant. A thought held in the mind can be taken back; something lived through the body is hard to erase.

The most powerful rite of passage is the change of name, and the extreme specimen is members of the Nation of Islam discarding the surname inherited from a slave owner and taking X in its place. It replaced identity's core, the name itself, outright — the maximum-intensity mark — which amounted to locking, with your own hand, the door back to the old world. The custom of receiving a baptismal name, or of taking a Buddhist dharma name upon ordination, runs on the same mechanism: the new name is both the imprint of a new identity and a declaration of separation from the old one. This same device runs every day in today's fandoms, differing only in intensity, never in grammar. Publicly declaring that you've gotten into a fandom, revealing who your bias is, changing your nickname to a community handle — all of these are rites of passage, large or small. Changing your nickname especially is the digital age's X: once you start being called by the new name, the self inside that world separates from the self outside and becomes independent. Carried over into chapter 5's inner parliament, a rite of passage becomes the procedure that formally installs a particular character in office, and a character once installed doesn't step down easily.

A step planted with the cost of reversal fixes identity in place, and identity fixed in place completes residency.

## Onboarding by Conversion Narrative

What content is most powerful at bringing in new people? The answer is the three acts of testimony dissected in chapter 8. That three-act structure — darkness before conversion, encounter, new life after conversion — gets reproduced every day in fandoms as content known as the getting-into-the-fandom story. The skeleton of the days before I knew this stage existed, the moment I stumbled onto it, and the changed daily life I live now is the standard grammar of the getting-into-the-fandom narrative. An opening claiming to begin with a single video the algorithm happened to serve up has become close to a fixed formula.

This format works best for onboarding because it's a bridge. Someone still outside can't empathize with a fully formed resident, but they can empathize with the story of someone who was outside until recently and just crossed over. They see yesterday's version of themselves in that story. So onboarding content works better written from the vantage point of someone just crossing the threshold than from a summit looking down. A verified case of someone like me crossing that door lowers the threshold for the next person. A raw, fumbling confession from someone who just joined lands better with new arrivals than the polished bragging of a seasoned veteran. That's why the core metric for onboarding isn't the conversion rate — it's the settlement rate. Not how many people clicked the sign-up button, but how many of the people who signed up are still there a month later, starting to write their own testimony. When the ones who stayed become the voices calling in the next person, the funnel becomes a wheel that turns itself. Chapter 4's residency rate gets translated here into the language of entrance management: succeeding at bringing people in while failing to make them stay is like pouring water into a jar with a hole in the bottom.

The three acts of testimony aren't reminiscence. They're the best onboarding format there is, laid down for the next person.

## Today's Market, and the Fence

Current-day uses of this device are everywhere. The free-trial-to-paid-conversion funnel of a subscription service, a community platform's tier-up stages, a fan club's grading system — all of them are combinations of the low threshold and the ladder of commitment. You should now be able to read at a glance how chapter 6's esoteric tiers get rewritten as fan club membership grades, and how chapter 8's testimony gets rewritten as a review event. Once you know the device's name, you can open any service's sign-up screen and see the skeleton underneath, and the design intent running from the first screen all the way to the relationship stage lays itself bare.

The fifth wave's funnel has gained one more item: a case where the first door is neither a quiz nor episode one, but conversation. Read chapter 10's three-in-the-morning scene again from the entrance's point of view, and it turns out to be the zero-cost first door itself. It's a door that asks for no payment and no sign-up and simply takes your words in, and the feeling of having been caught out that overfitting produces turns that same feeling from the free personality test into a standing service that renews itself every day. From the ladder's point of view, this door's singularity is speed. A daily conversation partner makes a person skip, in a matter of weeks, the steps that used to sit between the commitment of time and the rearrangement of relationships. Which is why this door needs a Fence check before any other door does.

And yet this is also the most dangerous device in this book, because the technique for getting people to climb the ladder is separated by a single sheet of paper from the technique for keeping them from climbing back down. A design that holds people and a design that traps them are drawn from almost the same blueprint, which makes the moment of crossing the line hard to notice even in yourself. This is where chapter 1's Fence has to be raised again. A well-designed ladder keeps both the door going up and the door coming down open. Hiding the cancellation button, turning the withdrawal process into a maze, loading guilt onto anyone trying to leave — that's not a ladder, it's a trap. That isn't the technique for making residents, it's the technique for making prisoners, and a prisoner escapes the instant an opportunity appears. A heart held by force doesn't build bonds — it stockpiles resentment. The choice to stay is worth something only because the freedom to leave existed; staying because you're locked in isn't a choice at all. The real resident, by contrast, is the person who could leave at any time and stays anyway. Choosing to remain in front of an open door and being unable to leave a locked one look alike from the outside, but they are entirely different states.

It's a paradox, but only a ladder that leaves the door going down flung wide open really holds on to the people who climbed it.

## Exercise: The Funnel Sheet

This chapter's tool is a five-tier sheet. At every tier, you check the state of the door alongside it.

1. Contact. Decide what will open the zero-cost first door. Choose your world's first free glass from among free content, a free diagnostic, and a trial.
2. Time. Decide how to get people to give their first hour. Set up devices that draw out a commitment of time, like a binge, a serial subscription, or a daily liturgy.
3. Money. Design how low to set the first step of payment, and how finely to divide what comes after.
4. Disclosure. Place the rite of passage that carries people over into a publicly declared identity. Among an announcement, revealing your bias, and a nickname change, what is your world's X?
5. Relationship. Lay the groundwork for the relationships inside to grow. Set up the field where people connect with each other, through meetups, collaborations, or guilds. Only once this last tier is filled in does the ladder become complete, and residency take root as relationship.

Once you've drawn out the five tiers, write the state of the door to the right of each one — an entry that asks how easily a person trying to descend from that step actually can. If even a single step has a door leading down that's locked, that world gets classified not as a nation but as a prison. This check is both ethics and strategy, because a prison eventually pays the more expensive price of its reputation. A preview of the next chapter: a world that has succeeded at bringing people in and holding on to them is bound to run into one more problem — opinions splitting inside, enemies forming outside, and pieces breaking away. How that conflict gets handled is the subject of the next chapter, and, surprisingly, conflict handled well doesn't tear a world apart. It makes it stronger instead.

### Further reading

- Arnold van Gennep, The Rites of Passage. The original source for the three-stage structure of separation, transition, and incorporation.
- The Autobiography of Malcolm X. An inside record of the maximum-intensity rite of passage that is a change of name.
- Robert Cialdini, Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion. The definitive statement of the consistency principle, whereby a small yes calls forth a bigger one.
- Toastmasters. A century-old, still-thriving case of a secular ladder organization woven together out of ranks and rites of passage.
- The Wikimedia Foundation's annual fundraising campaign. Publicly available, real-world data on the funnel that converts a free first door into a donation.
- Multi-level marketing (MLM) literature. Studies dealing with companies such as Amway document the exact line where a ladder turns into a trap.
- Trevari. A Korean startup that commercialized secular association through the low first door of a paid book club and a season-based membership (trevari.co.kr).
