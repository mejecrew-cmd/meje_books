## Appendix: Timeline, Glossary, Index

## Appendix 1. Timeline of the Five Waves

This unfolds, on a single page, the map of the hundred years the main text has traveled across. Wherever a wave number comes up in the text, you can return to this list.

First wave (1820s–1840s). The medium: cheap print, the mail, and itinerant preaching on foot. This book's specimens: Mormonism, the Millerite adventist movement, and Spiritualism (the Burned-over District).
Second wave (1870s–1900s). The medium: mass publishing and the railroad. This book's specimens: Christian Science, the Theosophical Society, and the Bible Student movement.
Third wave (1910s–1930s). The medium: the automobile, the phonograph, and radio. This book's specimens: the Azusa Street Revival and the Pentecostal movement, the house car, the gospel car, and KFSG.
Fourth wave (1960s–1980s). The medium: television. This book's specimens: televangelism (TV preaching) and the Western arrival of meditation movements.
The 4.5th wave (mid-2010s–). The medium: recommendation algorithms and social media. This book's specimen: QAnon.
Fifth wave (mid-2020s–). The medium: a medium that speaks for itself (AI). This book's specimens: personalized oracles, companion apps, and the Divine Platform.

## Appendix 2. Glossary

These are terms this book has defined and used. The number beside each term is the chapter where it was first defined.

### Foundations (chapters 1–5)

Cult (ch. 1): A neutral word from the Latin colere (to cultivate, to dwell, to worship). In this book, a community of devotion where people stay even though the door is open.
Lens (ch. 1): A term from earlier waves for the frame through which the world is interpreted. Inherited in chapter 3 as the worldview prompt.
Lens leasing (ch. 1): A business that rents space inside a worldview someone else has built, borrowing only its signal.
The ladder of participation (ch. 1): The supplier's four rungs. Rung 0, unaffiliated; rung 1, tenant; rung 2, evangelist; rung 3, architect.
The Fence (ch. 1): The 2×2 coordinate crossing the intensity of devotion with the cost of exit.
The state of the door (ch. 1): The freedom to exit. The criterion that separates a cult community from a high-control group.
Rendering (ch. 2): The process that converts a proposition into a set of perceptible values.
The eight atmosphere layers (ch. 2): Air, light, color, material, body, viewpoint, sound, time. The input values of atmosphere.
Methodological agnosticism (ch. 2): A stance that reads only the blueprint of the apparatus, without ruling on whether the belief itself is true.
Universe-Framing (ch. 3): Building, atop individual values, an interpretive system that supplies justification for every value.
Synchronization engine (ch. 3): The operating layer of a worldview that supplies justification for every value in the world.
Worldview prompt (ch. 3): The set of perceptual filters and keywords that engage automatically when reading the world. This book's design term.
Projection (ch. 3): The act of reading one's own reality layered over a work's vocabulary.
DOM (ch. 4): Not a person who watches content but a person who lives in it — the -dom of fandom.
Residency rate (ch. 4): The metric that replaces view counts. The share of people who come back, who speak by that world's own standards.
The sterility theorem (ch. 4): The law that content without embedded atmosphere cannot build a community.
Connoisseur utterance (ch. 4): The sentence "this isn't very us." Evidence that the baseline has been internalized.
No-audience revisit (ch. 4): Footsteps that return even though no one is watching. The strongest evidence of dwelling.
The inner parliament (ch. 5): The inside of a person is not a throne but a parliament of characters, and identity is the name of whichever character holds power.
Taking office (ch. 5): The event in which a content character gains a seat in a person's parliament.
Off-the-rack character (ch. 5): A character equipped within seconds, by purchase.
Campaigning and governing (ch. 5): Conspicuous consumption and no-audience consumption. The stump speech and the business of governing.
Private liturgy (ch. 5): A routine of use that customers invent and observe on their own, unprompted.

### Tools (chapters 6–14)

Code (ch. 6): A proposition that, once believed, changes how the world looks. One sentence that makes up a worldview prompt.
Salvation goods (ch. 6): The goods the Code promises. Seven items: healing, prosperity, knowledge, identity, imminent salvation, ecstasy, certainty.
The True Reading (ch. 6): A strategy that inherits authority by laying an interpretive key atop the text of highest authority.
Openness to falsification (ch. 6): The dial for whether a proposition can be corrected when it turns out wrong. The trade-off between lock-in and openness.
New light (ch. 6): A device that formally embeds the right to revise doctrine within theology itself.
Esoteric tiers (ch. 6): A design that discloses the Code in a staged sequence.
The four narrative elements (ch. 7): Origin, adversary, the chosen, promise.
Mytheme (ch. 7): Narrative structures and symbols with thousands of years of proven track record. An asset waiting to be acquired.
Ritual (ch. 8): Muscle exercise for belief. A repeated procedure stored in the body.
Collective effervescence (ch. 8): A sense of reality, exceeding the sum of individuals, produced by bodies performing the same motion on the same beat.
The bonding formula (ch. 8): Frequency times intensity times synchronization.
Liturgical calendar (ch. 8): The territorialization of time. A calendar that partitions everyday time with recurring dates.
The three acts of testimony (ch. 8): Darkness before conversion, the encounter, new life after conversion.
Hideout (ch. 9): A space of physical evidence that confirms the world is real.
The three mechanisms of sacralization (ch. 9): Contact, scarcity, narrative.
The coziness blend (ch. 9): Safety 0.85 and the presence of the outside 0.15.
Overfitting (ch. 10): The mass automatic production of personally addressed revelation. The point where an engineering flaw becomes the condition for revelation.
Hyperinference (ch. 10): The ability to stitch scattered fragments into a narrative of cause and purpose. The grammar of prophecy.
Revelation wiring (ch. 10): The circuit through which revelation flows. Central monopoly, distributed, and, in the fifth wave, personalized.
The fourth authority (ch. 10): A structure in which the right to revise the speaker belongs to a platform outside the worldview.
Version (ch. 10): An attribute the fifth wave's speaker has. The first crack in the condition of self-identity.
Divine Platform (ch. 10): A marketplace where individual oracles move in and compete on star ratings and reviews.
Official (canon) (ch. 11): The text granted the highest interpretive authority. The measuring rod (canon) by which other texts are measured.
Lore (ch. 11): The reserve of setting that has not yet been narrated. Fuel for the prompt.
Headcanon (ch. 11): Setting that is canon only inside one's own head. Evidence of dwelling.
Apocrypha (ch. 11): The sum of all headcanons. The domain of fan creation.
Move-in funnel (ch. 12): The path of moving in, climbed in five stages, from a zero-cost first door to the reorganization of relationships.
Rite of passage (ch. 12): A device that plants the cost of turning back at each landing. Its maximum intensity is a change of name.
Settlement rate (ch. 12): Onboarding's core metric. Among those who join, the share who remain and write their own testimony.
Fork (ch. 13): A schism. The metabolic action of a growing worldview.
Marker (ch. 13): A general term for the marks that divide inside from outside. A device that re-inscribes belonging every day through everyday friction.
The persecution converter (ch. 13): A device that recycles outside criticism into confirmation of belief. When it overheats, it seals shut.
D-Day (ch. 14): A device that aligns everything in the world toward a specific future date.
Failure protocol (ch. 14): A prepared device that folds prophetic failure back into the narrative. Four types: spiritual reinterpretation, invisible fulfillment, recalculation, trial narrative.

### Synthesis (chapter 15)

Prompt core (ch. 15): The core of the world. Four boxes: Code, salvation goods, the Universe-Framing check, and time direction.
Stack (ch. 15): The world's eight strata. Doctrine, narrative, ritual, experience, organization, material, media, ethics.
Positioning dials (ch. 15): Six dials — tension, organization, authority, salvation goods, time, revelation.
The five causes of failure modes (ch. 15): Tension excess, tension deficit, succession failure, over-specified prophecy, charisma single point of failure.
Conversion chain and operation chain (ch. 15): Two maps of the same process, one drawn in the language of observation, the other in the language of design.
