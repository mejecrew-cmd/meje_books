## 2. Thought Is Rendered: What an Illiterate Pilgrim Read in a Cathedral

One-line summary: Thought is not stated. It is rendered.

---

## The Illiterate's Reading

In the summer of 1225, a man is walking a path through the wheat fields of the Beauce, the granary plain southwest of Paris. It is the third day of a journey he set out on with his lord's permission after the harvest, and above the horizon two spires rise before the wheat ears do. In the middle of the flatland, the cathedral of Chartres approaches him not as a building but as a mountain. He has walked three days because of a rumor: that this cathedral houses the Virgin's tunic. A story has circulated through the villages of the plain that when a great fire burned the city a generation earlier, that piece of cloth alone survived unharmed. He cannot read. He knows no Latin, and he cannot even write his own name.

The moment he passes through the west door, summer is cut off. The stone's chill cools his sweat, and his eyes sink into darkness for a while before slowly finding color again. The high windows drop blue and red light onto the floor, and the air holds smoke from an incense he has never smelled in his village. A chant sounds from somewhere, its tail refusing to fade, so that it sounds less like human singing than like the building itself weeping. To see the ceiling he must bend his neck all the way back. Beneath a vault many times the height of his village bell tower, he learns the size of his own body anew, and, having learned it, kneels.

He does not understand a single word the priest at the altar speaks. The sermon is in Latin, and the only language he knows is his rural dialect. And yet, on the road home, he carries one certainty with him: that there exists an order higher than his own village, that God is great and man is small, and that he has just stepped briefly into the shadow of that greatness and out again. In later years, people would come to call cathedrals like this one the poor man's Bible. He did not read a single letter. And yet he had clearly read something on his way out. What, then, had he read that day?

## Adjectives and Parameters

Before rushing to an answer, let me bring in a tool. In Atmosphere Engineering, this book's sister series, I spent three installments arguing that atmosphere is not an adjective but a parameter. For readers who haven't read that series, here is as much of it as this book needs.

Craft manuals commonly advise you to create an ominous atmosphere, but there is no ominous key on a keyboard. Ominousness is the impression a reader forms after finishing the piece, an output value, and what a writer can actually touch is something else entirely: concrete values such as fog density, the direction of light, the saturation of color, the reverberation of sound. Anyone who has opened a game engine already knows this. What the developer enters is a fog density of 0.7, not ominousness, but by the time it reaches the audience, that number arrives as ominousness. Atmosphere Engineering organizes these values into what it calls the eight atmosphere layers: air, which fills the space between subject and audience; light, which carries direction and color temperature; color, which speaks through saturation and palette; material, with its texture and weight; body, which speaks through posture and state; viewpoint, which is a matter of where one looks from; sound, made of reverberation and silence; and time, made of tempo and repetition. Each layer carries a handful of dials that can be turned.

A creator who thinks in adjectives can make only as many atmospheres as the adjectives they know. Because an adjective is a discrete word, there is no name for the state halfway between ominous and forlorn. A dial, being a continuous value, can specify any point between two states, and so it can produce atmospheres that have no name yet. In short, the adjective is the audience's language; what the creator holds in hand is the input values of the eight layers.

With that single distinction, we are ready. Let us take this tool and go back to the pilgrim's day.

## The Cathedral's Eight Layers

Let us retrace what he passed through, layer by layer. Air comes first. The smoke of frankincense is a medium before it is a scent: it scatters the light entering through the high windows, making the shafts of light visible, and its smell tells him that the air of the cathedral is a different substance from the air of his village. Light has its direction engineered. Remove the windows at human eye level and let light descend only from above, and brightness becomes something that always comes from up high. Color is the work of the stained glass. Cobalt blue does not exist in the visual world of a medieval peasant, so the blue of Chartres is, in itself, evidence of something outside daily life.

Material is stone and gold. Stone of a weight that seems impossible for human hands to have stacked, and metal he will never touch in the rest of his life, gathered together in a single space: the pilgrim knows with his fingertips that this place lies outside the human scale. The value of body is engineered too. Three days of walking fatigue and hunger serve as a preprocessing step that raises his receptivity, and kneeling trains the proposition that man is low before God into the posture of his own body. Viewpoint is forced vertical. Human beings are animals that translate what they look up at into what is high, and what is high into what is great, so the angle at which the neck bends becomes the very angle of awe.

For sound, reverberation is the key. In a large stone-built space, the tail of a sound lingers for six or seven seconds, and human beings interpret a song whose ending never fades as something not of this world. Half the reason Gregorian chant is slow is not theology but acoustics. Last comes time. The bell cuts the day into pieces; the liturgical calendar cuts the year. Lay the calendar of Advent and Lent over the calendar of sowing and harvest, and ownership of time passes to the Church.

All eight layers point to the same proposition: that God is high and man is small. The awe the pilgrim carried away was the output value; what stonemasons, glaziers, and priests engineered across generations was the input value.

## Geertz's Five Clauses

Some readers will feel uneasy here, the unease of wondering whether reading a cathedral as a staging device and its liturgy as parameter manipulation is a blasphemy against faith. That unease has a ready answer. This reading is not my invention; it is common knowledge in religious studies.

In 1966, the American anthropologist Clifford Geertz defined religion in five clauses. In summary: religion is a system of symbols which acts to establish powerful, pervasive, and long-lasting moods and motivations in men by formulating conceptions of a general order of existence and clothing these conceptions with such an aura of factuality that the moods and motivations seem uniquely realistic. It is a definition that has sat near the opening of introductory texts in religious studies for more than half a century.

Translate the five clauses into this book's vocabulary and the correspondence is exact. The system of symbols is content. The establishment of mood is the output of the eight layers we just saw in the cathedral. The conceptions of the order of existence are worldview; the aura of factuality is what creative theory calls verisimilitude; and the unique sense of reality is the state this book will call dwelling. Right in the middle of the standard definition sits the word mood. Religious studies has, from the very start, described religion as an atmosphere-generating device.

Let me also make this book's stance clear here. Whether God exists is not within this book's jurisdiction. If God exists, this process is a facility humanity built to reach God; if not, this process is all there is; either way, the blueprint of the facility is the same. Religious studies calls this stance methodological agnosticism, which means offering the same blueprint to believing readers and unbelieving readers alike. This book deals only with the blueprint. And so this equation holds: religion was humanity's first and largest atmosphere-engineering project.

## The Genealogy of Actors and Pallbearers

Let us also verify the genealogy of the organizations that once handled this process. The profession of actor first belonged not to a theater troupe but to a congregation. Greek drama was a liturgical sequence within the festival of Dionysus, and the people who stood on stage belonged to a worship association called a thiasos, that is, to the cultus of Dionysus. By the Hellenistic period, these people had formed a professional guild called the Technitai of Dionysou; its member actors and musicians were recognized with priestly rank and enjoyed the privileges of safe passage across borders and exemption from taxes. Even Athens's practice of paying citizens a stipend to attend the theater existed because drama was a festival rite before it was entertainment. The first form the entertainment industry took was a cult organization.

The Korean peninsula's genealogy reaches the same place. The hyangdo of Silla and Goryeo were Buddhist devotional associations that burned incense together and buried aloeswood in the tidal flats while awaiting the age of Maitreya. As this association passed into the Joseon period, it secularized into an organization that took charge of a village's funerals, feasts, and labor. The word for this group, sangdukkun, those who carry the funeral bier, traces its etymology straight back to hyangdo-kkun, "hyangdo people." The maehyangbi (incense-burial steles) that these associations raised as they buried aloeswood still stand today along the shores of the West Sea and the South Sea. Since the wellspring of village governance that continues on through the dure (communal labor cooperative) and the gye (mutual-aid guild) turns out to be a devotional association, it is fair to call the operating system of the Korean village community a later version that branched off from a devotional association.

Why this genealogy matters is that this book's project is not an import. If performance came out of faith, and the village came out of the association, then building a community out of content is not a strange new invention but a recombination of what was originally one thing. Which genealogy the crowd's mass singalong at a concert descends from, we will take up in detail in chapter eight; here, let one sentence stand. The cult is not a concept to be imported. It is a concept to be restored.

## Redefining Content

Now we return to the modern office. Every company has a mission statement. Innovative. Customer-centered. Sustainable. There is not yet a single report of a customer reading those adjectives on the wall and feeling anything. Because an adjective is an output value, the moment it is written into a statement it stops being a design and becomes a wish.

One company has treated the same idea differently. Nowhere in an Apple store is the word minimalism written. Instead, floor-to-ceiling glass specifies the value of light; a table of a single species of wood specifies the value of material; a layout with more empty space than merchandise specifies the value of viewpoint; devices left powered on for customers to touch specify the value of body. The customer, instead of reading the word simplicity, passes through the sensory values of simplicity. It is the same thing the cathedral did to the illiterate pilgrim.

Let me give this process a name: rendering. In 3D graphics, rendering means the process of computing a model that exists as numerical data into a visible image on screen; in this book, I use the word for the entire process of converting a proposition into a set of sensory values. The reason I insist on this word instead of translation is a matter of direction. Translation moves from language to language, but rendering descends from proposition to sensation. Thought is not conveyed by statement. It is conveyed only by rendering.

Attempts to read brands as belief systems have existed before: checklist theories urging you to equip a creation myth, a creed, icons, and rituals, and that checklist still holds true today. What this book adds to it is two things: through what process the items on the checklist become sensory values, and what changes when a talking machine enters that process. The difference between a checklist and a process is this book's difference. And so the definition of content changes. Content is not a vessel that holds a story. It is the result of a thought that has been rendered.

Any reader who has used image-generating AI has already felt how far this process has been automated. Type an ominous alley into the prompt box, and the machine selects the fog density, the direction of light, and the saturation of color on its own and fills the screen. Enter an adjective, and the values of the eight layers are assigned automatically, which means the rendering just defined has been automated in its entirety. What took generations of stonemasons at Chartres is now commanded by a single line of text. What this age of the single line of text brings to the worldview industry is the subject of chapter ten.

## Exercise: The Rendering Audit

Let me close this chapter with a tool. It is called the rendering audit, and it takes three steps.

1. Extraction. Find and list every place your thought is stated as an adjective: mission statements, About pages, brand guides, job postings. Words like innovative, warm, and authentic are the raw material of this list.
2. Comparison. For each adjective, ask: where is the point at which this word has been translated into a value the customer can sense? Which dial on which of the eight layers? If you have no storefront, your website's loading speed, typeface, and white space, and even the sentence length of your customer-service emails, are all values of a layer. If you run a café, the audit extends to the color temperature of the lighting, the reverberation of the background music, and the recline angle of the chair backs.
3. Verdict. Mark the adjectives that are stated but never translated, and the statements that contradict their own translation. A company that talks about innovation while running a ten-year-old website template is the textbook case.

Once the audit is finished, most organizations arrive at the same result: most of their thought is hanging on the wall in adjective form, and whatever has been rendered was rendered only by accident. The problem with accidental rendering is not quality but direction. Each layer points to its own separate proposition, so even added together they do not add up to a world.

And the moment you try to fill in the blanks on the audit sheet, one question confronts you: by what standard should the values even be chosen? Why must the cathedral's reverberation be six or seven seconds? Why must Apple's wood be that particular species? What becomes necessary is a layer above the rest, one that gives the values their grounds and aligns the eight scattered layers in a single direction. The name of that layer is worldview, and it is the subject of the next chapter.

### Further reading

- Chartres Cathedral. Its history of stained glass and pilgrimage can be traced through its UNESCO World Heritage listing materials.
- Clifford Geertz, *The Interpretation of Cultures*. Contains "Religion as a Cultural System," the 1966 essay whose definition of religion this chapter summarizes.
- The Technitai of Dionysou. Records of the ancient actors' guild that was recognized with priestly rank.
- The Heungsa-ri Maehyangbi Stele in Sacheon. Surviving maehyangbi steles along the southwestern coast can be confirmed through Korea's national heritage records.
- The Bauhaus. A model twentieth-century design movement that rendered thought into form and material.
- Walt Disney Imagineering. A real division that turned the rendering of worldview into space into an actual job.
- teamLab and the boom in immersive exhibitions. The present-tense case of the 2020s exhibition market shifting from selling paintings to selling the input values of atmosphere (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TeamLab).
