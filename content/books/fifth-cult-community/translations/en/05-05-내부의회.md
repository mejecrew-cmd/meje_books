## 5. The Inner Parliament: Who Governs the Nation Called You

One-line summary: What buys a product is not the person but the character inside that person; conspicuous consumption is campaigning; no-audience consumption is governing.

---

## Seven A.M., No Audience

Every morning at seven, in a kitchen no one is watching, someone brews coffee with a scale and a stopwatch. Fifteen grams of beans in the dripper, the grind set to its usual notch, water at ninety-two degrees, a thirty-second bloom. Pouring the thin stream in a spiral, he watches for a moment as the grounds exhale gas and swell — a procedure he performed yesterday, and the day before that. The beans were chosen by reading the farm name and the processing method printed on the bag. These three minutes are never filmed, and they are posted nowhere.

Canned coffee at the convenience store is cheaper, faster, and delivers roughly the same amount of caffeine per cup, so by the arithmetic alone this procedure is waste. In terms of time, it is the difference between three seconds to pop a can and three minutes to work a scale. If the purpose is caffeine, the three-minute ritual is excess — and yet he will take out the scale again tomorrow morning. At the end of the last chapter, we named the no-audience revisit as the strongest evidence of dwelling; what happens in this kitchen is that same argument's consumption version. It is a procedure maintained though no one watches, a standard kept though it is proven to no one.

There is no audience to perform for, so the explanation of display does not hold here. The explanation of habit is only half right, because habit is what a procedure hardens into, not the reason the procedure began. One question remains, then. If the performance continues every day with no audience present, where exactly is that performance's audience sitting? This chapter goes looking for the seat. Not outside. Inside.

## The Inner Parliament

The last chapter ended on this sentence: when a person moves into a world, a character from that world takes office inside the person. It is time to explain what taking office means, but first we need to check what the inside of a person actually looks like.

The notion that the self is one solid entity has faced objections for a long time. Hume wrote that whenever he tried to enter into himself to find himself, he never encountered anything but this or that perception, and never once caught the owner of those perceptions. The self, in his account, is not a solid entity but a bundle of perceptions. James, who opened the door to psychology, held that a person has as many social selves as there are people who recognize him. Minsky, one of the fathers of artificial intelligence, saw the mind not as a single subject but as a society of small agents. Overlay the three conclusions and this is what results: the inside of a person is not a throne but a parliament. Everyone has lived this. Someone resolute in a meeting room turning into a different person after one phone call from a parent is not acting. It is a change of government.

This book will call that parliament the inner parliament. The self at work, the self in front of one's parents, the self awake alone before dawn are characters competing for the same seat, and identity is not the name of a single solid entity but the name of whichever character currently holds the ruling majority. This also resolves taking office from the last chapter. A character crossing over from content and winning a seat in this parliament — that was taking office. Let me head off one misreading immediately: this view is not a theory of consumer manipulation. Sovereignty belongs to that person's characters from beginning to end, and all an outside brand or piece of content can do is supply materiel to a particular character. In the language of an election, the candidates are already inside the voter, not outside; what a brand can supply is not the candidate but campaign supplies. This is politics, not brainwashing. It is logistics, not manipulation.

This parliament model is not for spectators. If you make content, you have to answer, first, which seat in the viewer's parliament what you are making right now is aiming for. View count is the number of people in the gallery, and dwelling is a matter of seats: a million spectators applaud and leave, but a single seat casts a vote in a decision made before dawn. Has anyone who watched your channel ever gone on to think, at the grocery store, in a bookstore, on a night spent agonizing over whether to quit their job, in the vocabulary of your world? If that moment has been confirmed even once, you already hold a seat. If not, everything you have grown so far has been growing the gallery.

## The Age of the Off-the-Rack Character

If there is a parliament, where do its members come from? The supply of characters has moved through generations. The anthropologist Linton divided a person's status into two compartments: ascribed status, granted at birth, and achieved status, earned through effort. In the age of ascription, a character was free but came with no choice. Someone born the eldest son of the head family inherited the character of ancestral-rites priest whether he wanted it or not, and there was no window anywhere to return it. In the age of achievement, a character became a matter of choice, but it was expensive. The price of the character called doctor was a decade and more of training; the price of the character called master craftsman was a lifetime. Achieved characters carry upkeep too, with licenses, promotions, and reputation sending their bills continuously.

Most of today's market occupies a third compartment: purchase. A single ticket equips the audience-member character, a single album equips the fan character, a single free-test link equips the personality-type character, all in a matter of seconds. If you remember the questionnaire from chapter 3, that was exactly the discount corner of the off-the-rack rack. This book will call the goods in this compartment off-the-rack characters. Not an inherited uniform, not a decade-long bespoke suit, but clothing you pick off the rack and put on.

As the price of characters collapsed, the inventory exploded. In the age of ascription, one person's parliament held three or four seats; in the age of the off-the-rack character, people live wearing dozens of them. Just as a fuller closet means more agonizing over what to wear today, more characters means more competition over who to live as today. The parliament became a multi-party system, and in a multi-party system the contest for power never stops; every competing character demands its own upkeep. The other counter of that upkeep market is the content industry. The collapse in the cost of acquiring a character produced character inflation, and that inflation is the soil this industry grows in.

What the fifth wave adds to this multi-party parliament is the subject of chapter 10, so here I will leave only a one-line preview. Until now, seats have crossed over from content and from products. A medium that speaks for itself is, for the first time, applying for a seat under a new qualification: a counterpart that remembers you and answers every day.

## Coffee's Third Wave

Marketing textbooks have long classified products with one old grid: a quadrant of high involvement versus low involvement, products bought with reason versus products bought with feeling. On that grid, cars and houses were printed in the high-involvement box; coffee, beer, and water sat in the low-involvement box. The premise was that every product is born with a fixed level of involvement.

That premise broke apart on coffee. The industry tells its own history as three waves: the first wave, when instant coffee made coffee a mass product; the second wave, when espresso franchises turned coffee into a space; and the third wave of specialty coffee, which weighs a bean's elevation, varietal, processing method, and extraction yield. Cupping notes record words like jasmine and bergamot, and customers memorize the names of coffee varietals. Around what was once the byword for low involvement — plain black water — a world has stood up that argues over the terroir (a growing region's climate and soil) of a single varietal. The very fact that the industry writes its own history using the word wave should be a welcome fractal for this book's readers. The wave law from chapter 1 is at work even inside a single category.

Beer has gone a step further. The world of craft beer has an internal language of bitterness units (IBU) and hop varietals, a liturgy of checking beers into an app after drinking them, a pilgrimage of touring breweries, and a holy day of standing in line for a limited release. If this list looks familiar, you are seeing it correctly. The evidence of dwelling named in chapter 4 has all gathered around a beer glass. Just what this entire kit of equipment, standing whole atop a low-involvement product, is a list of — that we take up piece by piece in the chapters ahead. For now, only the verdict: involvement is not a property a product is born with, but a measurement of how deeply the worldview prompt built on top of that product has penetrated. The grid was not wrong so much as short one axis. The same coffee is fuel to one person and a world to another.

## Water and the Experiment in Blankness

Granted, coffee and beer are one thing, but surely a genuinely meaningless product is the exception — water, say. That objection still stands. As it happens, the market already holds the result of exactly that extreme-case experiment.

Liquid Death is water in a can. By ingredients, it is indistinguishable from any other water. What the company did was dress that water, entirely, in the worldview of heavy metal. A skull on the can, a slogan that says murder your thirst, the aesthetics of a rock festival. It borrowed the shelf grammar of energy drinks and beer instead of the bottled-water aisle's grammar, and walked straight into the middle of rock-festival and tattoo culture. Customers drink water and come away with a gesture of rebellion. This brand, which defied everything conventional wisdom says about health drinks head-on, became a unicorn valued in the billions of dollars. Because water as a product converges toward zero room for differentiation, this success lets us hold the product factor constant and leave only the worldview factor standing clean. If the MBTI in chapter 3 was the pure-culture experiment on the content side, this is the pure-culture experiment on the product side.

There is an opposite extreme, too. MUJI is a brand that erases meaning. It strips away logos, drains out color, and says only that a thing is cheap for a reason — an aesthetic of blankness. And that blankness itself became a uniform. Walk into a store and the very absence of a logo functions as the biggest logo of all. Devotees unify their homes around that blankness, and stand in front of a new product to deliver the connoisseur utterance: this isn't very MUJI. In the vocabulary of the last chapter, they are residents of MUJI, and even the choice to refuse meaning turns out to be a uniform within the market of meaning. So the verdict flips. Neutrality is not for sale in the market of meaning. Just as the neutral store did not exist in chapter 1, the neutral product was never an option either.

## Campaigning and Governing

Now let us split consumption into two kinds. The line runs through whether an audience is present. On one side sits conspicuous consumption: proof-of-visit photos from a Seongsu-dong pop-up, tagged unboxing posts, selfies in an opening-run line. On the other sits no-audience consumption: a seven-a.m. pour-over (hand drip), a quiet untagged repurchase, a use no one is ever told about.

Translated into the language of the inner parliament, these two are different phases of the same character. Conspicuous consumption is campaigning: a stump speech in which a character still contesting for power announces its own existence to the outside electorate and to the inner parliament at once. No-audience consumption is governing: the phase in which a character that has already taken power runs its daily routine with no audience present. Purchase is a vote in the inner parliament and the wallet is the ballot box, so every payment is recorded as a vote of confidence in some character. Collective action such as a fandom's streaming campaign reads by this same grammar. It is diligent, audience-free governing by a fan character that has taken power and scheduled daily plays into its routine, and the chart ranking reads as that governing's approval rating.

This is where marketers' chronic measurement error shows up: they count election indicators while never gauging governing indicators. Tags, proof-of-visit posts, and viral spread are the noise of a campaign, so they are easy to measure, but a brand whose pop-up opening-run rush never leads into a no-audience repurchase is a regime that won the election and failed at governing. The core of a governing indicator is the no-audience repurchase rate and the settling-in of private liturgy — private liturgy being a use procedure a customer invents and keeps on their own, one the brand never ordered. Let us return to the man at seven in the morning and answer the question left open in the introduction. The gallery seat in that kitchen sits inside him, and what occupies that seat is the coffee-person character currently in power. Conspicuous consumption is campaigning, and no-audience consumption is governing.

## Exercise: The Character Sheet

This chapter's tool is a sheet with three questions.

1. Identify the character. What buys your product is not the person but the character inside that person. Give that character a name. What buys a power tool is the weekend-carpenter character; what buys specialty beans is the coffee-person character; what buys noise-canceling headphones is the deep-focus-worker character.
2. Assess its stake. Judge that character's current stake in the parliament. Is it the ruling party running the daily routine, the opposition that only gets a voice on weekends, or an outsider still outside the chamber, not yet in office? The supplies needed differ by stake: the outsider needs name recognition, the opposition needs an occasion to speak, and the ruling party needs the supplies of daily routine.
3. Write the materiel list. Write down the supplies you actually furnish to that character. Is it equipment, dress, a stage, a routine, a vocabulary? For the coffee-person character, the beans are equipment, the scale is the uniform, and seven in the morning is the routine. If the list ends after one line of equipment, a competitor is very likely filling in the remaining lines.

This sheet closes the theoretical chain. If the ladder from chapter 1 set architect as the goal, the five chapters up to this one have been the theoretical textbook for that building work. Thought is rendered; the rendering becomes a prompt; the prompt turns a viewer into a resident; and inside dwelling, a character takes power. What remains is the actual supply that keeps this chain turning: creed, myth, liturgy, sanctuary, oracle, canon, onboarding, schism, holy day. We take up, one by one from the next chapter on, the supplies gathered from a century's worth of waves, each chapter proceeding by first reading the prototype in religious history, then confirming its transposed case in today's market. The nine chapters from here on are the materiel list of the character munitions industry.

### Further reading

- David Hume, *A Treatise of Human Nature*; William James, *The Principles of Psychology*; Marvin Minsky, *The Society of Mind*. The three original sources for viewing the self as a parliament.
- Ralph Linton, *The Study of Man* (1936). The source of the distinction between ascribed status and achieved status.
- Liquid Death and MUJI. The history of these two pure-culture experiments can be confirmed through public company materials and press coverage.
- Harley Owners Group. A textbook case of an owner community operated directly by the brand.
- Marie Kondo and tidying culture. A case in which a single proposition — if it doesn't spark joy, discard it — stood up a character that governs everyday life.
- The Stanley Quencher tumbler craze. The case of a century-old vacuum-flask company undergoing rapid growth around 2023 through the campaigning consumption of a collector character (en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanley_Quencher).
