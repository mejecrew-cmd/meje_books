# Chapter 8. Dual Narration — Prose of External Metaphor and Internal Law

Suppose the reader's IP has a mechanic called “dimension travel”: the principle by which a character moves from their own dimension to another. How should a LOREBOOK describe it? There are two ways.

The first teaches the mechanic directly.

```
[Direct instruction]

Dimension travel is a core mechanic of this IP. It is the operating principle by
which a person moves from one dimension to another. Travel takes place through a
device called a coffin; the person's consciousness transfers while the body remains
in the original dimension. This mechanic underlies every dimensional event in the IP.
```

Nothing here is wrong, and the information is accurate. But one thing is weak: the reader has no foothold for imagining dimension travel. Terms such as “coffin” and “consciousness transfer” have no place in the reader's previous experience. They can store the names in memory, yet cannot grasp the operation itself.

The other way admits the reader through a familiar external metaphor.

```
[External metaphor]

The moment a runner lies in the coffin and turns its handle, it feels no different
to putting on a VR headset. What is actually happening, however, is the physical
process of the runner's consciousness crossing from one dimension to another. No one
calls it a “connection.” People simply say that someone “went in.”
```

This is **dual narration**. A familiar external metaphor—the VR headset—gives the reader a foothold, and the IP's underlying law—consciousness crossing dimensions—flows out beneath it in a single movement. The reader can now picture the mechanic.

The method applies to prose work a pattern established in Korean translation of foreign-language works. When an IP has distinctive mechanics—black holes, divine instruments, dimension travel, spirit possession, and the like—it lets readers enter them through familiarity without breaking the IP's atmosphere.

## Why side-by-side tables do not work

One trap of dual narration is presenting the external metaphor and internal law side by side in a table.

```
[Table pairing: an example to avoid]

| External metaphor | Internal law |
|---|---|
| VR headset | consciousness transfer |
| connecting to the Matrix | crossing dimensions |
| logging in | diving |
```

It is accurate as reference material, but creates two problems in LOREBOOK prose. First, it interrupts readability. When readers meet a table in the middle of prose, they scan it and return to the paragraph; the flow stops once.

Second, it breaks the prose's atmosphere. When metaphor and law line up one-to-one inside a table, readers handle them as separate pieces of data. The metaphor becomes a mere explanatory device, while the law is pinned inside the table instead of being experienced directly.

Avoid table pairing. Let the two dissolve naturally into prose, as in the external-metaphor example above, where both flow through one paragraph.

## A three-beat prose rhythm — experience, reality, resonance

The recommended rhythm of dual narration has three beats.

> **Experience.** What does the person using the mechanic feel through their own senses? This is the part closest to the external metaphor.

> **Reality.** What IP law lies beneath that experience? What is actually taking place? This is the internal law.

> **Resonance.** What character does the difference between the two create, or where do they naturally meet?

The earlier example separates as follows.

```
[Beat 1: Experience]
The moment a runner lies in the coffin and turns its handle, it feels no different
from putting on a VR headset.

[Beat 2: Reality]
What is actually happening is the physical process of consciousness crossing from
one dimension to another.

[Beat 3: Resonance]
No one calls it a “connection.” People simply say that someone “went in.”
```

The three beats continue in one natural flow. Readers enter through a familiar metaphor, meet the IP law, and then sense the subtle difference between the two. This is the standard recommendation for a first mechanic passage, not an inflexible rule for every passage.

## An example from the hypothetical IP “○○”

Consider a shamanic mechanic in the hypothetical IP “○○.” A passage on possession could read like this:

```
At the moment possession begins, it feels to the male shaman like two people riding
in one car. He holds the steering wheel, but someone sits beside him in the passenger
seat and adds a word now and then. That someone is called a god. What is actually
happening is that a domain of another self opens along one side of the shaman's
consciousness; within this IP's shamanic system, it is regarded as a support for the
shaman's work. When readers see shaman Kim Mi-seon working while possessed, it feels
as if one person is doing the work of two. That feeling is correct.
```

Its three beats are the automobile metaphor, the IP law of another self opening in one side of consciousness, and the lingering sense that one person is doing two people's work. Readers enter through the car, touch the change in consciousness, and finally confirm the metaphor's accuracy.

The same IP can describe divine descent through a different metaphor:

```
A divine-descent rite resembles a matriculation ceremony that occurs once in a
person's life. People gather, and one student formally receives a status there. In
this IP's shamanism, however, that status is a contract with a god rather than a
school, and formal registration happens beyond this human world. When the rite ends,
the shaman returns home as a passage for the god, and that passage never closes for
the rest of their life.
```

The metaphor of matriculation is familiar, the law of a divine contract flows naturally, and the lifelong unbroken passage conveys the mechanic's weight. Readers can construct their own IP's distinctive mechanics in the same way.

## Three criteria for choosing a metaphor

The largest decision in dual narration is which external metaphor to choose. A fitting metaphor lets readers enter at once; a misplaced one makes the entire passage awkward.

The first criterion is **something in the reader's everyday life**. Good metaphors come from things readers often encounter: driving, matriculation, a café meeting, eating, reading, or walking. In science fiction, the distance between everyday experience and the IP's law is wide; a good metaphor narrows it. Comparing dimension travel to a VR headset is such a case. In an IP closer to everyday life, the distance is smaller, and a slightly more distant metaphor can show the mechanic from a new angle.

The second is **whether it illuminates the mechanic's core**. A metaphor need not reproduce every feature. “Two people in one car” illuminates the core of possession: a second self's domain inside consciousness. This decision requires human judgment. An AI collaborator can propose candidates, but only someone who knows the IP deeply can choose the metaphor that accurately illuminates it.

The last criterion is **whether it fits the IP's character**. A familiar metaphor still fails if it conflicts with the IP. If “○○” is close to shamanism, cooking, and everyday life, science-fiction metaphors such as spacecraft, robots, and artificial intelligence feel unnatural; cars, matriculation, and meals fit. In an SF IP, mechanical metaphors fit naturally and everyday metaphors may be weak. Match the metaphor's tone to the IP's tone.

## Do not quote rule codes in body text

Dual narration has one absolute prohibition: never quote rule codes in body text. The IP's mechanics may be organized in source material with codes such as:

```
PHYS-M01 Principle of consciousness transfer in possession
PHYS-M02 Bodily changes during possession
PHYS-M03 Limits and dangers of possession
```

These are names for internal organization. They must not appear as they are in LOREBOOK prose.

```
[Rule-code quotation: an example to avoid]

Possession occurs according to PHYS-M01, the principle of consciousness transfer in
possession. This principle underlies the IP's shamanic system.
```

“PHYS-M01” is unfamiliar to readers, and exposing a name from internal organization breaks the LOREBOOK's atmosphere. Instead, dissolve what the code indicates—the principle of consciousness transfer—into prose, as the possession example did. Codes belong only in footnotes, Appendix 1's glossary, or private editorial notes in HTML comments, never in the body.

## Models in the market

Five examples show how dual narration has been used in published works.

**Ilúvatar's music in Tolkien's *The Silmarillion*.** The creation mechanic enters through the metaphor of a chorus. Rather than directly explaining the metaphysics by which divine beings make one music and its harmony and discord form the world, the work enters through everyday ideas—song, music, and movement—while cosmological law flows beneath.

**The Gethenians in Ursula K. Le Guin's *The Left Hand of Darkness*.** A science-fiction mechanic of an ambisexual people is rendered through romance, family, and everyday relationships. Terms such as hormonal cycles and sex-differentiation mechanics do not stand exposed; the mechanic emerges naturally from a person meeting another during kemmer and from daily scenes in families and taverns.

**Connection in the Wachowskis' *The Matrix*.** Virtual reality enters through telephones, headsets, and dreams. Before readers meet the SF material of a neural interface, familiar gestures—lifting a receiver, falling asleep—already make the mechanic acceptable.

**ZA/UM's *Disco Elysium* and the *Thought Cabinet*.** A character's process of thought is rendered through the metaphor of **putting a book on a shelf**. An idea is “being internalized,” then after about six game hours it is shown as having been placed in the cabinet. An abstract mechanic becomes accessible through an ordinary movement readers already know.

**FromSoftware's *Sekiro* and recovery from immortality.** The power not to die enters through medicine and injury. “Dragon's blood” appears, but its operation is explained through the time in which wounds heal and the limits of accumulated fatigue. Everyday sensations of wounds and exhaustion become a foothold for an SF-fantasy mechanic.

In all five models, an external metaphor starts readers from familiar ground and internal law flows naturally underneath. That is the method LOREBOOK prose should follow.

## IPs without mechanics

If a reader's IP has no distinctive mechanic, this method applies only in part. The pair of external metaphor and internal law will not arise as naturally. Yet a similar method applies to social institutions, occupational routines, and cultural customs.

For the limited-inheritance system of “○○,” in which a family's shamanic role passes to only one child, pair the external metaphor of choosing a successor to a family business with the IP's own rule for the institution. For a shaman's daily life, pair counselors, therapists, or clergy with the shaman's role in the IP. The same light application works for customs, festivals, and ordinary rites.

## Five acceptance criteria for review

Dual-narration prose has five review criteria.

> **Is the metaphor familiar to readers?** Confirm that readers have met the external metaphor in daily life; a very specialized metaphor, such as a chemistry experiment, may not be familiar.

> **Does the law flow naturally?** Confirm that the bridge from metaphor to law lets readers follow in one motion.

> **Is the three-beat rhythm natural?** Experience, reality, and resonance should continue as one flow. If one is missing or stretched too long, the prose becomes awkward.

> **Are rule codes absent from the body?** Run an automated grep for patterns such as `PHYS-*`; the body must contain zero matches.

> **Are the five absolute prohibitions absent?** Check that lecture-like voice, exaggerated destiny, game meta-talk, trendy idioms, and categorical determinism do not appear.

When dual narration is well constructed, readers meet an IP's mechanic through a scene from everyday life—a VR headset, two people in a car, a matriculation ceremony—and the IP's law flows naturally beneath it. This is how one book can handle unfamiliar mechanics in SF, fantasy, or shamanism while laying familiar footholds for its readers.
