# Appendix — Index of Key Terms and Collection of Market Examples

This end appendix is meant to remain open as a reference when readers apply the fourteen chapters to their own IP. Its four sections contain an alphabetical index, market examples, a one-page four-axis collaboration matrix, and one complete sample entry.

## Appendix A. Index of Key Terms

One-line definitions and first appearances of major terms in this book.

### A–C

**aliases**. Frontmatter field listing alternate names; strengthens search. Example: “spirit-inviting rite” and “naerimgut” for the Sinnaenim ritual. → Chapters 3, 5, 13

**artbook / official guide type** (Variant B). Visual material occupies 40–50 percent; one entry equals one two-page spread. → Chapter 2

**Bible**. A 10–20-page agreement gathering every LOREBOOK decision; Phase 0 deliverable. → Chapter 10

**B5 hardcover**. Standard LOREBOOK format, 182 × 257 mm hardback. → Chapter 1

**card entry**. An 800–2,000-character, one-page entry for an NPC, company, tool, and so on. → Chapter 5

**CMYK**. Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, and Key black print standard; requires conversion and proofing from screen RGB. → Chapter 11

**collaboration's four axes**. Specification, intent, expected effect, and limitation—the common format for human/AI division of work. → Chapters 1, 12

**concept**. One of nine categories: an abstract unit of thought or world rule, such as entailment or marriage economics. → Chapter 3

**creator**. The person responsible for a work from beginning to end; the intended reader of this book. → Chapter 1

### D–G

**dead-link graph view**. Obsidian visualization in which Markdown files are dots and wikilinks are lines. → Chapters 1, 3, 6

**deluxe option**. Main LOREBOOK, companion volume, and slipcase sold as one set. → Chapters 9, 10

**directory skeleton**. Phase 1's empty LOREBOOK structure. → Chapter 10

**double narration**. Prose that carries external metaphor and internal law together in a three-step rhythm: experience, reality, afterglow. → Chapter 8

**entry gate**. Acceptance checkpoint before a Phase; failure returns work to that Phase. → Chapters 10, 11

**field voice** (場). Sensory-first tone using smell, sound, distance, crowds, and close first or second person; mapped to Parts 4, 6, 7. → Chapter 7

**five-color palette**. Visual identity limited to narrative, technical, theme, danger, and accent colors. → Chapters 1, 13

**frontmatter**. YAML metadata at a Markdown file's head: eight inherited Librarying fields plus three LOREBOOK fields, usually 11–12 total. → Chapters 3, 5

**gate**. See entry gate.

**git tag**. A point-in-time Git snapshot such as v0.1, v1.0, or v2.0; basis of single-tree versioning. → Chapter 13

**gloss voice**. One-line definition and caption tone used in appendices. → Chapter 7

**graph view**. See dead-link graph view.

### H–L

**headerless introduction**. One to three paragraphs, 200–600 Korean characters, after the one-line summary; presents the entry's big picture. → Chapter 5

**HTML comment**. Text inside `<!-- -->`, hidden when Markdown becomes HTML; private editorial space. → Chapter 5

**hub**. Frequently cited term that becomes a 3,000–5,000-character deep entry. → Chapters 5, 8

**IP** (Intellectual Property). A creator's or company's body of worlds, characters, and stories. → Chapter 1

**ISBN**. International Standard Book Number; main and companion volumes receive separate numbers. → Chapters 9, 11

**leaf**. Rarely cited term that becomes an 800–2,000-character card entry. → Chapters 5, 8

**Librarying**. Sister volume: a four-stage method for organizing scattered IP materials into a 1,500-entry Obsidian Vault. → Chapters 1, 3

**lorebook_section**. Frontmatter location of an entry's Part, chapter, and section, such as `02-§3-Shamanic-System`. → Chapter 5

### M–P

**macro six sections**. A Preface/World Tone, B Cosmology/Laws, C History/Chronology, D Geography/Culture/Daily Life, E Species/Factions/People, F Catalogs/Appendices. → Chapter 4

**master**. Final LOREBOOK decision-maker; in solo work, the creator. Comparable to a showrunner. → Chapter 10

**MEJE IP standard**. Shared standard of five MEJE technical-asset volumes: creator and AI as two hands, four collaboration axes, and one Markdown source. → Chapters 1, 12

**mega-hub**. A central term heavier and more cited than a normal hub; receives 1:N mapping. → Chapter 6

**middle**. Term between hub and leaf, becoming a 1,500–3,000-character standard entry. → Chapters 5, 8

**mise-en-scène**. One of nine categories: atmosphere, repeated image, or sensory motif. → Chapter 3

**N:1 mapping**. Several Vault terms merge into one LOREBOOK entry; about 15 percent. → Chapters 3, 6

**narrative Bible** (Variant C). Confidential writers' room PDF, also called a Show Bible. → Chapter 2

**nine categories**. Terminology, people, places, objects, events, devices, mise-en-scène, concepts, and values. → Chapters 1, 3

**one-line summary**. Blockquote after frontmatter, no more than 100 Korean characters. → Chapter 5

**1:1 mapping**. One Vault term becomes one LOREBOOK entry; about 80 percent. → Chapters 3, 6

**1:N mapping**. One mega-hub splits into several entries; about 5 percent. → Chapters 3, 6

**Phase**. One of seven major stages, 0–6. → Chapters 10, 11

**pilot Part**. One Part written in Phase 2 before the GO/NO-GO decision; Part 2 or 4 recommended. → Chapter 10

**progress six states**. ☐ unwritten / ◐ metadata / ◑ first prose / ◕ prose complete / ● cross-referenced / ✓ proofread and approved. → Chapters 5, 10

**Pull Quote**. A 50–200-character story quotation box with source. → Chapter 9

### Q–S

**Quartz**. Tool converting an Obsidian Vault into a static site while preserving wikilinks and graph view; MEJE's first choice. → Chapter 11

**related entries**. Wikilinks at an entry's end, combining Librarying's `related` field with LOREBOOK additions; five to ten recommended. → Chapter 5

**return flow**. New terms found in LOREBOOK flow back into Librarying. → Chapter 13

**seven voices**. Chronicle, field, catalog, foreword, essay, law-explanation, and gloss voices. → Chapter 7

**Show Bible**. See narrative Bible.

**single source of truth** (SSOT). Criterion identifying where a fact is decided; character sheets and approved stories rank above LOREBOOK. → Chapters 9, 10

**skeleton**. Phase 1's directories, empty Markdown files, and empty READMEs. → Chapter 10

**standard encyclopedic type** (Variant A). This book's default: roughly 450-page B5 hardcover in twelve Parts. → Chapter 2

### T–Z

**tags**. Obsidian tag system: `category/*`, `axis/*`, `version/*`, and `lorebook/*`. → Chapter 3

**TBD** (to be determined). Temporary undecided marker; any surviving body occurrence fails review. → Chapter 5

**thirteen columns**. Librarying frontmatter fields, eight inherited by LOREBOOK. → Chapters 3, 5

**tone guide**. Two- to three-page Phase 0 mapping of voices for the IP. → Chapters 7, 10

**tool / device**. One of nine categories: an IP-specific operating principle or narrative device such as dimensional travel or possession. → Chapter 3

**two-page spread**. Left and right pages treated as one editorial unit; standard for Variant B. → Chapter 2

**variant**. Four alternatives to Type A: B artbook, C narrative Bible, D game-design Bible, E comics/webtoon official guide. → Chapter 2

**v_finalized**. A state in which an item will no longer change; starting point for downstream work such as art commissioning. → Chapter 13

**v_master**. A finalized state that also passed the final integrated build. → Chapter 13

**wikilink**. Obsidian link between Markdown entries, displayed as an edge in graph view.

**worldbuilding actor**. Occupation that embodies a genre, such as cyberpunk runner or wuxia warrior; mapped to Part 7. → Chapter 4

**writers' room**. Collaborative room for US television writing; home of the Show Bible. → Chapter 2

**YAML**. “YAML Ain't Markup Language,” the key-value format of frontmatter. → Chapters 3, 5

**NDA**. Non-Disclosure Agreement used when sharing narrative Bibles. → Chapter 2

**GDD**. Game Design Document, another name for a game-design Bible. → Chapter 2

## Appendix B. Collection of Market Examples

### B-1. Dictionary vs Guidebook (Chapter 1)

- Tolkien's *The Silmarillion* (1977): guidebook favoring continuous reading.
- D&D *Forgotten Realms Campaign Setting*, 3rd ed. (2001): search-first dictionary.
- LOREBOOK: designed to contain both.

### B-2. Separation of Print, Web, and Wiki (Chapter 1)

- Blizzard's *World of Warcraft*: three *Chronicle* volumes, Wowpedia, and official site; three media with update delays.
- LOREBOOK single-source principle: web and print share 1,500 Markdown files and update together.

### B-3. Market Examples of Five Variants (Chapter 2)

> **A. Standard encyclopedia**: D&D *Forgotten Realms* (~320p), *Eberron: Rising from the Last War*, *Lancer World Book* (~200p), *Heart* (~216p), *Coyote & Crow* (~530p).

> **B. Artbook / official guide**: *Final Fantasy X Ultimania* (~420p), *Witcher 3 World Compendium* (~200p), *Elden Ring Lore Book* (~350p), HoYoverse's *Genshin Impact* official guide.

> **C. Narrative Bible**: Netflix *Stranger Things*, HBO *Game of Thrones*, and Marvel MCU Bibles.

> **D. Game-design Bible**: console/PC concept Bibles, indie GDD appendices, post-release “Making of ○○” editions.

> **E. Comics/webtoon guide**: *Tower of God* guide, *Naruto* character book, *Death Note 13: How to Read*, *A Certain Magical Index* character dictionary.

### B-4. Models for Seven Voices (Chapter 7)

> **Chronicle (史)**: Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian*, Kim Busik's *Samguk Sagi*, *Annals of the Joseon Dynasty*, *Forgotten Realms* chronology appendices, *Warhammer* Imperial histories.

> **Field (場)**: Tolkien's Shire, Murakami's *Norwegian Wood*, Park Wan-suh's *Who Ate Up All the Shinga?*, Martinaise in *Disco Elysium*, temple scenes in *Sekiro*.

> **Catalog (鑑)**: Linnaeus's *Systema Naturae* (1735), D&D *Monster Manual*, *Pokédex*, *Star Wars Visual Dictionary*, Korean bird and plant field guides.

### B-5. Models for Double Narration (Chapter 8)

- Ilúvatar's music in *The Silmarillion*: chorus/music/movements outside, metaphysical creation laws inside.
- Ursula K. Le Guin's *The Left Hand of Darkness*: love/family/daily life outside, Gethenian sex mechanics inside.
- The Wachowskis' *The Matrix*: telephone/headset/dream outside, neural virtual-reality interface inside.

### B-6. Models for Transliteration and Translation (Chapter 9)

- *Harry Potter*: Hogwarts, Muggle, and Quidditch transliterated; Sorting Hat translated by function; Diagon Alley explained.
- Tolkien's *Guide to the Names*: Frodo transliterated, Bag End translated; SSOT for 40+ languages.
- Haruki Murakami: personal and place names transliterated, institutions explained, e.g. University of Tokyo.

### B-7. Models for Genre Actors (Chapter 4)

- *The Lord of the Rings*: Hobbit fellowship. *Harry Potter*: magic-school student. *Moby-Dick*: whaler.
- Cyberpunk IP: runner. Wuxia IP: warrior. Fictional IP “○○”: Paksu Mudang.

## Appendix C. Integrated Four-Axis Collaboration Matrix

> **Phase 0. Input + Bible.** Librarying Vault + IP decisions → Bible. Draft 2–3 hours + decisions 1–2 weeks. All eight decisions belong to people.

> **Phase 1. Skeleton.** Bible + thirteen-column CSV → empty structure. Build 5–10 minutes + review 30 minutes. People verify conversion.

> **Phase 2. Pilot.** Skeleton + guides → core pilot prose. Draft 2–3 hours + review 2–3 weeks. The master owns GO/NO-GO.

> **Phase 3. Expansion.** Passed pilot → first prose for eleven Parts. Draft 1–2 months + review 3–5 months. People own voice, prohibitions, and IP character.

> **Phase 4. Consistency.** 1,500 entries + external sources → `_Checklist.md`. Ninety percent automated; checks 1–2 hours + decisions 2–3 weeks. People resolve subtle SSOT conflicts.

> **Phase 5. Appendices.** Frontmatter + body → drafts of 4+4 appendices. Sixty to seventy percent automated; generation 30–60 minutes + review 2–3 weeks. People own maps and NPC cards.

> **Phase 6. Web + print.** LOREBOOK + print specs → web + PDF. Web 1–2 hours + typesetting 1–2 months. People own design and print ordering.

Using the same four axes in every Phase makes collaboration legible when readers apply the system to their IP.

> The rite in which a Paksu Mudang first receives a god—the heaviest moment of a mudang's life.

The Sinnaenim ritual resembles a once-in-a-lifetime entrance ceremony. People gather and a student formally receives a new status. In this IP, however, that status is a covenant with a god rather than admission to a school, and registration occurs beyond the human world. Afterward the mudang returns home as the god's channel, a passage that remains open for life.

The rite defines the [[Paksu Mudang]] and first releases [[Maethim]]. It is central because one ceremony decides both a person's vocation and a family's direction.

## Progress of the Rite

The rite lasts days or weeks. Family and neighbors gather at the mudang household, and the young candidate fasts for several days. As the fast ends, the body grows light, opening a channel for the god.

At dawn, the family spreads colored clothing before the shrine and shapes the ground with drums and song. The candidate sits and receives one garment. Its color indicates the god's character and differs by family.

## In the Mudang Household

The rite usually occurs in the family home. At its center stands a shrine with an incense burner and colored cloth. Incense burns long; smoke touches the ceiling and disperses. Neighbors sit in the yard and witness a life being decided.

The rite also ends there. The new mudang rises at the shrine's foot and enters the inner quarters with family. From that day, one room becomes the mudang's chamber.

## Changes after the Rite

If possession occurs, the candidate is recognized as a Paksu Mudang; failure cannot be retried. A successful mudang accepts [[Possession]] as lifelong work and may inherit the family's office.

The success rate in “○○” is said to be about sixty percent. Failed candidates may choose another vocation or leave the family. One rite divides an entire life, giving it its weight.

- [[Possession]] — resulting phenomenon

### Five Points to Copy

**1. Frontmatter's 8 + 3 split.** Inherit Librarying's eight fields through `translation_notes`; decide only `hub_or_leaf`, `lorebook_section`, and LOREBOOK-specific tags.

**2. One-line summary.** One sentence under 100 Korean characters; avoid meta language such as “This entry is …”.

**3. Bridge from introduction to body.** The introduction's last line should lead naturally into the first `##` heading.

**4. Natural section titles.** Avoid “Information,” “Details,” and “TBD”; use everyday language such as “Progress of the Rite.”

**5. Six editorial notes.** Source, synchronization, illustration, undecided matter, writing issue, and status. None appears in print or on the web.

These points are the acceptance line. Drafting three to five core entries this way naturally follows Chapter 10's pilot criteria.

## Relationship among the Five Sister Volumes

```text
Seoyeongak          — read foreign works deeply, set translation keywords — translation/adaptation data for one book
New Character Build — create one person from zero — a twelve-stage character sheet
Librarying          — bind scattered material into a dictionary — 1,500-entry Obsidian Vault
LOREBOOK            — bind material into an encyclopedia — ~450p B5 hardcover + web
Storytelling 100    — create 100 stories from the library — companion collection
```

All five proceed with the creator and AI work partner as two hands and use the same four collaboration axes. Readers can begin with any one and move naturally to the others.
