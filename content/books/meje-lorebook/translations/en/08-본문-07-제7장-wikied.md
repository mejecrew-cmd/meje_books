# Chapter 7. Three Voices — Chronicle, Field, and Catalog

Imagine three books on one shelf: Sima Qian's *Records of the Grand Historian*, Tolkien's *The Hobbit*, and Linnaeus's *Systema Naturae*. The first records the time, people, and result of an event dryly; the second fills its page with meadow color and chimney-smoke scent; the third arranges classification codes and distribution in cards. Their tones are entirely different, and therefore do not invade one another's domains. A LOREBOOK gathers all three into one volume.

The same event reads like pages from three different books when written in three voices. In “XX,” a seven-year-old girl receives a deity at a mudang household outside Meryton in spring of Horizontal Year 47 and becomes the family's first mudang.

```
[Chronicle]
In spring of Horizontal Year 47, a young girl received a deity at a mudang household outside Meryton. She was seven years old and became the family's first mudang. The event began three generations of hereditary service.

[Field]
That dawn seemed no different from any other. Incense burned long in the courtyard at the end of a narrow alley. A seven-year-old knelt before the shrine. Her fingertips touched the warmth of the brazier. In that brief interval, something entered her body.

[Catalog]
Male-mudang initiation case 047.
Date: spring, Horizontal Year 47. Place: mudang quarter outside Meryton.
Initiate: female, age seven. Family status: first mudang.
Average initiation age: six to nine. Average passage rate: about sixty percent.
```

The data is identical, but the first records a family moment, the second places readers in the courtyard, and the third registers the event in a taxonomist's table.

A 450-page book in one voice exhausts readers quickly. Distributing three core voices—chronicle, field, and catalog—and four supporting voices—preface, essay, law explanation, and tool—creates natural tonal change across twelve parts.

## Chronicle voice — 史

Chronicle is the historian's voice. It records when, where, and by whom an event occurred without loading it with emotion.

It has three traits. The narrator does not call tragedy tragic or glory glorious, leaving interpretation to the reader. Time and space coordinates are explicit: spring of Horizontal Year 47, outside Meryton, mudang Kim. Vague “sometime,” “somewhere,” and “someone” are avoided. Judgment is suspended. Saying that the event began three generations of hereditary service is a fact, while calling it a heavy destiny or glorious beginning is narrator judgment.

Chronicle suits Part 3 History and Chronology and the appendix chronology compendium. Models include Sima Qian, the *Samguk Sagi*, the *Annals of the Joseon Dynasty*, the *Forgotten Realms* chronology, and the imperial history of *Warhammer*.

## Field voice — 場

Field voice belongs to someone physically present. It unfolds smell, sound, distance, crowds, and everyday motion through the narrator's senses.

Its three traits are sensory priority, close first- or second-person distance, and concrete streets, crowds, and routines. Rather than a city in general, it watches one alley; rather than people in general, one passerby.

```
Late at night on Hip Street, after about two, the smell of oil grows stronger. A small restaurant deep in an alley prepares a late meal, while one or two pedestrians hurry past with hands in pockets. Beyond a café window, a male mudang drinks thin tea. Steam from his cup touches the glass and clouds it.
```

Field voice suits Part 4 Geography, Part 6 Everyday Life and Culture, and Part 7 Genre Actors. Models include the Shire in *The Hobbit*, urban streets in literary fiction, Martinaise in *Disco Elysium*, and temple landscapes in *Sekiro*. Sensory richness must remain balanced with information: one or two sensory details and one or two factual details per paragraph are natural.

## Catalog voice — 鑑

Catalog is the taxonomist's voice. Like a natural-history guide, it arranges a species through classification codes, measurements, distribution, and cards.

Its three traits are classification and measurement, suspended judgment, and a card format. A taxonomist records frequencies and differences without calling a species good or bad.

```
Male Mudang (scientific name: Mudang masculus)
Distribution: entire IP; urban mudang quarters 50%, rural homes 30%, itinerant 20%
Frequency: about 0.05% of the total population
Initiation: passage through the spirit-possession ritual
Subtypes: shrine keeper, ritual specialist, itinerant mudang
Related mechanics: possession, spirit descent, resentment
```

Catalog voice suits Part 8 Planes and Spaces, Part 9 Beings, and Part 10 Factions. Its models range from Linnaeus's *Systema Naturae* to D&D's *Monster Manual*, the Pokédex, and *Star Wars Visual Dictionary*. Cards alone interrupt continuous reading, so short prose paragraphs should connect them.

## Four supporting voices

The **preface voice** directly greets readers at the beginning of the book or a part: “Imagine one scene.” The **essay voice** follows the narrator's thought freely in Part 1 or an afterword. The **law-explanation voice** unfolds how a mechanic works in Part 2, close to catalog but centered on principles rather than measurements. The **tool voice** belongs to appendices: glossary definitions, index lines, map captions, and chronology rows that help readers use reference material.

Together with the three core voices, these form seven voices whose weights may change by IP.

## Mapping voices by part

Each part uses a primary voice for sixty to seventy percent and supporting voices for thirty to forty percent.

The preface uses preface voice. Part 1 World Tone uses essay with field support. Part 2 Laws uses law explanation with catalog support. Part 3 History uses chronicle with essay support. Part 4 Geography uses field with catalog support. Part 5 Society, Politics, and Economy uses catalog with chronicle support. Part 6 Everyday Life and Culture uses field with essay support. Part 7 Genre Actors uses field with catalog support. Part 8 Planes and Spaces uses catalog with chronicle support. Part 9 Beings uses catalog with field support. Part 10 Factions uses catalog with chronicle support. Appendices use tool voice.

Readers therefore feel a change while crossing parts, while the catalog-heavy Parts 5 and 8–10 retain continuity.

## Voices also disperse within one part

A part is not monophonic. Part 9 may introduce a person in catalog voice, describe daily life in field voice, and summarize lineage in chronicle voice.

```
Male Mudang Kim Mi-seon
Classification: person · shamanism
Distribution: Meryton mudang quarter
Initiation: spring of Horizontal Year 35, age seven
Subtype: shrine keeper (current)
              [Catalog]

Kim Mi-seon rises at three every morning. She replaces the shrine incense and loosens the ash hardened overnight. The moment her fingertips rest in the brazier's warmth begins her day.
              [Field]

Within the family she is the final heir to a mudang office inherited for four generations. Whether it ends with her remains undecided.
              [Chronicle]
```

Classification registers the person, field prose gives life, and chronicle gives time. This distribution prevents monotony.

## Five absolute prohibitions

All seven voices avoid five tones.

First is **lecture voice**, signaled by “this world has two mechanics” and “you must understand.” A LOREBOOK sits beside readers rather than teaching from above.

Second is **inflated destiny**: “the winds of fate chose a child and determined an eternal destiny.” Large language makes readers doubt accuracy; clear coordinates give an event weight.

Third is **game-meta reference**: players, dice rolls, and character creation belong in rulebooks. LOREBOOK assumes the reader is inside the IP rather than manipulating it from outside.

Fourth is **trendy slang**, which ages quickly in a book meant to last five or ten years.

Fifth is **categorical determinism**: “one hundred percent” and “absolute law.” Living IPs naturally contain exceptions; use “in most cases” and record variations.

The freedom of seven voices operates only after these five are excluded.

## Acceptance criteria for voice review

First, the primary voice should occupy at least seventy percent of a part, with supporting voices up to thirty percent. More means the part's voice has not settled.

Second, readers should clearly feel tonal change between parts—for example, from History to Geography and then Society.

Third, distribution within one part must not approach an even fifty-fifty split. If it does, the part requires full rewriting rather than local repair.

Automated checks can catch obvious lecture language or inflated destiny, but subtle drift—such as lecture voice entering a catalog paragraph—requires human judgment. Adjusting the standard mapping to the IP also remains human work.

When seven voices flow naturally, one part follows time, another stands in an alley, and another walks between classification cards. That polyphonic flow creates the continuous-reading experience of a LOREBOOK.
