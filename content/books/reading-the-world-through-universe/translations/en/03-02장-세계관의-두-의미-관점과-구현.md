# Chapter 02. Two Meanings of Worldview: Perspective and Implementation

## Summary

Why does a painstakingly prepared worldview plan collapse when it meets actual content? Usually because perspective and implementation pull apart. A worldview has two layers. One is a perspective on the world; the other is the way that perspective is implemented in actual content and community. Perspective alone becomes a slogan, while implementation alone becomes decoration. For an IP to become a fandom community, both must work together. In K-pop and the fandom industry, a production system is no longer an organization that makes only songs and performances. It also designs a sensory direction, concepts for activities, a language for fans to learn, and images and relationships that can be operated over the long term. This chapter examines worldview by separating perspective from implementation and explains why that distinction is the starting point of MEJE-style IP design.

## 1. Worldview Is Not a Single Meaning

The word *worldview* appears singular, but it actually contains two meanings. One is a perspective on the world; the other is the result of implementing that perspective as a concrete structure. Without this distinction, worldview quickly becomes vague. One person uses it to mean a creator’s philosophy, another a setting bible, and another a brand concept or character narrative. None is necessarily wrong, but they are mixing different layers.

Worldview as perspective answers what a world considers important. Does this world value freedom? Order? Growth and overcoming? Relationships and care? Competition and victory? Perspective determines the direction of a world. A world without perspective may have many elements, but no center.

Worldview as implementation is the way that perspective appears in practice. It includes a world’s physical laws, social structure, occupational system, character relationships, costumes, music, colors, platform operations, merchandise, and the language of the fan community. Implementation makes perspective visible, tangible, and repeatedly experienceable. A perspective without implementation remains a good statement but never becomes a world.

The difficulty of worldview design lies precisely in handling these two layers at once. Perspective is abstract and implementation concrete. Perspective creates direction; implementation creates experience. If perspective is unclear, implementation scatters. If implementation is weak, perspective cannot be verified. IPs that form fandom communities therefore tend to connect the two repeatedly.

## 2. The Transformation of Production Systems

Contemporary content production makes this distinction even more important. In the past, production moved toward relatively clear outputs: a song, an album, a video, or a performance. Concepts, images, and strategy certainly mattered then too, but in today’s production environment a single output no longer completes an IP.

In streaming environments, a song does not end with one release; algorithms, playlists, and short-form media continually rediscover it. On fan platforms, activities continue through messages, live broadcasts, and behind-the-scenes content. Merchandise, photo cards, and pop-ups materialize fan memories beyond music, while global fandom carries content into other language communities through translation, memes, and interpretation. All these processes expand the scope of production.

Production is no longer simply the work of making good content. It has become the work of designing the perspective through which people understand that content, the points of contact where they experience it repeatedly, and the language in which they share it. Here, the distinction between perspective and implementation acquires practical meaning.

Consider an idol project. Its perspective is its attitude toward the world. How does it regard youth? Failure? Intimacy? With what emotion does it define its relationship with fans? Implementation is the way that perspective appears in music, choreography, costumes, video, character imagery, the voice used on fan platforms, the form of merchandise, and principles of community operation.

Without perspective, implementation scatters in pursuit of trends. If one release adopts a fierce concept, the next a refreshing one, and the next attaches a worldview narrative, fans struggle to find a long-term axis of interpretation. Conversely, if there is perspective but weak implementation, fans cannot experience it. There may be a fine phrase but no memorable scene, a philosophy but no object to hold, and claims of a world but no entrance through which fans can enter.

## 3. Perspective Is Worldview

One important distinction in Kim Dong-eun’s theory of worldview and worlds is that between *worldview* and *universe setting*. Worldview is the perspective from which a creator or IP sees the world; universe setting is the form in which that perspective is implemented as a concrete world structure. This distinction is highly useful for understanding worldview.

Worldview is a standard of judgment for the world. What is beautiful? What is shameful? What counts as growth? As corruption? What creates relationships? What destroys a world? Answers to these questions form a worldview. It need not be explained directly. A strong worldview instead reveals itself in scenes, relationships, and choices.

IPs that sustain fandom communities over time generally possess a recurring worldview. Fans do not necessarily express it as a philosophical statement, but they know it by feel. They know what kinds of sadness the IP addresses, what forms of growth it favors, which relationships it values, and what images it repeatedly summons. Fans enter the world through this sensibility.

Worldview also affects the language of fandom. It explains why different fandoms interpret the same scene differently. Some interpret primarily through narrative, some through relationships, and others through performance and achievement. When the worldview offered by the IP interlocks with the worldview received by fans, fandom becomes not merely a consumer group but an interpretive community.

Worldview is therefore not a stylish sentence written at the beginning of a planning document. It is an attitude the IP repeatedly adopts. Once fans trust that attitude, even small changes in the IP become meaningful. A new album, character, piece of merchandise, or platform activity is received as another implementation of the same worldview.

## 4. Implementation Is Universe Setting

Universe setting is the structure in which worldview actually operates. In a fictional world, it includes geography, history, species, technology, magic systems, and political structures. In modern IP, however, its range is broader. Musical genre, the color palette of music videos, activity concepts, members’ roles, the material qualities of merchandise, the voice used on fan platforms, and methods of community operation all form part of implementation.

Implementation is the surface through which fans experience a world. Fans do not first understand worldview in the abstract. They feel the world through the mood of a song, the rhythm of a performance, the color of a video, relationships among members, recurring symbols, the texture of merchandise, and the voice of the community. Implementation is a device that converts perspective into sensation.

Implementation must be coherent. Coherence does not mean everything must be identical. Strong implementation allows variation, but requires each variation to emerge from the same perspective. Fans do not dislike change itself; they become uncomfortable when they cannot read its reason. If the world’s perspective is preserved, fans receive new implementation as expansion. If the perspective disappears, they experience it as rupture.

Implementation is particularly important in idol IP. The same group may attempt a different concept with every album. For fans to experience these as one world, however, a sensibility must run through the changes. If the stage, video, messages, merchandise, and fan community all point in entirely different directions, fans struggle to read the world. If each point of contact shows the same perspective in a different way, fans move further inside.

aespa’s presentation, from its debut period, of relationships between avatars and real members illustrates this well. The central issue is not the avatar setting itself, but the attitude with which it recurred across music, video, stage imagery, and fan interpretation. When the perspective is coherent, the avatar becomes not decoration but a way of viewing the world. Artist-based characters that expand into separate products and stories, such as BT21 and TinyTAN, pose the same question. The key is to preserve the perspective of the source artist’s emotions and image while moving it into an implementation as an independent character. If that connection breaks, the character may be cute but becomes a product unrelated to the world.

In MEJE’s production theory, implementation must be documented. If left to intuition, it wavers as the team grows. Writers, designers, video teams, merchandise teams, community managers, translators, and AI workers must be able to read the same standards. This is why the LOREBOOK and Vault are necessary.

## 5. When Perspective and Implementation Diverge

IPs usually fail to grow into fandom communities when perspective and implementation diverge. They may speak of a profound world without enabling people to experience it in actual content. Conversely, visual implementation may be spectacular while the perspective beneath it remains unknowable. In either case, fans find it difficult to stay.

Fandom detects this divergence quickly. Fans read recurring order more sensitively than they read official plans. They recognize when the voice changed, when a symbol suddenly disappeared, when a relationship shifted without explanation, and when a piece of merchandise does not fit the world’s sensibility. Fandom is the most sensitive reviewer of worldview.

In this sense, a fandom community is more than market response. It operates like collective intelligence that checks whether an IP’s perspective and implementation agree. This does not mean every fan reaction is correct, but discomfort felt by a fandom often contains an important signal. Operators must neither ignore that signal nor simply be dragged by it. They must read the fandom’s sensibility while holding a canonical standard.

Business is another domain in which perspective and implementation can diverge. A worldview may speak of relationship and belonging while actual operations feel like excessive pressure to purchase. It may speak of intimacy while community operations remain one-way announcements, or claim to respect fandom while trying to control fan interpretation. Such divergences weaken trust in the IP.

Worldview design is therefore not only an aesthetic and narrative problem, but also one of operational ethics. Perspective must be explicit, and how it is implemented at actual points of contact must be continuously reviewed. Worldview is not a declaration, but repeated practice.

## 6. Separating Perspective from Implementation

The distinction between perspective and implementation is practical because it supplies a standard for deciding what to preserve and what to change as an IP expands. Within the same IP, some elements belong to worldview, some to universe setting, and others to a character’s habits or the fandom’s insider language. Once these layers are separated, a new album or piece of merchandise can be judged as either a change in perspective or a variation in implementation.

MEJE’s process performs this separation through documents. It gathers traces of perspective, classifies them by layer, converts them into standards collaborators can read, and then translates them back into people and scenes. Chapter 1 introduced the names and roles of these processes; Part III will examine how each works, one chapter at a time. The point to confirm here is singular: perspective must be implemented, and a perspective that is never implemented cannot form a fandom community.

The distinction also matters in AI creation. AI can rapidly produce vast quantities of implementation—images, sentences, settings, and scenes. Standards are still needed to determine which implementations fit the worldview. In the AI era, a creator is no longer only a person who writes every sentence, but an architect who designs and reviews the relationship between perspective and implementation.

## 7. The Standard for a Fandom Community

For an IP to become a fandom community, fans must be able to feel the connection between perspective and implementation. They may not explain that connection theoretically, but they know it by feel. When an IP gives the sense that it speaks of this kind of world, shows it in this way, and invites fans in this language, fans remain longer.

Fandom communities grow through repeated implementation. When the same perspective recurs differently in songs, performances, videos, merchandise, messages, and community events, fans feel that the world is alive. Repetition becomes trust, not boredom. Fans do not want something wholly new every time. They want to confirm that the world they love is still continuing.

This is also where the subscription economy and fandom economy combine. Subscription presupposes repeated connection, so fans need reasons to enter every month, every week, and every day. Those reasons come not from quantity alone, but from the continuity of small signals that implement a perspective. Fans read, respond to, save, and share those signals with others.

Merchandise and commerce have the same structure. Merchandise is perspective implemented as an object. Merchandise that does not fit the worldview may still be a product, but is unlikely to remain in memory. Merchandise that interlocks precisely with the world’s perspective becomes evidence of belonging, and the object becomes part of the world.

## 8. Implementation Passes through Fandom Review

The relationship between perspective and implementation is not decided only in internal production meetings. Fandom continually reviews it. Fans remember the attitudes their favored IP has maintained. They quickly recognize which expressions belong to it, which scenes feel awkward, and which products do not fit the world’s order. Fan response is often not mere preference but a sensory review of worldview coherence.

This review is especially clear in IP based on real people and real activities, as in K-pop—in other words, real IP. Part IV will define real IP in detail. An idol project may attempt a new concept with every activity cycle, but fans do not reject change unconditionally. They look for the attitude that remains through change. They watch how accumulated relationships, stage images, members’ ways of speaking, and the distance established with fans continue into the new activity. If perspective is maintained, change is read as growth. If perspective breaks, change is read as rupture.

Platform environments make this review faster. In the past, fan response remained on fan cafés or message boards. Now it spreads immediately through short-form media, live comments, global translation accounts, community screenshots, and secondary edits. Fandom is not a group that passively awaits official content. It analyzes, compares, and interprets implementation, and sometimes generates pressure for revision. Those who operate a worldview cannot take this change lightly.

Fandom review must not, however, become the whole of production. Fandom supplies important sensibilities, but it does not design the IP’s perspective or long-term direction on behalf of its operators. Operators must read fan response while judging it alongside canonical standards. Reflecting every response immediately makes a worldview lose its center; ignoring every response makes it lose its relationship with fans. Managing this balance is the operation of perspective and implementation.

In MEJE terms, fandom response must enter the Vault, but it does not become canon unchanged. Responses become keywords, keywords acquire positions in a network of relationships, and some of them rise into LOREBOOK standards. Some remain fandom interpretations, some are absorbed into official setting, and others are recorded as warning signs. Worldview operation is the work of distinguishing among them.

## 9. Perspective and Implementation Create Business Trust

In fandom business, trust is not created merely by releasing high-quality products. Fans look not only at the quality of a product but at what it means inside the world. One photo card may feel like a random commodity, while another feels like an object preserving the memory of an activity. The difference lies not only in price or scarcity, but in whether the object belongs to the flow of perspective and implementation.

The same is true of subscription services. Fans maintain a subscription for more than content volume. They need to sense that the IP’s perspective continues to be implemented within it. The voice of messages, the nature of released behind-the-scenes material, the atmosphere of conversation permitted in the community, and the operator’s attitude toward fans are all implementation. The subscription economy has a structure of recurring payments, but inside fandom it is experienced as a structure of recurring relationships.

As models such as paid newsletters and creator memberships took root, one point became clear: people pay not for the volume of content, but for a perspective they can verify repeatedly. A medium that consistently supplies the same gaze builds a stronger subscription than one that merely supplies more of the same information. In the language of worldview, fans pay not for quantity but for worldview repeatedly confirmed, while implementation shows that worldview again in a different form each time. Yet both fail if density pursues only a niche and loses scale, or if scale blurs perspective. A healthy subscription designs for a balance between density and expandability.

The distinction between perspective and implementation also matters in commerce and licensing. When collaborating with an outside brand, expanding into a game or animation, or using AI to create new imagery, one must judge what belongs to the IP. Without that judgment, expansion may create short-term exposure but weaken long-term trust. Fandom wants to know how far an IP can expand, yet also hopes that expansion will not damage the world it loves.

Worldview in IP business is therefore not the language of the creative team alone. It is a standard that business, legal, design, platform, and community operations teams must all read. They must judge which collaborations are permissible, which should be avoided, which merchandise feels natural within the world, which looks like a mere promotion, which AI output expands the world, and which makes it indistinct.

When perspective and implementation have been organized, business becomes freer. Without standards, every expansion appears dangerous. With standards, knowing what must be preserved permits bolder transformation. A strong worldview does not freeze an IP; it clarifies the range within which transformation is possible and thereby enables expansion.

## 10. The Role of the Creative Architect

The distinction between perspective and implementation becomes more important in the AI era. AI rapidly generates implementation: images, synopses, dialogue, character settings, merchandise ideas, and drafts of fan messages can all be produced in a short time. Whether those results fit the worldview’s perspective is a separate question.

The more implementation AI produces, the clearer the creator’s role becomes. The creator is not the person who personally makes every output, but the person who establishes standards for the world’s perspective and implementation—who judges which results may enter this world, which must be excluded, which variations feel natural to fandom, and which feel alien. This is the role of the creative architect.

The creative architect holds the rights of design and validation over a worldview. Design authority determines what the world values; validation authority determines whether produced results fit that perspective. As AI increases production speed, these two authorities become more important, because incorrect implementation also spreads faster.

MEJE’s knowledge system supports this role. With a Vault and LOREBOOK, AI can be told which standards to follow. Character sheets make it possible to review a character’s voice and choices. Storytelling 100 helps verify the rhythm that everyday scenes in the world should have. The experiential axes of fandom activities help determine whether a generated service idea gives fans work or a relationship.

The important thing is not to stop AI, but to stop its use without standards. Once the relationship between perspective and implementation is organized, AI can become a tool for expanding worldview. Without that organization, it can become a tool for rapidly diluting it.

## 11. Conclusion

Worldview has two meanings: perspective and implementation. Perspective determines what a world values. Implementation is the way that perspective appears in music, video, characters, merchandise, communities, platforms, and fandom activities.

A fandom community emerges only when the two interlock. Implementation without perspective becomes decoration; perspective without implementation becomes a slogan. Fandom reads the world where the two meet, and a community forms when people who read the same world in the same way gather.

MEJE’s production theory turns this distinction into a concrete process. It finds scattered traces of perspective, separates them by layer, turns them into standards collaborators can read, and then translates them into people, scenes, and fan experience. What matters in this chapter is not the order of the process, but the fact that every stage exists to connect perspective and implementation.

The next chapter addresses genre. Genre is not merely a market category, but a grammar through which a worldview makes promises to readers and fans. When perspective and implementation pass through the grammar of genre, an IP gains a clearer entrance.

## Key Concepts

- Worldview: the perspective that determines what a world values and what it considers taboo.
- Universe setting: the structure in which that perspective is implemented through physics, society, people, aesthetics, platforms, and merchandise.
- Perspective and implementation: what a world considers important and the way that importance appears at actual points of contact.
- Coherence: the standard for checking whether implementations across many points of contact emerge from the same perspective.
- Creative architect: a designer who establishes standards for perspective and implementation and reviews generated outputs.
