# Chapter 6. The Everyday-Slice Scenario: The Character Writes Themself

In the previous chapter, we used five measures to extract a one-word identity. M became “the quiet witness,” and J “the idealist.” We noted that a single word reveals less than half of who the person is. This chapter dresses the empty half in prose for the first time.

We ask the character to write a short story: 1,500 to 2,000 Korean characters in the first-person protagonist point of view, a simulation in which the character takes the writer's seat and unfolds one slice of everyday life. Through this exercise, the half that could not be held by one word finally becomes visible in prose.

This is the largest validation tool in the process. It directly tests in prose whether a person defined by one seed line and one-word identity can actually move like someone alive.

## What the Everyday-Slice Scenario Is

Let us first define the form.

Virginia Woolf's *Mrs Dalloway* unfolds an entire life through a slice of one day in which a woman goes out to buy flowers before a party. Nothing monumental happens. As she walks the streets, remembers an old love, and hears the bells, Clarissa's entire inner life reveals itself. An ordinary fragment, rather than a major event, can prove a character. That is precisely what the everyday-slice scenario seeks.

> **Close-up · The regulars of Yaro Abe's *Midnight Diner*** The manga *Midnight Diner* is set in a small alleyway restaurant that opens at midnight. Each episode unfolds a fragment of one customer's life through a single “usual dish”: someone orders octopus-shaped Vienna sausages; someone else orders butter rice. Without a grand event, a whole person emerges from the everyday slice of what and how they eat. Each episode compresses this chapter's proposition—that an ordinary fragment, not an event, can prove a person—into the size of one plate of food.

**Everyday-slice scenario:** a short story depicting a fragment of a person's ordinary life in the first-person protagonist point of view. It is used here as a character-validation tool.

The form borrows from the first-person autobiographical fiction called 私小説 in Japanese and commonly rendered as *I-novel* in English-language scholarship. The nature of the borrowing is as follows.

The original I-novel is a current of modern Japanese literature in which writers confess private experiences in the first person. The writer characterizes themself and tells their own story. We adapt the form. Instead of the writer telling their own story, we simulate **the character under construction taking the writer's seat and describing their own everyday life in the first person**.

The output is one short story of 1,500 to 2,000 Korean characters. It is a validation artifact, not part of the main work, and does not enter the published story directly.

## How It Differs from Adjacent Forms

We should distinguish this form from other kinds of short prose.

Our everyday-slice scenario uses a first-person protagonist point of view, makes the character the narrator, and runs 1,500–2,000 Korean characters. Slice-of-life writing may freely use first or third person and often gives greater weight to outside observation, so it does not necessarily meet our validation conditions. An opening scenario is the beginning of the work, not a validation tool. A character monologue is a compressed version of the everyday slice and can substitute when time is short. A vignette is adjacent to the form, but may have weak causal movement.

**Why use an everyday-slice scenario?** Because it exposes the character's inner operation most directly. Slice-of-life writing can emphasize outside observation; an opening scenario is the first scene of a work rather than a character test; a short monologue may not contain enough causal movement; and a vignette can be vivid while remaining causally weak. Our form requires first person, the character as narrator, and the causal chain of one event at the same time.

At this stage, the character's tone, manner of speech, and place in the world are captured together. The exercise tests whether the person abstracted through five measures survives in actual prose. Failure is a signal to revise the results of those measures.

## Twenty-Five “I Remember / I Don't Remember” Sentences

Before drafting, take out one tool: write twenty-five short sentences while inhabiting the character. They become the raw material of the story.

Fifteen begin with “the character remembers.” Ten begin with “the character does not remember.” Arrange twenty-five short, concise sentences.

```
M remembers the color of her mother's wedding ring.
M remembers where the ring her mother wore fell when M was seven.
M remembers her wrists trembling the first time she stood before an autopsy table.
M does not remember her mother's last words.
M does not remember where the scent of baby powder first came from.
```

Only five are shown here, but the full list contains twenty-five. This is a task for which AI is particularly useful. Enter the seed and results of the five measures, and an AI work partner can produce a first set quickly. The producer selects the twenty-five most compelling candidates or rewrites one or two by hand.

Keep two principles in mind.

- Write complete sentences, not noun phrases such as “the color of Mother's wedding ring.”
- Choose words and expressions carefully. Each sentence is short, but should contain concrete nouns, numbers, or proper names.

These twenty-five sentences are the story's materials. One or two may become its first line, while others enter naturally at different points in the body.

Lists of sentences beginning “I remember” are also a literary technique. American artist and writer Joe Brainard composed *I Remember* from hundreds of such sentences, using them to portray both an era and a person. French writer Georges Perec took up the form in *I Remember*. The underlying idea is that a list of memories can itself become a human being.

## Five Candidates for the First Sentence

Next, choose the story's first sentence. Because that sentence determines the tone of the whole piece, do not settle immediately on one; select from five candidates.

The five patterns are as follows. The direct-event pattern takes the form “That day, [event] happened,” as in “That day, I saw my mother's ring again.” The mid-event description begins inside the event: “The ring rested calmly on the corpse's left index finger.” The aftermath description starts from the landscape after the event: “A single plastic bag lay on the autopsy table.” The causal-conjunction pattern begins with “So” or “Because”: “So Mother had been wearing the ring from the beginning.” The “I remember” pattern takes one of the twenty-five sentences directly: “M remembers the color of her mother's wedding ring.”

All five grasp the same event from different positions. The position changes the story's tone: direct event produces a quick tempo; mid-event description a slow one; aftermath description a retrospective tone; causal conjunction a speculative tone; and “I remember” a stream of consciousness.

Generating five candidates is an AI-assisted task. Provide the twenty-five sentences and seed line, and the candidates can be produced quickly. Selecting the one that draws you most strongly is the producer's work.

## Combining Reference Authors

Immediately before drafting, decide one more thing: a **combination of reference authors**. Pair one non-Korean writer with one Korean writer to establish the story's tone in advance.

The quickest way to turn a character from a “type” into an “individual” is to use concrete nouns. Raymond Carver's minimalist stories specify a character's occupation, neighborhood, and model of car, so readers see not “a generic worker” but this particular person. One exact car model brings a person into focus more clearly than ten abstract adjectives. It performs the same function as one prop in a comic or one costume in a film.

```
M's reference authors: Cormac McCarthy + Kim Hoon
“Dry labor and restraint with body heat: a tone suited to the hands of a forensic pathologist with thirty years of experience.”

J's reference authors: Kafka + Oh Jung-hee
“Bureaucratic absurdity + inner restraint: a tone suited to a writer trapped in self-doubt.”
```

The fastest method is to prepare around eight combinations and choose the one suited to the seed.

Cormac McCarthy + Kim Hoon provides dry labor and warm restraint, suitable for physical work, survival, and death. Ted Chiang + Oh Jung-hee combines technical precision and inner sensation for science fiction and occupational routine. Kafka + Kim Ae-ran combines bureaucratic absurdity and existential weight for systems, migration, and identity. Borges + Sung Sok-ze combines logical labyrinths with black humor for paradoxes and systems. Le Guin + Eun Hee-kyung joins calm observation with structural critique for species, classification, and identity. Bukowski + Cho Se-hui uses affectless anger and the materiality of labor for factories and class. Orwell + Pyun Hye-young joins institutional control with inner fracture for surveillance and discipline. Calvino + Park Min-gyu combines formal experiment and pop sensibility for metafiction and frame narratives.

Selecting the combination is the producer's direct work. Determining which pair fits the seed's tone is an area in which external tools perform poorly.

Once selected, summarize the style parameters line by line.

```
M's style parameters:
- Sentence length: short (8–15 Korean word units). Minimal conjunctions.
- Figurative method: primarily metonymy. Restrain figurative language and substitute material properties.
- Sensory priority: touch > sight > hearing.
- Dialogue ratio: 15–25%. Brief, dry dialogue.
- Emotional expression: do not name emotions. Use only action and materiality.
- Narrative speed: slow. Give one action 3–5 sentences.
```

These parameters become the first drafting guide.

**Metonymy:** an expression that names something through another thing touching or adjacent to it. In “the White House announced,” the White House stands for the administration. Unlike metaphor (“life is a voyage”), which depends on resemblance, metonymy depends on contiguity. It is especially useful when showing emotion through a nearby object rather than naming it directly. In M's story below, the “plastic bag folded twice” stands in for grief without using the word *grief*.

## Drafting the 1,500-Character Story

Now write one short story of 1,500 to 2,000 Korean characters. The point of view is first-person protagonist; the narrator is the character themself.

Drafting procedure:

1. Select one of the five first-sentence candidates.
2. Choose five to eight of the twenty-five sentences for insertion into the body.
3. Decide the story's event—one event with a beginning and end inside the piece.
4. Obtain a first draft. Supply the seed, five measures, twenty-five sentences, first sentence, author combination, and style parameters. An AI work partner can produce the first 1,500-character draft quickly.
5. Review the draft. Correct points inconsistent with the character's tone, deviations from the style parameters, and weak causal movement.
6. Set the acceptance threshold. Adopt a passing draft; request or write another if it fails.

This is the most time-consuming part of the stage. The first draft arrives quickly with an AI work partner, but review and correction take longer. Every line must be checked for the character's tone, so the work cannot be rushed.

## Four Additional Techniques

Four additional techniques apply to the short story.

Franz Kafka's *The Metamorphosis* begins with a sentence in which Gregor Samsa wakes from troubled dreams to find himself transformed in bed into an enormous insect. Without elaborate explanation, one shocking detail planted in the everyday slice of a person's morning overturns the entire world. One well-chosen detail can become the wedge that holds a world in place.

**Dialogue-ratio guide.** Twenty to forty percent of the body is an appropriate range. Above forty percent, insert action, movement, and sensory scenes. Below ten percent, the work risks becoming a monologic essay. One more rule: no dialogue that exists only to deliver information. Every exchange must reveal relationship, conflict, or character.

**Landing the ending.** Finish the final paragraph with a sensory, concrete image. Abstract declarations (“That was true freedom”), summarized realizations (“Now I understood that…”), thematic summaries, and named emotions (“I was sad”) are all forbidden. Six passing patterns are sensory afterimage, shift in action, contrast of material properties, return to the anchor, sensation of absence, and unresolved object.

**Dramatizing numbers.** Figures in the story—time, distance, cost, probability, weight—must appear as grounds for action or judgment, not explanation. Reject “M spends an average of forty-seven minutes per corpse at the autopsy table.” Accept “I finished one corpse in forty-seven minutes. It was the seventh that morning.”

**Drafting principle.** Let the character's texture permeate the prose. Let the character's choice determine the ending. Do not describe merely to fill space. Favor compressed beauty.

These four techniques become review items. If the first draft violates one or two, correct those portions.

## Example: An Extract from M's Story

Let us see the tone of M's story. The complete 1,500-character piece is too long, so only the opening and closing paragraphs are shown.

> That day, I saw my mother's ring again. It rested calmly on the corpse's left index finger. Thirty years. I was not the only one with calluses inside the two joints of the index finger. The corpse had the same callus in the same place. The fluorescent light above the autopsy table struck both calluses at the same angle.
>
> [omitted]
>
> I put the ring in a plastic bag. One corner of the bag was torn. I took out a new one. I put the ring in the new bag. I folded its mouth twice. Twice preserves a thing. Once is not enough and three times is excessive. Twice is exact.

Even this extract reveals M's tone: short sentences, metonymy, priority given to touch, no named emotions, an unresolved object in the last paragraph, and dramatized numbers. The Cormac McCarthy + Kim Hoon combination is alive in it.

## Example: An Extract from J's Story

J's story has a different tone.

> A long strip of paint had peeled away near the left side of the carousel horse's head. J does not remember what color it had been. J is certain they touched that place throughout the ride. The color of a place one has touched leaves behind only the sensation of touching it. A sensation without color.
>
> [omitted]
>
> J sat at the desk and stared at their wrist for a long time. There was nothing on it. There was no place whose color J could not remember, as with the carousel's paint. What, then, had J lost? Is it possible to lose something without a place where it was lost?

The Kafka + Oh Jung-hee combination is alive here: abstract speculation, reversal of self-consciousness, and an absurd question. Its tone differs from M's.

## Example: An Extract from E's Story

E's story has still another tone. E is a contract killer in an imaginary war setting; the prose deals with violence in dry, emotionally restrained language.

> It rained. When the burn on my left shoulder flared, I pressed the place with my palm. Pressing did not make it disappear. It only moved the pain. I had long been in the business of moving things that did not disappear.
>
> [omitted]
>
> A child sat in front of the target's house. About seven years old. I decided to go around and choose another day. I had been taught that only the weak make such decisions. And so today, too, I was weak. Rainwater ran over the burn on my shoulder.

E's physical anchor—the burn on the left shoulder—and concealed mercy permeate the extract together. The single action of turning away from the target reverses the seed “hide strength by pretending weakness.” Its texture differs from M's restraint and J's speculation.

The same process unfolds three characters into stories with distinct tones. This difference demonstrates that one seed line and the five measures produce different prose for different people.

## Seven Review Questions

When the story is complete, apply seven review questions. They verify again whether the story has adequately tested the character.

```
1. What influence did the character exert on the event's outcome?
2. Which aspect of the character led to a particular choice?
3. Which choice most strongly affected the event? What if the opposite choice had been made?
4. What if a close friend, rather than the character, had faced the same situation?
5. What motivated the character's action?
6. What was the character's greatest desire in the event?
7. Did the ending satisfy the desire and motive? If not, why not?
```

The questions can feel abstract on their own, so let us apply all seven to M's story.

```
1. M finds the ring on the corpse's hand and preserves it in a bag herself rather than following the procedure for transferring it to the family. M directly determines how the event is handled.
2. Thirty years of professional commitment to “preserving things exactly” leads to the choice to seal the ring with two folds.
3. The decisive choice is keeping it herself rather than reporting it according to regulation. If she had followed procedure, the story's tension would disappear.
4. A colleague in the same situation would have reported it immediately. The difference brings M's texture into relief.
5. Motive: the wish not to lose her mother's last trace.
6. Greatest desire: to hold absence in an exact form.
7. Only half satisfied. The ring is preserved, but her mother's whereabouts remain unknown. The unresolved half becomes the engine of the next stage.
```

These seven answers flow into later stages—Stage 7 flaws, deficiencies, and trauma, and Stage 8 goals. The short story does not end with this stage; it also produces materials for the next ones.

## Moving to the Next Chapter

This chapter can be summarized as follows. Ask a person defined by a seed and five measures to write one 1,500-character story, and their tone, manner of speech, and place in the world emerge together. AI is strong at first candidates for the twenty-five sentences, five first sentences, and the 1,500-character draft. The producer is strong at selecting the reference-author combination, reviewing the draft, judging the acceptance threshold, and answering the seven questions.

The first three of the twelve stages—seed, mirror, and everyday slice—are now complete. The character has undergone one compression. Beginning in the next chapter, we add flesh to what has been compressed.
