# Chapter 7. Twisting and Drama: Turning Clichés Inside Out, and the Physical Anchor

In the last chapter, we tested the character once in prose through an everyday-slice scenario. M's short story was built from short sentences and metonymy, while J's was built from speculation and reversals of self-consciousness. The fact that the two stories emerged in different tones from the same procedure was a signal that this process is working.

From this chapter onward, we enter the stage of making a character dimensional. If the first three stages compressed a person once, the next three put flesh on that compression. Today we begin with twisting and drama. Turning clichés inside out and the physical anchor are the two core tools.

## If You Want a Fresh Character, Start with Cliché

First, let us address cliché itself.

People often say that they want to create a fresh character. The impulse is understandable. But to create a fresh character, you first need to know what a cliché is. If you do not know the cliché, you cannot twist it; a twist made unknowingly simply becomes another cliché. The goal of this stage is not the denial of cliché, but its conscious operation.

The Joker in *The Dark Knight* turns head-on the old cliché that “a villain has a reason for being that way.” Every time he meets someone, he invents a different origin for his scars, and in the end offers no motive at all. Being motive-less chaos itself makes him unforgettable. It is a case in which knowing exactly what the cliché was made it possible to push all the way to its opposite. (Christopher Nolan) Rorschach in Alan Moore's *Watchmen* follows the same path. Knowing the superhero cliché of the “vigilante for justice,” he pushes it to the end and turns it into a fanatical extremist incapable of compromise.

We deliberately place a character on the coordinates of cliché. On those coordinates, we check which parts fit the person and which do not. We deliberately turn some parts inside out. This stage is the work of balancing the stability of cliché—the parts a reader accepts familiarly—with the freshness of the twist.

## A Six-Factor Stereotype Catalog

Cliché operates through six factors. Occupation (“writers are sensitive to spelling errors,” “doctors are cold”); age (“older people find gym exercise difficult,” “children cause trouble”); race (“Asians have slanted eyes,” “Black people are athletically gifted”); gender (“women do housework,” “men have short eyelashes”); region or group (“people from Gyeongsang Province support conservative parties,” “Indians are good at mathematics”); and family relationship (“only children are selfish,” “people raised without parents have no manners”).

All six factors operate on every person. Once a person's occupation, age, race, gender, region, and family relationship are set, a bundle of stereotypes automatically attaches to them. That bundle is the coordinate system of cliché.

Procedure:

1. Write down the character's six factors.
2. Write two or three stereotypes for each factor. This is a part that can be done with an AI work partner. Give it the seed and the results of the five measures, and it can quickly produce a first catalog of six factors times two or three stereotypes.
3. Mark how closely the person matches each stereotype in three levels: ○ / △ / ✕. This marking is the producer's direct work.

## The 6 × 3 Deconstruction Matrix

When the marking is finished, one matrix emerges.

M's six-factor matrix reads as follows. For occupation (forensic pathologist): calm before a corpse ○, indifference to private life ✕, death normalized in daily life ○. For age (a fifty-five-year-old woman): family first ✕, weakened social ambition ○, no concern for appearance ○. For race (Korean): awareness of occupational hierarchy △, family-centered ✕. For gender (woman): gentle communication ○ (outwardly) / ✕ (inwardly), rich emotional expression ✕. For region (Seoul): fast tempo ○, preference for anonymity ○. For family relationship (only child, mother absent): self-centered ✕, strong-willed ○.

This is how M's 6 × 3 matrix is composed: seven ○ marks, one △ mark, and four ✕ marks. That distribution reveals M's texture.

What the marks mean:

- **○ (70–100% match):** cliché. The part readers accept familiarly. This becomes a candidate for twisting.
- **△ (40–70% match):** the point at which stability and freshness balance. Leave this part as it is.
- **✕ (0–30% match):** reversal point. This is the part that makes the character appealing.

M has four ✕ marks in the matrix: indifference to the private sphere (M becomes lost at a supermarket self-checkout), family first (M has no family), family-centeredness (M's mother is absent), and self-centeredness (M has been beside a colleague for thirty years without knowing the colleague's first name). These four are M's points of appeal.

Building such a matrix means receiving a first assessment together with an AI work partner, then having the producer make the adoption.

## Universality and Particularity of Species, Nation, and Culture

The six factors of the twisting matrix are strong for contemporary, realistic works. Fantasy, science fiction, historical, and multicultural works, however, need one additional tool: consciously deciding the ratio of universality and particularity in species, nation, and culture.

There is another attitude toward cliché. In his postscript to *The Name of the Rose*, Umberto Eco wrote that postmodernism does not reject cliché, but rewrites it knowingly in quotation marks—in other words, through irony. By deliberately bringing back an expression one knows is stale and twisting it once, one can create familiarity and novelty together. That is what the twisting matrix aims for.

### Background

One thing often goes wrong when shaping fantasy or science-fiction characters. If a person belongs to a species such as Hobbits, Elves, or Dwarves and follows all the species' universal traits, they flatten into a sample of the species. If they depart from all of them, the reader can no longer tell what species they belong to. The ratio between the two areas is the key.

The same tool applies to historical and multicultural works. With a nineteenth-century British aristocrat or a twenty-first-century Korean office worker, the crucial appeal lies in how far they preserve the universality of the society and culture they belong to, and where they depart from it.

### Rationale for Adoption

For an ordinary contemporary drama set in Korea, the six-factor matrix is enough. But if a work includes a character from a distinctive species, period, or cultural sphere, one needs to work consciously with the fact that the character's appeal comes from **adjusting the ratio between the parts that preserve the group's universality and the parts that depart from it**.

### Expected Effect

After this tool, a species or cultural character neither flattens nor scatters without identity. Universality lets the reader receive the person as one member of a species or culture; particularity creates the person's unique appeal.

The form works as follows.

Take Hobbits, whose common traits are dislike of fighting, love of food, and indifference to knowledge. A character whose love of food matches the type (○), but who departs by enjoying fighting, gains an axis of appeal from that pair.

The appeal of the Hobbit character comes from the pair of loving food (preserved universality) and enjoying fighting (departing particularity). A Hobbit who dislikes food ceases to feel like a Hobbit; a Hobbit who preserves every universal trait cannot be distinguished from other Hobbits.

This work needs only a short table beside the six-factor matrix. We recommend consciously switching it on for characters in fantasy, science fiction, historical fiction, and migration narratives.

## Five Occupational Twists

There is a tool that twists cliché more actively: try dressing the same disposition in five different occupations.

The Bride in Quentin Tarantino's *Kill Bill* begins with the familiar cliché of a “female warrior on a quest for revenge.” But by placing her motherhood toward the child taken from her, and her trembling at the moment she draws a sword, in the foreground, the work twists her into not an invincible action hero but one wounded person. Even with the same cliché, where vulnerability is planted produces a completely different person.

Procedure:

1. Summarize the character's core texture (one-word identity + the black-and-white seven axes) in one line.
2. Imagine five occupations a person with that texture might have—five different from the seed's original occupation.
3. For each occupation, write one line on how the character's texture collides with it and how it produces synergy.
4. Make a note of the one or two most interesting of the five. Even if the seed's original occupation is retained, that note becomes material for ideas in later stages.

This is a part that is strong when done with an AI work partner. Give it the texture as input, and it quickly produces five occupational candidates and each occupation's synergies and conflicts.

## Example: Twisting E

There is one more person who demonstrates twisting well. This person returns in the latter half of the lecture, so let us establish the name in advance. We will call them **E**.

E's seed is as follows.

> E, a contract killer in a fictional war setting. At age seven, E witnessed C, who had been like a sibling, being killed by enemy forces; since then, E disguises weakness and hides strength.

E's one-word identity is “cold survivor.” E is Slytherin, and the MBTI type is ISTP.

When E is placed on the twisting matrix, occupational cliché works most strongly: the clichés of a contract killer—coldness, lack of trust, separation between occupational and private identity, and moral grayness. These clichés match E's seed almost 100 percent.

Here we attempt a twist. Keep E's texture (one-word identity and black-and-white seven axes) intact and change only the occupation five ways.

```
E's texture: “cold survivor.” A person precise at work their hands know well. Avoids private relationships. Trauma at age seven.

Five occupational candidates:
1. Major-crimes detective: synergy: precision in work their hands know. Conflict: the texture of moral grayness weakens.
2. Watch repairer: synergy: precision. Conflict: external threat disappears.
3. Surgeon: synergy: precision, moral grayness. Conflict: social standing changes.
4. Elementary-school teacher: synergy: the reverse of private avoidance, meeting small humans every day. Conflict: violence disappears.
5. Slaughterhouse worker: synergy: death normalized in daily life. Conflict: self-control weakens.
```

Of the five candidates, number 4 is the most interesting. What happens if a person with a rough mouth and trauma at age seven is deliberately changed into an elementary-school teacher? This twist may make E richer than leaving E as the contract killer in the seed.

Whether to adopt the twist is the producer's decision. Even if the original occupation in the seed is retained, the note for candidate 4 will recur in later stages. The hypothetical “What if E were an elementary-school teacher rather than a contract killer?” offers a different view when designing E's flaws, trauma, and conflict.

Even if the writer first accepts the contract-killer cliché and decides on the seed, the twisting stage requires them to go deliberately in the opposite direction once. What becomes clearer after going the other way is the seed's cliché, and at the same time, how that cliché should be twisted.

## Action and Motivation: Show, Don't Tell

Once twisting is complete, we begin putting flesh on the character in earnest. The first tool is action and motivation.

The weakest way to express a character's texture is to pile up adjectives: “M is a cold person.” “E is indifferent.” “J is compulsive.” Such bundles of adjectives have low information density and convey nothing to the reader. Show the same texture through action, and the information density increases roughly fivefold.

“Show, Don't Tell” is a principle shared by almost every narrative medium. Instead of a subtitle saying “He was lonely,” film shows one set of cutlery laid alone on an empty table. Instead of writing “She was angry,” comics draw a clenched fist and effect lines breaking beyond the panel. A small hand gesture on a theatre stage or a discarded diary page in a game does the same work. Explanation reaches the head; showing reaches the body.

Procedure:

1. Recall the character's core texture (one-word identity).
2. Write five actions that show that texture.
3. Assume one situation, and write five actions and five lines the character would take in that situation.
4. Write the character's single most immoral action and its motivation.

The five actions must be short and concrete.

```
M's five actions:
1. When touching a corpse's finger on the autopsy table, she checks its callused area once with the callus on her own index finger.
2. If a colleague brings a new autopsy kit, she rearranges it up to five times.
3. Before a corpse's family, she answers in short replies and leaves quickly.
4. At dawn on the anniversary of her mother's death each year, she simply stands for an hour before the autopsy table.
5. She does not touch a corpse that smells of baby powder. She asks a colleague to do it.
```

At a glance, the five actions show what kind of person M is: precise, repetitive, quick to cut off private relationships, ritualized in her fixation on her mother, and possessed of one trigger—baby powder.

Receive first candidates for these five actions with an AI work partner. The producer adopts the five that best suit the character.

The third task, “assume one situation and write five actions and five lines,” follows the same procedure. The input is the seed + five measures + five actions + an assumed situation. A first set of candidates emerges with an AI work partner, and the producer adopts it.

The fourth task—the most immoral action and its motivation—is important. A character needs a morally gray area in order to become dimensional. A person whose every action is moral flattens out. Decide in advance on the character's one most immoral action in the work, then summarize its motivation in one line.

```
M's most immoral action: On the day she finds her mother's wedding ring in a corpse's hand, she puts it in a plastic bag and leaves with it in her own bag. Procedure requires that it be handed to the bereaved family, but M puts it away without deciding who the ring's true bereaved family is.

Motivation: Her mother had been absent for thirty years; for thirty years she had not known her mother's last moments; that ring might have been her mother's last trace. She placed her mother's last trace before procedure.
```

This immoral action becomes material for Stage 7, flaws and trauma, and Stage 9, conflict.

## Physical Anchor: Fixing the Abstract in One Place

If action and motivation are the first tool for showing a character, the second is the physical anchor.

A physical anchor fixes an abstract character texture in the concrete sensation of one part of the body. Build it in five steps.

1. Decide the character's occupation (or retain the one already decided in the seed).
2. Find the body part used most in that occupation.
3. Decide the trace left there: a callus, scar, deformation, or pain.
4. Make that trace appear as sensation in the first line of the everyday-slice scenario.
5. Make the sensation vary with the situation throughout the I-novel.

A good anchor has four conditions. A microscopic coordinate names not simply “a hand,” but a precise location such as “two millimetres inside the second joint of the left index finger,” one point of occupational wear. Sensory specificity uses touch, pain, temperature, or pressure rather than sight: a compressed spinal disc, the grain of a callus. Narrative function tells us the character's occupation, history, and state: a callus on an index finger that has held a scalpel for thirty years. Repeated variation repeats for three or more chapters but changes its meaning each time: the callus means self-identity in Act 1, correspondence with the corpse in Act 3, and letting go in Act 5.

M's physical anchor:

> “The callus two joints inside M's left index finger. The place where she has held a scalpel for thirty years.”

This one line gathers M's texture in one place: thirty years of time, the tool of a scalpel, the trace of a callus, the microscopic coordinate inside an index finger—five pieces of information compressed into one line.

The anchor appears in the first line of the everyday-slice scenario (“I was not the only one with a callus embedded two joints inside the index finger,” from the previous chapter's excerpt). In the middle of the text, it meets the callus in the same place on a corpse and repeats for the second time; at the end of the short story, it varies its meaning a third time at the same place on the finger that puts the ring in an envelope.

A physical anchor is a different tool from one factor of appearance description. Appearance is visual and narrative; a physical anchor is sensory and repetitive. A character can have both.

Examples of fixing a person in one part of the body are everywhere. Captain Ahab in *Moby-Dick* carries his madness in the sound of the whale-bone prosthetic leg, replacing the leg lost to the white whale, striking the deck. In *Batman*, Two-Face's half-burned face becomes the self split in two. In *Oldboy*, Oh Dae-su traces the place where he was confined through his tongue's memory of eating only fried dumplings for fifteen years. One trace inscribed on the body compresses a person's entire history.

> **Close-up · Edward Elric in *Fullmetal Alchemist*** Edward's right arm and left leg are metal prostheses called “automail,” the price of failing to revive his dead mother. On cold days, the joints ache, and that pain renews his awareness of guilt and responsibility every day. The work clearly demonstrates this chapter's physical-anchor principle—fixing abstract guilt in one part of the body and varying it in every scene—through a boy's metal arm.

## Six Types of Good Anchors

There are six tested types of anchors: occupational wear (erased fingerprints on an index finger, calluses, traces of frostbite); implants and joins (an implant's tremor, a seam of attachment); pain and pressure (a compressed spine, stinging between salt-cracked fingers); body temperature and cold (the coldness of metal, four degrees in a storage room); pulse and vibration (a throbbing temple, vibration in a biological membrane); and skin reaction (blackened veins, cracked heels).

Map one of these types to the character. M has occupational wear (a callus on the index finger). E has pain and pressure (rainy-day pain in a burn on the left shoulder). J has skin reaction (a compulsion to wash until the hands turn red before writing). The three people have anchors of different types.

Deciding on a physical anchor is the producer's direct work. An AI work partner can offer candidates for each of the six types, but choosing the single part that simultaneously meets the four conditions—microscopic coordinate, sensory specificity, narrative function, and repeated variation—belongs to the producer.

## Backstory: Egri's Three Dimensions

Once action and motivation and the physical anchor have been decided, build the backstory next. This is the work of organizing in five to ten sentences how a character's past leads into the present.

The backstory must be balanced across all three dimensions.

The physiological dimension captures gender, age, height, appearance, health, and heredity. The social dimension captures origin, education, religion, political orientation, hobbies, nationality, and family. The psychological dimension captures personality, complexes, compulsions, trauma, aspiration, disillusionment, and moral code.

If one of the three dimensions dominates, the character flattens. If physiology alone is strong, only appearance description becomes thick. If society alone is strong, only background explanation grows long. If psychology alone is strong, abstraction becomes excessive. The three dimensions must balance one another.

Egri's three dimensions: a framework for character analysis proposed by playwright Lajos Egri in *The Art of Dramatic Writing*. Its idea is to view a person together through physiology (body), sociology (circumstance), and psychology (mind). If only one of the three is thick, the person leans to one side, so the framework serves as a checklist for seeing whether all three layers have been filled evenly.

A first five-to-ten-sentence backstory draft is a part to build with an AI work partner. Give it the seed, five measures, five actions, and physical anchor as input, and it will produce a first draft. The producer reviews it and corrects any imbalance among the three dimensions.

## Ability and Preference

Two brief tools remain: ability and preference.

**Ability** is one special talent the character possesses. It may be occupational, supernatural, or social. Decide on one, then organize its benefit and side effect.

```
M's ability: From only the callused area of a corpse's finger, she infers which tool that person held every day while alive.
Benefit: She does not miss traces of hand use, and checks one more clue in an autopsy record that colleagues may pass over.
Side effect: She cannot avoid being conscious of the callus on her own index finger. Every day she checks how her own callus differs from her mother's.
Possible situation: The day the callused area on a corpse's finger matches the callused area on her mother's finger—the seed incident.
```

An ability with only benefits is flat. It needs a side effect so that the ability contains the possibility of conflict. Generating first candidates for an ability is a part to do with an AI work partner; the one-line side effect is the producer's direct work.

**Preference and taste** organize the character's likes and dislikes in clothing, food, hobbies, colors, seasons, numbers, music, books, films, sports, and so on—one short line each.

The final line of preference is **reversal appeal**.

```
M's reversal appeal: Although she is a “quiet witness,” she attends a neighborhood singing class every Saturday afternoon. She only claps. She does not sing.
```

This one line adds a small contradiction to the character's texture. If every texture points in one direction, the person flattens; one small contradiction makes them dimensional.

## Stage 6, Identification: Fixing Name and Appearance

Once twisting and adding drama have established the person's interior and behavior, fix the name and appearance by which other people identify them. This is Stage 6, identification. M, J, and E, used in earlier stages, are working code names. When putting them into an actual work, replace them with names that suit the world and period, or make explicit why the initials themselves are retained.

There are four name strategies. The ordinary-anonymous type uses a common name so readers accept the person quickly. The initial type deliberately leaves some information empty. The nickname type uses a name arising from occupation, action, or relationship. The meaning-bearing type draws the work's theme from Chinese characters, etymology, myth, or foreign words. A name must not explain every meaning of a person in advance. A name is an identifying marker, not a summary that substitutes for the setting.

For appearance, emphasize one visual factor first and restrain the rest. Choose one of hairstyle, clothing, body type, one part of the face, or an object always carried. For M, a white shirt beneath autopsy clothing; for J, an unused yellow memo pad; for E, the posture of pressing the left shoulder on rainy days are candidates. One appearance factor identifies the person at first sight, while the physical anchor accumulates backstory and feeling through repetition.

Leave the identification record briefly, as follows.

```
Name strategy: ordinary-anonymous type / initial type / nickname type / meaning-bearing type
Name in the work:
One appearance factor:
Face and hair:
Body type and posture:
Clothing and possessions:
First impression of voice and manner of speech:
Physical anchor:
```

There are three review criteria. Does the name suit the world and period? Does the one appearance factor distinguish the person from other people? Does the physical anchor connect to action and backstory rather than serve as decoration? Once these three items pass, identification is complete. At Stage 12 output, this record becomes the name, appearance, and alias fields of the integrated sheet and the first-appearance scene.

## Next Chapter

Today's chapter can be summarized this way: operate the coordinates of cliché consciously, fix the character's texture in the sensation of one part of the body, then put flesh on it through action, backstory, ability, and preference.

The points strong with an AI work partner are the first assessment for the 6 × 3 matrix, five occupational candidates, first drafts of five actions and five lines, ability candidates, and a first backstory draft. The points the producer must decide directly are adopting the matrix, deciding on the twist, deciding the one physical-anchor location, deciding the immoral action and its motivation, and deciding the one-line reversal appeal.

As of today, six of the twelve stages are complete. M, E, and J have all passed once through the cliché matrix; their texture has been fixed in one bodily location; flesh has been added through backstory, ability, and preference; and they now have identification criteria for fixing name and appearance.

The next chapter is flaws, deficiencies, and trauma. This is where the greatest force that makes a character live and move is planted. We will also address the measure of moral grayness.
