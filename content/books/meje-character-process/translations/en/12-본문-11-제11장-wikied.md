# Chapter 11. Immersive Practice: Proust, Letters, Diaries, and Five-Act Change

By the last chapter, one person and the four people beside them were all aligned. Seed, mirror, everyday slice, twist, drama, identification, flaws, goals, conflict, and relationship network: ten of the twelve stages are complete.

This chapter goes deeply into that person, moving from a god’s-eye view to the viewpoint inside the person. It is the work of inhabiting a character to answer, write, and live as them, and the stage in this procedure where collaboration with an AI work partner is most pronounced.

We cover five tools: Proust’s twenty-two questions, character letters, character diaries, forms of address and speech, and five-act change in event order.

## Like a Deep Conversation Over a Cup of Tea

First, let us establish the stance.

The work so far has looked at the character from outside. Choosing a seed, abstracting through scales, building flaws, setting goals, designing conflict, and placing a cast: all of this has been the stage in which the writer objectifies the character.

This chapter is different. The writer enters the character. Become that person to answer twenty-two questions, write a letter to another person, write a diary, organize how forms of address change through time, and draw how the self changes along the five-act timeline.

This inhabitation is the richest part of the work. At the same time, it is the part that takes the longest. A first output appears quickly, but checking whether it truly matches the character’s texture takes still more time.

## Proust’s Twenty-Two Questions

The first tool is answering twenty-two questions while inhabiting the character. The format comes from a set of questions Marcel Proust once answered.

Sensory detail is the quickest passage into immersion. In *In Search of Lost Time*, Marcel Proust carries the narrator at once into childhood memory through the taste of a piece of madeleine soaked in tea. It is not a recollection summoned by the head: tongue and nose open the memory first. When inhabiting a person as well, a smell they take in or a texture they touch takes us inside them more quickly than abstract explanation.

```
01. Greatest achievement in life
02. On “perfect happiness”
03. Current state of mind and feeling
04. Favorite way to spend time
05. Most treasured possession
06. What or whom they truly love
07. Most noticeable characteristic
08. Happiest moment (when and where)
09. Most miserable moment (when and where)
10. Living person they dislike most
11. Living person they admire most
12. Greatest fear
13. Greatest luxury item
14. Greatest regret
15. Talent they wish to have
16. Place they wish to live
17. What they most hate about themself
18. What they most hate in others
19. What they value in a friend
20. Hero
21. When they lie
22. How they want to die
```

Answer these twenty-two questions while inhabiting the character. Each answer should be a complete sentence that preserves the character’s speech style and verbal habits.

Workflow:

1. Gather the character’s seed, five scales, flaws, goals, conflict, and cast results in one place.
2. Use those results as input to receive first answers to the twenty-two questions. With an AI work partner, the twenty-two answers arrive in a short time.
3. Review the first answers. Check each one for correspondence with the character’s tone, speech, and position in the world.
4. Rebuild answers that do not fit. A misfitting answer is where the character’s texture often shows most clearly, so spend time reviewing it.

You need not answer all twenty-two. When time is short, answer only the core six: **03 (current emotion), 09 (most miserable moment), 12 (greatest fear), 14 (greatest regret), 17 (self-hatred), and 22 (how they want to die).** These six answers reveal the character’s core texture.

## Example: M’s Core Six Proust Questions

Let us inhabit M and answer the core six.

```
03. Current state of mind and feeling: A plastic bag lies on the autopsy table. For thirty minutes, I have not been able to decide what should go inside it.

09. Most miserable moment: When my mother waved her hand for the last time when I was seven. That night, I learned that I was the only person who had seen that hand.

12. Greatest fear: Learning where my mother went. I am afraid that the thirty years I have lived without knowing will be nullified.

14. Greatest regret: Not holding my mother’s hand one more time that day. If I had known it was cold, I would have held it.

17. What I most hate about myself: Being precise before an autopsy table yet standing still at a supermarket self-checkout. The same hands behaving like different hands.

22. How I want to die: Before an autopsy table. In the posture in which I worked.
```

In these six answers, all of M’s texture gathers in one place. The review threshold is whether the tone feels as though the person in the seed answered directly while inhabited. When the tone is alive, the later tools—letter, diary, five-act change—also build naturally.

## Writing a Letter as the Character

The second tool is building a letter the character sends to another person. Four uses are common: confession of love, longing, accusation, and explanation.

One unsent letter can reveal an entire person. Celie in *The Color Purple* writes letters she cannot send to God and her sister and recovers her lost voice. Theodore in the film *Her* writes other people’s letters professionally, yet cannot send even one line of his own heart. Who a person cannot write to, what they cannot say, and why they cannot send it reveal their most vulnerable place. In Shunji Iwai’s *Love Letter*, a single letter sent to the dead reaches an unexpected namesake and slowly uncovers old feelings of the living and the dead.

Format:

```markdown
[Recipient: __, relationship to character: __]

To [recipient],

[First person of the character. Preserve speech style and verbal habits.
 300–800 characters.]

[Character’s name or form of address]
```

This format looks simple, but one letter reveals the relationship between two people at once. Let us build an unsent letter from M to her mother.

### Example: M’s Unsent Letter to Her Mother

```
Mother,

Today I saw your wedding ring on a corpse’s hand. It was your ring. The finger had the same callus on the inside of its second joint. Where are you, Mother?

I could not write this letter even once in thirty years. Not because I had no time, but because I thought that writing it would collapse the posture in which you were alive. I believed you were alive for thirty years. With that belief, I stood before the autopsy table every day. Remaining beside the dead supported the posture that you had not died.

Today that posture seemed to collapse. Yet the corpse was not you. Your ring was on another person’s finger. I still do not know where you are. Thirty years remain thirty years.

But, Mother, one thing is different today. I am no longer afraid to leave the unknown unknown. I think that today, for the first time, I accepted that in the place where I do not know where you are, the shape of your absence remains as it is.

I cannot send this letter. Today, for the first time, I write the letter that has had nowhere to go for thirty years.

From within absence,
M
```

One letter of about five hundred characters shows the texture of M’s change at once. The letter may not enter the work directly, or a part may be quoted in a scene. Either way, this stage verifies the character’s interior deeply.

Draft the letter with an AI work partner, then have the producer review it. If the first draft does not match the character’s tone, rebuild it. One letter is worth spending sufficient time on.

## Writing a Diary as the Character

The third tool is building one diary entry by the character.

In Hermann Hesse’s *Demian*, Sinclair unfolds his inner life in a first-person confession, drawing the reader into the center of his confusion. “The bird fights its way out of the egg” stays with us because we pass through that line together inside Sinclair’s mind. A first-person diary looking outward from inside a person is among the quickest passages into immersion.

> **Close-up · Rei Kiriyama in Chica Umino’s *March Comes in Like a Lion*** Rei, who became a professional shogi player at a young age, says little. Yet the manga unfolds his inner world through images: a room flooding with water, an endless sea, the feeling of ground giving way underfoot. Rather than dialogue, it lets us touch loneliness and anxiety through the landscape inside him. It realizes, panel by panel, the work of this chapter: move from a god’s-eye view into a person’s interior and become them for a while.

Diary form differs according to character.

An event-centered report form suits analytical, systematic characters. An emotion/psychology-centered form suits affective, contemplative characters. A form including drawings and diagrams suits visual characters. A stripped, concise form suits short, decisive characters.

An event-centered report form suits M’s diary: a tone fitting the hands of a medical examiner of thirty years. An emotion/psychology-centered form suits J’s diary: the consciousness stream of a writer.

One diary entry contains four elements.

```
1. Situational background: place and time
2. Main event
3. Feeling about the event
4. Description of nearby people who experienced the event together
```

Recommended length is 300–600 characters. Build about one to three diary entries for a period addressed in the work.

### Example: One Entry from M’s Diary (Dawn Before Her Mother’s Memorial Day)

```
[November 13, 2025, 03:22, Autopsy Department Office]

I stood before the autopsy table for an hour. One hour had passed since I put the ring in a plastic bag and placed it in my own bag. I knew where the ring was inside the bag, yet I did not touch it. If I touched it, I thought I would open the bag again.

The autopsy table was empty. I finished the seventh autopsy yesterday, and no body will arrive today. I looked at the empty table for an hour. The empty autopsy table was not emptiness. It was where a posture of thirty years had remained.

The colleague medical examiner opened the office door once, looked in, and closed it again. He asked nothing. That was how it had been for thirty years. The posture of not asking was the greatest trust he could give me. Only today, I wished he would ask. Yet not asking was more right. At the moment when I shook a posture of thirty years for the first time in thirty years, my colleague’s silence was the greatest support.

Tomorrow is my mother’s memorial day. It is the thirtieth. Yesterday I learned that the ring in my bag is not my mother’s. Still, it is the thirtieth memorial day. In the place of absence, the shape of absence remains as it is.
```

This diary shows one period of M’s work. It is built as an event-centered report, yet the texture of feeling remains alive through metonymy: the thirty-year posture, the ring in the bag, the empty autopsy table, the colleague’s silence. All are metonymy.

Diary writing, too, is a division of work: obtain a first draft with an AI work partner, then have the producer review it.

## Table of Changes in Forms of Address

The fourth tool organizes how a character’s forms of address change with time and relationship.

Forms of address change over time. Someone who called their mother “Mom” at seven may call her “Mother” thirty years later, and after an event may call her “that person.” This change shows the character’s time axis.

Format:

The table has four fields: person’s name, form of address in the early period, turning-point event, and form of address in the later period.

M’s table of changes in forms of address (summary): M calls her mother “Mom” before age seven; after the mother’s absence event this becomes “Mother,” then gradually silence, and “Mother” returns at the work’s end. She calls the colleague medical examiner “Professor” when they meet; after their first joint autopsy five years later, it remains “Professor” for thirty years. She calls the junior colleague “junior” on arrival; after teaching the first autopsy, this becomes “Professor’s junior.”

The turning point usually coincides with the core event of flaw and trauma. The turning point in M’s name for her mother is the mother’s absence event. After it, M’s “Mom” became “Mother.”

## Classification of Speech Styles

The tool paired with forms of address is speech style. Organize six types of speech a character uses by situation.

Formal (work/formality) uses formal endings. Informal (acquaintances) uses plain speech. Intimate (family/lovers) uses pet names and familiar endings. Self (monologue/diary) uses a stripped, concise style. In anger, the style shifts to profanity, short sentences, and informal speech. Before the vulnerable, it shifts to softness and honorific speech.

The Korean *eumseumche* is a style that ends sentences in nominal forms such as “-eum” or “-seum,” or removes endings. It is common in notes, diaries, and military-style reports. “Thirty-year posture. Will be nullified.” It strips away emotion and lays down only facts in short taps, making it suited to the monologue of a restrained character.

M’s speech types:

```
Formal (to colleagues in the autopsy department): Formal register. “Professor, today’s autopsies were number seven.”
Informal (prosecutor and junior): A mixture of plain and formal. “That corpse is still unidentified.”
Intimate (to mother, in memories outside the work): Pet name. “Mom, your hand was cold that day.”
Self (monologue/diary): Stripped form. “Thirty-year posture. Will be nullified.”
In anger: Almost absent. M’s anger is expressed as silence.
Before the vulnerable (bereaved family of corpses and junior): Gentle honorifics. “Please collect yourself and go slowly.”
```

The distribution of six types determines a character’s texture. M has clear formal, informal, intimate, and self types, and almost no anger: a posture of thirty years of restraint.

Receive a first draft of the table and speech types with an AI work partner, then have the producer adopt them. When writing the character’s dialogue in a work, these two textures are the passing line.

## Recording Slumps, Routines, and Dreams

Beyond the twenty-two questions, letter, and diary, three supporting tools verify a character’s interior: slump record, routine record, and dream record.

### Background

All three are sub-tools of established writing practice, gathered here for use together. The slump (a writer’s block) borrows from the posture of American writers recording their own blockage; routine borrows from the tradition of daily-life description in Japanese *shishōsetsu*; dream record borrows from Jungian psychology’s technique of active imagination.

### Purpose of Introduction

While the twenty-two questions, letter, and diary capture a slice of one moment and one event, the three tools capture the character’s time axis: the texture repeated every day (routine), the texture when blocked (slump), and the texture when consciousness breaks open (dream). When the three gather, we can see how one person lives in time outside the work.

### Expected Effect

Even if they do not appear directly in the work, they firmly establish the character’s texture. Do not apply them to every character. For protagonist-level people only, turn on one or two tools if time permits.

**Slump record.** When a character is blocked before work, relationship, or self, record that blockage in one paragraph (200–400 characters). M’s slump: “The night she put her mother’s wedding ring in her bag. An hour in which she could neither open nor close the bag.”

**Routine record.** Record five to seven daily repeated textures, one line each. M’s routine: wakes at 4:30 a.m.; arrives at the autopsy department at 7; aligns the autopsy kit five times; eats a packed lunch in the office (the same menu every day); leaves work at 19:00; reads one page of one book at 22:00 and sleeps.

**Dream record.** Record in one paragraph a dream the character often has, or a dream had once and never forgot. M’s dream: the callus on a corpse’s finger lies in exactly the same place as the callus on her own finger. It repeats two or three times a month for thirty years.

Place these three tools at the end of stage-11 work. After completing the twenty-two questions, letter, diary, forms of address, speech, and five-act change, pass through one or two more tools if time permits.

## Five-Act Change in Event Order

The fifth and final tool is the operation of a character’s time axis: five-act change.

If Want, Need, and Lie are a static structure, five-act change operates the time axis. It organizes how the character changes along the work’s timeline in five acts.

The idea of dividing a story into five pieces came from theater. Most of Shakespeare’s plays use a five-act structure, and the Roman poet Horace wrote early on that “a play should have five acts.” East Asian *kishōtenketsu* (four stages) and Japanese traditional performance’s *jo-ha-kyū* are kin. The rhythm of breaking a story into a few pieces to build and release tension is shared wisdom discovered separately in East and West.

Format:

The five-act table contains: Act 1 (introduction), the external seed situation and the inner state in which Lie is firm; Act 2 (rise), the start of external change and the first crack in Lie; Act 3 (peak), external change together with Lie ↔ Truth collision and the trauma-reenactment event; Act 4 (fall), external change together with the decision to abandon or reinforce Lie; Act 5 (resolution), the external result at the work’s end and an inner state of adopting Truth, fixing Lie, or ambivalence, plus the event that makes change visible.

M’s five-act change:

M’s five-act change: Act 1 opens with M, a medical examiner of thirty years, remaining beside corpses; the Lie, “If I do not know my mother’s last moments, she might still come back alive,” is firm; everyday autopsy work is the core event. In Act 2, at the quarter point, she finds a wedding ring on a corpse’s hand; Lie cracks for the first time; she puts the ring in her bag. In Act 3, at the halfway point, she reveals her posture to a colleague for the first time; Lie ↔ Truth collide; she remembers the mother’s absence event for the first time in thirty years. In Act 4, at the three-quarter point, the corpse is identified as someone other than her mother; she attempts to abandon Lie and decides how to face her procedural violation. In Act 5, at the work’s end, she accepts the shape of her mother’s absence as it is, adopts Truth, and makes a self-change of thirty years visible.

When five-act change is built on one page, the skeleton of the work’s time axis is in place. What event enters each act, and how acts shift into one another, can be seen at a glance.

Five-act change is another division of work: receive a first draft with an AI work partner and have the producer review it. Give the seed, flaws, goals, conflict, and cast as input, and a first five-act draft comes quickly. The producer checks that it matches the work’s tone.

## Operating the Five Acts: Three Arcs

How five-act change is built differs according to the chosen arc and emotional curve.

Vonnegut’s emotional curves appear distinctly in actual works. Chuck Noland in *Cast Away* draws a “man in a hole” curve—falling from good fortune into misfortune through the plane crash, then returning alive. Jack and Rose in *Titanic* draw a “boy meets girl” curve in which excitement and tragedy cross. Even stories of the same length give the audience entirely different emotional textures depending on the curve they take.

**Three arcs.** Positive Change abandons Lie, adopts Truth, and attains Need in Act 5. Flat possesses Truth from Act 1 and breaks the world’s Lie in Act 5. Negative Change reinforces Lie further in Act 5 and becomes tragedy. M is Positive (the texture of change), J is Flat (change in the world), and E is Negative (tragedy): the same procedure builds three fates.

In brief, E’s Negative arc has Lie firm and self-punishment through contract killing in Act 1; the seven-year-old hostage appears and Lie cracks first in Act 2; in Act 3, the order to kill the child collides with mercy in a trauma reenactment; Act 4 attempts to abandon Lie through refusing the order and breaking with the organization; Act 5 fails to save the child after all, and Lie is reinforced further as tragedy. Its form is the same as M’s five acts, but the ending is the opposite.

When arranging the event order of five acts, keeping in mind the classical tragic skeleton “tragic flaw → reversal of fortune → recognition” strengthens causality. If you wish to look further into that curve and tragedy theory, refer to the supporting-tool bundle at the end of this chapter.

## Going Deeper: Theoretical Foundations of Five Acts (Optional)

These are supporting tools to turn on only when you want to refine the five acts further.

**Vonnegut’s eight story shapes.**[^c11n1] They capture the visual shape of the time axis: Man in a Hole (good fortune → bad fortune → good fortune, the basic heroic pattern); Boy Meets Girl (repeated oscillation, romance); From Bad to Worse (descent to the end, tragedy); Which Way Is Up? (oscillation and ambiguity, absurdist drama); Creation Story (rise, discovery); Old Testament (rise → fall, the *Macbeth* pattern); New Testament (return after a fall, resurrection); Cinderella (fall then recovery). M follows Man in a Hole, E follows From Bad to Worse, and J follows Which Way Is Up?

**Aristotle’s three elements of tragedy.**[^c11n2] They are the oldest foundation of the five acts: hamartia (tragic flaw, the flaw/Lie of Act 1), peripeteia (reversal of fortune, the Act-3 peak), and anagnorisis (recognition, becoming conscious of Lie in Act 4). A tragic person experiences reversal through flaw and reaches recognition—Hamlet, Oedipus, Othello. If they reach recognition but cannot abandon Lie, the result is a fall arc (E’s work); if they go beyond recognition and abandon Lie, the result is a growth arc (M’s work).
