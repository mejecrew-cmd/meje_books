# Chapter 2. From Seed to Output: The Flow of Twelve Stages

The previous chapter established two stances: treat a character as a human being, and let the producer lead the work with an AI work partner. They are the two pillars of this lecture.

Today, we unfold the map of the path that follows from those stances: the flow of twelve stages. Practical work starts in the next chapter, but beginning with a map prevents fatigue on the road.

Start with the map in your hands.

## Input and Output

The input and output of this work are simple.

**Input = a handful of seed.** A seed is any starting point that makes one person come to mind: an externally supplied bundle of keywords—“this work needs a detective in her thirties carrying an unsolved case”—or five seed cards drawn by random shuffle. Chapter 4 covers the detailed format; for now, the seed is simply the starting point.

**Output = one integrated sheet for a person plus supporting artifacts.** The integrated sheet gathers that person's core on one page. Supporting artifacts include a 1,500-character story the character writes about themself, a letter sent or unsent to another person, a diary addressed to the self, answers to twenty-two deep questions, and a five-stage timeline of change. If the integrated sheet is a sword, the supporting artifacts are its hammer marks and tempering traces. The sword alone can be enough, but the traces let us remember how to strike it again in the next work.

Many fields retain production traces beside finished work. Animation studios preserve model sheets of expression and angle; game development uses a character bible for voice and history. A cartoonist's setting notebook and an actor's character-analysis memo serve the same purpose. The traces become the entry point when the character returns.

Twelve stages lie between input and output. They do not all take equal time; some take one hour, others two. Time is proportional to the stage's weight.

## The Twelve Stages in One Line Each

First, the names:

```
Stage 1: Seed decision
Stage 2: Self-objectification mirror
Stage 3: Everyday-slice scenario
Stage 4: Typing and cliché twisting
Stage 5: Adding drama
Stage 6: Identification
Stage 7: Flaws, deficiencies, and trauma
Stage 8: Goals
Stage 9: Conflict design
Stage 10: Relationship network
Stage 11: Immersion operation
Stage 12: Output standardization
```

They form four groups. The first group—Stages 1–3, seed, mirror, and everyday slice—starts and compresses a person once. The second—Stages 4–6, twisting, drama, identification—puts flesh on that compression. The third—Stages 7–9, flaws, goals, conflict—installs the engine that moves the person. The fourth—Stages 10–12, relationship, immersion, output—places the person among others and unfolds them into media.

## First Group: Compress Once (Stages 1–3)

The first three stages deliberately make a person **flat**. As the previous chapter noted, hands stop when we try to draw a fully dimensional person from the beginning. Here that stance becomes a procedure.

Kim Ji-young in Cho Nam-joo's *Kim Ji-young, Born 1982* begins with the almost demographic seed “an ordinary Korean woman born in 1982.” Its deliberate ordinariness lets many readers experience it as their own story. Keiko in Sayaka Murata's *Convenience Store Woman* similarly begins as “an ordinary woman who has worked part-time at a convenience store for many years,” then expands into a sharp question about normality.

> **Close-up · Eleanor in *The Good Place*** Eleanor begins as “a selfish, rude woman wrongly assigned to heaven after death,” a nearly caricature-flat seed. Across four seasons, her lies are exposed, she learns ethics, and repeatedly stakes herself for friends, building the right to belong. The series stretches across its length the direction of these twelve stages: build dimensionality layer by layer from a flat line.

**Stage 1: Seed decision.** Draw five of thirty seed cards, or organize external keywords. Attempt to bind five answers into one person and discard one or two that do not bind. The output is a **one-line compression**: “A forensic pathologist with thirty years of experience finds her mother's wedding ring on a corpse.” That line becomes the acceptance threshold for the next eleven stages.

**Stage 2: Self-objectification mirror.** Apply five measures to the person in that line: four letters from a personality-type test, four houses from a value classification, seven black/white sliders, thirty short questions, and a developmental-stage hierarchy. Each cuts one face of the person. At the end, extract a one-word identity: idealist, cold survivor, quiet witness. It reveals less than half the person by design; the next stage fills the blank half.

Nina in *Black Swan* begins as “a ballerina chasing perfection.” Look into the mirror, and a divided self pulled between the pure white swan and sensual black swan emerges. The mirror turns a vague line into a precise self-image. (Darren Aronofsky)

**Stage 3: Everyday-slice scenario.** Ask the character to write a 1,500-character first-person story about themself. It borrows the form of an I-novel: a simulation in which the character takes the writer's seat and unfolds one ordinary slice of life. Once complete, tone, manner of speech, and place in the world appear together. The compressed figure from Stages 1 and 2 tries on prose.

**I-novel (私小說):** a modern Japanese form in which a writer presents daily life and interiority almost directly in the first person. Here it means a simulation where the character writes their own day as if they were the author. What they explain at length or skip immediately reveals voice and gaze.

After these three stages, the skeleton is complete. That does not mean the person is finished; it means a foundation exists for adding flesh.

## Second Group: Add Flesh (Stages 4–6)

Here the person begins to become dimensional. A skeleton alone is not a body.

**Stage 4: Typing and cliché twisting.** Place the person deliberately on clichés formed by six factors—occupation, age, gender, race, region, family relation. Identify what fits and what does not, then reverse selected parts. Try placing the rough-mouthed homicide detective cliché in an elementary-school teacher, for example. The purpose is not to reject cliché but to balance its reassuring familiarity with the freshness of its twist.

**Cliché:** a setting, scene, or character type made familiar through excessive use, such as “a terminally ill heroine” or “a detective burning for revenge.” Cliché is not inherently bad; readers gain rapid stability from it. Skill lies in deciding what remains and what twists.

Examples of characters made fresh by twisting one familiar frame:

- Princess Fiona in *Shrek* reverses the fairy-tale expectation that a curse ending makes her beautiful, choosing to remain an ogre.
- Rocket in *Guardians of the Galaxy* adds self-loathing and anger to the cute-animal-mascot cliché, becoming funny and painful at once.
- Eleven in *Stranger Things* adds a love of waffles and awkward everyday speech to the secret-laboratory superpowered-girl frame.

**Stage 5: Adding drama.** Add flesh in five areas: behavior, motivation, backstory, talent, and preference. Its central tool is the physical anchor: fixing abstract character texture to sensation in one bodily location. A callus on the index finger from thirty years holding a scalpel, a shoulder burn that flares on rainy days, compulsive washing until hands turn red before writing—one sensation contains occupation, history, and deficiency together. Do not describe the body head to toe; compress through one meaningful part.

**Stage 6: Identification.** Give a name, decide one appearance factor, and organize five bodily elements. Four naming strategies are universal anonymity (Park, Kim Ji-young), initials (A, P, B), nickname (Zeze, Goblin Hunter), and meaningful naming (borrowing Sino-Korean or foreign-language etymology). The choice determines the clothing placed on the person. Emphasize one appearance factor and restrain the other four; that factor must connect to drama and gain meaning somewhere in the work.

After these stages, the person has flesh but still does not move. The next group installs the engine.

## Third Group: Install the Engine (Stages 7–9)

Flaws, goals, and conflict make a person move. Without them, even a well-made person remains in place.

Tony Stark in *Iron Man* shows flaw and ability sharing one root. His arrogance drives him into crisis, but that same limitless confidence lets him build the first suit while trapped in a cave. A well-made flaw is not mere weakness; it rises from the same spring as strength.

**Stage 7: Flaws, deficiencies, and trauma.** Organize flaws along four axes—desire, belief, moral code, values—and trace them through a trauma–deficiency chain: “Because of [trauma], this person acquired [deficiency], and to conceal it developed [flaw].” Trauma may be a major event or ordinary damage to self-esteem. Additional measures for morally gray characters, villains, and antiheroes are also called here.

**Stage 8: Goals.** Write three lines: what the person consciously wants (external goal), truly needs (internal goal), and falsely believes (Lie). Their misalignment becomes the engine. A writer may consciously want a bestseller, truly need to retain pleasure in writing, and believe “a result has value only if it is entertaining.”

Joe Gardner in *Soul* consciously wants to play at a jazz club but truly needs to live ordinary days fully rather than attain one great stage. The gap moves him throughout the film. (Pete Docter)

**Stage 9: Conflict design.** Separate external and internal conflict, map the person to three of eight conflict types, and inspect what may be lost across five layers: physical, occupational, identity, relationship, existential. Strong conflict usually places three or more layers at stake. Then inspect the fun engine: relationship drive, world-mechanic wonder, logical contradiction, time-limit tension, or expectation-reversing turn. At least two engines should run.

The person now moves, but movement gains meaning only among other people. The last group provides that placement.

## Fourth Group: Place Among Others and Unfold into Media (Stages 10–12)

The final three stages place a person among others, enter the person's interior deeply, and unfold them into five media.

Christopher Vogler's *The Writer's Journey*, borrowing Joseph Campbell, lists seven archetypes: hero, mentor, threshold guardian, herald, shapeshifter, shadow, trickster. Luke Skywalker, Obi-Wan Kenobi, and Han Solo in *Star Wars* illustrate them. They provide a useful map for placing a cast.

**Stage 10: Relationship network.** Put the character at the center and place five others—family, colleague, rival, lover, antagonist, or any combination. Do not merely list them; distribute them through a five-axis matrix: four personality quadrants, four value houses, three directions of change, what each stakes, and dominant emotion. If any axis is not distributed, identification and conflict weaken.

**Stage 11: Immersion operation.** Inhabit the character to answer twenty-two deep questions, write a letter to another person and a diary to the self, organize address-change and speech types, and draw a five-act transformation along time. This takes the longest. Prose clothing thickens once more.

**Stage 12: Output standardization.** Unfold the person into five media: the integrated-library standard sheet; a TRPG/game-NPC sheet; a one-page character bible for film/drama; a visual package for animation/comics; and a compressed sheet for web fiction. One full build produces five different media outfits.

At the end, the person exists as a sufficiently living being: integrated sheet, story, letter, diary, twenty-two answers, five-act change, and five-media outputs gathered in one folder. The folder is the door through which they return in the next work.

## Time Budget

Total time by tier assumes AI collaboration; hand-only work takes two or three times longer. A protagonist receives 8–15 hours for all twelve stages and five-media output. A major character receives 4–7 hours with some abbreviation. A supporting character receives 1–2 hours for roughly Stages 1, 2, 5, 6, and 12. A minor character receives 30–60 minutes for Stages 1, 6, and 12: name, one appearance factor, one line.

For a protagonist, stage budgets are: Stage 1 seed 10–30 minutes; Stage 2 mirror 30–60; Stage 3 everyday slice 60–120; Stage 4 typing/twisting 30–60; Stage 5 drama 70–100; Stage 6 identification 20–40; Stage 7 flaws/deficiencies/trauma 40–60; Stage 8 goals 30–45; Stage 9 conflict 40–60; Stage 10 relationship network 40–70; Stage 11 immersion 80–140; Stage 12 output 25–40; validation (five diagnostics and redesign) 25–45. Stage 11 is longest because of reviewing questions, letters, diaries, and five acts. Stages 1, 6, and 12 are shortest: one-line compression, naming, and format conversion.

## Thirty-Minute Entry Guide

Alongside full build is a shortened route producing a first sheet in 30 minutes to 3 hours. Its seven steps are: one-line seed from one card and external keyword; partial mirror using MBTI dominant/subordinate function and three black/white axes; a 1,500-character everyday-slice story; one flaw and one-line trauma chain; Want/Need/Lie; six core Proust questions; and integrated-sheet plus one or two media outputs. In thirty minutes, do only 1, 2, 6, and 7; in one hour, cover 1–7 briefly; in three hours, do all seven fully. If tier rises during the work, add omitted Stages 4, 5, 9, and 10 in sequence.

**Proust questionnaire:** short questions such as “When were you happiest?” and “What do you fear most?” that quickly expose a person's taste and values. It is named after Marcel Proust, who enjoyed answering it, and works well for abbreviated builds.

## Preview of the Division of Work at Each Stage

Before practical work, preview responsibility. At Stage 1, the producer binds five answers and decides one-line compression; AI produces first answers for card keywords. At Stage 2, the producer adopts measures and decides the one-word identity; AI drafts answers to thirty short questions. At Stage 3, the producer reviews tone and acceptance threshold; AI drafts the 1,500-character story. At Stage 4, the producer chooses cliché and twist; AI prepares the six-factor matrix and five variants. At Stage 5, the producer chooses one physical anchor; AI drafts five behaviors and ability benefits/side effects. At Stage 6, the producer decides name and one appearance factor; AI offers names and appearance branches. At Stage 7, the producer decides four flaw axes and reviews careless trauma use; AI compresses the chain. At Stage 8, the producer phrases Want/Need/Lie precisely; AI drafts seven-line compression and desire-hierarchy mapping. At Stage 9, the producer compresses one core conflict; AI maps eight types and checks five layers. At Stage 10, the producer judges cast tone and work fit; AI distributes the five-axis matrix. At Stage 11, the producer reviews character tone and speech threshold; AI drafts questions, letters, and diaries. At Stage 12, the producer judges media-specific thresholds; AI converts across five formats.

The larger pattern is simple: **AI is strongest at rapid first output, format conversion, and repetition; the producer is strongest at decision, compression, and acceptance judgment.** The two parts separate inside every stage.

This division reappears throughout the remaining chapters.
